This is the final practice: three complete readings and a synthesis quiz. It teaches no new material. Every figure, rule, technique, and answer aid has already been introduced.

The work now is practice rather than instruction. You will read three unfamiliar charts. The first tests whether you can use the method on a question class that has not been fully worked before. The second tests whether you can keep the reading order when the chart has strong findings on both sides. The third asks one question in both systems and tests whether you can keep the shield method and Gerard's method separate.

Each reading has a **staged model solution** in three parts: cast verification, findings to review, and the model reading. Open the stages in order. The models use the Lesson 10 answer template because it is a useful teaching aid. Your reading may use those headings or ordinary prose, but it must give a clear answer, reasons, timing if supported, advice, and limits.

## The check-in

Before casting anything, have two things ready: the **Gerard card** from Lesson 12 and the **question map** from Lesson 11. If either is missing, finish it before beginning.

Retake Lesson 10's Exercise 4 before the first sitting. Then read your old answer critically. Mark three things: what still holds, what now seems overstated, and what applies only to the shield method. Reading II tests the reading order directly.

## How to do the final practice

Do three sittings, one reading each, with at least a day between sittings. Do not run the three charts in one evening. For each sitting, write the full chart record before the answer. Use the worksheet if it helps you keep the order straight. Do not skip a step because it looks obvious.

**What is open.** Use the corpus and library, [the placement lookup](/tools/placement-lookup/), the technique pages, and your two cards. This is not a memory test. The test is whether you can use the sources and keep the findings in their proper roles.

**On supplied questions.** The charts come with questions attached, as the old casebooks' teaching charts did. Treat each as the assigned question: decide who the querent is, write that down first, and read for that person. The advice part especially needs a person addressed.

**What is closed.** Keep the model solutions closed until the proper stage. Each reading has three stages. **Stage 1, the cast, verified** gives only structural facts. Open it after your derivation or erection is written and checked, before reading. **Stage 2, the findings to review** gives relevant findings and rules without an answer. Open it only if stuck, and record that you did. **Stage 3, the model reading** gives a complete written reading. Open it only after your own answer is written. Treat it as another worked reading, not an answer key.

The model solutions are not answer keys. The scan has factual parts: perfection is present or absent, and mistakes there are mistakes. A complete reading follows rules, but a student can differ from the model if the cast is correct, the source notes are clear, and each finding stays in its role.

**What each sitting must produce.** Write one complete reading. It must include the question and class, the house assignment with a one-sentence source note, the full cast or erection, the Lesson 9 chart record, the reading rules named where they do work, a clear answer with reasons and limits, and a grading note after Stage 3.

## Reading I: the thing hoped for

*A querent asks about a hoped-for thing that depends partly on other people's help:* **"Will the querent get what they hope for?"**

This first reading applies the method to a class no worked example has read in full. Assign the question to its house and support the assignment in one sentence. Then run the full reading procedure: verified derivation, triad and row, significators and springings, perfection scan and direction, review, refinements, reading rules, and answer. Quote the quesited figure's Book II column. Add a parallel-source note only where it helps explain a finding or a limit. In an unfamiliar class, read the house's chapter-head before looking at the chart; it tells you what the house can answer.

## Reading II: the prisoner

*A man lies in prison. One who loves him asks for him:* **"Will the prisoner be released?"**

The second reading tests the reading order. Much of the chart will tempt you toward denial, but the method gives another answer if you scan before reviewing the details. The question belongs to the twelfth house: prison, where the asker has no direct sight of the matter. Run the method in order and write each step before the next.

This chart tests whether you keep each finding in its role. A reader who adds up difficult findings may deny release. A triad-first reader will see the Judge and deny release. A lookup-only reader will read the prison column and deny release. The course method does something different: scan first, then assign each finding to the question it can answer. Write the result plainly, with the serious-answer discipline from Lesson 11.

There is conjunction: **the querent's Tristitia stands in the eleventh, beside the twelfth**, while nothing of the prison approaches the first. The chart perfects by the querent's approach. By Rule 1, the scan gives the answer: *he comes out.* Now place the contrary finding. **The quesited's finding:**

  <Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-12/cauda-draconis" />

  "Signifyeth **to holde a prisoner**… For all the demaundes which ye may make in this house this figure is ill." The prison's own column says *held*. That is strong finding about how the prison holds, but it is not the answer. The scan gives the answer. The triad then supports it: Acquisitio and Via yield Amissio, and the row (`cattan/book-3/witness-judge/acquisitio--via`) says **"prison: come out."** The chart is divided: the prison column says held; the scan and triad row say delivered. The reading order resolves the division. Rule 1 gives whether he comes out to the scan. Rule 2 gives the triad the end and course. The column describes the resistance. **The Judge.** Amissio means loss, and Rule 9 makes the question decisive: in a prison question, loss can mean loss of the bonds. The reading is flagged as the course's application of Rule 9, supported by Amissio's first-house exception for prisoners and by the row's own *come out*. The deliverance still costs: the row says substance ill, and Puer is doubled in the second and third. Carcer on the tenth shows resistant officials. The Part of Fortune in the twelfth on Cauda makes the exit the only gain in the matter. The way of the point ends in the eighth, showing fear of the worst.

  **The written answer.** **Answer:** delivered; he comes out of prison, because the chart perfects and the triad row says *come out*. **Manner:** with difficulty and against resistance from officials. **End:** loss of the bonds, but also loss of money or estate in getting him free. **Timing:** slow, with stable figures on the asker and the officials; the exact when is a separate question. **Advice:** keep pressing the suit; the perfection is the asker's approach, so continued effort is part of the answer. Spend what must be spent, and tell the prisoner only what the chart says: he comes out, but not quickly. **Limits:** the prison column's *held* remains recorded as the main contrary finding. This reading answers release only, not guilt, charge, or life after release.
</SolutionStage>

## Reading III: one question, two systems

*A marriage is intended, and the families are asking. The question can be read in both systems:* **"Will the intended marriage go ahead?"**

The last reading asks the same question in both systems. First, cast and read the shield chart by the full shield method. Second, separately erect and read Gerard's chart on the same question. Use Gerard's signs, lords, and parts. If you use the Lesson 10 answer template for Gerard, add the source note: **This template comes from Lesson 10. Gerard does not prescribe it.** Finally, write one comparison note: *agree*, *disagree*, or *answer different parts*, and say exactly where. Do not use a sign, lord, rule, or term from one system in the other.

Use this order. Finish and date the shield reading before reading the Gerard chart. Otherwise the second reading will be shaped by the first. This sitting tests whether you can keep two complete methods separate until both answers are written.

## Ongoing practice

After the third sitting, the course's lessons are done and practice begins. Cast only real questions. Read each chart carefully. When both systems suit the question, read both systems separately and note whether they agree. Keep enough record to check yourself later: the question, the chart, the answer, and the outcome when known. Checking readings against outcomes is how the practice improves.

## Grading yourself

Self-assessment needs a rubric because vague review is easy to distort. Do not ask "was that good?" Ask the five questions below and answer them in writing beside each sitting:

| Criterion | Passes when | Fails when |
|---|---|---|
| The cast | Your derivation or erection agrees with Stage 1 exactly, verified in writing before reading | Any figure, sign or placement differs: a miscast chart fails the cast, not the reader; recast and resit |
| The placements | Every finding used cites its source: a column, a row, a technique, a part | Any sentence of the reading that cannot answer "says who?" |
| The rules | Each rule that did work is named where it worked, and no rule is made to do another's work | Deciding by instinct with citations added afterward |
| The answer | The answer is clear, the reasons are traceable, timing is given only when supported, and advice does not soften the result | The answer is evasive, the reasons are unclear, or advice changes the answer |
| The systems | In Reading III and after it: each system is read in its own terms, and the Lesson 10 template is named if used with Gerard | One sign, lord, or rule is imported from the other system without a source note |

Three grading rules follow. First, **a supported difference may be acceptable**. If your answer differs from the model reading but cites its sources and keeps the reading order, record and support the difference. A scan error is different; it fails the reading. Second, **Stage 2 openings are recorded, not punished** if used honestly. Third, **one resit per reading** is allowed after a day, with both attempts kept.

## You have finished this course when…

- You completed all three readings and graded them with the rubric.
- The cast or erection is exact in all three readings.
- Every difference from a model solution is either conceded as an error or supported in writing.
- Reading II's answer is *delivered*. If you denied release instead, you resat the chart after a day and wrote why your denial still follows the reading order.
- Reading III uses one question in two systems, keeps the systems separate, and includes a written comparison note.
- You took the synthesis quiz from memory, checked it against the key, and either corrected or supported every miss.
- You kept the Gerard card and the question map for use after the course.

## After the Course

There is no next lesson. The site remains as a working library. Use the [library](/library/) for the sources, especially Cattan's worked cases and the Gerard chapters not assigned in the course. Use the [technique pages](/techniques/) during readings. Use the [figures](/figures/) and [houses](/houses/) for placement columns. Study the Witness-Judge tables slowly. The [course charts](/course/charts/) remain as standing questions you can recast and compare against new charts. From here, keep your own record: the question, the chart, the reading, and the outcome when known.

The course closes with one source's own limit. Cattan, in the middle of Book III, pausing between techniques, wrote this about a use of geomancy he was about to teach:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-19-to-knowe-what-an-other-thinketh" />

"Not to the intent that any man give any credit or trust to it more then to the rest of the Arte, which was for none other purpose invented (as I have many times protested and said) but for the pastime and pleasure of the wittie heads and inventive." Treat that as a limit stated inside the source. Cattan wrote a serious method and still left room for caution and pleasure. The practical rule is simple: use the method carefully, keep clear records, and do not claim more certainty than the sources give.

The course is finished when you have completed the hope reading, the prison reading, and the marriage reading in both systems. Practice continues after the course. Ask one clear question. Cast one chart. Write one complete reading. Later, check the answer against what happened.
