This lesson teaches how to read the two Witnesses and the Judge at the foot of the shield. Their office is limited: they state how the matter, taken as a whole, tends to end.

The three figures give a summary reading before any house is weighed. The Right Witness gathers one side of the chart, the Left Witness gathers the other, and the Judge is made from both. Its question is the issue of the matter: does the business come to a good end or an ill one? Cattan gives sixteen pages of tables for reading that summary. But this is not yet the same thing as answering the querent. Whether a marriage takes effect, a partnership is restored, or a suit is won depends on the houses and significators, which Lesson 7 treats.

Lesson 4 proved that the Judge of a true chart is always even. Here the same arithmetic does more work: it checks the 1591 print of Cattan's tables, identifies three corrupt columns, and shows how they should read.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lesson 4's derivation (Right Witness from the First and Second Nieces, Left Witness from the Third and Fourth, Judge from the two Witnesses) and parity proof. If you cannot say why a true Judge must be even, reread that proof first. It also assumes Lesson 5's houses and significators, because the lesson ends by separating what the Judge can decide from what only the houses can decide.

Crowley's chapter IV is mostly tables: the same Witness–Judge material recompiled for the Golden Dawn, arranged by the Left Witness where Cattan's leaves are headed by the Right, and with modern row labels. This lesson applies the method to all three standing charts, with Chart A treated at full length.

## The two Witnesses

Begin where Lesson 4 began, with Cattan's own chapter; read it again now, with five lessons behind you instead of three:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-03-of-the-two-witnesses-chap" />

Two distinct framings of the Witnesses live in this one paragraph, and the sources use both.

The first framing is by **parties**. "The right witnes is put for the querant," Cattan says, and the left for "the thing demaunded, propounded and enquired." Each Witness represents one side of the question. Cattan also gives the figures each Witness gathers up. The Right Witness contains what is discerned from the first, second, third, fourth, ninth, and tenth figures. The Left Witness contains the fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, eleventh, and twelfth. These lists follow the derivation. Houses one through four are the Mothers, and houses nine and ten are the First and Second Nieces, so Cattan's right-hand list is exactly the ancestry of the Right Witness. The left-hand list is the same for the Left Witness: the Daughters in houses five through eight, and their Nieces in the eleventh and twelfth. Since the first house belongs to the querent and the seventh often belongs to the other party or thing asked about, the right side belongs to the asker and the left to the matter asked.

The second framing is by **time**. In the same passage Cattan says the Right Witness signifies the matter lately passed, and the Left Witness the thing to come. The Right Witness is the matter's beginning and past; the Left Witness is its future course; the Judge, as the next section shows, is its end. Do not take Cattan's "joy" and "heavinesse" as fixed meanings for the two sides. The figure itself decides the quality. A Rubeus on the right is not joy; it is a hot and disordered beginning.

The two framings are not rivals. The right side of the shield is the side of origin: the Mothers came directly from the cast, and the querent belongs with the beginning of the question. The left side is the side of development: the Daughters are derived from the Mothers, and the thing asked about belongs with what the matter becomes. In practice, use the compound reading: the Right Witness is where the matter comes from, the querent's side and the beginning; the Left Witness is where it goes, the matter's side and the course to come. When the two framings pull apart, as when a querent asks about another person's affairs, record the tension instead of forcing one rule to cover it.

Note, finally, the part you have met twice already and will now use: the Witnesses "be no houses, neither naturall Figures, but bee onely accidentalles, taken from the other to give a judgement certaine on the question propounded." They have no address on the wheel and no department of life; they cannot be significators; Step 0 never looks at them. They exist for one purpose only: to be weighed, and to beget the figure that weighs them.

## The Judge and the End

Then the fifteenth figure, in Cattan's chapter, also a re-reading, and richer the second time:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-04-of-the-judge-chap" />

Take the rule in three pieces. First, the office: the Judge is "procreated of the two witnesses to judge the ende of all the signification of the demaund, to know if it be good or bad." Its office concerns the end of the matter as a whole, not the parties, circumstances, or route. "If the Judge be good, the signification of the demand shall come to a good ende, and if he be ill, it shall come to an ill ende." Whether the matter comes to the querent, so that the asker obtains the thing, is a different question. Cattan does not say the Judge alone answers it.

Second, the validity part, which you proved in Lesson 4: "The which Judge ought alwayes of necessitie to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false." Before the lesson is over, that rule will expose a printing error.

Third, for later lessons, Cattan gives a rule of *accord*: if the Judge agrees with the first figure and the right side, that is good for the querent in the thing demanded; if it agrees with the left side, it shows "an ill issue of the thing demaunded." Agreement here means the kinds of kinship learned in Lesson 3: same figure, same element, same planet. The fuller rules for likeness between figures arrive in Lessons 8 and 9.

Heydon's chapter "Of the Judge" reprints Cattan's chapter almost verbatim, so it counts as the same source line again, not a second one (Heydon, *Theomagia* (London, 1663-4), Book II, ch. 17; you quoted a piece of it in Lesson 1). The modern comparison gives the rule in one line:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/witnesses-judge" />

"If the judge be good the figure is good, and vice-versâ" is the Golden Dawn's working summary. Within its limits it is true. The next line is not in the older sources: the "Reconciler," a sixteenth figure made by adding the Judge to the first-house figure. The Golden Dawn used it as a final harmonising finding. Cattan and Heydon do not use it. Treat it as a modern comparison, not as part of the Renaissance method.

## Ill witnesses, good Judge

The three figures make one summary, not three isolated omens. The Witnesses describe the beginning and the course; the Judge gives the end. They need not agree:

- **Good Witnesses, good Judge**: well begun, well carried, well ended: the matter prospers throughout, and there is nothing complicated to do.
- **Ill Witnesses, good Judge**: ill begun and hard in the carriage, but ending in good: the matter passes through difficulty to a gainful conclusion. The end is favourable; the course is not, and an honest reading says both.
- **Good Witnesses, ill Judge**: fair beginnings that come to nothing, or worse: the difficult shape, because everything looks prosperous until the end miscarries. The summary follows the Judge; fair witnesses soften the course, not the conclusion.
- **Mixed Witnesses**: one side fair, one foul: read the time-framing. A good Right and ill Left is a matter that leaves a good past for a worsening course; the reverse is a hard start mending. The Judge still owns the end.

The rule for every combination: **when Witnesses and Judge disagree, the Judge gives the end and the Witnesses qualify the course.** That is the plain sense of Cattan's chapters: the Witnesses exist "to give a judgement certaine," and the Judge judges "the ende." The tables also follow this rule: the same Judge is read differently under different Witnesses.

The addition that makes the Judge is parity arithmetic, not moral arithmetic. Two harsh figures can sum to a good Judge: Chart A has Rubeus plus Tristitia yielding Acquisitio. Two gentle figures can also sum to loss: Laetitia plus Albus gives Amissio. Work the lines yourself, head to feet: fire, 1 and 2, odd, one point; air, 2 and 2, even, two; water, 2 and 1, odd, one; earth, 2 and 2, even, two; lines 1, 2, 1, 2, Amissio. The figures' characters do not add; their points do. A good Judge after difficult Witnesses is not a contradiction. It is exactly the sort of pattern the rule is meant to read.

## The eight possible Judges

Only eight figures can ever give the final summary: the Judge must be even, and only Populus, Via, Conjunctio, Carcer, Acquisitio, Amissio, Fortuna Major and Fortuna Minor carry an even count of points. The other eight figures (the five- and seven-pointed) may stand anywhere else in the chart, Witnesses included, but never at its foot.

Add the corollary this lesson needs. The Judge's count is even exactly when the two Witnesses *together* hold an even count, and that was a link in your own proof. Since the Judge is always even, the two Witnesses of a true chart always hold an even count between them, which can only happen if **the two Witnesses are of the same parity**: both even-pointed, or both odd-pointed. An even Witness on the right and an odd one on the left is as sure a sign of miscasting as an odd Judge. It holds in every chart: Chart A's Rubeus and Tristitia, seven points and seven; Chart B's Tristitia and Cauda Draconis, seven and five; Chart C's Caput Draconis and Albus, five and seven. Same parity, every time, in every chart that can exist. This corollary organizes the tables and supplies one of the two checks used below on the printed columns.

Every reading from here begins with the Judge. These are the eight possible Judges and the tendency each brings to the end of a matter. They are not new rules; they are the natures from Lesson 3 narrowed to one office:

| Judge | Points | The end of the matter |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fortuna Major | 6 | The best ending of the eight: lasting good, won slowly and by one's own strength; an end that holds. |
| Acquisitio | 6 | Gain: the matter ends in getting. Best of all where the question is itself about obtaining; gain, though, says nothing of joy. |
| Fortuna Minor | 6 | Good, but swift and slighter: an end won quickly, by help of others, and held less surely. Favours haste; mistrust it for things meant to last. |
| Conjunctio | 6 | Joining: parties meet, things lost are recovered, threads tie. A neutral conjoiner; it takes colour from its company, ending well among good figures and ill among bad. |
| Populus | 8 | The crowd, the mirror: pure passivity. As Judge it reflects rather than rules, and the sources have a special rule for it, treated below. |
| Via | 4 | Change: the matter ends in motion. Excellent for journeys, removals, and everything that wants to move; dissolving for everything that wants to stay. |
| Amissio | 6 | Loss: the matter goes out of the hand. Ill for keeping and getting; good only where losing is the wish: escape, discharge, the end of a sickness or a debt. |
| Carcer | 6 | Binding: the matter sticks, with delay, restriction, an end that closes around the question. Ill for liberty and progress; good, narrowly, for holding fast and keeping safe. |

The possible Judges are the four self-counterpart figures (Populus, Via, Conjunctio, Carcer) and the two counterpart pairs Acquisitio–Amissio and Fortuna Major–Fortuna Minor. One arithmetic rule is useful: when the two Witnesses are counterparts of one another, each line of their sum is odd, and the Judge is Via. Perfectly opposed Witnesses produce change.

## Cattan's Tables

Between the Judge's chapter and the rest of Book III, Cattan gives sixteen leaves of tables (printed pages 162 through 177, leaves 180 to 195 of the surviving copy's scan). Each leaf is headed by one of the sixteen figures as **Right Witness** and sets it against each of the eight figures of the same parity as **Left Witness**. Eight columns by sixteen leaves gives one hundred and twenty-eight columns: every possible pair of Witnesses, with no impossible mixed-parity pair included. The parity rule from Lesson 4 explains the size of the tables. Cattan's epilogue calls this the signification "of the Judge, and of the witnesses even and uneven": even figures with even figures, uneven with uneven.

Each column gives the two Witnesses, the Judge they make, and ten answer rows for ten common matters. In the 1591 spellings, the rows are: **life, substance, worshippe, possession, a wife, woman with child, sicknesse, prison, iourney, thing lost.** With Lesson 5 in hand, these are house questions: life is the first house; substance the second; worship the tenth; possession the fourth; a wife the seventh; woman with child the fifth; sickness the sixth; prison the twelfth; journey the ninth; and the thing lost is the multi-house recovery case you mapped earlier. Crowley's recompilation states the general rule: "the judgement concerning a wife (*e.g.*) will hold good for all demands of the 7th House. So of the others" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. IV). The row is not about wives only. It is the column's answer for seventh-house matters as a class.

To use the tables, find the leaf headed by the Right Witness, then the column whose second figure is the Left Witness. Check the printed Judge against your own addition. Then read the row that belongs to the question: *substance good*, *prison hard*, *thing lost part yeelded*. Here is one complete column: the Witnesses Via and Populus, whose Judge is Via.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/via--populus" />

Read top to bottom, the column shows the rule at work. The Judge is Via, change. Where the matter wants holding, change is ill: substance ill, possession ill, the thing lost *not found*. Where the matter itself involves motion, change is good: the journey is "good by water"; the prisoner comes "out for nothing." The column is not ten unrelated answers. It is one Judge's nature applied to ten departments, just as Lesson 5 taught you to read a figure through its house.

Two features matter before you use the columns. First, "meane" means middling, indifferent, neither good nor ill. Second, some cells do not answer directly but send you back into the chart: "after the 5," "after the 1," meaning read by the figure in that house. Crowley's version prints these as bare numerals with the gloss that the answer "is determined by the figure in that House of Heaven." The tables therefore state their own limit: some questions must be read by the houses.

All one hundred and twenty-eight columns are transcribed in this site's corpus from the 1591 scan and can be cited by leaf. They are not listed as a standalone lookup page. The course quotes them where the method is being taught, so the table is not separated from the rule that governs its use.

## A Printing Error Found by Arithmetic

Setting one hundred and twenty-eight columns of dot-figures in metal type was difficult work, and in three places the 1591 printing is wrong. The important point is that the errors can be detected and repaired from the table itself. Every Witness–Judge column makes a checkable claim: *the Judge must equal the line-by-line parity sum of its two Witnesses.* Three printed columns make a false claim.

Take the cleanest case, on leaf 181, printed page 163, the table of Laetitia. The seventh column, as printed, reads: first Witness Laetitia (lines 1, 2, 2, 2), second Witness Tristitia (2, 2, 2, 1), Judge Populus (2, 2, 2, 2). Add the Witnesses, head to feet: fire, 1 and 2, odd, one point; air, 2 and 2, even, two; water, 2 and 2, even, two; earth, 2 and 1, odd, one. Lines 1, 2, 2, 1: **Carcer**, not Populus. The printed column is impossible as it stands. Lesson 4's total-parity check would not catch this: Laetitia and Tristitia carry fourteen points between them, and the printed Populus is even. Only line-by-line addition catches the error, which is why the journal procedure requires re-derivation and not merely counting.

Something in the column is wrong, but there are three possible repairs: change the Judge to Carcer, change the first Witness, or change the second. The structure of the leaf decides the issue. Each leaf must pair its headline figure with all eight figures of its parity, once each. On the Laetitia leaf as printed, Tristitia appears twice as partner, and Laetitia itself is missing. The seventh column should therefore be Laetitia with Laetitia. Adding any figure to itself gives Populus, so the printed Judge is correct. The error is the second Witness, duplicated from the previous column. Parity arithmetic and the leaf's completeness point to the same repair:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/laetitia--laetitia" />

The other two corrupt columns are on the Acquisitio leaf (printed page 173). Exercise 5 gives you the Laetitia case to work yourself, with the Acquisitio cases as comparisons. In one, the compositor again duplicated a neighbouring partner: Fortuna Major appears where the series requires Conjunctio, under a printed Judge that no pair on the leaf can produce. Completeness restores Acquisitio with Conjunctio, whose Judge is Fortuna Major. In the other, the partner-Witness and the Judge seem to have been exchanged: the column prints Fortuna Minor as partner where the series requires Carcer, and Acquisitio with Carcer yields Fortuna Minor. On this site, all three columns are transcribed with their reconstructions, marked at a lower quality grade, and annotated with both the 1591 reading and the reason for the correction.

The practical point is simple: because the shield is a structure, it can be checked. The same line-by-line verification that corrects a printed column also protects your own charts.

## Populus as Judge

One of the eight possible Judges has a special rule. Cattan states it in an "advertisement" appended to the tables, on the same leaf where chapter 6 begins. After giving Populus columns for completeness, he writes: "Moreover, I have here placed the figure *Populus* for a Judge, contrarie to the opinion of all the Doctors in this Science, the which say all with one accorde, that when she is found in this place, that then the figure formed is not to be made, or question propounded, is not to be judged by him, but that question must bee judged by the foure angles and other rules, which wee will hereafter shewe, by the which they may be as well and certainely judged, as by the Judge himselfe" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 6, "An advertisement of this Table here going before," prefixed to the chapter).

The rule is clear: when Populus is Judge, do not read by the Judge alone. Read instead "by the foure angles" (the first, fourth, seventh, and tenth houses) and by the other rules. Cattan prints the Populus columns for completeness, but the advertisement says they are not enough for a full reading.

The arithmetic explains why. Adding Populus to any figure returns that figure unchanged. Populus contributes no change of parity. Populus becomes Judge exactly when the two Witnesses agree on every line: **Populus is Judge precisely when the Right and Left Witnesses are the same figure.** When the two Witnesses are identical, the summary gives no contrast between the sides. The information must be sought in the houses.

Course practice: **when the Judge is Populus, record the triad and the table column as usual, but mark the summary *withheld*, and note that the answer must come from the four angles and the houses.** Lesson 7 teaches that method.

## What the table cannot see

The triad lookup is useful: a chart's triad can be looked up in Cattan's own words across ten common matters. Its limits matter as much as its use.

**A lookup is a finding, not a reading.** Three boundaries, in ascending order of importance:

First, *the columns assume the matter*. Ten rows serve ten classes of question, and your question must be mapped to its row by the house rules of Lesson 5: the row of "a wife" for the seventh house's accords and adversaries, "substance" for the second's gains, and so through Crowley's generalisation. The table does not know what was asked. You do, and the mapping is your act and your responsibility, supported the way Lesson 5 taught you to support a placement.

Second, *the table itself defers*. You have seen cells that read "after the 5" and "after the 1," sending the reader into the houses, and you have seen the advertisement set Populus aside entirely, with sixteen columns of the hundred and twenty-eight pointing past themselves to the four angles. A tool that states its limits in its own cells should not be treated as a complete reading.

Third, and most important: *the answer columns read the matter in general; the houses make the chart particular*. One hundred and twenty-eight columns stand for sixty-five thousand five hundred and thirty-six possible charts. Each column serves five hundred and twelve different charts in the same way. It cannot see which figure rises, what holds the seventh, or how the significators regard each other. The Witnesses and Judge state the matter's general shape; the houses decide whether this querent obtains this thing. The sources' name for the joining of significators is **perfection**, and it is the subject of Lesson 7.

The boundary in one line: *the Judge tells you how the matter ends; the houses tell you whose end it is.*

The querent asks whether something can be restored, and the Judge is the one figure that says nothing remains as it was. Via is not evil in itself; for travel, removal, or escape it can be good. ### Chart C: the conflict, sharpened

"Will the querent win the lawsuit?" The triad: Right Witness **Caput Draconis**, Left Witness **Albus**, Judge **Acquisitio**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['amissio', 'amissio', 'puella', 'fortuna-minor']} caption="Chart C: Right Witness Caput Draconis, Left Witness Albus, Judge Acquisitio." />

The summary is favorable throughout. The beginning is Caput Draconis, a good entrance. The course is Albus, calm and orderly. The end is Acquisitio, gain. The table agrees in nearly every row:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/caput-draconis--albus" />

Life good, substance good, worship good, possession good, the thing lost *found*. For a lawsuit, the relevant rows could scarcely read better: the querent's honour good, his goods good, what he seeks recoverable. Judge and table agree that the matter ends well.

The houses tell another story. The seventh house, which contains the adversary and the suit, holds **Cauda Draconis**, and the figure repeats in the eleventh. The querent's own house holds **Amissio**, and the figure repeats in the second, the house of his substance. Every placement fact now known points one way; the Witness–Judge summary points the other.

This is not a contradiction. The Judge's summary and the houses answer different questions. The summary concerns the matter as a whole; the houses concern the parties, their condition, and whether the querent's significator reaches the quesited's. A suit can end in gain without that gain belonging to the querent. It can be settled or profitable to someone while the asker's own places show loss. Nothing you have yet learned can decide which of those readings is correct. The conflict is therefore recorded, not resolved. Lesson 10 will weigh Chart C with the full method.

## Update the journal

The third procedure upgrade adds the Witness–Judge summary. Everything from Lessons 4 and 5 remains: cast, verification, Step 0, placements, significators, and placement-level observations. Add the following for the three course charts and every chart in your journal:

1. **The triad, recorded.** Right Witness, Left Witness, Judge, each named with its points, and the parity noted: both Witnesses of one parity, the Judge even.
2. **The summary, in one line.** Both framings compressed, as this lesson has modelled: "begins X, runs Y, ends Z" with the parties' sides noted where they add anything. Plain figure rules only.
3. **The Judge's tendency, named** from the table above, in your own words. Where the Judge is Populus: write *summary withheld: read by the angles*, cite the advertisement, and stop there.
4. **The table's column, looked up.** Find your pair on its leaf (Right Witness's table, Left Witness's column), confirm the printed Judge against your derivation, and copy out the row nearest your question, with the row's actual words, "meane" and "part yeelded" and all, and the citation in the course's form.
5. **Still no answers.** The line you may not cross moves one step and no further: you may now write the matter's summary; you may not write the question's answer. "The matter ends in gain" is a Lesson 6 entry. "I will win the suit" is not; and you have just watched Chart C demonstrate, at length, exactly why the one does not yield the other. The houses are unweighed; perfection is unexamined; the answer column of your journal stays empty one lesson longer.

**All ten matters, read as a column.** Read the rows with their houses. *Life* (first house): meane. *Substance* (second): meane. *Worshippe* (tenth): meane. *Possession* (fourth): meane. The gainful Judge is narrowed by harsh Witnesses. *A wife* (seventh, the row for this question): **ill**. *Woman with child* (fifth): a daughter. *Sicknesse* (sixth): health. *Prison* (twelfth): difficult. *Iourney* (ninth): ill. *Thing lost*: part yeelded. The table serves all matters at once, and the reader uses only the rows the question owns.

Read the row that matters. The standing question is a seventh-house accord, and the seventh-house row says "a wife: ill." The Judge is gainful, and much of the column is middling or fair, but the row for the question itself is bad. The table reads the Judge through the Witnesses. At this level, Chart A reads: the matter ends in gain; the course is harsh; and the row for marriage warns against the bond itself. Gain is not the same thing as happiness.

**And now, what the table cannot see.** The column does not know that Via stands in this chart's seventh house, the house of the accord, or that Via also stands in the second. It does not know that Conjunctio rises in the first, that the chart has four doubled figures, or that both significators are mobile. Five hundred and twelve charts share the triad Rubeus-Tristitia-Acquisitio, and this column serves them all. What makes Chart A this marriage between these parties belongs to the houses. Whether the marriage perfects, so that the matter reaches the querent, is the rule of perfection, and it opens in Lesson 7. The journal entry at this level: *Triad: RW Rubeus (7), LW Tristitia (7), Judge Acquisitio (6); parities valid, derivation verified. Summary: ill begun, hard carried, ends in gain. Table (leaf of Rubeus, col. Tristitia, p. 167): a wife ill; thing lost part yeelded; estates meane. The matter's end is gainful; the bond's own row is bad; whether the significators meet: pending, Lesson 7.* That is a complete Lesson 6 reading.

<Exercise n={5} title="Repair the printed column" kind="drill">
  The seventh column of the Laetitia leaf (leaf 181, printed page 163) of the 1591 tables reads, as printed: first Witness Laetitia, lines 1, 2, 2, 2; second Witness Tristitia, lines 2, 2, 2, 1; Judge Populus, lines 2, 2, 2, 2. Show by parity arithmetic that the column cannot be right as printed; state which checks fail and which, instructively, do not; then determine (from the table's own structure) what the column must originally have said, and support your repair against the alternatives. Write the answer in three parts: the printed claim, the arithmetic, and the reconstruction.

  <ModelAnswer>
    **The printed claim.** Every column of the tables asserts an addition: Judge equals Right Witness plus Left Witness, line by line, by parity. The printed column asserts that Laetitia and Tristitia sum to Populus.

    **The arithmetic.** Perform the addition the column asserts. Fire: 1 and 2, odd, one point. Air: 2 and 2, even, two. Water: 2 and 2, even, two. Earth: 2 and 1, odd, one. Lines 1, 2, 2, 1: **Carcer**. The printed Judge is Populus, lines 2, 2, 2, 2. The assertion is false; the column is corrupt; this is certainty, not probability, exactly as Cattan's own validity part has it: a Judge that does not answer to its Witnesses means "the figure should be false." The coarse checks all pass: Laetitia and Tristitia are both seven-point figures, lawfully of one parity; their joint count, fourteen, is even; the printed Judge's count, eight, is even. A student checking only total parity would miss the error. Only full line-by-line addition catches it, which is why the journal procedure demands re-derivation and not merely counting.

    **The reconstruction.** Three single repairs are arithmetically available: emend the Judge to Carcer, keeping both Witnesses; emend the first Witness; or emend the second. Arithmetic alone cannot choose, so use the table's structure. Each leaf should run its headline figure once against each of the eight figures of its parity. On the Laetitia leaf as printed, Tristitia appears as partner twice and Laetitia itself appears nowhere as partner. The missing column is therefore Laetitia with Laetitia. A figure added to itself yields Populus, so Laetitia with Laetitia gives Judge **Populus**, matching the printed Judge. The printed Judge should remain; the printed partner should be corrected. Emending the Judge to Carcer would fix this column's arithmetic but leave two Tristitia columns and no Laetitia-with-Laetitia column. Emending the first Witness would alter the leaf's headline. ## You are ready to continue when…

- You can derive the triad from any shield without hesitation, verify it line by line, and state both parity checks (the Judge even, the Witnesses of one parity) with the reason for each.
- You can give both framings of the Witnesses, reconcile them from the shield's structure, and cite Cattan's chapter for both, knowing exactly which of Heydon's chapters is independent and which repeats Cattan.
- You can name the eight possible Judges from memory, give each Judge's tendency in a line, and prove why no other figure can ever be Judge.
- You can find any pair of Witnesses in the tables, map any well-formed question to its row by house, and read a column as one Judge refracted through ten departments, predicting its drift before you look.
- You can state the Populus rule (its source in the advertisement, and the structural proof that Populus only occurs with identical Witnesses) and your procedure withholds the summary accordingly.
- You can run the line-by-line check on any printed Witness–Judge column and explain, with the leaf-181 case, why total parity can pass while full re-derivation catches the error.
- You can recite the three course charts' triads and summaries, quote each one's table row for its standing question, and state precisely, without resolving, what each reading leaves open and which lesson owns the resolution.
- Your journal carries the full third procedure: triads, summaries, tendencies, lookups, surprises, and not one answer.
