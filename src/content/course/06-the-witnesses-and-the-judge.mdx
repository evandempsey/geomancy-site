The Witnesses and the Judge have stood at the foot of every shield since Lesson 4, derived, verified and named but never read. This lesson reads them, and it is the first in which a chart is permitted to *say* something about its question.

The three figures are the shield's own verdict: a summary judgment on the whole matter, delivered before a single house is consulted. The Right Witness gathers up one half of the chart, the Left the other, and the Judge, born of both, pronounces on the *issue*: whether the business comes, on the whole, to a good end or an ill one. The tradition built a remarkable instrument for reading it, sixteen pages of tables, the largest single structure in Cattan's book. But a sentence on the issue is not an answer to the question: whether the marriage is granted, the partnership restored, the suit won are questions about *parties*, and parties live in houses, which is Lesson 7's doctrine. This lesson teaches you to hear the court above the wheel; the next, whether the wheel concurs.

The parity theorem from Lesson 4, that the Judge of a true chart is always even, was introduced as a smoke alarm for your own arithmetic. Here it does more: applied to the 1591 print of Cattan's tables, it convicts three columns a compositor set wrong four hundred and thirty years ago, and reconstructs what they must have said.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lesson 4's derivation (Right Witness from the First and Second Nieces, Left from the Third and Fourth, Judge from the two Witnesses) and parity proof, which it extends by one link and puts to forensic work; if you cannot say why a true Judge must be even, reread that proof first. It also assumes Lesson 5's houses and significators, since its closing argument turns on the boundary between what the Judge decides and what only the houses can.

Crowley's chapter IV is not prose but a battery of tables, the same Witness–Judge tables recompiled for the Golden Dawn: classed by the Left Witness where Cattan's leaves are headed by the Right, with modern row-labels where Cattan's are Elizabethan. All three standing charts come before the bench in this lesson, one at full length.

## The two Witnesses

Begin where Lesson 4 began, with Cattan's own chapter; read it again now, with five lessons behind you instead of three:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-03-of-the-two-witnesses-chap" />

Two distinct framings of the Witnesses live in this one paragraph, and the tradition uses both.

The first framing is by **parties**. "The right witnes is put for the querant," Cattan says, and the left "the thing demaunded, propounded and enquired." Each Witness speaks for one side of the question, and Cattan even tells you which figures each one gathers up. The Right Witness "signifyeth all that which by the first Figure, and all the others which bee on the right side (that is to say the second, third, fourth, ninth and tenth) is discerned"; the Left contains the fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, eleventh and twelfth. Those two lists are not arbitrary: houses one through four are the Mothers, and houses nine and ten are the First and Second Nieces (the Nieces born of the Mothers), which is to say, Cattan's right-hand list is exactly the Right Witness's *ancestry*. The figure summarises the very figures it descends from. The left-hand list is likewise the Left Witness's whole lineage: the Daughters in houses five through eight and their Nieces in the eleventh and twelfth. The doctrine and the derivation are one structure. And since the first house (the querent, always) stands among the Mothers, while the seventh (the other party, the matter of most questions) stands among the Daughters, the right side naturally belongs to the asker and the left to the thing asked.

The second framing is by **time**. In the same breath Cattan says the Right Witness "signifyeth joy and happines of the thing lately passed," and the Left "heavinesse, unquietnesse and mishap of the thing to come." The Right Witness is the matter's past and beginning; the Left is its future and course; the Judge, as the next section will show, is its end. Read this way, the three figures at the shield's foot are the question's whole history compressed into a sentence: whence the matter comes, whither it tends, and how it concludes. (Do not be misled by Cattan's "joy" and "heavinesse" into thinking the Right Witness is always glad and the Left always grim; the figures themselves decide the quality; his point is the *direction of time*, the right side looking back and the left side looking forward. A Rubeus on the right is not joy; it is a hot and disordered past.)

The two framings are not rivals; both read the same geometry. The right side of the shield is the side of *origins*: the Mothers are the four figures that came directly from your hand, the question's first matter, the place the cast began, and the querent, whose moved soul made the cast, belongs with the origins, as does everything already done and past. The left side is the side of the *derived*: the Daughters are the Mothers' own lines rearranged, what the beginning turns into, and the thing asked about, the outcome in doubt, belongs with what develops, as does everything still to come. The querent and the past share the right side because both are where the matter comes from; the quesited and the future share the left because both are where it is going. In practice you will use the compound: the Right Witness is *whence the matter comes*, the querent's side and the beginning; the Left Witness is *whither it goes*, the matter's side and the course of the thing to come. When the two framings pull apart (and in odd cases they can, as when a querent asks about another's affairs and "the querent's side" and "the beginning of the matter" are not obviously the same thing) the tradition does not legislate, and your journal should record the tension rather than flatten it.

Note, finally, the clause you have met twice already and will now use: the Witnesses "be no houses, neither naturall Figures, but bee onely accidentalles, taken from the other to give a judgement certaine on the question propounded." They have no address on the wheel and no department of life; they cannot be significators; Step 0 never looks at them. They exist for one purpose only: to be weighed, and to beget the figure that weighs them.

"A certain Resolution, Sentence, and stay upon the question": that is the office of the three figures in six words, and "stay" is the best of them, the place where the chart's own deliberation comes to rest. ## The Judge and his sentence

Then the fifteenth figure, in Cattan's chapter, also a re-reading, and richer the second time:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-04-of-the-judge-chap" />

Take the doctrine in three pieces. First, the office: the Judge is "procreated of the two witnesses to judge the ende of all the signification of the demaund, to know if it be good or bad." The end, not the parties, not the road, not the circumstances: the issue of the whole matter. "If the Judge be good, the signification of the demand shall come to a good ende, and if he be ill, it shall come to an ill ende." Its scope is exactly its words: the *signification of the demand*, the matter as a whole, comes to its end well or ill. Whether it comes to the *querent* (whether the asker gets the thing) is a different question, and Cattan does not claim the Judge alone answers it.

Second, the validity clause, which you proved as a theorem in Lesson 4 and now see in its native habitat: "The which Judge ought alwayes of necessitie to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false." Before the lesson is out it will catch a compositor red-handed.

Third (for later lessons, not now), a doctrine of *accord*: if the Judge agrees with the first figure and the right side, good to the querent in the thing demanded; if with the left side, "an ill issue of the thing demaunded." Agreement here means kinship of the kinds you learned in Lesson 3 (same figure, same element, same planet) and you can already see the shape of the idea: a Judge that resembles the querent's side has, so to speak, found for the plaintiff; a Judge that resembles the other side has found against him. The full machinery of likeness between figures (company, aspects, the springing of figures) arrives in Lessons 8 and 9; when it does, this clause of Cattan's will be waiting for it.

Heydon's chapter "Of the Judge" reprints this chapter of Cattan's as nearly verbatim as his witnesses chapter did the other: one witness again, not two (Heydon, *Theomagia* (London, 1663–4), Book II, ch. 17; you quoted a piece of it as long ago as Lesson 1). And the modern comparison compresses the whole doctrine to a single stroke:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/witnesses-judge" />

"If the judge be good the figure is good, and vice-versâ": the Golden Dawn's working summary, brisk to the point of bluntness, and within its limits true. The line that follows it is *not* in the old sources and must be flagged as modern: the "Reconciler," a sixteenth figure made by adding the Judge to the figure of the first house, which the Golden Dawn used as a final harmonising testimony. Cattan and Heydon know no such figure. Use it or not in your own later practice, but never read it back into the Renaissance art; in this course it appears only as the modern comparison it is.

## Ill witnesses, good Judge

The three figures make a *sentence*, not three isolated omens: the Judge tells the end, the Witnesses the beginning and the course, and the three need not agree. The combinations read almost as grammar:

- **Good Witnesses, good Judge**: well begun, well carried, well ended: the matter prospers throughout, and there is nothing subtle to do.
- **Ill Witnesses, good Judge**: ill begun and hard in the carriage, but ending in good: the matter passes through difficulty to a gainful conclusion. The end is favourable; the road is not, and an honest reading says both.
- **Good Witnesses, ill Judge**: fair beginnings that come to nothing, or worse: the most treacherous shape, because everything *looks* prosperous until the end miscarries. The sentence is the Judge's; fair witnesses soften the road, not the conclusion.
- **Mixed Witnesses**: one side fair, one foul: read the time-framing. A good Right and ill Left is a matter that leaves a good past for a worsening course; the reverse is a hard start mending. The Judge still owns the end.

The rule that governs every combination: **when Witnesses and Judge disagree, the Judge carries the sentence and the Witnesses qualify the road.** That is the plain sense of Cattan's chapters (the Witnesses exist "to give a judgement certaine," the Judge "to judge the ende") and the tradition's tables, as you are about to see, are built on exactly this grammar: the same Judge is read differently under different Witnesses.

The addition that makes the Judge is *parity* arithmetic, not moral arithmetic. Two of the harshest figures in the art can sum to one of the kindest (you have known since Lesson 4 that Rubeus added to Tristitia yields Acquisitio, because Chart A does exactly that) and two gentle figures can sum to ruin: Laetitia added to Albus, joy and peace, yields Amissio, loss. Work the lines yourself, head to feet: fire, 1 and 2, odd, one point; air, 2 and 2, even, two; water, 2 and 1, odd, one; earth, 2 and 2, even, two; lines 1, 2, 1, 2, Amissio. The figures' characters do not add; their points do. A bright Judge above dark Witnesses is not a contradiction in the chart, any more than a hard war ending in a good peace is a contradiction in history. It is information, and the tradition's grammar exists precisely to read it.

## The eight figures of the bench

Only eight figures can ever deliver the sentence, and you have known which eight since Lesson 4: the Judge must be even, and only Populus, Via, Conjunctio, Carcer, Acquisitio, Amissio, Fortuna Major and Fortuna Minor carry an even count of points. The other eight figures (the five- and seven-pointed) may stand anywhere else in the chart, Witnesses included, but never at its foot.

Add now the corollary that Lesson 4 stopped short of, because this lesson needs it. The Judge's count is even exactly when the two Witnesses *together* hold an even count, and that was a link in your own proof. And since the Judge is always even, it follows that the two Witnesses of a true chart always hold an even count between them, which can only happen if **the two Witnesses are of the same parity**: both even-pointed, or both odd-pointed. An even Witness on the right and an odd one on the left is as sure a sign of miscasting as an odd Judge. It holds in every chart: Chart A's Rubeus and Tristitia, seven points and seven; Chart B's Tristitia and Cauda Draconis, seven and five; Chart C's Caput Draconis and Albus, five and seven. Same parity, every time, in every chart that can exist. This corollary is the organising principle of the tables you are about to meet, and one of the two instruments of the forensic work after.

Every reading from here begins with the Judge, so here is the bench: the eight possible Judges and the tendency each brings to the end of a matter. These are not new doctrines but the natures of Lesson 3 narrowed to one office:

| Judge | Points | The end of the matter |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fortuna Major | 6 | The best sentence of the eight: lasting good, won slowly and by one's own strength; an end that holds. |
| Acquisitio | 6 | Gain: the matter ends in getting. Best of all where the question is itself about obtaining; gain, though, says nothing of joy. |
| Fortuna Minor | 6 | Good, but swift and slighter: an end won quickly, by help of others, and held less surely. Favours haste; mistrust it for things meant to last. |
| Conjunctio | 6 | Joining: parties meet, things lost are recovered, threads tie. A neutral conjoiner; it takes colour from its company, ending well among good figures and ill among bad. |
| Populus | 8 | The crowd, the mirror: pure passivity. As Judge it reflects rather than rules, and the tradition has a special doctrine for it, treated below. |
| Via | 4 | Change: the matter ends in motion. Excellent for journeys, removals, and everything that wants to move; dissolving for everything that wants to stay. |
| Amissio | 6 | Loss: the matter goes out of the hand. Ill for keeping and getting; good only where losing is the wish: escape, discharge, the end of a sickness or a debt. |
| Carcer | 6 | Binding: the matter sticks, with delay, restriction, an end that closes around the question. Ill for liberty and progress; good, narrowly, for holding fast and keeping safe. |

The bench is the four self-counterpart figures (Populus, Via, Conjunctio, Carcer) together with the two counterpart pairs Acquisitio–Amissio and Fortuna Major–Fortuna Minor: the figures that judge are the figures of balance. One arithmetical elegance: whenever the two Witnesses are counterparts of one another (each the other's lines inverted, as Acquisitio and Amissio are), every line of their sum is odd, and the Judge is Via. A beginning and an end that are perfect opposites can only be judged *change*.

## Cattan's tables: the art's ready reckoner

Between the Judge's chapter and the rest of Book III, the 1591 Cattan does something no other part of the book attempts. Chapter 5 is not a chapter of prose at all: it is sixteen leaves of tables (printed pages 162 through 177, leaves 180 to 195 of the surviving copy's scan) one full-page table for each of the sixteen figures. Each table takes its headline figure as the **Right Witness** and runs it against each of the eight figures of the same parity as **Left Witness**, eight columns to a leaf. Eight columns by sixteen leaves is one hundred and twenty-eight columns: by the corollary you proved above, that is *every pair of Witnesses that can exist*, with not one column wasted on an impossible mixed-parity pair. The same theorem that audits your charts dictated the very dimensions of the tradition's printed instrument. Cattan's epilogue to the tables calls this the signification "of the Judge, and of the witnesses even and uneven": the even figures' tables and the uneven figures' tables, the two parities never mixing.

Each column gives three things: the pair of Witnesses in their dot-figures, the Judge they beget, and then ten rows of verdicts, ten standard matters, each with its short sentence. In the 1591 spellings, the ten are: **life, substance, worshippe, possession, a wife, woman with child, sicknesse, prison, iourney, thing lost.** Read that list with Lesson 5's eyes and it resolves instantly into ten house-questions: life is the first house (the querent's person and length of days); substance the second (money and goods); worship (honour, dignity, preferment) the tenth; possession the fourth (lands and inheritances, "the goodes and succession of his father," as Cattan's epilogue glosses it); a wife the seventh; woman with child the fifth; sickness the sixth; prison the twelfth; journey the ninth; and the thing lost, the multi-house case you mapped in Lesson 5's first exercise. The ten matters are the ten commonest questions of a sixteenth-century consulting room, and the table is a ready reckoner across all of them at once. The modern comparison states the generalisation outright: in Crowley's recompilation of these same tables, "the judgement concerning a wife (*e.g.*) will hold good for all demands of the 7th House. So of the others" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. IV). The row is not about wives only; it is the column's verdict for seventh-house matters as a class, which is precisely what makes the tables usable for questions the ten labels never name.

The instrument was a working tool before it was a text. A practitioner of 1591 casts for a client, derives the shield, and has Witnesses and Judge in hand. He turns to the leaf headed by his Right Witness, runs his finger along the columns to the one whose second figure is his Left Witness, and reads. The Judge printed at the column's head confirms his own addition (the table is, incidentally, a printed answer-key for the very derivation you drilled in Lesson 4) and the row of his client's matter gives him the sentence: *substance good*, *prison hard*, *thing lost part yeelded*. One lookup, ten matters deep. Here is one column entire, transcribed on this site as all one hundred and twenty-eight now are: the Witnesses Via and Populus, whose Judge is Via:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/via--populus" />

Read top to bottom, the column shows the doctrine operating. The Judge is Via, change. Where the matter wants holding, change is ill: substance ill, possession ill, the thing lost *not found*, for what is gone moves on. Where the matter *is* motion, change is good: the journey is "good by water" (Via and Populus are both the Moon's company, and the Moon's roads are the wet ones); the prisoner comes "out for nothing," release, the one matter where dissolution is mercy. The column is not ten unrelated oracles; it is one Judge's nature refracted through ten departments, exactly as Lesson 5 taught you a figure refracts through the houses. The tables are vast, but they are not arbitrary, and a student who knows the natures can very nearly *predict* a column before reading it, an excellent drill, and Exercise 2 is built on it.

Two features of the columns need flagging before you use them. First, the verdicts have a vocabulary: "meane" is the Elizabethan *mean* (middling, indifferent, neither good nor ill) and you will meet whole columns of it where the Witnesses pull against the Judge. Second, some cells do not answer at all but *point back into the chart*: "after the 5," "after the 1": judge this matter according to the figure of the fifth house, or the first. Crowley's recompilation prints these as bare numerals with the editorial gloss that the judgment "is determined by the figure in that House of Heaven." They are the tables' own confession that a lookup cannot always decide: some questions go to the houses even when the bench is in session. The limits section below will widen that crack; the tables opened it themselves.

These one hundred and twenty-eight columns are transcribed in this site's corpus, each from the 1591 scan, each citable to its leaf; but you will not find them on any listing page, and that is by design. A table of verdicts detached from the doctrine of its use is exactly the kind of shortcut this course exists to prevent; the columns are quoted where teaching warrants, as here, and your own lookups go through the leaves themselves or through exercises that keep the doctrine attached.

## A printing error caught at four hundred years' distance

Now the promised forensics. Setting one hundred and twenty-eight columns of dot-figures in metal type (thousands of single points, in a script the compositor likely could not "read" in any meaningful sense) was painstaking work, and in three places the 1591 printing house got it wrong. What is remarkable is not that three columns are corrupt; it is that the corruption is *detectable and repairable from the table itself*, by arithmetic you have owned since Lesson 4, and that nothing else would have caught it. No proofreader comparing verdicts against copy would blink at "life good, substance meane"; the verdicts are not where the errors live. The errors live in the dot-figures, and the dot-figures police themselves, because a Witness–Judge column makes a checkable claim: *the Judge must equal the line-by-line parity-sum of its two Witnesses.* Every column asserts an addition. Three of the printed additions are false.

Take the cleanest case, on leaf 181, printed page 163, the table of Laetitia. The seventh column of that leaf, as printed, reads: first Witness Laetitia (lines 1, 2, 2, 2), second Witness Tristitia (2, 2, 2, 1), Judge Populus (2, 2, 2, 2). Add the Witnesses, head to feet: fire, 1 and 2, odd, one point; air, 2 and 2, even, two; water, 2 and 2, even, two; earth, 2 and 1, odd, one. Lines 1, 2, 2, 1: **Carcer**, not Populus. The printed column is impossible as it stands. (Notice, in passing, which alarm rang. Lesson 4's *total*-parity check is silent here: Laetitia and Tristitia carry fourteen points between them and the printed Populus is even, so the smoke alarm sleeps. It is the full line-by-line addition that convicts. The coarse check catches casting errors; the fine one catches everything, which is why your journal protocol has always demanded re-derivation and not merely the count.)

Something in the column is wrong, but what? Three repairs are arithmetically conceivable: emend the Judge to Carcer, emend the first Witness, or emend the second. Here the table's own architecture decides, and elegantly. Each leaf must run its headline figure against all eight figures of its parity, once each; that is what the instrument *is*. On the Laetitia leaf as printed, Tristitia appears as partner twice (in this column and in the column immediately before it) and one partner is missing entirely: Laetitia itself. The seventh column should be Laetitia with Laetitia. And adding any figure to itself yields Populus, as Lesson 4 noted: Laetitia with Laetitia gives Judge *Populus*, exactly what the compositor printed. The printed Judge is correct; the printed partner is a duplication, the compositor's eye having slipped to the previous column's Witness while setting a page of near-identical dot-clusters. Two independent arguments (the parity arithmetic and the leaf's completeness) converge on the same single-character repair, and the column is restored with a confidence few emendations in any textual tradition can claim:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/laetitia--laetitia" />

The other two convicts stand together on the Acquisitio leaf (printed page 173), and Exercise 5 will give you the first of the three to prosecute yourself, with the other two as study pieces. In one column the compositor again duplicated a neighbouring partner: printing Fortuna Major where the series requires Conjunctio, under a printed Judge (Carcer) that no pair of figures on the leaf can produce; the leaf's completeness restores Acquisitio-with-Conjunctio, whose Judge is Fortuna Major. In the other, more curiously, the partner-Witness and the Judge appear to have changed places: the column prints Fortuna Minor as partner where the series requires Carcer, and Acquisitio with Carcer yields Judge *Fortuna Minor*: the compositor set the answer in the question's seat. On this site all three columns are transcribed with their reconstructions, marked at a lower quality grade than their hundred and twenty-five sound neighbours, each carrying an editorial note stating exactly what the 1591 print reads and exactly what argument corrects it. That marking is course policy made visible: a transcription that silently repaired the print would be tidier and falser, and you could never again be sure, reading any column, whether you were reading Cattan or his editors. The honest path (print preserved, error demonstrated, repair argued) is longer and is the only one this course will ever show you.

A theorem you proved in Lesson 4, about a divination system, turned out to be a *property of the printed book* strong enough to detect and repair its corruptions across four hundred and thirty years. The shield is not a heap of omens but a structure, and structures testify. Every time you verify a chart line by line, you do to your own work what was done to the compositor's.

## Populus on the bench

One Judge of the eight has a doctrine all his own, and Cattan placed it, fittingly, in an "advertisement" (a notice to the reader) appended to the tables themselves, on the same leaf where chapter 6 begins. Having walked the reader through all the tables' significations, he confesses to a deliberate liberty: "Moreover, I have here placed the figure *Populus* for a Judge, contrarie to the opinion of all the Doctors in this Science, the which say all with one accorde, that when she is found in this place, that then the figure formed is not to be made, or question propounded, is not to be judged by him, but that question must bee judged by the foure angles and other rules, which wee will hereafter shewe, by the which they may be as well and certainely judged, as by the Judge himselfe" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 6, "An advertisement of this Table here going before," prefixed to the chapter).

The claim is stronger than it looks. *All* the doctors, "with one accorde": Cattan, who delights in reporting the tradition's quarrels, here reports a unanimity: when Populus is the Judge, do not judge by the Judge. Judge instead "by the foure angles" (the figures of the first, fourth, seventh and tenth houses, the strong places of Lesson 5) "and other rules": go down into the wheel, where the parties and circumstances stand, and build the sentence from there. Cattan prints the Populus columns anyway, against the doctors and for completeness (sixteen of his hundred and twenty-eight columns have Populus at their head) but the advertisement makes plain that he prints them as reference, not as licence.

Why should one Judge of eight be recused? The figure's nature answers, and so does the arithmetic. Populus is the mirror: pure passivity, every line doubled, the crowd that takes the shape of whatever moves through it. Adding Populus to any figure returns that figure unchanged; a figure with no parities of its own to contribute has nothing to say. And when does Populus become Judge? (Exercise 4 makes you derive it.) The Judge's every line is even exactly when the two Witnesses agree on every line: **Populus is Judge precisely when the Right and Left Witnesses are the same figure.** Identical beginning and end; both sides of the question wearing one face. The bench falls silent not arbitrarily but because the two testimonies it exists to weigh have cancelled into perfect symmetry; there is no difference between the sides for a sentence to pronounce upon. The doctors' rule is the structure's own conclusion: when the shield's summary collapses into a mirror, the information is in the houses, and to the houses you must go.

Course practice, then, fixed from this lesson: **when the Judge is Populus, record the triad and the table's column as always, but mark the sentence *withheld*, and note that judgment falls to the four angles and the houses, machinery that begins in Lesson 7.** One chart in sixteen, in the long run, seats Populus on the bench.

## What the table cannot see

You now hold a powerful instrument: any chart's triad, looked up in one motion, ten matters deep, in the tradition's own words. Its limits matter as much as its power.

**A lookup is testimony, not judgment.** Three boundaries, in ascending order of importance:

First, *the columns assume the matter*. Ten rows serve ten classes of question, and your question must be mapped to its row by the house-doctrine of Lesson 5: the row of "a wife" for the seventh house's accords and adversaries, "substance" for the second's gains, and so through Crowley's generalisation. The table does not know what was asked. You do, and the mapping is your act and your responsibility, defended the way Lesson 5 taught you to defend a placement.

Second, *the table itself defers*. You have seen the cells that read "after the 5" and "after the 1" (columns where the tradition's own ready reckoner declines to answer and sends the reader into the houses) and you have seen the advertisement recuse Populus entirely, sixteen columns of the hundred and twenty-eight pointing past themselves to the four angles. An instrument that confesses its limits in its own cells should not be credited with omniscience by its users.

Third, and decisively: *the verdict columns judge the matter in general; the houses individuate this chart*. Count what the table can see. One hundred and twenty-eight columns stand for sixty-five thousand five hundred and thirty-six possible charts: every column serves exactly five hundred and twelve different charts, and serves them identically. Every chart whose Nieces sum to Rubeus and Tristitia receives the same ten sentences, whatever stands in its houses: whoever rises, whatever holds the seventh, however the significators regard each other. The column cannot see Via in the house of the marriage; it cannot see Cauda Draconis in the house of the suit; it cannot see anything that makes your chart *yours*. The Witnesses and Judge are the shield's verdict on the whole matter, and the table is that verdict spelled out across ten departments; but the granting or refusal of the thing asked, between this querent and this quesited, is written in the houses or nowhere. The tradition's name for the houses' grant is **perfection**, and it is the entire subject of Lesson 7.

The boundary in a sentence: *the Judge tells you how the matter ends; the houses tell you whose end it is.*

## The course charts before the bench

The three standing charts, in order, read by Witnesses and Judge alone: the first reading this course has permitted. For each: the triad, the sentence in both framings, the table's column, and a strict accounting of what remains open.

### Chart A: harsh witnesses, gainful end

"Will the marriage go ahead?" Right Witness **Rubeus**, Left Witness **Tristitia**, Judge **Acquisitio**, the very triad whose derivation you performed line by line in Lesson 4. The sentence, in the grammar of this lesson: an ill beginning (Rubeus on the right: the matter's origin hot, disordered, passionate), a heavy course (Tristitia on the left: the thing to come weighed down, slow, sorrowful), and a gainful end (Acquisitio: the matter concludes in getting). Ill begun, hard carried, profitably ended, the second of the four combinations, read straight. The marriage matter, says the shield's own court, will cost what it gains and gain nonetheless. This chart is the worked example below, where its full column is read; here it only takes its place and yields the floor.

### Chart B: a restoration judged by change

"Will the querent recover the partnership?" The triad: Right Witness **Tristitia**, Left Witness **Cauda Draconis**, Judge **Via**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['puella', 'rubeus', 'rubeus', 'amissio']} caption="Chart B: Right Witness Tristitia, Left Witness Cauda Draconis, Judge Via." />

Read the sentence. The beginning, on the querent's side: Tristitia, sorrow, the fallen point, the weight of the breach already suffered; no figure better describes how a broken partnership *feels* from inside it. The course and the matter's side: Cauda Draconis, the Dragon's Tail, the figure of endings and departures, the way out. And the end: Via, change. Lesson 4, which could read nothing yet, still flagged this shield's shape exactly: a chart about restoration whose final word is *change*. Now you can say it doctrinally: the querent asks whether something can be put back as it was, and the Judge (the end of all the signification of the demand) is the one figure that means *nothing stays as it was*. Via is not an evil figure; for a journey, a removal, an escape, it is a friend. But this question wants restoration, and the sentence is motion.

Then the table darkens it. Here is the column, the leaf of Tristitia, the column of Cauda Draconis:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/tristitia--cauda-draconis" />

This is one of the blackest columns on the sixteen leaves. For the matters nearest a partnership question, the rows are merciless: substance *very ill*, possession *very ill*, the thing lost *not found*; and a thing lost and sought again is, at sentence level, very nearly what this question is. Where the bare Judge said only "change," the column (which sees the Witnesses, sorrow feeding into departure) says change *of the losing kind*. Note the one glimmer for completeness: the journey row is merely "meane," Via's nature favouring the only matter in the list that moves; it does not touch this question.

And now the strict accounting. Sentence-level, this chart is dark: a sorrowful beginning, an ending-shaped course, a Judge of change over a question of restoration, and a table column that calls everything ownable very ill. But you read Lesson 5, and you know what none of this has yet consulted: the houses, where Puella (the gentlest figure in the art) holds the querent's own first house, and where the seventh, the partnership's place, holds Carcer, the figure that does not let go. Whether the houses *grant* anything that the bench has darkened, whether the bond is locked shut or locked in: the instruments for that question begin in Lesson 7, and this course will weigh Chart B's houses against Chart B's sentence when, and only when, you hold them. Note the tension in your journal in exactly that unresolved form. It is not a defect of the reading; it is the reading, honestly sized.

### Chart C: the conflict, sharpened

"Will the querent win the lawsuit?" The triad: Right Witness **Caput Draconis**, Left Witness **Albus**, Judge **Acquisitio**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['amissio', 'amissio', 'puella', 'fortuna-minor']} caption="Chart C: Right Witness Caput Draconis, Left Witness Albus, Judge Acquisitio." />

Read the sentence and it is fair throughout, the *first* combination, the easy one. The beginning: Caput Draconis, the Dragon's Head, the figure of thresholds and fortunate entrances, a matter well begun, a door opening. The course: Albus, white peace, the figure of wisdom and quiet, a course that runs smooth. The end: Acquisitio, gain. Well begun, well carried, gainfully ended. And the table concurs in nearly every row:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/caput-draconis--albus" />

Life good, substance good, worship good, possession good, the thing lost *found*. For a lawsuit (a matter of standing and substance, a tenth-and-second-house affair wrapped around a seventh-house adversary) the relevant rows could scarcely read better: the querent's honour good, his goods good, what he seeks recoverable. Judge and table, bench and book, speak with one voice: this matter ends well.

The houses tell another story. The house of this very question (the seventh, the adversary, the suit) holds **Cauda Draconis**, one of the two figures dreadful enough that Step 0 exists for them, and it holds it doubled, repeating in the eleventh. The querent's own house holds **Amissio** (loss itself) doubled into the second, the house of his substance, so that the asker and his money both wear the figure of losing. Every placement-level fact you own pulls one way; every sentence-level fact you have just learned pulls the other. A bright Judge, a fair column, over the darkest pair of significators in the three course charts.

This is *not* a contradiction in the art: the Judge's sentence and the houses' testimony are answers to different questions. The sentence concerns the overall issue of the matter: how the business, as a whole, concludes. The houses concern its circumstances and its parties: who stands where, in what strength, wearing what figure, and whether the querent's significator and the quesited's ever reach one another at all. A suit can end in gain that is not the querent's gain; it can end well as a *matter* (settled, concluded, profitable to someone) while the asker's own places wear loss; it can be granted by the sentence and refused by the houses, or the reverse. Which of those this chart is saying (whether the gain is the querent's victory, or his adversary's, or a settlement that gains him half and costs him more, or something subtler than any of these) cannot be decided by anything you now hold, and this course will not pretend otherwise. The conflict is now fully drawn: Lesson 4 saw it structurally, Lesson 5 located it in the houses, and this lesson has sharpened it to its true shape: the shield's court against the wheel's testimony. Its resolution requires the whole remaining apparatus of judgment (perfection, dignity, aspect, the weighing of all testimonies) and it is reserved, deliberately and explicitly, for **Lesson 10**, where Chart C will stand as the master example of how a trained judge weighs a divided chart. Until then it stays open in your journal.

## The journal, upgraded

The third protocol upgrade, and the first that lets your charts speak. Everything from Lessons 4 and 5 stands (the cast, the verification, Step 0, the placements, the significators, the placement-level observations) and to it you now add, for the three course charts and every cast in your journal, current and future:

1. **The triad, recorded.** Right Witness, Left Witness, Judge, each named with its points, and the parity noted: both Witnesses of one parity, the Judge even. (You have been recording the figures since Lesson 4; you are now recording them *as a court*.)
2. **The sentence, in one sentence.** Both framings compressed, as this lesson has modelled: "begins X, runs Y, ends Z" with the parties' sides noted where they add anything. Plain figure-doctrine only.
3. **The Judge's tendency, named** from the bench-table above, in your own words. Where the Judge is Populus: write *sentence withheld: judge by the angles*, cite the advertisement, and stop there.
4. **The table's column, looked up.** Find your pair on its leaf (Right Witness's table, Left Witness's column), confirm the printed Judge against your derivation, and copy out the row nearest your question, with the row's actual words, "meane" and "part yeelded" and all, and the citation in the course's form.
5. **Still no verdicts.** The line you may not cross moves one step and no further: you may now write the matter's sentence; you may not write the question's answer. "The matter ends in gain" is a Lesson 6 entry. "I will win the suit" is not; and you have just watched Chart C demonstrate, at length, exactly why the one does not yield the other. The houses are unweighed; perfection is unexamined; the verdict column of your journal stays empty one lesson longer.

## Worked example

Chart A before the bench at full length: the complete Witness–Judge protocol, ending where this lesson's licence runs out.

The standing question: **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** The shield, from Mothers Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis:

<ShieldChart mothers={['conjunctio', 'via', 'amissio', 'caput-draconis']} caption="Chart A: Right Witness Rubeus, Left Witness Tristitia, Judge Acquisitio." />

**The triad, re-derived.** Never read a triad you have not verified. First Niece Carcer added to Second Niece Puer: fire, 1 and 1, even, two points; air, 2 and 1, odd, one; water, 2 and 2, even, two; earth, 1 and 1, even, two; lines 2, 1, 2, 2, **Rubeus**, the Right Witness. Third Niece Puella added to Fourth Niece Amissio: fire, 1 and 1, two; air, 2 and 2, two; water, 1 and 1, two; earth, 1 and 2, one; lines 2, 2, 2, 1, **Tristitia**, the Left Witness. Rubeus added to Tristitia: fire, 2 and 2, two; air, 1 and 2, one; water, 2 and 2, two; earth, 2 and 1, one; lines 2, 1, 2, 1, **Acquisitio**, the Judge. Checks: Rubeus seven points and Tristitia seven, one parity, as must be; the Judge six points, even, as must be. The court is lawfully constituted.

**The sentence.** By time: the matter begins in Rubeus, hot, hasty, disordered; whatever brought this marriage question to the diviner's table, the chart calls its origin turbulent. It runs through Tristitia: the course of the thing to come is heavy, slow, freighted with sorrow or at the least with gravity. It ends in Acquisitio: the matter concludes in gain. By parties: the querent's side wears the red figure, the matter's side the leaden one; neither party's Witness flatters them. The compound sentence, as your journal should compress it: *ill begun, hard carried, ends in gain.*

**The lookup.** The leaf of Rubeus (the Right Witness heads the table) and along its columns to Tristitia. The printed Judge: Acquisitio, agreeing with the hand derivation, as it must. The column entire:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/rubeus--tristitia" />

**All ten matters, read as a column.** Walk the rows with their houses, as a 1591 practitioner would have walked them for ten different clients in a week. *Life* (the first house's question): meane, for under these Witnesses, even a gainful Judge promises the body and person nothing better than the middling. *Substance* (second): meane, gain but hedged. *Worshippe* (tenth): meane, no honours pour from this triad. *Possession* (fourth): meane, four "meanes" in a row, and notice what they are: Acquisitio's native generosity in exactly the four estate-matters it loves best, filed down to indifference by the two harshest parents on the sixteen leaves. The column is teaching you, in four words, how Witnesses qualify a Judge. *A wife* (seventh, the row our question lives in): **ill**. We return to it. *Woman with child* (fifth): a daughter, for the column answers a question nobody here asked; a reminder that the table serves all matters at once and the reader takes only the rows the question owns. *Sicknesse* (sixth): health, for Acquisitio holds what it has, including the patient. *Prison* (twelfth): difficult, for the holding figure holds prisoners too; gain's grip has two faces. *Iourney* (ninth): ill, for Rubeus and Tristitia make poor travelling companions, and Acquisitio is a figure of staying and keeping, not of roads. *Thing lost*: part yeelded, the most Acquisitio-after-harsh-Witnesses verdict imaginable: you recover something, and not all.

Now the row that matters. The standing question is a seventh-house accord, and the seventh-house row of this column ("a wife: ill") is the *one dark verdict among the ten*. The Judge is gainful; the column's general tenor is middling-to-fair; and on the precise matter this chart was cast for, the tradition's own table says *ill*. This is the grammar of the lesson operating at full precision: the table reads the Judge *through* the Witnesses, and Rubeus-into-Tristitia (passion souring into sorrow) is a biography no one would wish on a marriage, whatever the estate ends up worth. Sentence-level, Chart A now reads: the matter ends in gain; the road there is harsh; and the table warns that for the marriage itself, as a bond between two people, this triad bodes ill even while the end-of-matter promises profit. Gain, the column quietly insists, is not the same thing as happiness.

**And now, what the table cannot see.** The column does not know that Via (the most changeable figure of the sixteen) stands in this chart's seventh house, the house of the very accord in question. It does not know that the same Via stands again in the second, directly beside the querent. It does not know that Conjunctio, the joiner, rises in the first house; that the chart carries four doubled figures; that both significators are mobile. It cannot know any of it: five hundred and twelve different charts share the triad Rubeus–Tristitia–Acquisitio, and this same column serves every one of them identically: charts where the marriage's house holds Puella, charts where it holds Carcer, charts of every complexion the wheel allows. Everything that makes Chart A *this* marriage between *these* parties lives in the houses, below the court's line of sight. Whether the accord is *granted* (whether the querent's Conjunctio and the matter's Via reach one another by any of the ways the tradition allows) is the doctrine of perfection, and it opens in Lesson 7. The journal entry, in full, at this lesson's level: *Triad: RW Rubeus (7), LW Tristitia (7), Judge Acquisitio (6); parities lawful, derivation verified. Sentence: ill begun, hard carried, ends in gain. Table (leaf of Rubeus, col. Tristitia, p. 167): a wife ill; thing lost part yeelded; estates meane. The matter's end is gainful; the bond's own row is dark; whether the houses grant the accord: pending, Lesson 7.* That is a complete Lesson 6 reading: a true sentence, honestly bounded.

<Exercise n={5} title="Prosecute the compositor" kind="drill">
  The seventh column of the Laetitia leaf (leaf 181, printed page 163) of the 1591 tables reads, as printed: first Witness Laetitia, lines 1, 2, 2, 2; second Witness Tristitia, lines 2, 2, 2, 1; Judge Populus, lines 2, 2, 2, 2. Show by parity arithmetic that the column cannot be right as printed; state which checks fail and which, instructively, do not; then determine (from the table's own structure) what the column must originally have said, and defend your repair against the alternatives. Write it as a prosecution: the charge, the evidence, the reconstruction.

  <ModelAnswer>
    **The charge.** Every column of the tables asserts an addition: Judge equals Right Witness plus Left Witness, line by line, by parity. The printed column asserts that Laetitia and Tristitia sum to Populus.

    **The evidence.** Perform the addition the column asserts. Fire: 1 and 2, odd, one point. Air: 2 and 2, even, two. Water: 2 and 2, even, two. Earth: 2 and 1, odd, one. Lines 1, 2, 2, 1: **Carcer**. The printed Judge is Populus, lines 2, 2, 2, 2. The assertion is false; the column is corrupt; this is certainty, not probability, exactly as Cattan's own validity clause has it: a Judge that does not answer to its Witnesses means "the figure should be false." Note which alarms ring. The coarse checks all *pass*: Laetitia and Tristitia are both seven-point figures, lawfully of one parity; their joint count, fourteen, is even; the printed Judge's count, eight, is even. A student armed only with Lesson 4's smoke alarm (total parity) would walk past this column. Only the full line-by-line addition convicts, which is why the journal protocol demands re-derivation and not merely counting.

    **The reconstruction.** Three single repairs are arithmetically available: emend the Judge to Carcer, keeping both Witnesses; emend the first Witness; or emend the second. Arithmetic alone cannot choose, but the column does not stand alone, and the table's architecture is itself evidence. Each leaf must run its headline figure once against each of the eight figures of its parity; that completeness is what makes the instrument an instrument. Survey the Laetitia leaf as printed: Tristitia appears as partner *twice* (in this column and in the one immediately preceding) and one lawful partner appears nowhere: Laetitia itself. The leaf is missing exactly the column Laetitia-with-Laetitia. Now consult the arithmetic once more: a figure added to itself yields even sums on every line: Populus. Laetitia with Laetitia gives Judge **Populus**, precisely the Judge the compositor set. The printed Judge is sound; the printed partner is a duplication, the compositor's eye having slipped to the previous column while setting a leaf of near-identical dot-clusters. Against the alternatives: emending the Judge to Carcer would salvage this column's arithmetic at the cost of the leaf's coherence (two Tristitia columns, no Laetitia column) repairing one error by institutionalising two; emending the *first* Witness would deface the leaf's own headline. Only the partner-emendation satisfies arithmetic and architecture at once, and the printed Judge (the very element a careless critic would emend first) turns out to be the column's one incorruptible witness, corroborating the repair. ## You are ready to continue when…

- You can derive the triad from any shield without hesitation, verify it line by line, and state both parity checks (the Judge even, the Witnesses of one parity) with the reason for each.
- You can give both framings of the Witnesses, reconcile them from the shield's structure, and cite Cattan's chapter for both, knowing exactly which of Heydon's chapters is an independent witness and which is Cattan in a second coat.
- You can name the eight figures of the bench from memory, give each Judge's tendency in a sentence, and prove why no other figure can ever deliver a sentence.
- You can find any pair of Witnesses in the tables, map any well-formed question to its row by house, and read a column as one Judge refracted through ten departments, predicting its drift before you look.
- You can state the Populus doctrine (the rule, its source in the advertisement, and the structural proof that Populus judges only identical Witnesses) and your protocol withholds the sentence accordingly.
- You can run the line-by-line check on any printed Witness–Judge column and explain, with the leaf-181 case, why total parity sleeps where full re-derivation convicts.
- You can recite the three course charts' triads and sentences, quote each one's table row for its standing question, and state precisely, without resolving, what each reading leaves open and which lesson owns the resolution.
- Your journal carries the full third protocol: triads, sentences, tendencies, lookups, surprises, and not one verdict.

## Where you stand

Your charts now speak, in one register: you can constitute the court at the foot of any shield, hear its sentence, and say what that sentence does and does not decide. The three standing charts stand in three conditions: Chart A's harsh road ends in gain, Chart B's dark sentence awaits the houses' reply, and Chart C is split clean, a bright court above a dark wheel, held open until Lesson 10.

What you cannot yet do is the thing every querent actually asks: whether the matter is *granted*. Whether the houses concur with the sentence (whether the querent's figure and the quesited's reach one another, by standing in one another's places, by meeting in a neighbouring house, by the ways the tradition counts as *yes*) is a different doctrine: **perfection**, the hinge of all geomantic judgment and the difference between "this matter ends in gain" and "you will obtain it." It is the whole subject of Lesson 7, at [the course index](/course/).
