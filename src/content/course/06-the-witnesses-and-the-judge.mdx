This lesson teaches how to read the two Witnesses and the Judge at the foot of the shield. Their office is limited: they state how the matter, taken as a whole, tends to end. The Right Witness gathers one side of the chart, the Left the other, and the Judge is made from both; Cattan gives sixteen pages of tables for reading that summary. But the summary is not yet an answer to the querent: whether a marriage takes effect or a suit is won depends on the houses and significators, which Lesson 7 treats.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lesson 4's derivation (Right Witness from the First and Second Nieces, Left Witness from the Third and Fourth, Judge from the two Witnesses) and the even-Judge rule. It also assumes Lesson 5's houses and significators, because the lesson ends by separating what the Judge can decide from what only the houses can decide.

Crowley's chapter IV is the same Witness–Judge material recompiled for the Golden Dawn, arranged by the Left Witness where Cattan's leaves are headed by the Right. This lesson applies the method to all three standing charts, with Chart A at full length.

## The two Witnesses

Begin where Lesson 4 began, with Cattan's own chapter; read it again now, with five lessons behind you instead of three:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-03-of-the-two-witnesses-chap" />

This one paragraph contains two distinct framings of the Witnesses, and the sources use both.

The first framing is by **parties**. "The right witnes is put for the querant," Cattan says, and the left for "the thing demaunded, propounded and enquired." Cattan also lists the figures each Witness gathers: the Right Witness the first, second, third, fourth, ninth and tenth, the Left Witness the rest. The lists follow the derivation: each Witness's list is exactly its own ancestry, the Mothers and their Nieces on the right, the Daughters and theirs on the left. The right side belongs to the asker, the left to the matter asked.

The second framing is by **time**. In the same passage Cattan says the Right Witness signifies the matter lately passed, and the Left Witness the thing to come. The Right Witness is the matter's beginning and past; the Left Witness is its future course; the Judge, as the next section shows, is its end. Do not take Cattan's "joy" and "heavinesse" as fixed meanings for the two sides. The figure itself decides the quality. A Rubeus on the right is not joy; it is a hot and disordered beginning.

The two framings are not rivals. The right side of the shield is the side of origin, the left the side of development. In practice, use the compound reading: the Right Witness is where the matter comes from, the querent's side and the beginning; the Left Witness is where it goes, the matter's side and the course to come. When the two framings pull apart, as when a querent asks about another person's affairs, record the tension instead of forcing one rule to cover it.

Note, finally, the part you have met twice already and will now use: the Witnesses "be no houses, neither naturall Figures, but bee onely accidentalles, taken from the other to give a judgement certaine on the question propounded." They have no address on the wheel and no department of life; they cannot be significators; Step 0 never looks at them. They exist for one purpose: to be weighed against each other, and to produce the Judge.

## The Judge and the End

Now the fifteenth figure, again in Cattan's chapter:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-04-of-the-judge-chap" />

Take the rule in three pieces. First, the office: the Judge is "procreated of the two witnesses to judge the ende of all the signification of the demaund, to know if it be good or bad." Its office concerns the end of the matter as a whole. Whether the matter comes to the querent, so that the asker obtains the thing, is a different question, and Cattan does not say the Judge alone answers it.

Second, the validity rule from Lesson 4: "The which Judge ought alwayes of necessitie to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false."

Third, for later lessons, Cattan gives a rule of *accord*: if the Judge agrees with the first figure and the right side, that is good for the querent in the thing demanded; if it agrees with the left side, it shows "an ill issue of the thing demaunded." Agreement here means the kinds of kinship learned in Lesson 3: same figure, same element, same planet. The fuller rules for likeness between figures arrive in Lessons 8 and 9.

Heydon's chapter "Of the Judge" again reprints Cattan almost verbatim (Heydon, *Theomagia* (London, 1663-4), Book II, ch. 17). The modern comparison gives the rule in one line:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/witnesses-judge" />

"If the judge be good the figure is good, and vice-versâ" is the Golden Dawn's working summary. Within its limits it is true. The next line is not in the older sources: the "Reconciler," a sixteenth figure made by adding the Judge to the first-house figure. The Golden Dawn used it as a final harmonising finding. Cattan and Heydon do not use it. Treat it as a modern comparison, not as part of the Renaissance method.

## Ill witnesses, good Judge

The three figures make one summary, not three isolated omens. The Witnesses describe the beginning and the course; the Judge gives the end. They need not agree:

- **Good Witnesses, good Judge**: well begun, well carried, well ended.
- **Ill Witnesses, good Judge**: ill begun and hard in the carriage, but ending in good. The end is favourable; the course is not, and an honest reading says both.
- **Good Witnesses, ill Judge**: fair beginnings that miscarry at the end. The summary follows the Judge; fair Witnesses soften the course, not the conclusion.
- **Mixed Witnesses**: read the time-framing. A good Right and ill Left is a good past with a worsening course; the reverse is a hard start mending. The Judge still owns the end.

The rule for every combination: **when Witnesses and Judge disagree, the Judge gives the end and the Witnesses qualify the course.** That is the plain sense of Cattan's chapters: the Witnesses exist "to give a judgement certaine," and the Judge judges "the ende." The tables also follow this rule: the same Judge is read differently under different Witnesses.

The addition that makes the Judge is parity arithmetic. Two harsh figures can sum to a good Judge: Chart A has Rubeus plus Tristitia yielding Acquisitio. Two gentle figures can also sum to loss: Laetitia plus Albus gives Amissio. Work the lines yourself, head to feet: fire, 1 and 2, odd, one point; air, 2 and 2, even, two; water, 2 and 1, odd, one; earth, 2 and 2, even, two; lines 1, 2, 1, 2, Amissio. The figures' characters do not add; their points do. A good Judge after difficult Witnesses is not a contradiction. It is exactly the sort of pattern the rule is meant to read.

## The eight possible Judges

Only eight figures can ever give the final summary: the Judge must be even, and only Populus, Via, Conjunctio, Carcer, Acquisitio, Amissio, Fortuna Major and Fortuna Minor carry an even count of points. The other eight figures (the five- and seven-pointed) may stand anywhere else in the chart, Witnesses included, but never at its foot.

One corollary matters here. Since the Judge is always even, the two Witnesses of a true chart always hold an even count between them, which can only happen if **the two Witnesses are of the same parity**: both even-pointed, or both odd-pointed. An even Witness on the right and an odd one on the left is as sure a sign of miscasting as an odd Judge. Check the course charts: Rubeus and Tristitia, seven points and seven; Tristitia and Cauda Draconis, seven and five; Caput Draconis and Albus, five and seven. This corollary also organizes the tables.

Every reading from here begins with the Judge. These are the eight possible Judges and the tendency each brings to the end of a matter. They are not new rules; they are the natures from Lesson 3 narrowed to one office:

| Judge | Points | The end of the matter |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fortuna Major | 6 | The best ending of the eight: lasting good, won slowly and by one's own strength; an end that holds. |
| Acquisitio | 6 | Gain: the matter ends in getting. Best of all where the question is itself about obtaining; gain, though, says nothing of joy. |
| Fortuna Minor | 6 | Good, but swift and slighter: an end won quickly, by help of others, and held less surely. Favours haste; mistrust it for things meant to last. |
| Conjunctio | 6 | Joining: parties meet, things lost are recovered, threads tie. A neutral conjoiner; it takes colour from its company, ending well among good figures and ill among bad. |
| Populus | 8 | Pure passivity: as Judge it reflects its company rather than ruling, and the sources have a special rule for it, treated below. |
| Via | 4 | Change: the matter ends in motion. Excellent for journeys, removals, and everything that wants to move; dissolving for everything that wants to stay. |
| Amissio | 6 | Loss: the matter goes out of the hand. Ill for keeping and getting; good only where losing is the wish: escape, discharge, the end of a sickness or a debt. |
| Carcer | 6 | Binding: the matter sticks, with delay, restriction, an end that closes around the question. Ill for liberty and progress; good, narrowly, for holding fast and keeping safe. |

The possible Judges are the four self-counterpart figures (Populus, Via, Conjunctio, Carcer) and the two counterpart pairs Acquisitio–Amissio and Fortuna Major–Fortuna Minor. One arithmetic rule is useful: when the two Witnesses are counterparts of one another, each line of their sum is odd, and the Judge is Via. Perfectly opposed Witnesses produce change.

## Cattan's Tables

Between the Judge's chapter and the rest of Book III, Cattan gives sixteen leaves of tables (printed pages 162 through 177, leaves 180 to 195 of the surviving copy's scan). Each leaf is headed by one of the sixteen figures as **Right Witness** and sets it against each of the eight figures of the same parity as **Left Witness**. Eight columns by sixteen leaves gives one hundred and twenty-eight columns: every possible pair of Witnesses, with no impossible mixed-parity pair included. The parity rule from Lesson 4 explains the size of the tables. Cattan's epilogue calls this the signification "of the Judge, and of the witnesses even and uneven": even figures with even figures, uneven with uneven.

Each column gives the two Witnesses, the Judge they make, and ten answer rows for ten common matters. In the 1591 spellings, the rows are: **life, substance, worshippe, possession, a wife, woman with child, sicknesse, prison, iourney, thing lost.** With Lesson 5 in hand, these are house questions: life is the first house; substance the second; worship the tenth; possession the fourth; a wife the seventh; woman with child the fifth; sickness the sixth; prison the twelfth; journey the ninth; and the thing lost is the multi-house recovery case you mapped earlier. Crowley's recompilation states the general rule: "the judgement concerning a wife (*e.g.*) will hold good for all demands of the 7th House. So of the others" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. IV). The row is not about wives only. It is the column's answer for seventh-house matters as a class.

To use the tables, find the leaf headed by the Right Witness, then the column whose second figure is the Left Witness. Check the printed Judge against your own addition. Then read the row that belongs to the question: *substance good*, *prison hard*, *thing lost part yeelded*. Here is one complete column: the Witnesses Via and Populus, whose Judge is Via.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/via--populus" />

Read top to bottom, the column shows the rule at work. The Judge is Via, change. Where the matter wants holding, change is ill: substance ill, possession ill, the thing lost *not found*. Where the matter itself involves motion, change is good: the journey is "good by water"; the prisoner comes "out for nothing." The column is not ten unrelated answers. It is one Judge's nature applied to ten departments, just as Lesson 5 taught you to read a figure through its house.

Two features matter before you use the columns. First, "meane" means middling, indifferent, neither good nor ill. Second, some cells do not answer directly but send you back into the chart: "after the 5," "after the 1," meaning read by the figure in that house. Crowley's version prints these as bare numerals with the gloss that the answer "is determined by the figure in that House of Heaven." The tables therefore state their own limit: some questions must be read by the houses.

All one hundred and twenty-eight columns are transcribed in this site's corpus from the 1591 scan and can be cited by leaf. Three columns of the 1591 print are corrupt; the site's transcriptions correct them, with notes giving the printed reading and the reason. Always check a printed Judge against your own addition of the Witnesses: that habit catches printing errors and your own.

## Populus as Judge

One of the eight possible Judges has a special rule. Cattan states it in an "advertisement" appended to the tables, on the same leaf where chapter 6 begins. After giving Populus columns for completeness, he writes: "Moreover, I have here placed the figure *Populus* for a Judge, contrarie to the opinion of all the Doctors in this Science, the which say all with one accorde, that when she is found in this place, that then the figure formed is not to be made, or question propounded, is not to be judged by him, but that question must bee judged by the foure angles and other rules, which wee will hereafter shewe, by the which they may be as well and certainely judged, as by the Judge himselfe" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 6, "An advertisement of this Table here going before," prefixed to the chapter).

The rule is clear: when Populus is Judge, do not read by the Judge alone. Read instead "by the foure angles" (the first, fourth, seventh, and tenth houses) and by the other rules. Cattan prints the Populus columns for completeness, but the advertisement says they are not enough for a full reading.

The arithmetic explains why. Adding Populus to any figure returns that figure unchanged. Populus contributes no change of parity. Populus becomes Judge exactly when the two Witnesses agree on every line: **Populus is Judge precisely when the Right and Left Witnesses are the same figure.** When the two Witnesses are identical, the summary gives no contrast between the sides. The information must be sought in the houses.

Course practice: **when the Judge is Populus, record the triad and the table column as usual, but mark the summary *withheld*, and note that the answer must come from the four angles and the houses.** Lesson 7 teaches that method.

## What the table cannot see

The triad lookup is useful: a chart's triad can be looked up in Cattan's own words across ten common matters. Learn its limits as well as its use.

**A lookup is a finding, not a reading.** Three boundaries, in ascending order of importance:

First, *the columns assume the matter*. Your question must be mapped to its row by the house rules of Lesson 5: "a wife" for seventh-house matters, "substance" for the second's, and so on through Crowley's generalisation. The table does not know what was asked; the mapping is your act.

Second, *the table itself defers*. Some cells read "after the 5" or "after the 1," sending the reader into the houses, and the advertisement sets Populus aside entirely. A tool that states its limits in its own cells is not a complete reading.

Third, and most important: *the columns read the matter in general; the houses make the chart particular*. Each column serves five hundred and twelve different charts in the same way. It cannot see which figure rises, what holds the seventh, or how the significators regard each other. The Witnesses and Judge state the matter's general shape; the houses decide whether this querent obtains this thing. The sources' name for the joining of significators is **perfection**, the subject of Lesson 7.

The boundary in one line: *the Judge tells you how the matter ends; the houses tell you whose end it is.*

## The Course Charts by Witnesses and Judge

Read the three standing charts by Witnesses and Judge alone. For each chart, record the triad, the summary in both framings, the table column, and what remains open.

### Chart A: harsh witnesses, gainful end

"Will the marriage go ahead?" Right Witness **Rubeus**, Left Witness **Tristitia**, Judge **Acquisitio**, the triad whose derivation you performed in Lesson 4. The summary is direct: an ill beginning (Rubeus), a heavy course (Tristitia), and a gainful end (Acquisitio). The matter begins badly, proceeds with difficulty, and ends in gain. This chart is the worked example below, where its full column is read.

### Chart B: a restoration ending in change

"Will the querent recover the partnership?" The triad: Right Witness **Tristitia**, Left Witness **Cauda Draconis**, Judge **Via**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['puella', 'rubeus', 'rubeus', 'amissio']} caption="Chart B: Right Witness Tristitia, Left Witness Cauda Draconis, Judge Via." />

Read the summary. The beginning, on the querent's side, is Tristitia: sorrow, delay, and the weight of the breach already suffered. The course and the matter's side is Cauda Draconis: ending and departure. The end is Via: change. The querent asks whether something can be restored, and the Judge is the one figure that says nothing remains as it was. Via is not evil in itself; for travel, removal, or escape it can be good. But this question wants restoration, and the summary gives motion.

The table makes the result worse. Here is the column, the leaf of Tristitia, the column of Cauda Draconis:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/tristitia--cauda-draconis" />

This is one of the harshest columns in the tables. The rows nearest a partnership question read badly: substance *very ill*, possession *very ill*, the thing lost *not found*. Where the Judge alone said "change," the column says the change tends toward loss. The journey row is only "meane," because Via suits movement, but that row does not answer this question.

At this level, Chart B is difficult: sorrow at the beginning, departure in the course, change as the end, and a column that is very ill for property and recovery. Whether the significators (Puella in the first, Carcer in the seventh) meet is a Lesson 7 question.

### Chart C: the conflict, sharpened

"Will the querent win the lawsuit?" The triad: Right Witness **Caput Draconis**, Left Witness **Albus**, Judge **Acquisitio**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['amissio', 'amissio', 'puella', 'fortuna-minor']} caption="Chart C: Right Witness Caput Draconis, Left Witness Albus, Judge Acquisitio." />

The summary is favorable throughout. The beginning is Caput Draconis, a good entrance. The course is Albus, calm and orderly. The end is Acquisitio, gain. The table agrees in nearly every row:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/caput-draconis--albus" />

Life good, substance good, worship good, possession good, the thing lost *found*. For a lawsuit, the relevant rows could scarcely read better: the querent's honour good, his goods good, what he seeks recoverable. Judge and table agree that the matter ends well.

The houses tell another story. The seventh house, which contains the adversary and the suit, holds **Cauda Draconis**, and the figure repeats in the eleventh. The querent's own house holds **Amissio**, and the figure repeats in the second, the house of his substance. Every placement fact now known points one way; the Witness–Judge summary points the other.

This is not a contradiction. The summary concerns the matter as a whole; the houses concern the parties and whether the querent's significator reaches the quesited's. A suit can end in gain without that gain belonging to the querent. Nothing you have yet learned can decide between those readings, so the conflict is recorded, not resolved. Lesson 10 weighs Chart C with the full method.

## Worked example

Chart A at full length: the complete Witness–Judge procedure, ending at this lesson's limit.

The standing question: **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** The shield, from Mothers Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis:

<ShieldChart mothers={['conjunctio', 'via', 'amissio', 'caput-draconis']} caption="Chart A: Right Witness Rubeus, Left Witness Tristitia, Judge Acquisitio." />

**The triad, re-derived.** Never read a triad you have not verified. Carcer added to Puer gives lines 2, 1, 2, 2: **Rubeus**, the Right Witness. Puella added to Amissio gives 2, 2, 2, 1: **Tristitia**, the Left Witness. Rubeus added to Tristitia gives 2, 1, 2, 1: **Acquisitio**, the Judge. Checks: Rubeus and Tristitia both carry seven points, one parity; the Judge carries six, even.

**The summary.** By time: the matter begins in Rubeus, hot, hasty, and disordered. It runs through Tristitia: slow, heavy, and sorrowful. It ends in Acquisitio: gain. By parties: the querent's side has Rubeus and the matter's side has Tristitia, so neither side is shown easily. Summary line: *ill begun, hard carried, ends in gain.*

**The lookup.** The leaf of Rubeus (the Right Witness heads the table) and along its columns to Tristitia. The printed Judge: Acquisitio, agreeing with the hand derivation, as it must. The full column:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/rubeus--tristitia" />

**All ten matters, read as a column.** Read the rows with their houses. *Life* (first house): meane. *Substance* (second): meane. *Worshippe* (tenth): meane. *Possession* (fourth): meane. The gainful Judge is narrowed by harsh Witnesses. *A wife* (seventh, the row for this question): **ill**. *Woman with child* (fifth): a daughter. *Sicknesse* (sixth): health. *Prison* (twelfth): difficult. *Iourney* (ninth): ill. *Thing lost*: part yeelded. The table serves all matters at once, and the reader uses only the rows the question owns.

Read the relevant row. The standing question is a seventh-house accord, and the seventh-house row says "a wife: ill." The Judge is gainful, and much of the column is middling or fair, but the row for the question itself is bad. The table reads the Judge through the Witnesses. At this level, Chart A reads: the matter ends in gain; the course is harsh; and the row for marriage warns against the bond itself. Gain is not the same thing as happiness.

**And now, what the table cannot see.** The column does not know that Via stands in this chart's seventh house, the house of the accord, or that Via also stands in the second. It does not know that Conjunctio rises in the first, that the chart has four doubled figures, or that both significators are mobile. Five hundred and twelve charts share the triad Rubeus-Tristitia-Acquisitio, and this column serves them all. What makes Chart A this marriage between these parties belongs to the houses. Whether the marriage perfects, so that the matter reaches the querent, is the rule of perfection, and it opens in Lesson 7.

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. The Right Witness stands for the querent ("the right witnes is put for the querant"), the Left for "the thing demaunded, propounded and enquired" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 3).
    2. The first, second, third, fourth, ninth and tenth: exactly its own ancestry, the four Mothers and the two Nieces born of them. The Left Witness's list (fifth through eighth, eleventh and twelfth) is likewise its whole lineage through the Daughters.
    3. The Right signifies the thing lately passed, the matter's beginning; the Left the thing to come, its future and course (ibid.).
