Lesson 12 teaches Gerard's method as a whole. This is the older branch of geomancy. One cast figure sets a zodiac sign on the Ascendant, and the planets are placed in houses by counted points.

Nothing here changes the shield method. Its reading rules, triad, perfection scan, Witnesses, Judge, and written answer stay as they were. Gerard's method is a second system. It begins with points pricked without counting. It then uses one figure to set the Ascendant, places the twelve signs in order, projects nine planets by point-counts, and reads by planets, lords, and house strength.

The course teaches it for three reasons. The figures belonged to more than one system. Gerard's text is older than Cattan's. And Gerard has his own way to reassign houses in the physician question. Reading Gerard on his own terms keeps the two methods separate.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Part 3 is complete. Lesson 11's checklist should be clean, and your question map should handle third-party questions without turning every variant into a memory task. Gerard's system is new, but the discipline is the same as before. Ask careful questions, keep the method boundaries clear, and make no claims beyond the sources.

The readings are the textbook for this lesson. Gerard's treatise is short and complete on this site: read the [introduction](/library/gerard/introduction/) before the next section and again after the worked examples. The [first-house chapter](/library/gerard/first-house/) gives the working vocabulary, the [sixth-house chapter](/library/gerard/sixth-house/) is used in full, and the [seventh-house chapter](/library/gerard/seventh-house/) supplies the final exercise chart.

Keep the boundary clear. **Carried over:** the broad meanings of the twelve houses, the casting discipline of Lesson 4, the earnest-question rule, and the one-cast rule. **Not carried over:** the shield method's rules and tools. Gerard has no Step 0, no Witnesses, no Judge, no triad, no perfection scan, no company, no springing, and no way of the point. The Lesson 10 answer template is optional; if you use it for Gerard, say that Gerard did not prescribe it.

## A second, older system

The treatise survives as *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, printed in English by Robert Turner in 1655 in the same volume as the pseudo-Agrippa. Its Latin original is medieval and circulated under the name of Gerard of Cremona. The course records the usual attribution and cites it that way. The opening paragraph states the method's purpose:

<Quote id="gerard/why-astronomical" />

The paragraph gives the system in brief. Full astrology needs tables, ephemerides, and the observed hour. Gerard keeps the idea of reading a celestial chart but supplies the chart from points pricked by hand. Cattan treats the figures as the main material and borrows some astrological structure. Gerard treats astrology as the structure and uses the geomantic figure only to set the Ascendant.

## Erecting the figure

Erecting the chart takes two steps. First cast one figure for the Ascendant, then place the signs in order. Here is the first step, in the treatise's own words:

<Quote id="gerard/erecting-the-figure" />

Make four unequal lines of points, pair off the points, and read the remainders as one geomantic figure. In the shield method that figure would be the First Mother. Here it has one job: it names the sign rising on the Ascendant, using Gerard's own table:

<Quote id="gerard/attributions" />

Sixteen figures must fit twelve signs, so four signs receive two figures. Laetitia or Fortuna Minor gives Taurus, Puer or Rubeus gives Gemini, Conjunctio or the Dragon's Head gives Virgo, and Amissio or Tristitia gives Scorpio. In this system those pairs are equivalent for setting the Ascendant. Every sign is reachable. Aries, empty in the course's default attributions, belongs here to Acquisitio. Now the second step:

<Quote id="gerard/the-square-figure" />

The signs then follow in zodiac order. Whatever sign rises, the next sign takes the second house, the next the third, and so on. Nothing else is computed. One figure has set all twelve houses. Gerard then says not to regard the Witnesses, Judge, or anything else belonging to geomancy, but only the sixteen figures as a way to get the twelve signs. So the two branches are separate systems. Each has its own method.

The treatise then draws "one square figure divided into twelve equal parts". Here it is, exactly as the 1655 printing's diagram has it, with Acquisitio cast and Aries ascending. The same component will render every chart in this lesson:

<GerardChart ascendant="acquisitio" caption="The treatise's own diagram, reconstructed: Acquisitio cast for the Ascendant, so Aries stands in the first house, 'and so may any other Sign be ascending in his turn, as Aries is here.'" />

## Projecting the planets

The chart now has twelve signs but no planets. Projecting the planets places them:

<Quote id="gerard/projection-of-planets" />

For every planet, cast four fresh lines of points. This time you do not read the parities as a figure. Count all the points, divide the total by twelve, and let the remainder name the house. A remainder of nine puts the planet in the ninth; a remainder of two puts it in the second. If the division has no remainder, the planet goes in the twelfth. Thus 31 points gives house 7, 25 gives house 1, and 24 gives house 12.

The order is fixed: Sun, Moon, Venus, Mercury, Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Dragon's Head, Dragon's Tail. That makes nine projections: seven planets and the two lunar nodes, which Gerard treats as planets for reading. Erecting a Gerard chart therefore takes thirty-six lines after the Ascendant's four.

One technical term needs defining before the cautions. Gerard repeatedly judges by planets being *joined*. He writes, "whether his Lord be joined with an evil Planet, then he shall die soon." In degree-bearing astrology, conjunction is a matter of arc, but this chart has no degrees. The course reads **joined as standing in the same house**, because that is the only relation Gerard's arithmetic can show. That is a course note, flagged, and it will be used in Chart F. The same flag belongs on a phrase you will meet in the first-house chapter: the lord "void of course." That phrase comes from precision astrology, where it means a planet making no applying aspect before it leaves its sign. Gerard's chart cannot compute it. Where the phrase appears, state the limit plainly.

Last, the treatise's casting discipline, which you should read beside Lesson 4's:

<Quote id="gerard/casting-caution" />

Do not cast in rain, cloud, or high wind, in anger, or in distraction. Do not cast for mockers, and do not repeat the same question under the same form. These are the same practical ethics used in the shield method. Keep a quiet mind, ask an earnest question, and cast once per matter.

## The lords

Gerard's readings run through lords. The lord of a house is the planet that rules the sign standing in that house. Gerard uses this language constantly but never gives the rulership table; he assumes the reader knows it. The course gives the working table here.

| Sign | Lord | Sign | Lord |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aries | Mars | Libra | Venus |
| Taurus | Venus | Scorpio | Mars |
| Gemini | Mercury | Sagittarius | Jupiter |
| Cancer | the Moon | Capricorn | Saturn |
| Leo | the Sun | Aquarius | Saturn |
| Virgo | Mercury | Pisces | Jupiter |

The luminaries rule one house each, and the five planets rule two. The Dragon's Head and Tail rule nothing. They are used in the reading but hold no lordships. Use this table whenever Gerard asks for a house's lord.

One more assumed vocabulary item is needed. Gerard uses the terms good and evil planets, or fortunes and infortunes, but does not state a list. His sixth-house chapter shows the list in practice:

<Quote id="gerard/house-06/whether-to-go-and-heal" />

"If he shall be with *Saturn, Mars* or the *Dragons Tail*… go not unto him; but if *Jupiter, Venus* or the *Dragons Head* be in the first, or in the seventh, go." The infortunes are Saturn, Mars, and the Dragon's Tail. The fortunes are Jupiter, Venus, and the Dragon's Head. Mercury is usually judged by company. The Sun and Moon are not classified here; Gerard reads them case by case. Where the text is silent, the course records the silence rather than assigning a value.

The same passage also shows house reassignment inside Gerard's method. "Give the first house of the Physician" means the physician-querent receives the first house. Chart G will use that reassignment.

## The lord's strength and condition

With the chart and its lords in hand, Gerard's reading asks three questions of a lord. The first-house chapter teaches all three. The first question is *where does the lord stand*, and his house gives his strength:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/length-of-life" />

Angles, succedents, and cadents are familiar from Lesson 8. Houses 1, 4, 7, and 10 are strong, houses 2, 5, 8, and 11 are middle, and houses 3, 6, 9, and 12 are weak. In the shield method this grades the house a finding comes from. In Gerard it grades the lord himself. For length of life, an angular lord means long life, a succedent lord middle age, and a cadent lord short life. The part reads the lord's place, not his nature. Chart E shows why that distinction matters.

How long is a long life? The treatise answers with the older planetary years:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/planetary-years" />

Each planet has lesser, mean, and greater years. A cadent lord gives the lesser years, a succedent lord the mean, and an angular lord the greater. The printed numbers are corrupt in places, and the course quotes them as printed. Venus's mean and greater years duplicate the Sun's, and Mars's duplicate Jupiter's. The course records the problem but does not quietly replace Gerard's printed numbers with another table.

The second question is *what stands near the lord*. The chapter's main passage stacks configurations the way Cattan's entries stacked conditions. Chart F is read by this passage:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/mortal-configurations" />

The method reads configurations. Mars or Saturn in the first, the lord of the eighth with them, and the Sun in the eighth together produce the death answer. The lord void of course with Mars in the eighth is another configuration. Sun and Moon joined in the seventh with Venus in the second gives the favorable case. Gerard does not give a stated reading order for conflicts. He gives named patterns that either assemble or fail. The weakness of that method appears when two patterns conflict.

The third question is *what is the querent really asking*. The chapter ends with the intention test, the nearest Gerard has to Step 0:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/querents-intention" />

The lord of the Ascendant also gives an intention part. Saturn's querent asks about sickness or a prince and carries grief, Mercury's seeks a lost thing, and Jupiter's asks about restitution or an office. Erect the chart, find the lord of the Ascendant, and compare the intention part with the question asked. Agreement is useful corroboration. Disagreement does not veto the question. Rule 9 still applies, so the question asked has the last word.

The chapter closes with the planets' persons and professions. These tell you who each planet stands for when the question needs a person:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/planetary-professions" />

Jupiter gives bishops and judges, the Sun gives kings, and Venus gives queens, tailors, and sellers of cloth. Mercury gives clerks, astrologers, and skilled artists, and Mars gives warriors, barbers, and hangmen. This is Gerard's counterpart to the figure-portraits in Cattan, and the theft chapter uses it when the seventh house must describe the thief.

The first worked example uses the first-house chapter's opening question, *"Will the querent live a long life?"* The Ascendant cast gives **Fortuna Major**. In Gerard's table this gives **Aquarius**, not Leo, so the lord of the Ascendant is *Saturn*. The nine projections are:

The counts are: Sun 31, remainder 7; Moon 27, remainder 3; Venus 17, remainder 5; Mercury 26, remainder 2; **Saturn 22, remainder 10**; Jupiter 25, remainder 1; Mars 33, remainder 9; Dragon's Head 16, remainder 4; Dragon's Tail 30, remainder 6. Now judge by the chapter.

**The lord's place.** Saturn stands in the tenth, an angle, so the answer is *long life*, at Saturn's greater years, which is **fifty-eight**. The part reads place, not nature. Saturn is an infortune, but Gerard gives no instruction to discount the result for the lord's character.

**The mortal configurations.** Mars or Saturn is not in the first. The lord of the eighth is in the second, not the first. The Sun is not in the eighth. No death configuration assembles, and neither does the favorable one. **The answer is a long life, by Saturn's greater years, with a fortune on the Ascendant and no mortal configuration assembled.**

**The intention check.** Saturn's part says the querent asks about sickness or a prince and keeps grief in his heart. For a question about the length of life, that is close enough to corroborate the cast.

## Gerard's full sickness method

Lesson 11 used Gerard's sickness triangle but left his full method for this lesson. Chart F now uses it. The question is Gerard's own, *"Whether the sick man shall recover his health, or die?"* The Ascendant cast gives **Carcer**, which in Gerard means **Pisces**, so the lord of the sick man is *Jupiter*. Here is the full rule. Lesson 11 quoted only part of this passage:

<Quote id="gerard/house-06/recover-or-die" />

The triangle is the same as in Lesson 11. The sick man takes the first house, the disease the sixth, and death the eighth. Gerard's tools are planets and lords. The chapter asks five things: whether malefics stand in the first, whether the lord of the first is joined with evil, how the first and eighth relate, whether evil planets occupy angles, and whether the recovery part assembles. Chart F tests those parts one by one.

*"See if Saturn, or Mars, or the Dragons Tail be in the first."* **Saturn is in the first**, so one danger part assembles. The first-house mortal configuration assembles in full as well. Saturn is in the first, the eighth house is Libra, whose lady **Venus** is also in the first with Saturn, and **the Sun is in the eighth**. Gerard's reading here is direct: *the querent shall not live*. The sick man's own lord, Jupiter, is in the fifth and **joined with Mars**, which gives the further part that he shall die soon. Evil planets also occupy angles, since Saturn is in the first and the Dragon's Tail is in the tenth. The recovery part fails. Good planets do not stand in the first, sixth, and eighth together, the angles are not clean, and the lord of the first is not free from the eighth's finding. **The answer: he will not recover. He will die, and soon.**

Compare this with Lesson 11's sickness chart. The triangle is the same and both charts deny recovery, but the shield uses a stated reading order while Gerard tests named configurations. The shield method is better when findings conflict, because it tells you how to handle conflicts. Gerard is more direct when a named configuration is present. In either system, the answer should say what the chart shows and where its limits are.

## The physician's wheel

The third worked example shows Gerard's house reassignment for a physician's question. Here is the passage from the sixth-house chapter:

<Quote id="gerard/house-06/the-physician-reassigned" />

"Give the first house of the Physician, the tenth to the sick, the seventh to his diseases, and the fourth to the medicines." This is written instruction inside Gerard's method. The physician-querent receives the first house, the patient the tenth, the diseases the seventh, and the medicines the fourth. Do not move this rule back into the shield method unless you label it as borrowed.

Chart G applies the instruction. *"Will the physician cure the sick man with his medicines?"* The Ascendant cast gives **Puella**, which in Gerard means **Libra**, not the default Taurus. The first house is the physician, the tenth the sick man, the seventh the diseases, and the fourth the medicines.

<GerardChart
  ascendant="puella"
  planets={{ Sun: 4, Moon: 7, Venus: 12, Mercury: 9, Saturn: 10, Jupiter: 1, Mars: 7, 'Caput Draconis': 8, 'Cauda Draconis': 2 }}
  caption="Chart G: Puella cast, Libra ascending, the wheel reassigned: the physician in the first, the sick man in the tenth, the diseases in the seventh, the medicines in the fourth."
/>

Note one projection especially. Venus's lines total **thirty-six**. Dividing by twelve leaves no remainder, so Venus goes to the twelfth. She is also the lady of the Ascendant, the physician's own significatrix, cadent in the twelfth. Now judge the four reassigned houses by Gerard's parts.

**The first: the physician.** *Jupiter* stands there, so Gerard's part says he will be profitable to the sick man. **The tenth: the sick man.** *Saturn* stands there. Gerard says an evil planet in the tenth shows the sick person is himself a cause of the disease and that the disease changes from one condition to another. **The seventh: the diseases.** *Mars* stands there with the *Moon*. The diseases' house holds an infortune, and the Moon is joined to it, so the whether-to-go part reads this as a bad sign. **The fourth: the medicines.** The *Sun* stands alone. Gerard judges medicines by evil planets versus fortunes, and the Sun is on neither list. The medicines' finding is therefore unresolved on Gerard's terms. The clearest fortune is with the physician, not with the medicines.

**The reading, assembled.** Gerard gives parts, not a reading order, so the synthesis is the course's own. The physician will help the patient, but not chiefly through the medicines. The disease will shift, and the patient will resist part of the cure. The physician's benefit will come more from managing the case than from the medicines themselves. In Chart F the sick man was the first house because he was the querent. Here he is the tenth because the physician asks.

## The twelve houses surveyed

The worked examples used two chapters, but Gerard has twelve. The survey below maps them. Read the chapters themselves in the [library](/library/gerard/introduction/) as your practice needs them. The house meanings mostly match the wheel you know; the differences are small but worth marking.

**The first** covers life, length of life, planetary years, accidents of nativity, office and craft, and intention. **The second** covers substance and, in theft, the goods. **The third** covers brethren and kin. **The fourth** covers father and inheritance. **The fifth** covers children: birth, sex, legitimacy, and thriving. **The sixth** covers sickness, recovery, the physician's wheel, and the diagnostic coda below. **The seventh** covers theft, marriage, fidelity, fellowship, war, and the longer-liver of two people; it is the longest chapter, and the survey quotes it here:

<Quote id="gerard/house-07/theft" />

It resembles Cattan's columns, but the material is lords and planets in houses, not figure-portraits. The lord of the seventh in the first promises restitution. The lord of the first in the seventh means the thing will be sought a long time and found. Malefics in the second hinder the goods. Chart I uses this column. Read the marriage part too:

<Quote id="gerard/house-07/marriage" />

If the lord of the Ascendant or the Moon is joined to the lord of the seventh, or if the lord of the seventh is in the first or with the first's lord, the marriage is effected. This resembles perfection by occupation or conjunction in the shield method, but it is written in planets rather than figures. The two systems differ in their tools, but they share some of the same ideas.

**The eighth** covers death and the absent person. **The ninth** covers journeys and faith. **The tenth** covers kings, offices, and dignity sued for. **The eleventh** covers friends and hopes. **The twelfth** covers prison and hidden enemies. The sixth chapter also gives the diagnostic coda that maps the zodiac onto the body:

<Quote id="gerard/house-06/members-of-the-body" />

Aries rules the head, Taurus the neck, and so on to Pisces and the feet. This is older medical astrology inside Gerard's geomantic method.

## The two systems compared

Here the two systems are compared plainly.

**What is shared.** Both systems begin with points pricked without counting. Both require the quiet mind, the earnest question, and one cast per matter. Both use the twelve houses in broadly similar ways. Both can judge two parties by whether their significators meet, enter one another's houses, or fail to do so.

**What is not shared.** The shield method is built from derivation. It takes sixteen figures from four Mothers, then the Witnesses and Judge, the triad, the perfection scan, and a stated reading order. Gerard's method is built from location. One figure sets the Ascendant, signs follow in order, planets are projected by counted points, and the reading proceeds by configurations of lords and planets. The shield method has clearer rules for handling conflict. Gerard gives more direct planetary descriptions, but no clear rule when configurations collide. House reassignment also differs. The shield method uses only the limited third-party practice taught in Lesson 11, while Gerard gives a written reassignment for the physician's question.

**When to use which.** The shield remains the main method because its reading order is more complete. Use Gerard when the question belongs naturally to his system, such as length of life, sickness with a physician, or theft where the thief's identity is central. You can also use Gerard as an independent second reading. Keep the systems separate. Gerard's signs, lords, parts, and physician reassignment stay in Gerard.
