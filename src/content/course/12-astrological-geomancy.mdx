This course has signposted Gerard's method since Lesson 1: the older branch in which the first figure erects what is effectively a horoscope, a zodiac sign on the Ascendant and the planets distributed through the houses by counts of points. Lesson 1 set it aside as "a distinct and older branch of the art"; Lesson 11 quoted his sixth-house chapter for the sickness triangle and deferred his machinery, planets and lords, here entire; and its closing page promised astrological geomancy met "whole, as a second system with its own integrity rather than a flavour of the first." This is that lesson.

This lesson is unlike any before it: Lessons 5 through 11 added instruments to one machine; tonight the course changes machines. Nothing you learn here amends the shield method: no rule of weighing is revised, no clause of the finished judgment touched. Instead you will learn a second, complete, self-contained art that happens to begin the way yours begins, with lines of points pricked without counting, and then walks away from everything else you know: no Mothers and no Daughters, no shield, no Witnesses, no Judge, no bench, no perfection: the treatise will tell you so itself, in so many words. In their place: a single figure whose only office is to name a zodiac sign; twelve signs standing in order in the square chart; nine planets scattered through the houses by raw counts of points; and a judgment that runs entirely on *which planet*, *whose lord*, and *what manner of house*. It is the apparatus Renaissance geomancy kept borrowing in fragments (Lesson 8's aspects, Lesson 9's part of fortune, Lesson 11's turning all came from this direction), met at last in its own country, where it is not borrowing but the whole law.

Why the course teaches it, beyond the keeping of promises. First, range: a geomancer who knows only the shield mistakes his branch for the tree. The figures served two distinct systems for centuries, and the differences are instruction; you will never understand more clearly what the shield method *is* than in the hour you spend judging without it. Second, history: this treatise is the elder. Its Latin original circulated in the Middle Ages, long before Cattan's French or the pseudo-Agrippa's compilations, and half the astrological furniture the Renaissance books track through this course's technique pages stands here fully assembled. Third, honesty, the course's standing discipline: Lesson 11 taught you to label every turned house by the layer of its warrant, and the fourth layer (full derived-house doctrine, houses reassigned wholesale) was flagged as an astrological import with its home elsewhere. Tonight you visit the home. When Chart G hands the first house to the physician on Gerard's plain written instruction, the doctrine that was an import in Cattan's country is native law here, and you will know at last exactly where the border runs.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Part 3 closed: Lesson 11's checklist clean, the four turning layers recitable with their warrants, the journal current and every verdict in it final and six-claused. The new system will lean on that maturity constantly, not for its machinery, which is genuinely new, but for its *discipline*: question-craft, source-criticism, the refusal of unwarranted certainty. A student who met Gerard before Lesson 10 would read him as a drawer of fortune-cookie sentences; you will read him as a judge reads a second legal tradition, alert to what is attested, what is assumed, and what is silently missing.

The readings are this lesson's actual textbook, more than in any lesson before. The course's corpus quotes the load-bearing passages below, but Gerard's treatise is short, complete on this site, and meant to be read whole: the [introduction](/library/gerard/introduction/) gives the entire method in two pages (erection, projection, cautions) and you should read it before the next section and again after the worked examples. The [first-house chapter](/library/gerard/first-house/) is the system's working vocabulary: lords and their strength, the planetary years, the querent's intention, the professions. The [sixth-house chapter](/library/gerard/sixth-house/) you have already read once, in Lesson 11, as a preview with its machinery embargoed; tonight the embargo lifts. The other eight house chapters stay open in the [library](/library/gerard/introduction/); the survey section below maps them.

What carries over from your training, and what does not, because the temptation to smuggle shield doctrine across the border will be constant. **Carried over:** the twelve houses' *meanings*, where Gerard's first house is the querent and his life, his sixth the sickness, his seventh the adversary, the wife and the thief, his tenth the office, exactly as the wheel you know (the survey section notes the small dissents); the casting discipline of Lesson 4, the quiet mind, the earnest question, one cast per matter; the journal; and the entire honesty apparatus of flags, warrants, recorded minorities. **Not carried over:** everything else. No Step 0, for there is no figure in the first house to inspect, and the system's nearest analogue, the intention test, works on the lord instead. No bench, for there are no Witnesses and no Judge, and the treatise says so itself with a bluntness the course relishes, in the quote that closes the erection section below. No perfection scan, no company, no springing, no way of the point. The six clauses of the finished judgment remain available to you, but as *your* instrument, the course's form, lent to Gerard's verdicts and flagged as lent; he never wrote a judgment-form, and Exercise 5 will make you keep the distinction on paper.

## A second, older system

The treatise survives as *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, printed in English by Robert Turner in 1655 in the same volume as the pseudo-Agrippa's *Fourth Book of Occult Philosophy*, the very volume this course's Agrippa and Heydon-adjacent material descends from. Its Latin original is medieval, centuries older than anything else in this course's corpus, and it circulated under the name of Gerard of Cremona, the great twelfth-century translator of Toledo through whose hands Arabic astrology and astronomy entered Latin Europe. Whether the attribution is truly his or a later namesake's is a question the course leaves with the historians, recording only that the book is medieval, that the tradition believed it Gerard's, and that the course cites it as the tradition did. What matters tonight is the opening paragraph, which is as candid a statement of purpose as any source in this corpus ever makes:

<Quote id="gerard/why-astronomical" />

That paragraph is the whole system in miniature. Astrology is the master art, "transcendent and subtil"; its full practice demands tables, ephemerides, the observed hour; and most practitioners are not equal to it. *Astronomical geomancy is astrology with the astronomy removed*: the judgment of a celestial chart, kept entire, with the chart itself supplied not by the heavens but by the geomancer's own hand pricking points. "It is not requisite to behold neither the Ascendant, nor the hour in a Table", the sentence every modern historian of geomancy circles, because it names the genre exactly. Where Cattan's art takes the figures as its alphabet and borrows astrological furniture to arrange them, Gerard's art takes astrology as its law and uses the figures only once, at the threshold, as a key to the door. Two systems, one family of techniques between them, approached from opposite ends; and now you will learn the other end.

## Erecting the figure

The erection has two movements: one figure for the Ascendant, then the signs in order. Here is the first, in the treatise's own words:

<Quote id="gerard/erecting-the-figure" />

Four unequal lines of points, made casually, exactly as you have made them since Lesson 2: pair off the points, read the remainders, and you hold one geomantic figure. In the shield method that figure would be the First Mother, the seed of fifteen more. Here it is the whole harvest: its single office is to name the sign that ascends, and the table that names it is the treatise's own:

<Quote id="gerard/attributions" />

The table's shape, before its rows: sixteen figures must share twelve signs, so four signs are doubled: Laetitia *or* Fortuna Minor gives Taurus, Puer *or* Rubeus Gemini, Conjunctio *or* the Dragon's Head Virgo, Amissio *or* Tristitia Scorpio. The doubling is information: in this system those pairs are simply *equivalent at the threshold*, distinct figures collapsing into one ascendant. Every sign is reachable, and Aries (which the course's default attributions, you remember from Lesson 3, leave empty) here belongs to Acquisitio. Then the second movement, and with it the system's declaration of independence:

<Quote id="gerard/the-square-figure" />

The signs simply *follow*: whatever ascends, the next sign in the zodiac takes the second house, the next the third, around to the end. Nothing is computed; the zodiac's own fixed order does all the work, and one figure has determined all twelve houses. The closing sentence, as the course promised: "neither are we here to regard the witnesses, or Judge, or any other thing which belongs to Geomancy; but only the sixteen figures, that by them we may have the twelve signs." The treatise knows the other art exists (witnesses, Judge, "any other thing") and dismisses the whole apparatus in a relative clause. This is why Lesson 11 called the two branches *systems* and not *variants*: each is complete, each knows of the other's furniture, and neither will have it.

The treatise then draws "one square figure divided into twelve equal parts", and here it is, exactly as the 1655 printing's diagram has it, with Acquisitio cast and Aries ascending, drawn by the same component that will render every chart in this lesson:

<GerardChart ascendant="acquisitio" caption="The treatise's own diagram, reconstructed: Acquisitio cast for the Ascendant, so Aries stands in the first house, 'and so may any other Sign be ascending in his turn, as Aries is here.'" />

### The two tongues

The matter the course has been preparing since Lesson 3: **the same figure speaks two different signs in the two systems, and you must be fluent in both tongues and never mix them.** The course's default signs are the pseudo-Agrippa's "infallible comparisons", the system that leaves Aries empty and gives Scorpio three tenants. Gerard's ascendant table is its own law. Set them side by side:

| Figure | Gerard's ascendant sign | The course's default (Lesson 3) |
|---|---|---|
| Acquisitio | Aries | Pisces |
| Laetitia | Taurus | Sagittarius |
| Fortuna Minor | Taurus | Leo |
| Puer | Gemini | Scorpio |
| Rubeus | Gemini | Scorpio |
| Albus | Cancer | Gemini |
| Via | Leo | Cancer |
| Conjunctio | Virgo | Virgo |
| Caput Draconis | Virgo | Capricorn |
| Puella | Libra | Taurus |
| Amissio | Scorpio | Libra |
| Tristitia | Scorpio | Aquarius |
| Cauda Draconis | Sagittarius | Scorpio |
| Populus | Capricorn | Cancer |
| Fortuna Major | Aquarius | Leo |
| Carcer | Pisces | Capricorn |

One figure in sixteen (Conjunctio, faithful to Virgo) speaks the same sign in both tongues. Every other row disagrees, and some disagreements are violent: Fortuna Major, the Sun's own figure in every Renaissance source and the course's Leo, here puts *Aquarius* on the Ascendant, Saturn's sign, the Sun's opposite house; Via trades the Moon's Cancer for the Sun's Leo; Populus trades it for Saturn's Capricorn. The course makes no attempt to reconcile them, because the sources don't: these are two attribution systems built for two different jobs. The pseudo-Agrippa's comparisons grade the figures' natures against the planets; Gerard's table is a *key*, a mechanism for getting from one cast figure to one rising sign, and its logic (if it ever had one beyond the mechanism) is not recorded. The standing doctrine from Lesson 3 covers this exactly: attribution variants are recorded, sourced, and used each within its own system. Every [figure page](/figures/) on this site carries Gerard's sign beside the others in its variants table. Your only obligations are fluency and hygiene: know both, cite by system, and never let a Leo from one tongue wander into a judgment conducted in the other. Exercise 1 drills the fluency; the rest of your practice enforces the hygiene.

## Projecting the planets

The chart so far holds twelve signs and no life. Now the second engine, and the system's genuinely beautiful idea:

<Quote id="gerard/projection-of-planets" />

For *every planet*, four fresh lines of points (cast as casually as the first, "by points casually pricked down"), but this time the figure is not read for its parities. The points themselves are counted, the whole sum divided by twelve, and **the remainder names the house**: nine remains, the planet stands in the ninth; two remains, the second. One special case, which the treatise states with perfect care and students bungle perpetually: "that which remaineth above twelve, *or the twelfth itself, if a greater number doth not remain*", and when the division comes out even and the remainder is nought, the remainder *is twelve*, and the planet stands in the twelfth house. Thirty-one points is house 7; twenty-five points is house 1; twenty-four points is house 12, not nothing, not an error, the twelfth. Two of this lesson's five charts contain a remainder-of-nought projection, because the rule earns its drilling.

The order is fixed, given by the treatise as a liturgy: "you ought always to begin from the *Sun,* and afterwards from the *Moon,* then from *Venus* and *Mercury,* and from *Saturn, Jupiter* and *Mars,* and the *Dragons Head* and *Dragons Tail*." Nine projections: seven planets and the two nodes of the Moon, the Dragon's Head and Tail, which this system counts among the planets and weighs in judgment exactly as heavily. Thirty-six lines of points after the Ascendant's four: erecting a Gerard chart is more handwork than casting a shield, a slow, ceremonious business; and the slowness is not waste. It is the same virtue Lesson 4 found in the sixteen rows: the hand busy, the mind emptying, the question settling.

One term of art before the cautions. Gerard will repeatedly judge by planets being *joined*: "whether his Lord be joined with an evil Planet, then he shall die soon." In degree-bearing astrology, conjunction is a matter of arc; this chart has no degrees, and on the treatise's own usage the course reads **joined as standing in the same house**, the only reading its arithmetic can carry. That operationalization is the course's, flagged; it will bear weight in Chart F. The same flag, more heavily, on a phrase you will meet in the first-house chapter: the lord "void of course." That is precision astrology (a planet making no applying aspect before it leaves its sign), and Gerard's chart cannot compute it; he has imported a term whose machinery he left behind. The course notes the import, reads it loosely where it must (a lord standing alone, joined with nothing), and tells you so, the same honesty Lesson 9 brought to Cattan's borrowed part of fortune, now pointed the other way.

Last, the treatise's casting discipline, which you should read beside Lesson 4's:

<Quote id="gerard/casting-caution" />

No question in rain, cloud or high wind, a survival of the art's geomantic root, when the figures were made in earth under open sky; no question in anger or distraction, which is Cattan's quiet mind, four centuries earlier; none "for tempters or deriders", the earnest-question doctrine entire; and no repeating a question "under the same figure or form; for that is error", the one-cast rule you have kept since Lesson 4, here with medieval seniority. Two systems, one ethic. The conditions of the art, it turns out, are older than either method.

## The lords, and where they come from

Everything in Gerard's judgment runs through lords. "Behold the Lord of the Ascendant"; "see the Lord of the seventh"; "if the Lord of the second be in the eighth": the phrase is on nearly every line of the twelve house chapters. The lord of a house is the planet that rules the sign standing there; the planet's *house of its own* is the sign it rules. And **Gerard never tells you the rulerships.** Not once in the treatise. He assumes them, as a medieval writer could safely assume the one piece of astrology every literate reader knew; and a course that quoted "the Lord of the Ascendant" without telling you where lordship comes from would be teaching incantation, not art. So: the table, and then its pedigree, measured to the clause in the Lesson 11 manner.

| Sign | Lord | Sign | Lord |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aries | Mars | Libra | Venus |
| Taurus | Venus | Scorpio | Mars |
| Gemini | Mercury | Sagittarius | Jupiter |
| Cancer | the Moon | Capricorn | Saturn |
| Leo | the Sun | Aquarius | Saturn |
| Virgo | Mercury | Pisces | Jupiter |

The luminaries rule one house each; the five planets rule two; the Dragon's Head and Tail rule nothing: they are weighed in judgment but hold no lordships. This is the standard rulership scheme of all traditional astrology, and the course's own corpus carries a near-complete witness to it in a passage you have already met once, in Lesson 3, doing a different job:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/signs" />

Its *parenthetical* content matters: not which figure has which sign, but which sign is "the house of" which planet: Leo the Sun's house, Cancer the Moon's, Pisces and Sagittarius Jupiter's, Taurus and Libra Venus's, Virgo and Gemini Mercury's, Scorpio Mars's, Capricorn and Aquarius Saturn's. Every pairing in the table above is attested there, except one. The passage never mentions Aries, so **Aries = Mars is course-supplied**, from the common doctrine of the art that every astrological source assumes; it is the least controversial sentence in this lesson, and the course flags it anyway, because the discipline is the discipline. (And the witness's own flaw, in the Lesson 11 manner: the printed text reads "*Puella* and *Rubeus* have for their sign *Scorpio*" where the sentence's own logic, Puella already given to Taurus a clause earlier, proves it a misprint for *Puer*; the corpus records the editorial correction on the quote itself. Even your rulership witness needs source-criticism.)

One more piece of assumed vocabulary, and the judgment can begin. Gerard sorts the planets into good and evil ("fortunes" and "evil Planets") and again states no list; but here he convicts himself in his own practice, in the sixth-house chapter's advice to a physician wondering whether to take a case:

<Quote id="gerard/house-06/whether-to-go-and-heal" />

"If he shall be with *Saturn, Mars* or the *Dragons Tail*… go not unto him; but if *Jupiter, Venus* or the *Dragons Head* be in the first, or in the seventh, go." There is the doctrine, performed: **the infortunes are Saturn, Mars and the Dragon's Tail; the fortunes Jupiter, Venus and the Dragon's Head.** Mercury, by long tradition, takes the colour of his company, and Gerard barely judges him alone. And the Sun and Moon are *left unclassified*: the treatise reads them case by case, sometimes bright (the Sun and Moon conjoined in the seventh promise life "well" in the first chapter), sometimes dire (the Sun in the eighth kills), and the course follows the treatise: where a luminary's goodness matters, the clause at hand decides, and where the treatise is silent, the course says so rather than inventing. Chart G will hand you exactly that silence, live.

What the passage just did pays Lesson 11's promise already. "Give the first house of the Physician": the querent is a physician asking about going to a patient, and the first house is simply *his*, reassigned to his profession's question without ceremony. The full performance (the whole wheel redistributed) is Chart G's; but the habit of mind is already visible, native and unremarked, exactly as the course told you it would be.

## The strength and speech of the lord

With the chart and its lords in hand, Gerard's judgment asks three questions of a lord, and the first-house chapter teaches all three. First, *where does he stand*, and the standing is strength:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/length-of-life" />

Angles, succedents, cadents: houses 1, 4, 7 and 10 are the angles, the strong places; 2, 5, 8 and 11 succeed them, the middle places; 3, 6, 9 and 12 fall away cadent, the weak. This trichotomy is familiar: Lesson 8 imported it for the shield as Rule 4's geometry, and Lesson 10 weighed houses by it. Here it is at home, and its office differs: in the shield method the trichotomy graded *houses*, the places testimony came from; here it grades the *lord himself*, and in the length-of-life question it is very nearly the whole judgment: lord angular, long life; succedent, middle age; cadent, short. The clause reads the lord's *place*, not his nature: an angular Saturn promises length exactly as an angular Jupiter does, and Chart E will turn on precisely that point. Whether a lord's evil nature should nonetheless colour what his strong place promises is a question the chapter does not raise in this clause (it raises it elsewhere, in the mortal configurations), and the course follows the chapter's own boundaries, flagged.

How long is a long life? The treatise answers with the tradition's planetary years:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/planetary-years" />

Lesser, mean and greater years for each planet: the lord cadent gives his lesser years, succedent his mean, angular his greater. And the course's source-criticism, which this passage needs badly: **Turner's printed numbers are corrupt in places, and the course quotes them as printed, flagged.** Venus's mean and greater years ("forty five" and "eighty two") duplicate the Sun's exactly (the classical tables give Venus 45 and 82 to the *Sun*, and other figures to Venus); Mars's forty and forty-seven duplicate Jupiter's. Whether the corruption is Turner's, his printer's, or his Latin manuscript's, the course cannot say; what it can say is what it always says: the text as printed, the doubt on the record, and no silent "correction" smuggled in from tables Gerard never cited. The numbers you drill are the 1655 printing's, oddities included; they are the only ones this source attests.

Second, *what does the lord's neighbourhood say*. The chapter's central passage stacks configurations the way Cattan's delineations stacked conditions, and it is the hinge of Chart F:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/mortal-configurations" />

Its grammar: Mars or Saturn in the first *and* the lord of the eighth with them *and* the Sun in the eighth: testimony compounds; the dire verdict needs the configuration entire. The lord void of course *and* Mars in the eighth: the imported term, flagged above, in its actual habitat. Then the bright mirror: Sun and Moon conjoined in the seventh, Venus in the second, "he shall live well." This is the system's idiom of weighing: not nine ranked rules but configurations, each a recipe of placements that either assembles or does not. It reads, the course observes, much more like the bench's tables than like Cattan's scan: specific inputs, specific sentence, no residue. What it lacks (and the comparison section will press this) is precisely what Lesson 10's method supplied: a stated hierarchy for the day two configurations collide.

Third, *what is the querent really asking*. The chapter ends with the system's most charming instrument, and the nearest thing this side of the border owns to Step 0:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/querents-intention" />

The lord of the Ascendant tells you the question before the querent does: Saturn's querent "enquireth about some sickness, or concerning a Prince… and keepeth silence, but hath some great grief or anguish in his heart"; Mercury's seeks a lost thing; Jupiter's wants restitution or "some office which he desireth to have." Step 0's office in the shield method: the first house inspected before the question is engaged, the chart asked whether it is fit to speak to *this* matter at all. The intention test is that office in the other tongue: erect the chart, find the lord, and check his speech against the question in hand. When they agree, the chart has, as it were, taken the case. When they disagree (and in practice they often will) the course's standing canon governs unchanged, because it is a canon about the *art*, not about either system: the question always has the last word (Rule 9; Lesson 3's oldest doctrine). The intention clause is a check on the unspoken matter, a hint that something else weighs on the querent; it is never a veto over the question actually asked. Exercise 3 makes you run the test across all this lesson's charts and adjudicate the disagreements on paper.

The chapter closes with the planets' persons and professions, who each planet *is*, when the question needs a who:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/planetary-professions" />

Jupiter's bishops and judges, the Sun's kings, Venus's queens and tailors and "sellers of Cloth," Mercury's clerks and astrologers and "all subtil Artists," Mars's warriors and barbers and hangmen. This is the system's counterpart to the figure-portraits of Cattan's Book II, and the theft chapter will lean on it when the seventh house must say *what manner of man* the thief is.

## The first judgment: the length of life

Now the system runs whole, on the first of three worked examples. The course's chart canon grows by three tonight (charts E, F and G, celestial charts all, frozen and published at [the course charts](/course/charts/) like their shield-method elders) and Chart E asks the first-house chapter's own opening question. *"Will the querent live a long life?"* The Ascendant cast gives **Fortuna Major**, and the first tongue-discipline of the night: not Leo. In Gerard's table the Sun's great fortune puts **Aquarius** on the Ascendant, Saturn's sign, and the lord of this nativity-flavoured question is *Saturn*. The nine projections fall as the chart records:

The erection, as the dossier of record freezes it (every count is published with the chart): the Sun's four lines total 31: divide by twelve, seven remains, the seventh house. The Moon's 27 leave three; Venus's 17 leave five; Mercury's 26 leave two; **Saturn's 22 leave ten**; Jupiter's 25 leave one; Mars's 33 leave nine; the Head's 16 leave four; the Tail's 30 leave six. Now the judgment, by the chapter, in order.

**The lord's place.** Saturn stands in the tenth, an angle, and no mere angle but the midheaven. "Behold the Lord of the Ascendant, who if he be in strong Angles, it signifies long life… and if he be in strong Angles, he signifies greater years." The answer is *long life*, and the measure is the greater years of Saturn: **fifty-eight**. This is the lesson in miniature: the lord is the *great infortune*, and the clause does not care. The length-of-life doctrine reads place, not nature: an angular lord is a strong lord, and a strong Saturn promises length as faithfully as a strong Jupiter would have promised it (he would have given only forty-seven years; Saturn's greater years outbid him). A shield-trained instinct wants to discount the promise for the promiser's character; the chapter's own text gives that instinct nothing to stand on, and the course follows the text, noting, honestly, that a sterner practitioner might import the discount, and that Gerard did not.

**The mortal configurations**, each tested and each failing, and in this question, failure is mercy. Mars or Saturn in the first? No, the first house holds *Jupiter*, the greater fortune, the best single tenant the house could ask. The lord of the eighth with them? The eighth is Virgo, its lord Mercury, who stands harmlessly in the second. The Sun in the eighth? The Sun is in the seventh; the eighth stands empty. The bright mirror does not assemble either (the Sun and Moon are not conjoined in the seventh, Venus is in the fifth, not the second), so the chart earns no "he shall live well" flourish; it simply passes every test of death and rests on its angular lord. **The answer: a long life, by the greater years of Saturn (fifty-eight), with a fortune on the Ascendant and no mortal configuration assembled.**

**The intention check**, to close as the system closes. Saturn, lord of the Ascendant: the querent "enquireth about some sickness, or concerning a Prince; and keepeth silence, but hath some great grief or anguish in his heart." Set beside a question about the length of one's own life, that is uncomfortably apt: the man who asks *how long have I* is so often the man with a grief he is not saying. The chart took the case.

The same triangle the course taught with figures (the sick man in the first, the disease in the sixth, death in the eighth), but every instrument is a planet or a lord: malefics *in* the first; the lord of the first *joined with* evil; the traffic *between* the first and eighth and their lords; evil possessing the angles; and the recovery clause demanding good planets in the first, sixth and eighth together with clean angles and a lord free of the eighth's contagion. The greater fortune cannot help his man from inside an infortune's grip; the verdict gains its tempo: *soon*. *"And if evil Planets do possess the Angles, evil and destruction is threatened to the sick"*, the **Dragon's Tail holds the tenth**, the midheaven itself, and Saturn the first: two angles of four in malefic hands. *"But if good Planets shall be in the first, sixth and eighth…"*, the recovery clause, tested in mercy's last hope and failing point by point: the first holds Saturn (Venus stands there too, but captive in the configuration that kills, not curing); the sixth holds Mercury, no fortune, the chameleon alone; the eighth holds the Sun, who in this clause's own grammar is the assassin, not the rescuer. No good planet relieves the triangle; the angles are not "likewise" clean; the lord of the first is not "free from the eighth and his Lord" in any saving sense. The recovery clause does not assemble. **The answer: he shall not recover; he shall die, and soon.**

Beside its shield-method twin, the comparison is the night's whole curriculum compressed. In Lesson 11's Exercise 2 you judged a sickness unto death with figures: Cattan's conditional delineations resolved against the consenting houses, the bench's row, the fourth angle's testimony about the end, the six clauses delivered with the pastoral doctrine governing. Same class of question; same triangle; same dark answer. But the *seeing* is different. The shield chart showed you the sickness as a *state of affairs*: figures standing in houses, qualities to be weighed, a hierarchy ranking the testimony when it divided. Gerard's chart shows you a *mechanism*, named agents in named places, recipes that assemble or fail: the lady of death has entered the sick man's house, the lord of life is in the grip of Mars, the Tail sits on the midheaven like a crow on the ridgepole. Neither seeing is the other's superior; the course's point is that you now own both, and that they answer to different needs: the scan and the nine rules give you *adjudication* when testimony conflicts; Gerard's configurations give you *speech*, the most vivid pictorial language for a verdict the art possesses. As for delivery: the six clauses and Lesson 10's pastoral law (the answer said plainly, the counsel humane, the dates refused) stand ready to dress this verdict, and Exercise 5 will have you do exactly that for Chart I, with the provenance flag set. Gerard gives the sentence; the course's form, borrowed and labelled, gives the saying of it.

## The physician's wheel

The third worked example pays the strangest promise. Lesson 11's fourth layer ended at the border with a quotation and a forward pointer: Gerard "redistributes the whole wheel by fiat" when his question demands it, and that move (houses reassigned wholesale, the full derived-house habit) "is Lesson 12's." Here is the passage entire, from the sixth-house chapter:

<Quote id="gerard/house-06/the-physician-reassigned" />

"Give the first house of the Physician, the tenth to the sick, the seventh to his diseases, and the fourth to the medicines." In Cattan's country, a turned house was a fossil: a move performed twice and never named, generalized by the course with a flag on it. Here there is no fossil and no flag needed: the reassignment is *the written instruction*, four houses redistributed in a breath, the querent-physician seated on the Ascendant and his patient moved to the midheaven, the entire wheel re-deeded because the question changed shape. This is what the course meant by the astrological habit of mind at full strength, and why it told you derived houses "are truly at home" here. The border between the systems runs exactly through this sentence: what is doctrine on this side was, on the other side, an import to be labelled.

Chart G runs the instruction live. *"Will the physician cure the sick man with his medicines?"* The Ascendant cast gives **Puella** (Gerard's **Libra**, not the default Taurus, though the figure keeps Venus for a mistress in both tongues, the table's one half-agreement) and the wheel is dealt anew: the first house is the physician, the tenth the sick man, the seventh the diseases, the fourth the medicines.

(She is also this chart's lady of the Ascendant, the physician's own significatrix, cadent in the house of confinement.) Now the four reassigned houses, judged by the passage's own clauses.

**The first: the physician.** *Jupiter* stands there: "the fortunes do signify, that he shall be profitable to him." The physician's house could not be better tenanted; whatever else this chart says, the man should take the case; and indeed the chapter's adjacent advice on going to the sick (the fortunes-in-the-first clause you read in the lords section) concurs. **The tenth: the sick man.** *Saturn*: "if evil Planets do occupy the tenth house the sick person is the cause, for they testify, that he himself is the cause of his own disease." The treatise's psychology, blunt as a country doctor: the patient is his own disease's author (the regimen ignored, the wound picked at) and the clause that follows doubles the testimony: the evil planet there "changes the condition of the sick out of one disease into another." A shifting, self-fed illness. **The seventh: the diseases.** *Mars*, with the *Moon* beside him: the diseases' house holds the lesser infortune, and the Moon (the patient's natural significatrix in all traditional physic) sits joined with him in it. The whether-to-go clause read this exact configuration darkly: the Moon "with an evil Planet and especially in the seventh house, then thou shalt not go; because thou shalt profit him nothing." **The fourth: the medicines.** The *Sun* stands there alone; and here is the silence the course promised you, live. The clause judges the medicines by evil planets ("they do augment his grief") against the fortunes ("do mitigate and heal him"), and the Sun is on neither of Gerard's lists. The course will not invent his vote. The medicines' testimony is *unresolved on the treatise's own terms*, recorded as such, with the observation that the strongest fortune in the chart sits with the physician, not with his physic.

**The judgment, assembled.** Notice you must assemble it yourself, because Gerard supplies clauses, not a hierarchy: a good physician (fortune in the first, twice warranted), a self-harming patient (infortune in the tenth, doubled clause), malignant and shifting diseases (Mars and the Moon in the seventh; the go-not configuration), medicines of unresolvable testimony, and the physician's own lady cadent in the twelfth. The course's verdict, flagged as the course's synthesis from the treatise's clauses: *the physician shall profit him; and the cure will not be the medicines' doing nor a clean one; the disease will shift, the patient will undo half the work, and the physician's profit to him will be his governance more than his physic.* A grant, textured, Chart D's "granted and priced" in the other tongue. And mark, finally, what the reassigned wheel did to the question's grammar: the sick man, who in Chart F was the first house (the querent, the centre), is here the *tenth*, an object of the physician's art, because the *physician* is asking. The wheel serves the asker. That is the deepest single lesson the astrological habit of mind has to teach, and you have now seen it written, warranted and performed.

## The two systems compared

The course closes the doctrine with the comparison it has been staging all night, set down plainly.

**What is shared.** The casting act itself: lines of points pricked without counting, the hand's honesty. The casting ethic: the quiet mind, the earnest question, one cast per matter, now attested in both centuries. The twelve houses' meanings, near enough that your question-craft transfers whole: the asker's first, the adversary's seventh, the office's tenth. And the deep grammar of two-party judgment (significators that meet, enter one another's houses, or fail to) which you have now seen written in figures and in planets and can never again mistake for either system's private property.

**What is not shared.** Everything between the cast and the verdict. The shield method's engine is *derivation*: sixteen figures bred from four, a chart whose every member is arithmetically bound to every other, judged by a scan with a stated hierarchy: the nine rules, the bench above the houses, the six clauses at the end. Gerard's engine is *location*: one figure spent at the threshold, twelve signs laid by rote, nine planets scattered by raw counts, a chart with no internal arithmetic at all, judged by configurations: recipes of lords-in-places that assemble or fail, vivid, specific, and unranked. The shield has no persons in it; figures are qualities, and the judge must coax a *who* from a portrait. Gerard's chart is *all* persons (named planets with characters, trades and intentions) and weak precisely where the shield is strong: when two of his configurations collide, the treatise offers no Rule 8, no ranking, nothing but the judge's own synthesis (Chart G made you perform exactly that, and flagged it). The shield's houses are fixed and turning is an import to be labelled; Gerard's wheel re-deeds itself by written instruction. The shield gave you the bench's tables, the company, the springings, the way of the point; Gerard gives you the years of the planets, the intention test, the melothesia, fall and exaltation glimpsed sign-wise. Two toolkits, almost disjoint, hung on the same twelve pegs.

**When to reach for which.** The course's counsel, given as counsel and not law. The shield is your method: it is the art this course trained you in, its weighing doctrine is the more complete, and its sources are the richer casebook; your journal's standing questions stay with it. Reach for Gerard when the question is of his system's native shapes (the length of life, the sickness with its physician, the theft wanting a *who*) or when you want the second opinion of a genuinely independent machinery: two systems agreeing from different instruments is the strongest concord divination can offer, and their disagreement is itself information, a question not yet ripe. And reach for him always with the tongue-discipline kept: his signs, his lords, his clauses, nothing smuggled across the border in either direction. The journal protocol for running both is Exercise 8's, and it carries into the practicum.

One debt remains from Lesson 11, formally discharged: the fourth layer of the turning doctrine ("the full systematic turning: the modern import, flagged," the derived-house apparatus imported "without warrant in the Renaissance geomantic sources") is now grounded. You have stood in the country it was imported from, watched the wheel reassigned by written fiat, and judged a chart under the reassignment. When a modern manual tells you the father's money is the fifth house, you now know the whole pedigree of the move: native here, fossil in Cattan, doctrine in horary astrology, import in the shield method, and you will label it accordingly, which is all the course ever asked.

## You are ready to continue when…

- You can erect a Gerard chart cold: one cast to the ascendant sign by the table, the signs rippled in order, nine projections summed and divided without a slip, and the remainder of nought goes to the twelfth house every time, in your sleep.
- The two tongues are fluent and quarantined: both signs for any figure on demand, each with its warrant, and no sign has ever wandered between systems in your journal.
- The rulership table runs in your hands *with its pedigree attached* (eleven rows attested, one supplied, the witness's misprint known) and you can say in one sentence why the course refused to teach it unflagged.

## Where you stand

Part 4 is half done, and the half just finished did something no other lesson attempted: it made you bilingual. Eleven lessons built one art in you, patiently, instrument by instrument; this one handed you a second art whole (erection, projection, lords, years, configurations, the reassigned wheel) and the measure of your training is that it took one lesson to do it. Not because the second system is small, but because everything the course spent Part 3 teaching you (question-craft, source-criticism, the honest flag, the refusal of unwarranted certainty) turned out to be portable: the disciplines were never shield doctrine; they were *judgment*, and judgment crosses the border with you. The promises are paid in full: the horoscope erected from the first figure, as Lesson 1 foretold; the planets and lords taken up "entire and unplundered," as Lesson 11 swore; the wheel watched redistributing itself by fiat in its own country; and the second system met as promised, whole, with its own integrity, a branch and not a flavour.
