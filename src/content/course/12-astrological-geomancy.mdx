Gerard's method is the older branch in which one cast figure sets a zodiac sign on the Ascendant, and the planets are placed in houses by counted points. This lesson teaches that system as a whole.

Nothing here changes the shield method. Its rules of weighing, triad, perfection scan, Witnesses, Judge, and six-part reading remain as they were. Gerard's method is a second system. It begins with points pricked without counting, but then uses one figure to set the Ascendant, places the twelve signs in order, projects nine planets by point-counts, and reads by planets, lords, and house strength.

The reason for teaching it is simple. The figures belonged to more than one system. Gerard's text is older than Cattan's and shows much of the astrological material that later geomantic books borrow in fragments. It also explains rules Lesson 11 had to flag as imports, especially full derived-house logic. Seeing Gerard on his own terms helps keep the two methods separate.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Part 3 is complete: Lesson 11's checklist is clean and the four turning layers are known with their sources. Gerard's system is new, but the discipline is the same: careful questions, source criticism, and no claims beyond the sources.

The readings are the main textbook for this lesson. Gerard's treatise is short and complete on this site. Read the [introduction](/library/gerard/introduction/) before the next section and again after the worked examples. The [first-house chapter](/library/gerard/first-house/) gives the working vocabulary: lords, strength, planetary years, intention, and professions. The [sixth-house chapter](/library/gerard/sixth-house/) is now used in full. The [seventh-house chapter](/library/gerard/seventh-house/) supplies the final exercise chart. The other house chapters remain available in the [library](/library/gerard/introduction/), and the survey below maps them.

Keep the boundary clear. **Carried over:** the broad meanings of the twelve houses, the casting discipline of Lesson 4, the earnest-question rule, the one-cast rule, and the habit of flagging sources and minority finding. **Not carried over:** the shield method's rules and tools. Gerard has no Step 0, no Witnesses, no Judge, no triad, no perfection scan, no company, no springing, and no way of the point. The six-part reading form may still be used as the course's own reporting form, but it must be flagged as borrowed. Gerard did not prescribe it.

## A second, older system

The treatise survives as *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, printed in English by Robert Turner in 1655 in the same volume as the pseudo-Agrippa. Its Latin original is medieval and circulated under the name of Gerard of Cremona; the course records the usual attribution and cites it that way. The opening paragraph states the method's purpose:

<Quote id="gerard/why-astronomical" />

The paragraph gives the system in brief. Full astrology needs tables, ephemerides, and the observed hour. Gerard's astronomical geomancy keeps the idea of reading a celestial chart but supplies the chart from points pricked by hand. Cattan's method treats the figures as the main alphabet and borrows some astrological structure. Gerard's method treats astrology as the structure and uses the geomantic figure only to set the Ascendant.

## Erecting the figure

The erection has two movements: one figure for the Ascendant, then the signs in order. Here is the first, in the treatise's own words:

<Quote id="gerard/erecting-the-figure" />

Make four unequal lines of points, pair off the points, and read the remainders as one geomantic figure. In the shield method that figure would be the First Mother. Here it has one job: it names the sign rising on the Ascendant, using Gerard's own table:

<Quote id="gerard/attributions" />

Sixteen figures must fit twelve signs, so four signs receive two figures: Laetitia or Fortuna Minor gives Taurus, Puer or Rubeus gives Gemini, Conjunctio or the Dragon's Head gives Virgo, and Amissio or Tristitia gives Scorpio. In this system those pairs are equivalent for setting the Ascendant. Every sign is reachable, and Aries, empty in the course's default attributions, belongs here to Acquisitio. Then comes the second step:

<Quote id="gerard/the-square-figure" />

The signs then follow in zodiac order. Whatever sign rises, the next sign takes the second house, the next the third, and so on. Nothing else is computed. One figure has set all twelve houses. Gerard then says not to regard the Witnesses, Judge, or anything else belonging to geomancy, but only the sixteen figures as a way to get the twelve signs. This is why the two branches are systems, not variants: each has its own method.

The treatise then draws "one square figure divided into twelve equal parts", and here it is, exactly as the 1655 printing's diagram has it, with Acquisitio cast and Aries ascending, drawn by the same component that will render every chart in this lesson:

<GerardChart ascendant="acquisitio" caption="The treatise's own diagram, reconstructed: Acquisitio cast for the Ascendant, so Aries stands in the first house, 'and so may any other Sign be ascending in his turn, as Aries is here.'" />

## Projecting the planets

The chart now has twelve signs but no planets. The next step places them:

<Quote id="gerard/projection-of-planets" />

For every planet, cast four fresh lines of points. This time you do not read the parities as a figure. Count all the points, divide the total by twelve, and let the remainder name the house. A remainder of nine puts the planet in the ninth; a remainder of two puts it in the second. If the division has no remainder, the planet goes in the twelfth. Thus 31 points gives house 7, 25 gives house 1, and 24 gives house 12.

The order is fixed: Sun, Moon, Venus, Mercury, Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Dragon's Head, Dragon's Tail. That makes nine projections: seven planets and the two lunar nodes, which Gerard treats as planets for reading. Erecting a Gerard chart therefore takes thirty-six lines after the Ascendant's four.

One technical term before the cautions. Gerard will repeatedly judge by planets being *joined*: "whether his Lord be joined with an evil Planet, then he shall die soon." In degree-bearing astrology, conjunction is a matter of arc; this chart has no degrees, and on the treatise's own usage the course reads **joined as standing in the same house**, the only reading its arithmetic can carry. That working reading is the course's, flagged; it will bear weight in Chart F. The same flag, more heavily, on a phrase you will meet in the first-house chapter: the lord "void of course." That is precision astrology (a planet making no applying aspect before it leaves its sign), and Gerard's chart cannot compute it. The course notes the import, reads it loosely where it must (a lord standing alone, joined with nothing), and says so plainly.

Last, the treatise's casting discipline, which you should read beside Lesson 4's:

<Quote id="gerard/casting-caution" />

Do not cast in rain, cloud, or high wind. Do not cast in anger or distraction. Do not cast for mockers. Do not repeat the same question under the same form. These are the same practical ethics used in the shield method: quiet mind, earnest question, one cast per matter.

## The lords, and where they come from

Gerard's readings run through lords. The lord of a house is the planet that rules the sign standing in that house. Gerard uses this language constantly but never gives the rulership table; he assumes the reader knows it. The course gives the table here, then states its source.

| Sign | Lord | Sign | Lord |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aries | Mars | Libra | Venus |
| Taurus | Venus | Scorpio | Mars |
| Gemini | Mercury | Sagittarius | Jupiter |
| Cancer | the Moon | Capricorn | Saturn |
| Leo | the Sun | Aquarius | Saturn |
| Virgo | Mercury | Pisces | Jupiter |

The luminaries rule one house each; the five planets rule two; the Dragon's Head and Tail rule nothing: they are weighed in the reading but hold no lordships. This is the standard rulership scheme in older astrology, and the course's own corpus carries a near-complete witness to it in a passage you have already met once, in Lesson 3, doing a different job:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/signs" />

The useful part is the parenthetical list of signs as planetary houses: Leo for the Sun, Cancer for the Moon, Pisces and Sagittarius for Jupiter, Taurus and Libra for Venus, Virgo and Gemini for Mercury, Scorpio for Mars, and Capricorn and Aquarius for Saturn. Every row above is attested there except Aries. **Aries = Mars is course-supplied** from the standard astrological rule and flagged as such. The passage also needs source criticism: the printed text gives "*Puella* and *Rubeus*" for Scorpio, but the sentence's logic requires *Puer* and Rubeus, since Puella was already assigned Taurus. The corpus note records that correction.

One more assumed vocabulary item is needed. Gerard speaks of good and evil planets, or fortunes and infortunes, but does not state a list. His sixth-house chapter shows the list in practice:

<Quote id="gerard/house-06/whether-to-go-and-heal" />

"If he shall be with *Saturn, Mars* or the *Dragons Tail*… go not unto him; but if *Jupiter, Venus* or the *Dragons Head* be in the first, or in the seventh, go." The infortunes are Saturn, Mars, and the Dragon's Tail. The fortunes are Jupiter, Venus, and the Dragon's Head. Mercury is usually judged by company. The Sun and Moon are not classified here; Gerard reads them case by case. Where the text is silent, the course records the silence rather than assigning a value.

The same passage also shows the turning habit at home in Gerard. "Give the first house of the Physician" means the physician-querent receives the first house. Chart G will use the full reassignment.

## The strength and speech of the lord

With the chart and its lords in hand, Gerard's reading asks three questions of a lord, and the first-house chapter teaches all three. First, *where does he stand*, and the standing is strength:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/length-of-life" />

Angles, succedents, and cadents are familiar from Lesson 8. Houses 1, 4, 7, and 10 are strong; 2, 5, 8, and 11 are middle; 3, 6, 9, and 12 are weak. In the shield method this grades the house from which finding comes. In Gerard, it grades the lord himself. For length of life, an angular lord means long life, a succedent lord middle age, and a cadent lord short life. The part reads the lord's place, not the lord's nature. Chart E turns on that point.

How long is a long life? The treatise answers with the older planetary years:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/planetary-years" />

Each planet has lesser, mean, and greater years. A cadent lord gives the lesser years, a succedent lord the mean, and an angular lord the greater. The printed numbers are corrupt in places, and the course quotes them as printed. Venus's mean and greater years duplicate the Sun's; Mars's duplicate Jupiter's. The course records the problem but does not silently replace Gerard's printed numbers with another table.

Second, *what does the lord's neighbourhood say*. The chapter's central passage stacks configurations the way Cattan's entries stacked conditions, and it is the hinge of Chart F:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/mortal-configurations" />

The method is configurational. Mars or Saturn in the first, the lord of the eighth with them, and the Sun in the eighth together produce the death answer. The lord void of course with Mars in the eighth is another configuration. Sun and Moon joined in the seventh with Venus in the second gives the favorable case. Gerard does not give nine ranked rules. He gives named patterns that assemble or fail. The weakness of that method appears when two patterns conflict: the treatise gives no stated ranking.

Third, *what is the querent really asking*. The chapter ends with the intention test, the nearest Gerard has to Step 0:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/querents-intention" />

The lord of the Ascendant also gives an intention part. Saturn's querent asks about sickness or a prince and carries grief; Mercury's seeks a lost thing; Jupiter's asks about restitution or an office. This is the closest Gerard has to Step 0. Erect the chart, find the lord of the Ascendant, and compare the intention part with the question asked. Agreement is useful corroboration. Disagreement does not veto the question. Rule 9 still applies: the question asked has the last word.

The chapter closes with the planets' persons and professions, who each planet *is*, when the question needs a who:

<Quote id="gerard/house-01/planetary-professions" />

Jupiter gives bishops and judges; the Sun kings; Venus queens, tailors, and sellers of cloth; Mercury clerks, astrologers, and skilled artists; Mars warriors, barbers, and hangmen. This is Gerard's counterpart to the figure-portraits in Cattan, and the theft chapter uses it when the seventh house must describe the thief.

## The first reading: the length of life

The first worked example uses the first-house chapter's opening question: *"Will the querent live a long life?"* The Ascendant cast gives **Fortuna Major**. In Gerard's table this gives **Aquarius**, not Leo, so the lord of the Ascendant is *Saturn*. The nine projections are:

The counts are: Sun 31, remainder 7; Moon 27, remainder 3; Venus 17, remainder 5; Mercury 26, remainder 2; **Saturn 22, remainder 10**; Jupiter 25, remainder 1; Mars 33, remainder 9; Dragon's Head 16, remainder 4; Dragon's Tail 30, remainder 6. Now judge by the chapter.

**The lord's place.** Saturn stands in the tenth, an angle, so the answer is *long life*, at Saturn's greater years: **fifty-eight**. The part reads place, not nature: Saturn is an infortune, but Gerard gives no instruction to discount the result for the lord's character.

**The mortal configurations.** Mars or Saturn is not in the first; the lord of the eighth is in the second, not the first; the Sun is not in the eighth. No death configuration assembles, and neither does the favorable one. **The answer: a long life, by Saturn's greater years, with a fortune on the Ascendant and no mortal configuration assembled.**

**The intention check.** Saturn's part says the querent asks about sickness or a prince and keeps grief in his heart. For a question about the length of life, that is close enough to corroborate the cast.

## Gerard's full sickness method

Lesson 11 used Gerard's sickness triangle but left his full method for this lesson. Chart F now uses it. The question is Gerard's own: *"Whether the sick man shall recover his health, or die?"* The Ascendant cast gives **Carcer**, which in Gerard means **Pisces**, so the lord of the sick man is *Jupiter*. The projections are:

<GerardChart

The rule, first, whole, the passage Lesson 11 quoted in fragment and held back:

<Quote id="gerard/house-06/recover-or-die" />

The triangle is the same as in Lesson 11: sick man in the first, disease in the sixth, death in the eighth. Gerard's tools are planets and lords. The chapter asks whether malefics stand in the first, whether the lord of the first is joined with evil, how the first and eighth relate, whether evil planets occupy angles, and whether the recovery part assembles. Chart F tests those parts one by one.

*"See if Saturn, or Mars, or the Dragons Tail be in the first."* **Saturn is in the first**, so one danger part assembles. The first-house mortal configuration also assembles in full: Saturn is in the first; the eighth house is Libra, whose lady **Venus** is also in the first with Saturn; and **the Sun is in the eighth**. Gerard's sentence is direct: *the querent shall not live*. The sick man's own lord, Jupiter, is in the fifth and **joined with Mars**, which gives the further part that he shall die soon. Evil planets also occupy angles: Saturn is in the first and the Dragon's Tail is in the tenth. The recovery part fails. Good planets do not stand in the first, sixth, and eighth together; the angles are not clean; and the lord of the first is not free from the eighth's finding. **The answer: he will not recover; he will die, and soon.**

Compare this with Lesson 11's sickness chart: the triangle is the same and both charts deny recovery, but the shield weighs ranked findings while Gerard tests named configurations. The shield method is better when findings conflict, because it has explicit ranking rules; Gerard is more direct when a named configuration is present. The six parts remain the course's reporting form, flagged as borrowed when used with Gerard.

## The physician's wheel

The third worked example shows full house reassignment. Lesson 11 flagged this as the fourth layer of turning and deferred it to Gerard. Here is the passage from the sixth-house chapter:

<Quote id="gerard/house-06/the-physician-reassigned" />

"Give the first house of the Physician, the tenth to the sick, the seventh to his diseases, and the fourth to the medicines." In Cattan, turning had to be inferred and flagged. Here it is written instruction. The physician-querent receives the first house, the patient the tenth, the diseases the seventh, and the medicines the fourth. What was an import in the shield method is a native rule here.

Chart G applies the instruction. *"Will the physician cure the sick man with his medicines?"* The Ascendant cast gives **Puella**, which in Gerard means **Libra**, not the default Taurus. The first house is the physician, the tenth the sick man, the seventh the diseases, and the fourth the medicines.

<GerardChart
  ascendant="puella"
  planets={{ Sun: 4, Moon: 7, Venus: 12, Mercury: 9, Saturn: 10, Jupiter: 1, Mars: 7, 'Caput Draconis': 8, 'Cauda Draconis': 2 }}
  caption="Chart G: Puella cast, Libra ascending, the wheel reassigned: the physician in the first, the sick man in the tenth, the diseases in the seventh, the medicines in the fourth."
/>

Use one projection especially: Venus's lines total **thirty-six**. Dividing by twelve leaves no remainder, so Venus goes to the twelfth. She is also the lady of the Ascendant, the physician's own significatrix, cadent in the twelfth. Now judge the four reassigned houses by Gerard's parts.

**The first: the physician.** *Jupiter* stands there, so Gerard's part says he will be profitable to the sick man. **The tenth: the sick man.** *Saturn* stands there, and Gerard says an evil planet in the tenth shows the sick person is himself a cause of the disease and that the disease changes from one condition to another. **The seventh: the diseases.** *Mars* stands there with the *Moon*. The diseases' house holds an infortune, and the Moon is joined to it; the whether-to-go part reads this as a bad sign. **The fourth: the medicines.** The *Sun* stands alone. Gerard judges medicines by evil planets versus fortunes, and the Sun is on neither list. The medicines' finding is therefore unresolved on Gerard's terms. The clearest fortune is with the physician, not with the medicines.

**The reading, assembled.** Gerard gives parts, not a reading order, so the synthesis is the course's own: *the physician will help the patient, but not chiefly through the medicines; the disease will shift, the patient will resist part of the cure, and the physician's benefit will come more from management than from physic.* In Chart F the sick man was the first house because he was the querent; here he is the tenth because the physician asks.

## The twelve houses surveyed

The worked examples used two chapters, but Gerard has twelve. The survey below maps them. Read the chapters themselves in the [library](/library/gerard/introduction/) as your practice needs them. The house meanings mostly match the wheel you know; the differences are small but worth marking.

**The first** covers life, length of life, planetary years, accidents of nativity, office and craft, and intention. **The second** covers substance and, in theft, the goods. **The third** covers brethren and kin. **The fourth** covers father and inheritance. **The fifth** covers children: birth, sex, legitimacy, and thriving. **The sixth** covers sickness, recovery, the physician's wheel, and the diagnostic coda below. **The seventh** covers theft, marriage, fidelity, fellowship, war, and the longer-liver of two people; it is the longest chapter, and the survey quotes it here:

<Quote id="gerard/house-07/theft" />

It resembles Cattan's columns, but the material is different. Gerard gives no figure-portraits here, only lords and planets in houses. The lord of the seventh in the first promises restitution. The lord of the first in the seventh means the thing will be sought a long time and found. The Moon's place modifies the finding. Malefics in the second hinder the goods. The lord of the second's fall or exaltation is another astrological import used sign-wise. Chart I uses this column. Read the marriage part too, because you have judged marriage in the shield method since Chart A:

<Quote id="gerard/house-07/marriage" />

If the lord of the Ascendant or the Moon is joined to the lord of the seventh, or if the lord of the seventh is in the first or with the first's lord, the marriage is effected. This resembles perfection by occupation or conjunction in the shield method, but it is written in planets rather than figures. The systems are distinct, but not unrelated.

**The eighth** covers death and the absent person. **The ninth** covers journeys and faith. **The tenth** covers kings, offices, and dignity sued for. **The eleventh** covers friends and hopes. **The twelfth** covers prison and hidden enemies. The sixth chapter also gives the diagnostic coda that maps the zodiac onto the body:

<Quote id="gerard/house-06/members-of-the-body" />

Aries rules the head, Taurus the neck, and so on to Pisces and the feet. This is older medical astrology inside Gerard's geomantic method.

## The two systems compared

Close the section by comparing the two systems plainly.

**What is shared.** Both systems begin with points pricked without counting. Both require the quiet mind, the earnest question, and one cast per matter. Both use the twelve houses in broadly similar ways. Both can judge two parties by whether their significators meet, enter one another's houses, or fail to do so.

**What is not shared.** The shield method is built from derivation: sixteen figures from four Mothers, Witnesses and Judge, the triad, the perfection scan, and a stated reading order. Gerard's method is built from location: one figure sets the Ascendant, signs follow in order, planets are projected by counted points, and the reading proceeds by configurations of lords and planets. The shield method has stronger rules for ranking conflict. Gerard gives more direct planetary descriptions, but no clear ranking when configurations collide. Turning is also different: in the shield method it is limited and flagged; in Gerard it is written into the method.

**When to reach for which.** The shield remains the main method because its weighing rules are more complete. Use Gerard when the question belongs naturally to his system (length of life, sickness with a physician, theft where the thief's identity is central) or as an independent second reading. Keep the systems separate: Gerard's signs, lords, and parts stay in Gerard.

Lesson 11's fourth layer of turning is now grounded. Full derived-house reassignment is explicit in Gerard, partly evidenced in Cattan, ordinary in horary astrology, and an import when used in the shield method. Label it accordingly.
