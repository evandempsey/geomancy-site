You can build charts now. Lesson 5 begins chart reading by putting each figure in its place. A figure gives a quality such as gain, loss, change, or joy. The houses tell whose matter that quality belongs to. Acquisitio can mean gain of money, gain in marriage, gain for the querent, or gain for the adversary. Each house covers a department of life: the querent in the first, money in the second, and so on to prisons in the twelfth. The lesson holds a lot of information but one main skill. Given a question, name its house. The [house reference pages](/houses/) hold the significations in full.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 1 through 4. It relies most on Lesson 4's allocation: Mothers into houses one through four, Daughters into five through eight, Nieces into nine through twelve, and the Witnesses and Judge outside the wheel. If you cannot deal a shield into its houses without consulting that lesson, fix that first.

Do the assigned reading first. Crowley's chapter on the twelve houses is the modern comparison. The Golden Dawn deals the figures into the houses by a different rule than this course follows, and the lesson returns to that point. Gerard's first- and seventh-house chapters are the medieval source. They show what working a house looked like in the older branch of geomancy.

## What the houses are

In astrology, the twelve houses are divisions of the heavens relative to the place and moment of the question. The first house rises in the east, the tenth sits overhead, the seventh sets, and the fourth lies below. They are positions relative to the asker, and each covers a department of human life.

Geomancy uses the house system without the observed sky. The first twelve figures of the shield are dealt into the houses by the Lesson 4 allocation, and from that point each figure is the figure of a house. The figure in the fourth concerns the father, land, and the end of the matter, not everything the figure can mean. Cattan's Book II gives twelve chapters of figure-by-house entries, so the houses are a major part of the sources rather than a preliminary. The houses assign the question's parties and matters to their places. Later lessons add Witnesses, Judge, perfection, aspects, and further checks to the placed chart.

## The wheel: angles, succedents, cadents

Before the twelve houses are taken one by one, learn their three classes. Cattan names houses by these stations. Four houses are **angles**: the first, fourth, seventh, and tenth. After each angle comes a **succedent**: the second, fifth, eighth, and eleventh. After each succedent comes a **cadent**: the third, sixth, ninth, and twelfth.

The rule attached to these classes is strength by place: **a figure in an angle is strong, in a succedent middling, in a cadent weak.** An angular figure is present and able to act. A cadent figure is remote, declining, or weak. Gerard applies this directly in questions of life. A lord in strong angles signifies long life, in succedents middle age, and in cadents short life. Lesson 8 will use this rule formally.

The lesson follows the houses in order, grouped by quarter of the wheel. The eastern houses cover the querent and what is nearest. The north deals with ground, origin, issue, and service. The west is the other party. The south covers public standing, help, and confinement.

## The east: the querent and what is nearest

### The first house

The first house is the Ascendant, the rising place, the angle of the Orient, and it is the querent. Here is Cattan's chapter-opening, the first of the twelve:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/significations" />

Every item in the list concerns the asker: disposition, intent, dwelling, beginnings, length of life, body, health and sickness, joy and sadness, and the head. The working rule is plain. The querent owns the first house in every chart, and Step 0 makes it the first house inspected.

### The second house

The succedent of the eastern angle is the house of substance:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-02/significations" />

"Properly the house of gaine and profite": money, movable goods, gain to come, charges, and profit from voyage, place, or service. Cattan also places here "the place where the thing lost was stolen" and messenger's news, which overlap with other houses; the sources overlap at the edges, and the lesson records the overlaps.

### The third house

The cadent of the east is the house of brethren and near matters: brothers, sisters, kinsfolk, neighbors, letters, messages "which come not from any farre Countrey," short journeys, and road company. The pseudo-Agrippa gives the same shorter list: brethren, sisters, small journeys, and fidelities. Keep the phrase "not from any farre Countrey" in mind; long journeys belong to the ninth, the third's opposite house.

## The north: ground, issue and service

### The fourth house

The fourth house, "the angle septentrionall or the earth," covers the father and ancestors, land, houses, gardens, towns, forts, hidden treasure, buying lands and inheritances, besieged towns, and whether a ship comes to port. The pseudo-Agrippa agrees in broad terms. Note one use for later readings: the fourth is "the end of all the figures," **the end of the matter**. In full chart readings, the figure of the fourth becomes a regular check on how the matter finishes.

### The fifth house

The succedent of the north, which Cattan calls "the good fortune" (the classical *bona fortuna*), is the house of children and pleasure: the child, its birth, legitimacy and nature; whether a woman is with child and whether of a boy or a girl; food, drink and new clothes; promises and whether they will be performed; messengers, the speed of their coming and the contents of their letters; "all pleasures, as to kisse, coll, sing, daunce, banquet, and play on all instruments of musicke"; the fruits of the earth and whether the season will yield them (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 5).

### The sixth house

The cadent of the north, "the ill fortune" (*mala fortuna*), is the house of sickness and servants: diseases, their length, their humour, recovery or death; the physician and "whether it be good to take phisicke"; servants and subjects; small beasts, "sheepe, lambes, goates, swyne"; labourers, masons, carpenters and men of low condition; "feare and fright, shame, povertie and lacke" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 6). The pseudo-Agrippa agrees in substance: "infirmities, and medicines; of familiars and servants; of cattle and domestic animals."

One caution. The sixth is the house of the *querent's* sickness when the querent asks about his own body. When the question is the illness of someone else, such as a father, a wife, or a friend, the question goes first to the *person* asked about, in that person's own house. The technique for finding the sick man's sickness from the sick man's house, called "turning" the chart, comes in Lesson 11. For now, assign the question to the person, and use the sixth for sickness only when the sick body is the querent's own.

## The west: the other party

### The seventh house

The seventh house is the angle of the Occident, the setting-place, directly opposite the querent. Because it sits opposite the first, it is the house of everyone who stands opposite the querent. It is also the house of all three course-chart questions:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-07/significations" />

The seventh is the house of the other party: opponent, spouse, partner, thief, fugitive, judge, or open enemy. It covers suits, wars, marriage, partnerships, theft, and whether agreements endure. Every matter in the list is a relation, since each one stands between the querent and someone facing them. The pseudo-Agrippa gives the same broad field: wedlock, strife, controversies, friends, and judges. All three course charts ask seventh-house questions because the seventh is one of the most used houses in practical geomancy.

### The eighth house

The eighth is "the house of death": death, its manner and time, inheritance, and who killed the slain man. It also covers matters passing through another's hands: the other party's goods, a wife's dowry, things given to keep, money put to usury, and fear of harm. Since the second is the querent's substance, the eighth, second from the seventh, often serves as the other party's substance. Cattan also makes it the house of the absent person and of necromancy and invocations.

### The ninth house

The cadent of the west is "the house of God": temples, churches, monasteries; priests, religious persons and their doings; the scholar, his books and what estate he shall return in; benefices and whether the man shall have the bishopric he pretends to; dreams and whether they are true; "if the common brute be true or not" (the rumour, the report running through the town); and the long journey in all its forms: the voyage by sea or land, the ship and her sailing, the army on the water, the wind and the shipwreck (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 9). The pseudo-Agrippa compresses: "journeys, faith, and constancy; dreams, divine sciences, and religion." The ninth is the third house's far-country counterpart. The third's letters come "not from any farre Countrey," and everything farther off belongs here: far travel, along with religion, learning, and dreams. Cattan's fourth house also claims "whether the ship on the sea shall come to a good port," so the ninth owns the *voyage* while the fourth owns the *coming home to ground*. When two houses both claim a question, choose the one the question is mainly about. The exercises train that choice.

## The south: honours, friends and confinement

### The tenth house

The tenth house is the angle of the south, the midheaven, "the house of the heart of the heaven," the point overhead:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-10/significations" />

It covers honour and praise; kings, queens, princes, lords, popes, "open Officers and Magistrates," their laws and decrees; whether a lord loves you and will do you good; and preferment and office, including "any office touching warre." By the same logic that put the father at the bottom of the wheel, the **mother** sits at the top, with the grandmother and, Cattan adds, the wife or "Lemman." Two items can surprise a modern reader. The physician and his medicine appear here as well as in the sixth, the sixth owning the *sickness* and the tenth the *skill and office* that treats it. And "any thing lost or stolen" appears here, in the house of officials, alongside its other places in the second (the goods themselves and the place of the theft) and the seventh (the thief). The lost object is the sources' main multi-house case, and Exercise 1 makes you map it. The pseudo-Agrippa's tenth is a single part: "honours, and of magisterial offices." When the worked example below asks Chart A a question of preferment, this is the house it will ask in.

### The eleventh house

The succedent of the south, which Cattan calls "the good Angle" (the classical *bonus daemon*, the good spirit), is the house of friends and hopes: the friend, he or she, "the thing ye love, and whereof ye hope to have consolation, ayde and profite"; whether thy friend is faithful "or a traytor to thée"; the secret companion you give credit to; the fortune a man shall have; the court and the service of the king, and whether profit will come of going; the treasures, rents and revenues of the king; whether the promise is true; whether the year shall be good and plentiful or a year of dearth (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 11). Friendship sits in two houses, and the two readings differ. The seventh's friend is a *counterpart*, a bond between two facing parties, with its quarrels and its contracts. The eleventh's friend is a *support*, the one from whom you hope for aid and consolation. Which house a friendship question takes depends on which of those two relations the question is really about, and one of the tricky items in Exercise 1 turns on that difference.

### The twelfth house

The twelfth, "the evill Spirite," covers prison, desolation, lamentation, incurable sickness, traitors, ill servants, household thieves, hidden enemies, great beasts, banishment, and whether a prisoner comes out. Compare it with the seventh. The seventh holds the open enemy, the twelfth the hidden enemy, so enmity questions must be sorted between them. With the twelfth named, all twelve houses are covered.

Read it as a comparison, not as a second map to memorize. The course uses Cattan for its working house map. If a placement lookup later shows a useful parallel or a real disagreement, record it in the reading only when it affects the answer.

Gerard's house-chapters are worked reading procedures rather than lists of significations. They show how a house was used before the Renaissance arranged the material into tables.

Crowley adds one difference to watch for. In the Golden Dawn allocation, the First Mother goes to the tenth house, the Second to the Ascendant, and so on around a different circuit. The house *meanings* are the inherited astrological ones, but the dealing rule differs, so a Golden Dawn chart and a Cattan chart cast from the same Mothers place their figures differently. Do not let that allocation creep into your own practice. This course's allocation is fixed: **Mothers in one to four, Daughters in five to eight, Nieces in nine to twelve.** When you read any historical chart, identify its allocation first.

A figure's character changes with the house it occupies. **Puer in the tenth** gives honour "especially by the warres." **Carcer** can mean a stubborn adversary in the seventh or a literal jail in the twelfth. A placement is one finding, not the whole reading. **Via in the seventh** is difficult for marriage, and the Witnesses, Judge, perfection, and aspects later show how strongly that difficulty stands and how it fits the rest of the chart. ## Choosing significators

In every reading, two figures come before all the rest. They are the **significators**, a term borrowed from astrology, as the houses are. One signifies the querent. The other signifies the thing asked about, the **quesited**.

The querent's significator is the figure of the first house, always. This is not a convention the diviner may vary but the fixed starting point of the reading, as the first-house section showed. Whoever asks stands in the first house. When you cast for another person, *that person* is the querent and owns the first house, and you are only the hand.

The quesited's significator is the figure of the house where the question belongs. Marriage, partnership, and lawsuit take the seventh house; money the second; the father the fourth; office the tenth; a voyage the ninth; an imprisoned friend the twelfth. The procedure is always the same. Classify the matter, name its house, and the figure there represents the matter in later reading. From Lesson 7 onward, perfection depends on the relation between the querent's figure and the quesited's figure.

Two notes complete the rule. First, some questions use more than one house because they involve several parties. Theft is the standard case: goods in the second, thief in the seventh, and recovery or lost things also in the tenth. Sickness of another person is another case. The person has their own house, and the sickness is found from there by a technique taught in Lesson 11. For now, know that multi-house questions exist, and place the primary house correctly. Second, every house still covers its own department. The significators start the reading, and they are not the whole of it.

## Step 0: inspect the first house

The placement procedure begins with an inspection, before any significator is named. You met the rule it applies in Lessons 3 and 4; here it becomes routine. Crowley states it most bluntly:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/destroy-rule" />

**Step 0: before anything else, before significators and before any reading of any house, look at the first house. If Rubeus or Cauda Draconis stands there, this course's practice is to record the chart in full, mark the entry *not read*, and let the question rest.** No significators are chosen, no placements are read, and the question is not re-asked that day. If the matter still presses after some time has passed, frame it freshly and cast again. That is the whole of the practice. Run the inspection every time, until it is automatic.

The course follows Cattan's practice here. The mechanical formula in Crowley is a later form of the same caution. The working rule in this course is simple: if either figure rises in the first house, do not read the chart.

Step 0 inspects **the first house and nothing else**. Rubeus in the second house does not trigger it, and neither does Cauda Draconis in the seventh. A bad figure in the quesited's house is a finding, not a defect in the cast. The Witnesses and Judge also cannot trigger Step 0, because they stand outside the houses. All three course charts contain Rubeus or Cauda Draconis somewhere, and all three pass Step 0.

## The course charts, placed

Now take the three standing charts. For each one, run Step 0, name the significators, and make a first reading of the placement. Everything here is placement only.

### Chart A: "Will the marriage go ahead?"

Mothers Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis, dealt into the houses: Conjunctio in the first, Via in the second, Amissio in the third, Caput Draconis in the fourth; Daughters Conjunctio, Puer, Via, Acquisitio into the fifth through eighth; Nieces Carcer, Puer, Puella, Amissio into the ninth through twelfth. Step 0: the first house holds **Conjunctio**, neither Rubeus nor Cauda Draconis, so the chart is fit to proceed. Rubeus *is* in this chart, as Right Witness, where Step 0 does not look.

Significators for the standing question: the querent is **Conjunctio in the first**. The marriage is a seventh-house matter, on Cattan's "any accord of marriage, to know whether it shall take effect or not," and the seventh holds **Via**, so Via signifies the match.

The placement already shows something. The house of the question holds Via, the most changeable figure of the sixteen, in a question about the most binding of contracts, and the same Via stands again in the second house. Read plainly, the match is mobile, changeable, and tied to substance. Lessons 6 and 7 add the Witness-Judge summary and the perfection scan.

### Chart B: "Will the querent recover the partnership?"

Mothers Puella, Rubeus, Rubeus, Amissio. The wheel: Puella in the first, Rubeus in the second *and* third, Amissio in the fourth; Carcer in the fifth, Conjunctio in the sixth, Carcer in the seventh, Laetitia in the eighth; Via in the ninth, Cauda Draconis in the tenth, Via in the eleventh, Tristitia in the twelfth. Step 0 shows how narrow the rule is. This chart contains Rubeus twice and Cauda Draconis twice, and none of them stands in the first house. The first holds **Puella**, so the chart passes. Stopping the reading for the Rubeus in the second would apply a rule the sources never made.

Significators: the querent is **Puella in the first**. The partner and the partnership are the seventh's matter, the "companion appointed to you," the accord between two persons, and the seventh holds **Carcer**, so the quesited is **Carcer in the seventh**.

The placement shows this much. Both significators are stable, and the quesited's is Carcer, the prison, standing in the house of the broken partnership and repeated in the fifth. The question asks about *recovering* something, but the seventh-house figure does not move. Read plainly, the partnership is fixed, bound, and resistant to restoration. Later checks test whether the parties meet despite that.

### Chart C: "Will the querent win the lawsuit?"

Mothers Amissio, Amissio, Puella, Fortuna Minor. The wheel: Amissio in the first *and* second, Puella in the third, Fortuna Minor in the fourth; Via in the fifth, Tristitia in the sixth, Cauda Draconis in the seventh, Albus in the eighth; Populus in the ninth, Caput Draconis in the tenth, Cauda Draconis in the eleventh, Fortuna Minor in the twelfth. Step 0: the first house holds **Amissio**, a poor figure of loss, but not a forbidden one. The inspection asks only whether Rubeus or Cauda Draconis rises, and here the answer is *proceed*. (Cauda Draconis is in this chart twice, in the seventh and eleventh houses, and Step 0, which reads only the first, ignores both.)

Significators: the querent is **Amissio in the first**. The adversary and the suit are the seventh's matter, "all debates, sutes in lawe," and the seventh holds **Cauda Draconis**, so the quesited is **Cauda Draconis in the seventh**.

The placement shows this much. The querent's house holds Loss, doubled into the second, the house of his substance, while the adversary's house holds Cauda Draconis, repeated in the eleventh. Over the chart stands Judge Acquisitio, gain. The first and seventh are difficult, while the Judge is favorable. This chart will return whenever the course needs a divided example.

The three charts teach one thing together. All three questions are seventh-house questions, yet the three seventh houses could hardly differ more: changeable Via, immobile Carcer, ruinous Cauda Draconis. Each chart, once placed, already has a distinct character that the bare shields of Lesson 4 did not show. The figures gave you sixteen natures, and the houses give those natures a place.

## Worked example

Here is Chart A through the full procedure this lesson can teach. The placement procedure is run slowly, and then the same chart is asked two different questions, to fix the lesson's main point.

The standing question is **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** The chart, dealt into its houses:

<HouseChart mothers={['conjunctio', 'via', 'amissio', 'caput-draconis']} caption="Chart A in the twelve houses: Mothers Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis in houses 1–4; Daughters in 5–8; Nieces in 9–12." />

**Step 0.** The first house holds Conjunctio, points 2, 1, 1, 2, Mercury's figure of joining. It is neither Rubeus nor Cauda Draconis, so the chart proceeds. Note where the inspection did *not* look. Rubeus stands below the wheel as Right Witness, and Step 0 does not apply to it. The Witnesses are "no houses," as Cattan said in Lesson 4, and Step 0 is a rule about a house.

**The question's house.** A marriage asked by one of the parties belongs to the seventh, on Cattan's plain words, the house of "any accord of marriage, to know whether it shall take effect or not."

**The significators.** The querent is **Conjunctio in the first**. The placement fits the question: the person asking about a marriage is signified by the figure whose name means *joining together*, a mobile, airy figure of Mercury, standing in the asker's own place. The quesited, meaning the marriage itself and the other party to it, is **Via in the seventh**. Via is the Way, the Moon's figure, the only figure of four single points, and the most changeable of the sixteen, standing in the house of the most binding accord. Set the two figures side by side. They are alike, both mobile, both figures of motion and passage. Note again that Via stands twice in this chart, in the seventh and in the second. The repetitions you noted in Lesson 4 are now placed repetitions. Via ties the match to substance, Conjunctio ties the querent to pleasures and children, and the rest of the reading will show how much force those facts carry.

**Now hold the chart still and move the question.** Suppose the same querent and the same casting had instead asked a question of money: *"Shall I gain by my trade this year?"* Nothing in the chart changes. The same fifteen figures stand in the same fifteen places, because the chart is the record of one moment of asking. What changes is where the reading looks. The querent is still Conjunctio in the first, since the querent always owns the first house. But the quesited is now the substance, the second house, "properly the house of gaine and profite," and the second holds **Via**. The same figure that signified the marriage now signifies the money, because it stands in a different house and the question now points the reading to that house. The reading would now concern the first and the second. The seventh, central a moment ago, would drop back into the chart's general finding.

Now a third question on the same chart: *"Will I obtain the office I seek?"* Preferment, honour, and the favour of the great belong to the tenth house. The querent is Conjunctio in the first, as before. The quesited is Puer in the tenth. Here the strength rule adds one more point. Puer stands twice in this chart, in the tenth and in the sixth, once at an angle and once cadent. The same figure is strong in one place and weak in the other by position alone. The office-question's finding comes from the angular Puer, in the house assigned to the question. The sixth-house Puer belongs to a different department.

Across the three questions, one chart, the figures never moved. The houses are not properties of the figures. They are the fixed map under them, and the question decides which house is relevant. The querent's house is always the first, and the matter's house is wherever the matter belongs. Assigning that house accurately is this lesson's skill, and the later methods add more checks once the house is named. A complete Lesson 5 reading is just that: Step 0, the question's house, the significators, and the placement facts. It is limited, but at this level it is real reading.

    11. **The sick servant:** **sixth**. No trap here: servants and sickness both belong to the sixth (ch. 6).
    13. **The absent man:** **eighth**. Cattan gives it "how the person doth that is from home," and "when he that is from hence shall returne" (ch. 8). The ninth is the journey; the eighth is the absent person's condition and return.
    15. **The year's plenty:** **eleventh** by Cattan's "whether the yeare shall be good and plentifull, or that there shall be dearth of victuals" (ch. 11). The fifth and ninth overlap it, so a supported choice among those houses is full credit.
    16. **The besieged town:** **fourth**. Cattan asks "whether a Towne besieged shall be taken or not" there (ch. 4). The fifth repeats the question for a place besieged, but the fourth is primary for towns and forts.
    19. **The stolen sheep:** **sixth**. Small beasts belong there, and Cattan asks "whether the beastes lost shall not be found, and who is the theefe" in that house (ch. 6).

    When the house maps overlap, ask what the question most directly wants to know. Choose the house that answers that main matter, and name the supporting chapter when the choice is not obvious.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={4} title="Via around the wheel" kind="drill">
  Using the placement lookup above and the [Via reference pages](/figures/via/), trace Via through all twelve houses. For each house, write a short note: good, ill, or mixed, and for which kind of question. Then write one paragraph explaining the pattern. The paragraph should answer this question: when is Via's motion helpful, and when is it harmful?

  <ModelAnswer>
    The notes will vary in wording, but they run roughly as follows. In the first, an unsettled querent, disposed to motion and change. In the second, small gain and a poor grip on goods, though good for journeying. In the third, traffic on the road, meaning kin, letters, and short ways, which suit a figure of ways. In the fourth, lands and patrimony that do not hold still. In the fifth, comings and goings of messengers and news. In the sixth, sickness that moves, meaning changes, crises, and departures of disease. In the seventh, difficult for the bonds of marriage and partnership, with the other party changeable or departing, but better for voyages. In the eighth, change in the matters of death and the absent. In the ninth, Via's strongest house, the Way in the house of long journeys. In the tenth, offices and favours in flux. In the eleventh, friends and hopes inconstant. In the twelfth, movement even in the house of confinement, the prisoner's house touched by the figure of release and departure.

    The pattern is this: **Via keeps the same nature in every house. It means motion, change, and passage. The house decides whether that motion helps or harms the question.** If the question wants movement, Via is helpful: journeys, messages, removals, escape, and sickness passing away. If the question wants something to stay fixed, Via is harmful: goods kept, marriage held, partnership restored, office retained. This is how figure and house combine. The figure supplies the quality; the house supplies the matter. Chart A is delicate for this reason: it asks about marriage, but the seventh house holds the figure of passage. Later methods decide how to read that.
  </ModelAnswer>
