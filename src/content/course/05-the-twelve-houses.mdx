You can build charts now, but not yet read them. A figure gives a quality: gain, loss, change, imprisonment, joy. The houses tell whose matter that quality belongs to. Acquisitio can mean gain of money, gain in marriage, gain in a lawsuit, gain for the querent, or gain for the adversary. Without houses, the figure is not specific enough.

The places are the twelve houses. Each house owns a department of life: the querent in the first, money in the second, brothers in the third, the father in the fourth, and so on to prisons and hidden enemies in the twelfth. Every question belongs somewhere, and reading begins by placing it.

The lesson contains a lot of information, but one main skill: given a question, name its house. Learn the significations by drill and review. The [house reference pages](/houses/) hold them in full.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 1 through 4, and leans hardest on Lesson 4's allocation: Mothers into houses one through four, Daughters into five through eight, Nieces into nine through twelve, the Witnesses and Judge outside the wheel. If you cannot deal a shield into its houses without consulting the lesson, repair that first.

The assigned reading comes first. Crowley's chapter on the twelve houses is the modern comparison; the Golden Dawn deals the figures into the houses by a different rule than this course follows, which the lesson returns to. Gerard's first- and seventh-house chapters are the medieval source, showing what working a house looked like in the older branch of geomancy.

## A place for every matter

In astrology, the twelve houses are divisions of the heavens relative to the place and moment of a birth or question. The first house is rising in the east, the tenth is overhead, the seventh is setting in the west, and the fourth lies below. Houses are not signs or constellations. They are positions relative to the asker, and each position has a department of human life.

Geomancy uses the astrological house system without requiring the observed sky. The first twelve figures of the shield are dealt into the twelve houses by the Lesson 4 allocation. From that point, a figure is not only a Mother, Daughter, or Niece; it is also the figure of a house. The figure in the fourth house speaks about the father, land, and the end of the matter, not about every possible thing the figure can mean. Crowley says the house meanings are found primarily in astrology, which is true. Cattan goes much further: Book II gives twelve chapters, one per house, with figure-by-house entries. The houses are a major part of the sources, not a preliminary.

A question is about a matter in relation to the asker. "Will the marriage go ahead?" concerns the querent, the marriage, and the outcome. The houses assign those parties and matters their places: the querent in the first, marriage in the seventh, money in the second, office in the tenth. Lessons 6 through 10 teach how to read the figures in those places and the relations between them.

## The wheel: angles, succedents, cadents

Before the twelve houses are taken one by one, learn their three classes. Cattan names houses by these stations. Four houses are **angles**: the first, fourth, seventh, and tenth. After each angle comes a **succedent**: the second, fifth, eighth, and eleventh. After each succedent comes a **cadent**: the third, sixth, ninth, and twelfth.

The rule attached to these classes is strength by place: **a figure in an angle is strong, in a succedent middling, in a cadent weak.** An angular figure is present and able to act. A succedent figure is middling. A cadent figure is remote, declining, or weak. Gerard applies this directly in questions of life: a lord in strong angles signifies long life; in succedents, middle age; in cadents, short life. Lesson 8 will use this rule formally.

The lesson follows the houses in order, grouped by angle, succedent, and cadent. The east begins with the querent. The north concerns ground, origin, issue, and service. The west concerns the other party. The south concerns public standing, help, and confinement.

## The east: the querent and what is nearest

### The first house

The first house is the Ascendant (the rising place, the angle of the Orient) and it is the querent. Here is Cattan's own chapter-opening, the first of the twelve you will learn:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/significations" />

Every item in the catalogue concerns the asker: disposition, intent, dwelling, beginnings, length of life, body, health and sickness, joy and sadness, and the head. The closing instruction is the key: whatever the question, "give alwayes the first house unto... the motive of the querant." The asker owns the first house in every chart, and Step 0 makes it the first house inspected.

Why this house? In astrology the first house is the sky rising at the moment and place of the asking, stamped with that moment; and in Lesson 1's theory the moment belongs to the querent, whose moved hand made the chart. The chart exists because the querent asked, so the rising figure is the asker. The pseudo-Agrippa says it plainly: "The first house sheweth the person of the querent, as often as a question shall be proposed concerning himself, of his own matters, or any thing appertaining to him." Exercise 5 will quiz you on this argument, source by source.

### The second house

The succedent of the eastern angle is the house of substance:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-02/significations" />

"Properly the house of gaine and profite": money, movable goods, gain to come, charges, winnings, and profit from voyage, place, or service. It is the house next after the querent's own, so it holds the querent's belongings. Cattan also places here "the place where the thing lost was stolen" and messenger's news, which overlaps with other houses. The sources overlap at the edges, and the lesson records those overlaps.

### The third house

The cadent of the east is the house of brethren and near matters: brothers, sisters, kinsfolk, neighbors, letters, messages "which come not from any farre Countrey," short journeys, and road company. The pseudo-Agrippa gives the same shorter list: brethren, sisters, small journeys, and fidelities. Keep the phrase "not from any farre Countrey" in mind; long journeys belong to the ninth, the third's opposite house.

## The north: ground, issue and service

### The fourth house

The fourth house, "the angle septentrionall or the earth," covers the father and ancestors, land, houses, gardens, towns, forts, hidden treasure, buying lands and inheritances, besieged towns, and whether a ship comes to port. The pseudo-Agrippa agrees in broad terms. One item matters especially for later readings: the fourth is "the end of all the figures," **the end of the matter**. In Lesson 10, the figure of the fourth becomes a regular check on how the matter finishes.

### The fifth house

The succedent of the north, which Cattan calls "the good fortune" (the classical *bona fortuna*), is the house of children and pleasure: the child, its birth, legitimacy and nature; whether a woman is with child and whether of a boy or a girl; food, drink and new clothes; promises and whether they will be performed; messengers, the speed of their coming and the contents of their letters; "all pleasures, as to kisse, coll, sing, daunce, banquet, and play on all instruments of musicke"; the fruits of the earth and whether the season will yield them (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 5). What springs from a person and delights a person: issue in both senses. The pseudo-Agrippa's fifth, you will find in the comparison section below, is a strikingly different list, and Exercise 4 sends you to that difference with both texts open.

### The sixth house

The cadent of the north, "the ill fortune" (*mala fortuna*), is the house of sickness and servants: diseases, their length, their humour, recovery or death; the physician and "whether it be good to take phisicke"; servants and subjects; small beasts, "sheepe, lambes, goates, swyne"; labourers, masons, carpenters and men of low condition; "feare and fright, shame, povertie and lacke" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 6). The pseudo-Agrippa again agrees in substance: "infirmities, and medicines; of familiars and servants; of cattle and domestic animals." The unifying thought is the body and household in their *subjection*: what serves, what labours, what ails. One caution: the sixth is the house of the *querent's* sickness when the querent asks about his own body. When the question is the illness of someone else (a father, a wife, a friend) the question lives first with the *person* asked about, in that person's own house; the technique for finding the sick man's sickness from the sick man's house, "turning" the chart, is reserved for Lesson 11. For now: assign the question to the person, and the sixth to sickness only when the sick body is the querent's own.

## The west: the other

### The seventh house

The angle of the Occident, the setting-place, directly opposite the querent, and therefore, by the wheel's own geometry, the house of everyone who stands opposite the querent. This is the house of all three course-chart questions:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-07/significations" />

The seventh is the house of the other party: opponent, spouse, partner, thief, fugitive, judge, or open enemy. It covers suits, wars, marriage, partnerships, theft, and whether agreements endure. What unites the list is relation: each matter stands between the querent and someone facing them. The pseudo-Agrippa gives the same broad field: wedlock, strife, controversies, friends, and judges. All three course charts ask seventh-house questions because the seventh is one of the most used houses in practical geomancy.

### The eighth house

The eighth is "the house of death": death, its manner and time, inheritance, and who killed the slain man. It also covers matters passing through another's hands: the other party's goods, a wife's dowry, things given to keep, money put to usury, and fear of harm. Since the second is the querent's substance, the eighth, second from the seventh, often serves as the other party's substance. Cattan also makes it the house of the absent person and of necromancy and invocations.

### The ninth house

The cadent of the west is "the house of God": temples, churches, monasteries; priests, religious persons and their doings; the scholar, his books and what estate he shall return in; benefices and whether the man shall have the bishopric he pretends to; dreams and whether they are true; "if the common brute be true or not" (the rumour, the report running through the town); and the long journey in all its forms: the voyage by sea or land, the ship and her sailing, the army on the water, the wind and the shipwreck (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 9). The pseudo-Agrippa compresses: "journeys, faith, and constancy; dreams, divine sciences, and religion." Here is the third house's far-country counterpart: the third's letters come "not from any farre Countrey," and everything farther belongs here: far travel, and the farthest travel of all, the mind's traffic with heaven in religion, learning and dreams. Cattan's fourth house also claims "whether the ship on the sea shall come to a good port": the ninth owns the *voyage*, the fourth the *coming home to ground*. Choosing between overlapping claims by the question's centre of gravity is a skill the exercises train.

## The south: the world's high places

### The tenth house

The angle of the south, the midheaven, "the house of the heart of the heaven," the point overhead:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-10/significations" />

Honour and praise; kings, queens, princes, lords, popes, "open Officers and Magistrates," their laws and decrees; whether a lord loves you and will do you good; preferment and office, including "any office touching warre"; and (by the same logic that put the father at the bottom of the wheel) the **mother** at the top of it, with the grandmother and (Cattan adds, with Renaissance frankness) the wife or "Lemman." Two tenants surprise the modern student. The physician and his medicine appear here as well as in the sixth: the sixth owning the *sickness*, the tenth the *skill and office* that treats it. And "any thing lost or stolen" appears here, in the house of officials, alongside its other lodgings in the second (the goods themselves and the place of the theft) and the seventh (the thief): the lost-object question is the sources' great multi-house case, and Exercise 1 makes you map it. The pseudo-Agrippa's tenth is a single clean part: "honours, and of magisterial offices." When the worked example below asks Chart A a question of preferment, this is the house it will ask in.

### The eleventh house

The succedent of the south, which Cattan calls "the good Angle" (the classical *bonus daemon*, the good spirit), is the house of friends and hopes: the friend, he or she, "the thing ye love, and whereof ye hope to have consolation, ayde and profite"; whether thy friend is faithful "or a traytor to thée"; the secret companion you give credit to; the fortune a man shall have; the court and the service of the king, and whether profit will come of going; the treasures, rents and revenues of the king; whether the promise is true; whether the year shall be good and plentiful or a year of dearth (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 11). Friendship sits in two houses, and the division is instructive: the seventh's friend is a *counterpart* (friendship as a bond between two facing parties, with its quarrels and its contracts) while the eleventh's friend is a *support*, the one from whom you hope for aid and consolation. Which house a friendship question takes depends on which of those two relations the question is really about, and one of the tricky items in Exercise 1 turns exactly on the difference.

### The twelfth house

The twelfth, "the evill Spirite," covers prison, desolation, lamentation, incurable sickness, traitors, ill servants, household thieves, hidden enemies, great beasts, banishment, and whether a prisoner comes out. Set it beside the seventh: the seventh holds the open enemy, while the twelfth holds the hidden enemy. Enmity questions must be sorted accordingly. With the twelfth, every major department of life has an address.

## One map, several surveyors

You have heard mostly Cattan because his Book II is the fullest house treatise in the course sources and the basis for this site's [house pages](/houses/). But the other sources still matter. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa's house-list as Turner prints it:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/houses-significations" />

Read it against Cattan and three points appear. First, the broad agreement is strong: querent in the first, substance in the second, brethren and short journeys in the third, father and patrimony in the fourth, sickness and servants in the sixth, wedlock and strife in the seventh, death and dowry in the eighth, journeys and religion in the ninth, honours in the tenth. Second, the divergences are local: the pseudo-Agrippa's fifth house overlaps Cattan's ninth on rumours and tenth on honour. Exercise 4 tests this. Third, Turner's printed passage runs only to the tenth house; the eleventh and twelfth must be reconstructed from later entries. Where the course needs one working answer, it follows Cattan and records the split where there is one.

Gerard, the medieval source, you have already met in the readings: his house-chapters are not lists of significations but worked reading procedures (look to the lord of the Ascendant, count to the eighth, read by angle and fall) and they show you what the map was *for* before the Renaissance systematised it. And Crowley, the modern comparison, contributes mostly a difference you must not let corrupt your practice: in the Golden Dawn allocation that his chapter III tabulates, the figures are *not* dealt Mothers-into-one-through-four; the First Mother goes to the tenth house, the Second to the Ascendant, and so on around a different circuit. Within his system the house *meanings* are the inherited astrological ones (on that, the revival changed nothing) but the dealing rule differs, which means a Golden Dawn chart and a Cattan chart cast from the same four Mothers house their figures differently. Lesson 4 fixed this course's allocation once and for all: **Mothers in one to four, Daughters in five to eight, Nieces in nine to twelve.** Know that Crowley's differs, so that his example charts do not silently mislead you; and when you read any historical chart, identify its allocation before you read a single house.

A figure's character changes with the house it occupies. **Puer in the tenth** gives honour "especially by the warres"; **Carcer** can mean a stubborn adversary in the seventh or a literal jail in the twelfth. A placement is finding, not a answer. **Via in the seventh** is difficult for marriage, but you do not yet have the Witnesses, Judge, perfection, and aspects needed to weigh the whole chart. ## Choosing significators

In every reading, two figures matter before all others, the **significators** (the term, like the houses, is borrowed from astrology): the figure that signifies the querent, and the figure that signifies the thing asked about, the **quesited**.

The querent's significator is the figure of the first house. Always: not a convention the diviner may vary but the fixed starting point of the reading, as the first-house section showed. Whoever asks stands in the first house; when you cast for another person, *that person* is the querent and owns it, and you are only the hand.

The quesited's significator is the figure of the house where the question belongs. Marriage, partnership, and lawsuit: seventh house. Money: second. Father: fourth. Office: tenth. Voyage: ninth. Imprisoned friend: twelfth. The procedure is always the same: classify the matter, name its house, and the figure there represents the matter in later reading. From Lesson 7 onward, perfection depends on the relation between the querent's figure and the quesited's figure.

Two notes complete the rule. First, some questions use more than one house because they involve several parties. Theft is the standard case: goods in the second, thief in the seventh, recovery or lost things also in the tenth. Sickness of another person is another case: the person has their own house, and the sickness is found from there by a technique taught in Lesson 11. For now, know that multi-house questions exist and place the primary house correctly. Second, every house remains finding about its own department. The significators start the reading; they are not the whole reading.

## Step 0: inspect the first house

The placement procedure begins, before any significator is named, with an inspection. The rule it applies you have met before (Lessons 3 and 4); here it becomes a habit of the hands. Crowley's formulation is the bluntest:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/destroy-rule" />

**Step 0: before anything else (before significators, before any reading of any house) look at the first house. If Rubeus or Cauda Draconis stands there, this course's practice is: record the chart in full, mark the entry *not read*, and let the question rest.** No significators are chosen, no placements are read, and the question is not re-asked that day; if the matter still presses when time has passed, frame it freshly and cast again. That is the whole of the practice; perform the inspection invariably, until a first house containing anything else barely registers.

Know exactly whose rule you are keeping. The kernel is Renaissance and older: Cattan reports that the Egyptians "and a great part of the Latines" held that a chart with Rubeus in the first house "should not be judged. But that figure must be broken and make an other," and he sides with them on his own experience, extending the same opinion, with less conviction, to Cauda Draconis (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 1). The mechanical formula (both figures equally, instant destruction, the two-hour quarantine) is the Golden Dawn's hardening of that kernel, and is always to be flagged as modern. And the dissent is on the record: Heydon knows the custom and rejects it, "I am of the opinion that the Judgment herein should not be delayd"; a terrible answer, he holds, is still an answer (Heydon, *Theomagia* (London, 1663-4), Book II, ch. 1, s.v. Cauda Draconis). This course follows Cattan's line as practice, as Lesson 3 said it would, while requiring you to know the source lines: a geomancer should always be able to say which habits come from the broad source set and which come from one line.

Step 0 inspects **the first house and nothing else**. Rubeus in the second house does not trigger it. Cauda Draconis in the seventh does not trigger it. A bad figure in the quesited's house is finding, not a defect in the cast. The Witnesses and Judge also cannot trigger Step 0 because they stand outside the houses. All three course charts contain Rubeus or Cauda Draconis somewhere, and all three pass Step 0.

## The course charts, placed

Now the three standing charts, each through Step 0, then the significators of its question, then a first look at where things stand. What follows stops short of a reading: everything here is placement only.

### Chart A: "Will the marriage go ahead?"

Mothers Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis; dealt into the houses: Conjunctio in the first, Via in the second, Amissio in the third, Caput Draconis in the fourth; Daughters Conjunctio, Puer, Via, Acquisitio into the fifth through eighth; Nieces Carcer, Puer, Puella, Amissio into the ninth through twelfth. Step 0: the first house holds **Conjunctio**, neither Rubeus nor Cauda Draconis; the chart is fit to proceed, and note in passing that Rubeus *is* in this chart, as Right Witness, where Step 0 does not look. Significators for the standing question: the querent is **Conjunctio in the first**; the marriage is a seventh-house matter (Cattan's "any accord of marriage, to know whether it shall take effect or not") and the seventh holds **Via**, so Via signifies the match. What placement lets you observe: the house of the question holds the most changeable figure of the sixteen (Via, the Moon's figure, mobile) in a question about the most binding of contracts; and the same Via stands again in the second house, one of the four repetitions Lesson 4 had you notice. What those facts *mean* for the marriage is the business of Lessons 6 and 7, and the worked example below returns to this chart at full length.

### Chart B: "Will the querent recover the partnership?"

Mothers Puella, Rubeus, Rubeus, Amissio. The wheel: Puella in the first, Rubeus in the second *and* third, Amissio in the fourth; Carcer in the fifth, Conjunctio in the sixth, Carcer in the seventh, Laetitia in the eighth; Via in the ninth, Cauda Draconis in the tenth, Via in the eleventh, Tristitia in the twelfth. Step 0, and here the rule's precision earns its keep: this chart contains Rubeus twice and Cauda Draconis twice (in the tenth house and as Left Witness), and *none of them stands in the first house*. The first holds **Puella**, the gentle figure of Venus; the chart passes, and a student who broke it for the Rubeus in the second would be keeping a rule the sources never made. Significators: the querent is **Puella in the first**; the partner and the partnership (the seventh's "companion appointed to you," the accord between two persons) are **Carcer in the seventh**. Placement-level observations: both significators are stable figures, and the quesited's is Carcer, the prison, Saturn's figure of holding and delay, standing in the house of the broken partnership, while the same Carcer stands also in the fifth, another of the chart's repetitions. A question about *recovering* something faces a seventh house whose figure does not move. Whether immobility here means the partnership is locked shut or locked *in* (whether the prison holds the breach or the bond) is precisely the kind of question the reading lessons exist to answer, and you should feel, and resist, the itch to answer it now.

### Chart C: "Will the querent win the lawsuit?"

Mothers Amissio, Amissio, Puella, Fortuna Minor. The wheel: Amissio in the first *and* second, Puella in the third, Fortuna Minor in the fourth; Via in the fifth, Tristitia in the sixth, Cauda Draconis in the seventh, Albus in the eighth; Populus in the ninth, Caput Draconis in the tenth, Cauda Draconis in the eleventh, Fortuna Minor in the twelfth. Step 0: the first house holds **Amissio**, a poor figure, loss itself, but not a forbidden one; the inspection asks one narrow question and the answer is *proceed*. (Cauda Draconis is in this chart twice, seventh and eleventh houses, and again Step 0, which reads only the first, is untouched.) Significators: the querent is **Amissio in the first**; the adversary and the suit ("all debates, sutes in lawe") are **Cauda Draconis in the seventh**. Placement shows this much: the querent's own house holds Loss, doubled into the second, the house of his substance, while the adversary's house holds Cauda Draconis, repeated in the eleventh. Over the chart stands Judge Acquisitio, gain. The first and seventh houses are difficult, while the Judge is favorable. The tension flagged when this chart was first cast is now located, and its resolution is deferred to the lessons that can resolve it. This is the conflict chart, and it will earn its keep in Lesson 10.

The three charts have taught something between them. All three questions are seventh-house questions, yet the three seventh houses could hardly differ more (changeable Via, immobile Carcer, ruinous Cauda Draconis), and each chart's character as a *placed* thing is already distinct in a way the bare shields of Lesson 4 were not. The figures gave you sixteen natures; the houses give those natures addresses.

## Update the journal

Your casting journal now takes its second procedure. Everything from Lesson 4 stands (the question in full, the moment, the sixteen rows, the verified shield, the house allocation, the labelled first impressions) and to it you now add, for every chart, current and future:

1. **Step 0, recorded.** One line: the figure of the first house, and "proceeds", or, when the rule bites, "Rubeus rising" or "Cauda rising," the entry marked **not read**, and nothing further written for that chart beyond the record itself.
2. **The house placements, written out.** The twelve figures in their houses, by name and number, not merely the wheel drawn: writing "7th: Via" engages the memory in a way the diagram alone does not.
3. **The significators, named.** The querent's figure, and the quesited's house and figure, with one sentence supporting the choice of house from the significations ("the partnership is the seventh's *companion appointed to you*") because the source support is the skill, and a habit of supporting placements now will save you from unsupported ones later.
4. **Placements, but no answers.** You may now write placement-level observations of the kind this lesson has modelled ("the question's house holds a mobile figure"; "both significators stable"; "the quesited's figure repeats in the eleventh") clearly grounded in house and figure rules. You may not write answers. No "the marriage will happen," no "the suit looks lost," not even hedged. Lesson 6 gives you the Witnesses and Judge, Lesson 7 the modes of perfection; full reading begins when the tools are in hand, and not an entry sooner.

The restraint is Lesson 4's, moved one step up: placement and answer are separate acts, and a journal full of clean, supported placements without answers is the exact training the reader needs. Two lessons from now, full reading begins.

## Worked example

Chart A, end to end, at the level this lesson can reach: the full placement procedure, performed slowly, the way your journal entries should perform it, and then the same chart asked two different questions, to fix the lesson's deepest point.

The standing question is **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** The chart, dealt into its houses:

<HouseChart mothers={['conjunctio', 'via', 'amissio', 'caput-draconis']} caption="Chart A in the twelve houses: Mothers Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis in houses 1–4; Daughters in 5–8; Nieces in 9–12." />

**Step 0.** The first house holds Conjunctio, points 2, 1, 1, 2, Mercury's figure of joining. Neither Rubeus nor Cauda Draconis: the chart proceeds. Write the line in the journal form: *1st: Conjunctio; proceeds.* For discipline's sake, note where the inspection did *not* look: Rubeus stands below the wheel as Right Witness, and the rule is indifferent to it; the Witnesses are "no houses," as Cattan said in Lesson 4, and Step 0 is a rule about a house.

**The question's house.** A marriage, asked by one of the parties: the seventh, on Cattan's plain words, the house of "any accord of marriage, to know whether it shall take effect or not." Note the source support in the journal: the question is about an accord between the querent and a facing party, the seventh's defining relation.

**The significators.** The querent: **Conjunctio in the first**, and pause on the small irony of the placement, which costs nothing to notice: the person asking about a marriage is signified by the figure whose name means *joining together*; mobile, airy, Mercury's figure, the conjoiner standing in the asker's own place. The quesited (the marriage itself, and the other party to it) is **Via in the seventh**: the Way, the Moon's figure, the only figure of four single points, the most changeable thing in the alphabet, standing in the house of the most binding accord. Placement lets you set those two figures side by side and notice their kinship (both mobile, both figures of motion and passage) and to notice, again, that Via stands twice in this chart, in the seventh and in the second. The chart record of repetitions you made in Lesson 4 is now a chart record of *placed* repetitions; what they prove about the marriage, Lessons 6 and 7 will extract.

**Now hold the chart still and move the question.** Suppose the same querent, the same casting, had asked instead a question of money: *"Shall I gain by my trade this year?"* Nothing in the chart changes: the same fifteen figures stand in the same fifteen places, because the chart is the record of one moment of asking. What changes is where the reading looks. The querent is still Conjunctio in the first; the asker's address never moves. But the quesited is now the substance, the second house, "properly the house of gaine and profite", and the second holds **Via**. The same figure that signified the marriage now signifies the money, *because it stands in a different house and the question has moved the reader's eye to that house*. The reading would now concern the first and the second, and the seventh, so central a moment ago, would recede into the chart's general finding.

And a third question, the same chart again: *"Will I obtain the office I seek?"* Preferment, honour, and the favour of the great belong to the tenth house. The querent is Conjunctio in the first, as before. The quesited is Puer in the tenth, a figure whose tenth-house entry you read in the placement lookup's first errand. Here the strength rule gives one more observation: Puer stands twice in this chart, in the tenth and in the sixth, once at an angle, once cadent. The same figure is strong in one appearance and weak in the other by place alone. Which appearance carries the office-question's finding? The angular one, in the house assigned to the question; the sixth-house Puer belongs to a different department altogether.

Three questions, one chart, one constant: the figures never moved. The houses are not properties of the figures; they are the fixed map underneath them. The question determines which house is relevant. The querent's house is always the first. The matter's house is wherever the matter belongs. Assigning that house accurately is this lesson's skill; the reading lessons teach what to do after the house is named. In your journal, the worked entry for Chart A now reads: *Step 0: 1st Conjunctio, proceeds. Question: marriage, 7th house (accord between querent and facing party). Significators: querent Conjunctio (1st); quesited Via (7th). Placements: both significators mobile; Via repeats in 2nd; no answer, tools pending.* That is a complete Lesson 5 reading. It is not yet an answer, and it is exactly as far as an honest reader can currently go.

  <ModelAnswer>
    The plain ones first. 1: the **seventh**, Cattan's "any accord of marriage, to know whether it shall take effect or not" (Book II, ch. 7). 2: the **second**, "properly the house of gaine and profite" (ch. 2). 3: the **third**, letters and messages "not from any farre Countrey," and the brother besides (ch. 3). 5: the **tenth**, honour and office (ch. 10). 8: the **twelfth**, "whether the prisoner shall parte out of prison, and when" (ch. 12). 12: the **eighth**, the pseudo-Agrippa's "dowry or portion of a wife," and Cattan's treaty of the marriage of a woman (Agrippa (attrib.), *Of Geomancy*, trans.     The tricky ones:

    4. **The father's sickness:** **fourth**. The trap is the sixth, but the sixth is the querent's own sickness. Cattan's fourth asks directly "whether the Father be dead or shall die quickly" (ch. 4). Lesson 11 teaches how to find the father's sickness from the father's house by turning the chart.
    6. **The rumour:** **ninth**. Cattan asks "if the common brute be true or not" there (ch. 9). The pseudo-Agrippa puts "rumours, news" in the fifth, so record the disagreement; this course follows Cattan here.
    7. **The stolen ring:** **second** for "will I have it again." The second holds the goods and "the place where the thing lost was stolen" (ch. 2). The seventh reads the thief, and Cattan's tenth also claims "any thing lost or stolen" (ch. 10). Lesson 11 teaches the full theft chain.
    9. **The faithful friend:** **eleventh**. Cattan asks "if thy friend be faithfull or a traytor to thée" there (ch. 11). His seventh also weighs friendship between two people (ch. 7), but "faithful to me" is the supporter's question.
    10. **The ship:** **ninth**. It owns ships, sailing, and the voyage (ch. 9). The fourth can read safe arrival in port (ch. 4), so either house can be supported; this course chooses the ninth because the question asks about the voyage and its hazards.
    11. **The sick servant:** **sixth**. No trap here: servants and sickness both belong to the sixth (ch. 6).
    13. **The absent man:** **eighth**. Cattan gives it "how the person doth that is from home," and "when he that is from hence shall returne" (ch. 8). The ninth is the journey; the eighth is the absent person's condition and return.
    15. **The year's plenty:** **eleventh** by Cattan's "whether the yeare shall be good and plentifull, or that there shall be dearth of victuals" (ch. 11). The fifth and ninth overlap it, so a supported choice among those houses is full credit.
    16. **The besieged town:** **fourth**. Cattan asks "whether a Towne besieged shall be taken or not" there (ch. 4). The fifth repeats the question for a place besieged, but the fourth is primary for towns and forts.
    19. **The stolen sheep:** **sixth**. Small beasts belong there, and Cattan asks "whether the beastes lost shall not be found, and who is the theefe" in that house (ch. 6).

    The lesson of the difficult cases: when the house maps overlap, choose by the question's centre of gravity, cite the chapter that supports your choice, and note the rival claim. An assignment you cannot support from a chapter is a guess.

<Exercise n={3} title="Angles, succedents, cadents" kind="drill">
  First, from memory: classify all twelve houses as angular, succedent or cadent, and state the strength rule. Then apply it to the course charts' repetitions: each of the following figures stands twice in its chart; say, for each pair of appearances, which placement is the stronger by place alone. Chart A: Conjunctio (houses 1 and 5), Via (2 and 7), Amissio (3 and 12), Puer (6 and 10). Chart B: Rubeus (2 and 3), Carcer (5 and 7), Via (9 and 11).   <ModelAnswer>
    The classification: angles 1, 4, 7, 10; succedents 2, 5, 8, 11; cadents 3, 6, 9, 12: each angle followed, in house order, by its succedent and then its cadent. The rule: a figure in an angle is strong (present, established, able to act and endure); in a succedent, middling; in a cadent, weak (remote, declining, departing). Gerard's first-house procedure states it as working law: the lord of the Ascendant "in strong Angles... signifies long life; in succedents, a middle age; and in cadent houses, a short life" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), questions of the first house).

    The repetitions, worked as a table:

    | Chart | Figure | Houses | Classes | Stronger placement |
    | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
    | A | Conjunctio | 1 and 5 | angle, succedent | the 1st |
    | A | Via | 2 and 7 | succedent, angle | the 7th |
    | A | Amissio | 3 and 12 | cadent, cadent | neither: both weak |
    | A | Puer | 6 and 10 | cadent, angle | the 10th |
    | B | Rubeus | 2 and 3 | succedent, cadent | the 2nd |
    | B | Carcer | 5 and 7 | succedent, angle | the 7th |
    | B | Via | 9 and 11 | cadent, succedent | the 11th |

And in all three charts the standing question's quesited sits at an angle, because the seventh is one: every seventh-house significator is angular by definition, which is part of why the sources use that house so heavily. The other party always meets you in strength of place.

<Exercise n={5} title="Why the querent is always the first house" kind="quiz">
  The one never-argued assignment in geomancy, tested from its foundations.

  1. Give Cattan's instruction on the first house, in his words. Cite it.
  2. How does Gerard's procedure presuppose the rule before stating it?
  3. What is the Ascendant in astrology, and whose is it?
  4. In the geomantic layer, what makes the querent the chart's efficient cause?
  5. When the diviner casts on behalf of another person:
     - a) the diviner owns the first house, as projector of the points
     - b) the querent whose desire drives the question owns the first house
     - c) the first house is divided between them
  6. Why is this assignment never argued, in any source, in any century?

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. "Give alwayes the first house unto that that the question which is made or propounded doth note to, that is to say, the motive of the querant" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 1).
    2. His first-house chapter reads the length of the querent's life from the lord of the Ascendant, and recovers "the intentions of the Querent" from where that lord stands: "where you find the Lord of the Ascendant, he comes to inquire about something pertaining to that house" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), questions of the first house).
    3. The rising place: the degree coming up over the eastern horizon at the moment and place of the asking, the moment's signature; and the moment belongs to the one whose act marks it, in a horary question the querent.
    4. The moved soul moves the hand, the hand makes the points, the points make the figures (Agrippa (attrib.), *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), opening): the chart does not merely happen near the querent, it proceeds from the querent.
    5. b). The querent is the one whose desire drives the question; the diviner is only the hand the desire borrowed.
    6. Because it is not an assignment at all but the structure of the situation: no querent, no question; no question, no chart; and the place where the chart rises is the asker, as surely as a signature is the signer's.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={6} title="Via around the wheel" kind="drill">
  Using the placement lookup above and the [Via reference pages](/figures/via/), trace Via through all twelve houses: for each house, read what the sources say of the placement and note, in a phrase, the finding's drift: good, ill, or mixed, and for what matters. Then write a paragraph stating the pattern: what one through-line explains Via's behaviour in every house?

  <ModelAnswer>
    The notes will vary in wording; the drift, read across the corpus, runs roughly so: in the first, an unsettled querent, disposed to motion and change; in the second, small gain and a poor grip on goods, though good for journeying; in the third, traffic on the road: kin, letters, short ways, where a figure of ways is at home; in the fourth, lands and patrimony that do not hold still; in the fifth, comings and goings of messengers and news; in the sixth, sickness that moves: changes, crises, departures of disease; in the seventh, ill for the bonds of marriage and partnership, the other party changeable or departing, but excepted, in Cattan's own words, for voyages; in the eighth, change in the matters of death and the absent; in the ninth, the figure's best seat: the long journey, where the Way in the house of ways is finding of passage itself; in the tenth, offices and favours in flux; in the eleventh, friends and hopes inconstant; in the twelfth, movement even in the house of confinement: the prisoner's house touched by the figure of release and departure.

    The through-line, in one sentence: **Via does not change its nature in any house; it delivers the same finding (motion, change, passage) into twelve different departments, and the house decides whether motion is good news.** Where the matter asked about is itself a motion (a journey, a message, a removal, an escape) Via favours it: the lookup shows Cattan excepting voyages by name even in houses where he calls the figure ill for everything else. Where the matter is a *holding* (goods, marriage, partnership, office, anything the querent wants to keep fixed) the same motion is dissolution, and Via points against it. This is the figure's Lesson 3 character (the Moon, mobile, the only figure of four single points, pure change) applied through the map of Lesson 5, and it demonstrates how figure and house combine: the figure supplies the general quality, the house supplies the matter, and the reading must include both. It also explains, in advance, why Chart A is delicate: its marriage question puts the figure of passage in the house of the bond, and what that means will depend on methods taught later.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

## You are ready to continue when…

- You can assign any plain question to its house at once, and any tricky one with source support you could cite chapter for.
- You can recite the significations of all twelve houses (Cattan's core, at minimum) and name the houses where the pseudo-Agrippa's list visibly diverges.
- You can classify every house as angular, succedent or cadent from memory and state strength by place, with Gerard's first-house procedure as your citation.
- You can say why the querent is always the first house in the sources' own terms, and cite two sources for the rule.
- Step 0 is a reflex: you inspect the first house of any chart before doing anything else with it, you can state the course's practice for Rubeus or Cauda Draconis rising, and you can name the rule's lineage (Cattan's line and the Golden Dawn's hardening) with Heydon's dissent on record.
- You can place all three course charts from memory or near it: each chart's first and seventh houses, each standing question's significators, and each chart's Step 0 result.
- Your journal is fully updated: every chart placed into houses, significators named and supported, placements observed, and not an answer anywhere in it.
