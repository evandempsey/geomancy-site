You can build charts now but not read them. A figure answers "what?": gain, loss, change, imprisonment, joy. It cannot answer "whose, and about what?" Acquisitio is gain, but gain of money, of a wife, of a lawsuit? Gain *for the querent*, or for the adversary he is asking about? Until the chart has places as well as figures, its sentences have verbs and no subjects.

The places are the twelve houses, geomancy's map of the human world: twelve divisions of the chart, each owning a department of life (the querent in the first, money in the second, brothers in the third, the father in the fourth, on around to the prisons and secret enemies of the twelfth). Every question lives in one of them, and before you can weigh an answer you must know which house it stands in.

The lesson holds a great deal of matter (twelve houses, each with a paragraph of significations) but only one new *skill*: given a question, name its house. The significations are learned by drill and return, like the figures in Lessons 2 and 3; the [house reference pages](/houses/) hold them in full.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 1 through 4, and leans hardest on Lesson 4's allocation: Mothers into houses one through four, Daughters into five through eight, Nieces into nine through twelve, the Witnesses and Judge outside the wheel. If you cannot deal a shield into its houses without consulting the lesson, repair that first.

The assigned reading comes first. Crowley's chapter on the twelve houses is the modern comparison; the Golden Dawn deals the figures into the houses by a different rule than this course follows, which the lesson returns to. Gerard's first- and seventh-house chapters are the medieval witness, showing what working a house looked like in the older branch of the art.

## A place for every matter

What is a house? In astrology, the mother art, the twelve houses are twelve divisions of the heavens as seen from the place and moment of a birth or a question: the first house is the slice of sky just rising over the eastern horizon, the tenth is the region overhead, the seventh is setting in the west, the fourth lies underfoot, and the rest fill the spaces between. Because the whole sky wheels past a fixed observer once a day, the houses are not constellations or signs; they are *positions relative to the asker*, anchored to the asker's own horizon. And from the earliest astrology each position was assigned a department of human life, so that the heavens at any moment became a fully addressed map of the asker's world.

Geomancy took this map whole. It could not take the sky (the art's entire bargain, as Gerard states it and Lesson 1 examined, is that "it is not requisite to behold neither the Ascendant, nor the hour in a Table, as it is in Astrology"), so it raised a heaven of its own from the points, and housed it. The first twelve figures of the shield are dealt into the twelve houses by the allocation you learned in Lesson 4, and from that moment each figure is no longer merely the Third Mother or the Second Niece: it is *the figure of the fourth house*, standing in the place of the father and of lands and of the ends of things, and it will be read as testimony about exactly those matters and no others. Crowley's modern handbook opens its houses chapter by saying that the meaning of the twelve houses "is to be found, primarily, in any text-book of Astrology" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. III), which is true, and is also the Golden Dawn's characteristically brisk way of skipping the part of the art that the Renaissance manuals considered worth two hundred pages. Cattan's entire Book II, twelve chapters, one per house, is a tour of the houses and of what every figure signifies in each; the pseudo-Agrippa gives a compact house-list of his own and then, like Cattan, delineates the figures house by house. The houses are not a preliminary to the tradition's teaching. They *are* the bulk of it.

A question is always about a relation. "Will the marriage go ahead?" concerns *this asker* and *that match*: two parties and an outcome between them. The houses are how the chart assigns parties their addresses: the querent in the first, the matter asked about in the house of its kind, the marriage in the seventh, the money in the second, the office in the tenth. Judgment, the work of Lessons 6 through 10, is a jurisprudence performed on the figures at those addresses and the relations between them.

## The wheel: angles, succedents, cadents

Before the twelve are taken one by one, learn the skeleton they hang on, because Cattan names every house by it. Open any chapter of his Book II and the house is introduced not by its number alone but by its station: the second house is "the succidant of the Angle of the Orient," the third is "the Cadant from the ascendant," the sixth is "the Cadant from the Angle septentrionall." The skeleton is this. Four houses are **angles**: the first (the Ascendant, the angle of the east, Cattan's "Angle of the Orient"), the fourth (the angle of the north, the earth beneath), the seventh (the angle of the west, the Occident), and the tenth (the angle of the south, the midheaven, Cattan's "house of the heart of the heaven"). These are the four pillars of the wheel, the rising, setting, upper and lower hinges of the sky. After each angle, in the order of the houses, comes a **succedent**: a house that "succeeds," following the angle and feeding it: the second, fifth, eighth and eleventh. And after each succedent comes a **cadent**: a house "falling away" from the angle: the third, sixth, ninth and twelfth.

The doctrine attached to the skeleton is a doctrine of strength, and it comes straight from the mother art: **a figure in an angle is strong, in a succedent middling, in a cadent weak.** What stands at an angle stands square to the world: present, established, able to act and to last. What stands in a succedent is on its way to that condition; what stands in a cadent is falling out of it: remote, declining, departing. Gerard applies the doctrine with a directness that makes it unforgettable: asked about the length of a life, he looks at the lord of the Ascendant, "who if he be in strong Angles, it signifies long life; in succedents, a middle age; and in cadent houses, a short life" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), questions of the first house). Strong place, long life; falling place, short one. The same logic will run through the whole of judgment: testimony from an angle outweighs testimony from a cadent house, a significator angular is a party in possession of the field, a significator cadent is a party already halfway out the door. You will use this doctrine formally from Lesson 8 onward, when the dignities of figures are weighed; learn it now because it is built into the houses' very names, and because it gives the wheel a shape your memory can grip: four strong pillars, each trailing a middling house and a weak one.

The skeleton also dictates this lesson's order of march. The twelve houses are taught below in their plain order, first to twelfth, but grouped in fours (each angle followed by its succedent and its cadent) because that is how Cattan's own chapter-openings present them, and because the grouping carries meaning: each quarter of the wheel is a region of life anchored on its angle. The east is the querent and what is nearest him; the north, beneath the earth, is origin and ground; the west is the other party; the south, overhead, is the world's high places. Hold the four anchors and the eight remaining houses arrange themselves around them.

## The east: the querent and what is nearest

### The first house

The first house is the Ascendant (the rising place, the angle of the Orient) and it is the querent. Here is Cattan's own chapter-opening, the first of the twelve you will learn:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/significations" />

Every item in his catalogue is the asker: disposition, intent, dwelling, the beginning of enterprises, length of life, the quality of the heart, the make and physiognomy of the body, health and sickness, joy and sadness, and (by the tradition's anatomical assignment) the head. The closing instruction is the cornerstone: whatever the question, "give alwayes the first house unto... the motive of the querant." The asker owns the first house in every chart, the fixed pole of the whole map, and Step 0 below makes it the first move of every placement.

Why this house? In the mother art the first house is the sky rising at the moment and place of the asking, stamped with that moment; and on Lesson 1's doctrine the moment belongs to the querent, whose moved hand made the chart. The chart exists because the querent asked, so the rising figure is the asker. The pseudo-Agrippa says it plainly: "The first house sheweth the person of the querent, as often as a question shall be proposed concerning himself, of his own matters, or any thing appertaining to him." Exercise 5 will quiz you on this argument, source by source.

### The second house

The succedent of the eastern angle is the house of substance:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-02/significations" />

"Properly the house of gaine and profite": money, moveable goods, gain to come and whether it comes by labour, inheritance or gift; the charges a person shall bear; winnings at play; the profit of a voyage or a place or a service. The logic of its position: the house that *succeeds* the querent's own holds the querent's *belongings*, what follows a person without being the self. Two stray tenants for later: Cattan lodges here "the place where the thing lost was stolen" (one corner of the lost-and-stolen matter, whose other corners you meet in the seventh and tenth houses) and the news brought by a messenger, which the fifth house will claim again. The sources' house-lists overlap at the edges, and this lesson points the overlaps out rather than pretend the map is tidier than it is.

### The third house

The cadent of the east is the house of brethren and of the near-at-hand: brothers, sisters, "nephewes, and other kinsfolkes and alies"; neighbours and their amity; the scholar at his studies; "embassages, letters, and messages which come not from any farre Countrey"; short journeys and whether there is ill company on the road (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 3). The pseudo-Agrippa's entry is the same house in miniature: "brethren, sisters and collaterals in blood: it judgeth of small journeys, and fidelities of men." The genius of the place is *nearness without identity*: the people who share your blood and your street without being you, the letters that travel a day's ride and not an ocean. Keep the qualifier "not from any farre Countrey" in mind; its far-country counterpart is the ninth house, the third's opposite across the wheel, and the pairing of short road and long road is one of several axes by which the wheel answers itself house against house.

## The north: ground, issue and service

### The fourth house

The fourth house, "the angle septentrionall or the earth," lies at the bottom of the wheel, beneath the asker's feet, and everything in it is groundwork: the father, grandfather and great-grandfather; "houses, landes, vines, gardens, medowes"; cities and towns and the people in them; castles, palaces, forts; "the place where it is thought that treasure is hidden"; whether it is good to buy lands and inheritances; whether a besieged town will be taken; whether the ship at sea will come to port (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 4). The pseudo-Agrippa concurs: "father and grandfathers, patrimony and inheritance, possessions, buildings, fields, treasure, and things hidden." One item outranks the rest for judgment: the fourth house contains the question "upon the end of all the figures to know if the issue shall be good or bad", **the end of the matter**. Whatever the question, the fourth house holds its final state, the way the earth holds whatever falls. In Lesson 10's full method, the figure of the fourth is a standing witness, consulted in every chart for how the business finishes.

### The fifth house

The succedent of the north, which Cattan calls "the good fortune" (the classical *bona fortuna*), is the house of children and pleasure: the child, its birth, legitimacy and nature; whether a woman is with child and whether of a boy or a girl; food, drink and new clothes; promises and whether they will be performed; messengers, the speed of their coming and the contents of their letters; "all pleasures, as to kisse, coll, sing, daunce, banquet, and play on all instruments of musicke"; the fruits of the earth and whether the season will yield them (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 5). What springs from a person and delights a person: issue in both senses. The pseudo-Agrippa's fifth, you will find in the comparison section below, is a strikingly different list, and Exercise 4 sends you to that difference with both texts open.

### The sixth house

The cadent of the north, "the ill fortune" (*mala fortuna*), is the house of sickness and servants: diseases, their length, their humour, recovery or death; the physician and "whether it be good to take phisicke"; servants and subjects; small beasts, "sheepe, lambes, goates, swyne"; labourers, masons, carpenters and men of low condition; "feare and fright, shame, povertie and lacke" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 6). The pseudo-Agrippa again agrees in substance: "infirmities, and medicines; of familiars and servants; of cattle and domestic animals." The unifying thought is the body and household in their *subjection*: what serves, what labours, what ails. One caution: the sixth is the house of the *querent's* sickness when the querent asks about his own body. When the question is the illness of someone else (a father, a wife, a friend) the question lives first with the *person* asked about, in that person's own house; how the tradition then finds the sick man's sickness from the sick man's house is a technique (the "turning" of the chart) reserved for Lesson 11. For now: assign the question to the person, and the sixth to sickness only when the sick body is the querent's own.

## The west: the other

### The seventh house

The angle of the Occident, the setting-place, directly opposite the querent, and therefore, by the wheel's own geometry, the house of everyone who stands opposite the querent. This is the house of all three course-chart questions:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-07/significations" />

The chapter is a portrait gallery of the *other party*. The opponent: "all debates, sutes in lawe," which of two armies shall have the victory, war or peace, the ill will between two persons. The partner and the spouse: "any accord of marriage, to know whether it shall take effect or not" (which Chart A's standing question puts in plain words), the riches of the prospective wife, whether the agreement made between two persons shall continue, whether the companion appointed to you is good or bad. The thief and the fugitive: whether the stolen thing is in the house, who did the robbery, whether the lost woman or friend shall be recovered. Marriage, lawsuit, partnership, enmity, theft: what unites this apparently miscellaneous crowd is the relation: each is a matter between the querent and a facing party. The pseudo-Agrippa's entry draws the same circle, "wedlock, whoredom, and fornication; rendereth judgment of friends, strifes, and controversies; rendereth judgment of Judges", and that last clause, the seventh as the house of judges and arbiters, completes the portrait: everyone across the table, whether they face you as bride, rival, or magistrate. All three course charts ask seventh-house questions (a marriage, a broken partnership, a suit at law) which is no accident of this course's design: the seventh is, with the first, the most worked house in the practical literature, because most of what people urgently ask concerns another person.

### The eighth house

The succedent of the west is "the house of death": death and its manner and time, who shall inherit, who killed the slain man; and, clustering around death, the matters that pass through other hands: the goods and substance of a friend, "the treatie of the marriage of a woman" and (in the pseudo-Agrippa) "the dowry or portion of a wife"; things given to another to keep and whether they will be restored; money put to usury; fear of harm to come (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 8). Note the structural echo: as the second house is the querent's substance, the eighth (second from the seventh) is the *other party's* substance, the wife's dowry, the friend's goods, the debtor's ability. The eighth is also, in Cattan, the house of the absent: "how the person doth that is from home," when he that is from hence shall return, and what profit he has done abroad; the house where the tradition looks for those who have passed out of sight, whether over the horizon or under the earth. And at the chapter's end, characteristically, "the experience of Nigromancie, invocations of Spirites... and other divellish Artes": death's house keeps death's company.

### The ninth house

The cadent of the west is "the house of God": temples, churches, monasteries; priests, religious persons and their doings; the scholar, his books and what estate he shall return in; benefices and whether the man shall have the bishopric he pretends to; dreams and whether they are true; "if the common brute be true or not" (the rumour, the report running through the town); and the long journey in all its forms: the voyage by sea or land, the ship and her sailing, the army on the water, the wind and the shipwreck (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 9). The pseudo-Agrippa compresses: "journeys, faith, and constancy; dreams, divine sciences, and religion." Here is the third house's far-country counterpart: the third's letters come "not from any farre Countrey," and everything farther belongs here: far travel, and the farthest travel of all, the mind's traffic with heaven in religion, learning and dreams. Cattan's fourth house also claims "whether the ship on the sea shall come to a good port": the ninth owns the *voyage*, the fourth the *coming home to ground*. Choosing between overlapping claims by the question's centre of gravity is a skill the exercises train.

## The south: the world's high places

### The tenth house

The angle of the south, the midheaven, "the house of the heart of the heaven," the point overhead:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-10/significations" />

Honour and praise; kings, queens, princes, lords, popes, "open Officers and Magistrates," their laws and decrees; whether a lord loves you and will do you good; preferment and office, including "any office touching warre"; and (by the same logic that put the father at the bottom of the wheel) the **mother** at the top of it, with the grandmother and (Cattan adds, with Renaissance frankness) the wife or "Lemman." Two tenants surprise the modern student. The physician and his medicine appear here as well as in the sixth: the sixth owning the *sickness*, the tenth the *art and authority* that treats it. And "any thing lost or stolen" appears here, in the house of authority, alongside its other lodgings in the second (the goods themselves and the place of the theft) and the seventh (the thief): the lost-object question is the tradition's great multi-house case, and Exercise 1 makes you map it. The pseudo-Agrippa's tenth is a single clean clause: "honours, and of magisterial offices." When the worked example below asks Chart A a question of preferment, this is the house it will ask in.

### The eleventh house

The succedent of the south, which Cattan calls "the good Angle" (the classical *bonus daemon*, the good spirit), is the house of friends and hopes: the friend, he or she, "the thing ye love, and whereof ye hope to have consolation, ayde and profite"; whether thy friend is faithful "or a traytor to thée"; the secret companion you give credit to; the fortune a man shall have; the court and the service of the king, and whether profit will come of going; the treasures, rents and revenues of the king; whether the promise is true; whether the year shall be good and plentiful or a year of dearth (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 11). Friendship sits in two houses, and the division is instructive: the seventh's friend is a *counterpart* (friendship as a bond between two facing parties, with its quarrels and its contracts) while the eleventh's friend is a *support*, the one from whom you hope for aid and consolation. Which house a friendship question takes depends on which of those two relations the question is really about, and one of the tricky items in Exercise 1 turns exactly on the difference.

### The twelfth house

The cadent of the south, "the evill Spirite" (*malus daemon*), closes the wheel with everything that undermines a life from the dark: "a prison, and of the darkenesse thereof, and also of the prisoner therein detained"; desolation, lamentation and mourning; incurable sickness: "the leprosie, the gowte, the paulsie"; the traitor, the ill servant, the thief *of the house*; "a privie ennemy rather then upon an open"; great beasts: oxen, camels, elephants, horses, "all other beastes that beare and be ridden upon"; banishment; whether the prisoner shall come out of prison, and when (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 12). Set the twelfth beside the seventh and the wheel teaches you its own finest distinction: the seventh holds the *open* enemy, the adversary who faces you in court or field; the twelfth holds the *privy* enemy, the one inside your walls. An enmity question must be sorted accordingly, and the sources are explicit that the sorting matters. With the twelfth the map is complete: every department of a human life (self, substance, kin, ground, issue, service, the other, death, God, honour, hope, and the dark) has an address.

## One map, several surveyors

You have now heard mostly Cattan, and that is deliberate: his Book II is the fullest and most systematic house-treatise in this course's sources, and it is the backbone of this site's [house pages](/houses/) and of this course's teaching. But the course's standing method (teach the disagreements, count the witnesses) applies to houses as much as to figures, so set the other surveyors' maps beside his. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa's house-list entire, as Turner prints it:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/houses-significations" />

Read it against the chapters above and three things show. First, the broad agreement is massive, and it is the agreement that matters: querent in the first, substance in the second, brethren and small journeys in the third, father and patrimony in the fourth, sickness and servants in the sixth, wedlock and strife in the seventh, death and dowry in the eighth, journeys and religion in the ninth, honours in the tenth. This is one map, surveyed twice; the houses are among the most stable doctrines in the entire tradition, far more stable than the elemental and zodiacal attributions whose quarrels you learned in Lesson 3. Second, the divergences are real and local: the pseudo-Agrippa's fifth house ("legates, messengers, rumours, news; of honour, and of accidents after death") visibly overlaps what Cattan distributes to the ninth (rumours) and the tenth (honour), and Exercise 4 will quiz you on exactly this. Third, and a smaller honesty, the passage as Turner prints it runs only to the tenth house; for the eleventh and twelfth in this list's voice you must go to the pseudo-Agrippa's figure-by-figure delineations later in the treatise. When this course's prose needs a single answer (where does a rumour live?) it follows Cattan, and says so; where the sources split, the model answers will teach you to report the split, because in this art a divergence noted is knowledge and a divergence flattened is error.

Gerard, the medieval witness, you have already met in the readings: his house-chapters are not lists of significations but worked judgment procedures (look to the lord of the Ascendant, count to the eighth, judge by angle and fall) and they show you what the map was *for* before the Renaissance systematised it. And Crowley, the modern comparison, contributes mostly a difference you must not let corrupt your practice: in the Golden Dawn allocation that his chapter III tabulates, the figures are *not* dealt Mothers-into-one-through-four; the First Mother goes to the tenth house, the Second to the Ascendant, and so on around a different circuit. Within his system the house *meanings* are the inherited astrological ones (on that, the revival changed nothing) but the dealing rule differs, which means a Golden Dawn chart and a Cattan chart cast from the same four Mothers house their figures differently. Lesson 4 fixed this course's allocation once and for all: **Mothers in one to four, Daughters in five to eight, Nieces in nine to twelve.** Know that Crowley's differs, so that his example charts do not silently mislead you; and when you read any historical chart, identify its allocation before you read a single house.

## Choosing significators

In every reading, two figures matter before all others, the **significators** (the term, like the houses, is borrowed from astrology): the figure that signifies the querent, and the figure that signifies the thing asked about, the **quesited**.

The querent's significator is the figure of the first house. Always: not a convention the diviner may vary but the fixed pole of the art, as the first-house section showed. Whoever asks stands in the first house; when you cast for another person, *that person* is the querent and owns it, and you are only the hand.

The quesited's significator is the figure of the house where the question lives, and finding that house is the judgment-call this whole lesson has been training. The marriage, the partnership, the lawsuit: seventh house, so the seventh's figure signifies the match, the partner, the adversary. The money: second; the figure of the second *is* the money, for this chart and this question. The father: fourth; the office: tenth; the voyage: ninth; the imprisoned friend: twelfth. The procedure is always the same: classify the matter, name its house from the significations you have learned, and the figure standing there becomes the matter's representative in everything that follows. From Lesson 7 onward, the entire doctrine of perfection (how a chart says yes) is built on the relation between these two figures and these two houses; you cannot yet judge that relation, but after this lesson you can always *name the parties*, and naming the parties correctly is half of every sound judgment in the literature.

Two refinements complete the doctrine. First, some questions occupy more than one house at once, because they involve more than two parties. The stolen goods are the great standard case, and you have already watched it scatter across the wheel: the goods themselves in the second, the thief in the seventh, the authority that recovers them (and the lost thing generally) claimed also by the tenth. A theft question therefore reads several houses together, each for its own party. Sickness of another person, as the sixth-house section warned, is the other standard case: the sick father lives in the fourth, and his sickness is found by an operation on *his* house. Both techniques belong to Lesson 11, where the question-classes are treated one by one; this lesson only requires you to know that multi-house questions exist, and to place their *primary* house correctly. Second, the houses do not exhaust their usefulness in the two significators. Once placed, every house of the chart is testimony about its own department (the fourth about how the matter ends, the eleventh about the help the querent can hope for) and the full method of Lesson 10 reads them all. The significators are not the whole reading. They are where the reading begins.

## Step 0: inspect the first house

The placement protocol begins, before any significator is named, with an inspection. The rule it applies you have met before (Lessons 3 and 4); here it becomes a habit of the hands. Crowley's formulation is the bluntest:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/destroy-rule" />

**Step 0: before anything else (before significators, before any reading of any house) look at the first house. If Rubeus or Cauda Draconis stands there, this course's practice is: record the chart in full, mark the entry *unjudged*, and let the question rest.** No significators are chosen, no placements are read, and the question is not re-asked that day; if the matter still presses when time has passed, frame it freshly and cast again. That is the whole of the practice; perform the inspection invariably, until a first house containing anything else barely registers.

And because this course never teaches *a* tradition as *the* tradition: know exactly whose rule you are keeping. The kernel is Renaissance and older: Cattan reports that the Egyptians "and a great part of the Latines" held that a chart with Rubeus in the first house "should not be judged. But that figure must be broken and make an other," and he sides with them on his own experience, extending the same opinion, with less conviction, to Cauda Draconis (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 1). The mechanical formula (both figures equally, instant destruction, the two-hour quarantine) is the Golden Dawn's hardening of that kernel, and is always to be flagged as modern. And the dissent is on the record: Heydon knows the custom and rejects it, "I am of the opinion that the Judgment herein should not be delayd"; a terrible answer, he holds, is still an answer (Heydon, *Theomagia* (London, 1663–4), Book II, ch. 1, s.v. Cauda Draconis). This course follows Cattan's line as practice, as Lesson 3 said it would, while requiring you to know the lineages: a geomancer should always be able to say which of his habits are the tradition and which are a tradition.

Step 0 inspects **the first house and nothing else**. Rubeus in the second house does not trigger it; Cauda Draconis in the seventh does not trigger it: a dreadful figure in the quesited's house is *testimony*, to be weighed when you can weigh, not a defect in the cast. And the Witnesses and Judge, standing outside the wheel, can never trigger it: Rubeus as Right Witness is a harsh voice in the chart's final sentence, not a corruption of the casting. All three course charts, as you are about to see, contain Rubeus or Cauda Draconis somewhere prominent, and all three pass Step 0, which is exactly why they can teach you the difference between a chart that should not be judged and a chart that is hard to judge.

## The course charts, placed

Now the three standing charts, each through Step 0, then the significators of its question, then a first look at where things stand. What follows stops short of judgment: everything here is placement only.

### Chart A: "Will the marriage go ahead?"

Mothers Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis; dealt into the houses: Conjunctio in the first, Via in the second, Amissio in the third, Caput Draconis in the fourth; Daughters Conjunctio, Puer, Via, Acquisitio into the fifth through eighth; Nieces Carcer, Puer, Puella, Amissio into the ninth through twelfth. Step 0: the first house holds **Conjunctio**, neither Rubeus nor Cauda Draconis; the chart is fit to proceed, and note in passing that Rubeus *is* in this chart, as Right Witness, where Step 0 does not look. Significators for the standing question: the querent is **Conjunctio in the first**; the marriage is a seventh-house matter (Cattan's "any accord of marriage, to know whether it shall take effect or not") and the seventh holds **Via**, so Via signifies the match. What placement licenses you to observe: the house of the question holds the most changeable figure of the sixteen (Via, the Moon's figure, mobile) in a question about the most binding of contracts; and the same Via stands again in the second house, one of the four repetitions Lesson 4 had you notice. What those facts *mean* for the marriage is the business of Lessons 6 and 7, and the worked example below returns to this chart at full length.

### Chart B: "Will the querent recover the partnership?"

Mothers Puella, Rubeus, Rubeus, Amissio. The wheel: Puella in the first, Rubeus in the second *and* third, Amissio in the fourth; Carcer in the fifth, Conjunctio in the sixth, Carcer in the seventh, Laetitia in the eighth; Via in the ninth, Cauda Draconis in the tenth, Via in the eleventh, Tristitia in the twelfth. Step 0, and here the rule's precision earns its keep: this chart contains Rubeus twice and Cauda Draconis twice (in the tenth house and as Left Witness), and *none of them stands in the first house*. The first holds **Puella**, the gentle figure of Venus; the chart passes, and a student who broke it for the Rubeus in the second would be keeping a rule the tradition never made. Significators: the querent is **Puella in the first**; the partner and the partnership (the seventh's "companion appointed to you," the accord between two persons) are **Carcer in the seventh**. Placement-level observations: both significators are stable figures, and the quesited's is Carcer, the prison, Saturn's figure of holding and delay, standing in the house of the broken partnership, while the same Carcer stands also in the fifth, another of the chart's repetitions. A question about *recovering* something faces a seventh house whose figure does not move. Whether immobility here means the partnership is locked shut or locked *in* (whether the prison holds the breach or the bond) is precisely the kind of question the judgment lessons exist to answer, and you should feel, and resist, the itch to answer it now.

### Chart C: "Will the querent win the lawsuit?"

Mothers Amissio, Amissio, Puella, Fortuna Minor. The wheel: Amissio in the first *and* second, Puella in the third, Fortuna Minor in the fourth; Via in the fifth, Tristitia in the sixth, Cauda Draconis in the seventh, Albus in the eighth; Populus in the ninth, Caput Draconis in the tenth, Cauda Draconis in the eleventh, Fortuna Minor in the twelfth. Step 0: the first house holds **Amissio**, a poor figure, loss itself, but not a forbidden one; the inspection asks one narrow question and the answer is *proceed*. (Cauda Draconis is in this chart twice, seventh and eleventh houses, and again Step 0, which reads only the first, is untouched.) Significators: the querent is **Amissio in the first**; the adversary and the suit ("all debates, sutes in lawe") are **Cauda Draconis in the seventh**. Placement licenses this much: the querent's own house holds Loss, doubled into the second, the house of his substance (a lawsuit chart in which the asker's place and his money's place both wear the figure of losing) while the house of the adversary holds the Dragon's Tail, one of the two figures of dread, repeated in the eleventh. And over all of it, as Lesson 4 had you note, stands the Judge Acquisitio, gain, among the best figures in the art. A dark first house, a dark seventh, a bright Judge: the tension flagged when this chart was first cast is now *located* (you know exactly which houses carry it) and its resolution is deferred, deliberately, to the lessons that can resolve it. This is the conflict chart, and it will earn its keep in Lesson 10.

The three charts have taught something between them. All three questions are seventh-house questions, yet the three seventh houses could hardly differ more (changeable Via, immobile Carcer, ruinous Cauda Draconis), and each chart's character as a *placed* thing is already distinct in a way the bare shields of Lesson 4 were not. The figures gave you sixteen natures; the houses give those natures addresses.

## The journal, upgraded

Your casting journal now takes its second protocol. Everything from Lesson 4 stands (the question in full, the moment, the sixteen rows, the verified shield, the house allocation, the labelled first impressions) and to it you now add, for every chart, current and future:

1. **Step 0, recorded.** One line: the figure of the first house, and "proceeds", or, when the rule bites, "Rubeus rising" or "Cauda rising," the entry marked **unjudged**, and nothing further written for that chart beyond the record itself.
2. **The house placements, written out.** The twelve figures in their houses, by name and number, not merely the wheel drawn: writing "7th: Via" engages the memory in a way the diagram alone does not.
3. **The significators, named.** The querent's figure, and the quesited's house and figure, with one sentence defending the choice of house from the significations ("the partnership is the seventh's *companion appointed to you*") because the defence is the skill, and a habit of defending placements now will save you from undefended ones later.
4. **Placements, but no verdicts.** You may now write placement-level observations of the kind this lesson has modelled ("the question's house holds a mobile figure"; "both significators stable"; "the quesited's figure repeats in the eleventh") clearly grounded in house and figure doctrine. You may not write answers. No "the marriage will happen," no "the suit looks lost," not even hedged. Lesson 6 gives you the Witnesses and Judge, Lesson 7 the modes of perfection; judgment begins when the instruments are in hand, and not an entry sooner.

The restraint is Lesson 4's, moved one step up: placement and judgment are separate acts, and a journal full of clean, defended, unjudged placements is the exact training a judge needs. Two lessons from now, judgment begins.

## Worked example

Chart A, end to end, at the level this lesson can reach: the full placement protocol, performed slowly, the way your journal entries should perform it, and then the same chart asked two different questions, to fix the lesson's deepest point.

The standing question is **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** The chart, dealt into its houses:

<HouseChart mothers={['conjunctio', 'via', 'amissio', 'caput-draconis']} caption="Chart A in the twelve houses: Mothers Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis in houses 1–4; Daughters in 5–8; Nieces in 9–12." />

**Step 0.** The first house holds Conjunctio, points 2, 1, 1, 2, Mercury's figure of joining. Neither Rubeus nor Cauda Draconis: the chart proceeds. Write the line in the journal form: *1st: Conjunctio; proceeds.* For discipline's sake, note where the inspection did *not* look: Rubeus stands below the wheel as Right Witness, and the rule is indifferent to it; the Witnesses are "no houses," as Cattan said in Lesson 4, and Step 0 is a rule about a house.

**The question's house.** A marriage, asked by one of the parties: the seventh, on Cattan's plain words, the house of "any accord of marriage, to know whether it shall take effect or not." Note the defence in the journal: the question is about an accord between the querent and a facing party, the seventh's defining relation.

**The significators.** The querent: **Conjunctio in the first**, and pause on the small irony of the placement, which costs nothing to notice: the person asking about a marriage is signified by the figure whose name means *joining together*; mobile, airy, Mercury's figure, the conjoiner standing in the asker's own place. The quesited (the marriage itself, and the other party to it) is **Via in the seventh**: the Way, the Moon's figure, the only figure of four single points, the most changeable thing in the alphabet, standing in the house of the most binding accord. Placement licenses you to set those two figures side by side and notice their kinship (both mobile, both figures of motion and passage) and to notice, again, that Via stands twice in this chart, in the seventh and in the second. The dossier of repetitions you made in Lesson 4 is now a dossier of *placed* repetitions; what they prove about the marriage, Lessons 6 and 7 will extract.

**Now hold the chart still and move the question.** Suppose the same querent, the same casting, had asked instead a question of money: *"Shall I gain by my trade this year?"* Nothing in the chart changes: the same fifteen figures stand in the same fifteen places, because the chart is the record of one moment of asking. What changes is where the reading looks. The querent is still Conjunctio in the first; the asker's address never moves. But the quesited is now the substance, the second house, "properly the house of gaine and profite", and the second holds **Via**. The same figure that signified the marriage now signifies the money, *because it stands in a different house and the question has moved the reader's eye to that house*. The reading would now concern the first and the second, and the seventh, so central a moment ago, would recede into the chart's general testimony.

And a third question, the same chart again: *"Shall I obtain the office I sue for?"* Preferment, honour, the favour of the great: the tenth house, the heart of heaven. The querent: Conjunctio in the first, as ever. The quesited: the tenth holds **Puer**, Mars's soldier in the house of kings, a figure whose tenth-house delineation you read in the placement lookup's first errand. And here the strength doctrine gives you one more licensed observation: Puer stands twice in this chart, in the tenth and in the sixth, once at an angle, once cadent; the same figure, by place alone, strong in one appearance and weak in the other. Which appearance carries the office-question's testimony? The angular one, in the house the question lives in; the sixth-house Puer belongs to a different department altogether.

Three questions, one chart, one constant: the figures never moved. The houses are not properties of the figures; they are the fixed map underneath them, and the question is a lamp the reader carries around the wheel, lighting one district at a time. The querent's district is always the first. The matter's district is wherever the matter lives. Carrying the lamp accurately is this lesson's whole skill; the judgment lessons teach what to do once a district is lit. In your journal, the worked entry for Chart A now reads: *Step 0: 1st Conjunctio, proceeds. Question: marriage, 7th house (accord between querent and facing party). Significators: querent Conjunctio (1st); quesited Via (7th). Placements: both significators mobile; Via repeats in 2nd; no verdict, instruments pending.* That is a complete Lesson 5 reading. It is not yet an answer, and it is exactly as far as an honest reader can currently go.

  <ModelAnswer>
    The notes will vary in wording; the drift, read across the corpus, runs roughly so: in the first, an unsettled querent, disposed to motion and change; in the second, small gain and a poor grip on goods, though good for journeying; in the third, traffic on the road: kin, letters, short ways, where a figure of ways is at home; in the fourth, lands and patrimony that do not hold still; in the fifth, comings and goings of messengers and news; in the sixth, sickness that moves: changes, crises, departures of disease; in the seventh, ill for the bonds of marriage and partnership, the other party changeable or departing, but excepted, in Cattan's own words, for voyages; in the eighth, change in the matters of death and the absent; in the ninth, the figure's best seat: the long journey, where the Way in the house of ways is testimony of passage itself; in the tenth, offices and favours in flux; in the eleventh, friends and hopes inconstant; in the twelfth, movement even in the house of confinement: the prisoner's house touched by the figure of release and departure.

    The through-line, in one sentence: **Via does not change its nature in any house; it delivers the same testimony (motion, change, passage) into twelve different departments, and the house decides whether motion is good news.** Where the matter asked about is itself a motion (a journey, a message, a removal, an escape) Via favours it: the lookup shows Cattan excepting voyages by name even in houses where he calls the figure ill for everything else. Where the matter is a *holding* (goods, marriage, partnership, office, anything the querent wants to keep fixed) the same motion is dissolution, and Via testifies against it. This is the figure's Lesson 3 character (the Moon, mobile, the only figure of four single points, pure change) refracted through the map of Lesson 5, and it is the cleanest possible demonstration of how figure and house combine: the figure supplies the verb, the house supplies the subject, and the judgment, when you are equipped to make one, must read both. It also explains, in advance, why Chart A is delicate: its marriage question puts the figure of passage in the house of the bond, and what that means will depend on machinery this lesson does not own.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

## You are ready to continue when…

- You can assign any plain question to its house at once, and any tricky one with a defence you could cite chapter for.
- You can recite the significations of all twelve houses (Cattan's core, at minimum) and name the houses where the pseudo-Agrippa's list visibly diverges.
- You can classify every house as angular, succedent or cadent from memory and state the doctrine of strength by place, with Gerard's first-house procedure as your citation.
- You can say why the querent is always the first house, in the tradition's own terms, and cite two sources for the rule.
- Step 0 is a reflex: you inspect the first house of any chart before doing anything else with it, you can state the course's practice for Rubeus or Cauda Draconis rising, and you can name the rule's lineage (Cattan's line and the Golden Dawn's hardening) with Heydon's dissent on record.
- You can place all three course charts from memory or near it: each chart's first and seventh houses, each standing question's significators, and each chart's Step 0 result.
- Your journal is fully upgraded: every chart placed into houses, significators named and defended, placements observed, and not a verdict anywhere in it.
