Lesson 2 gave you the figures as shapes; this lesson gives them meanings: for each figure, its planet, sign, element, character, the people it signifies, and its rank in the order of fortune. Each figure is a character to know, not a one-word label, predictable in temperament and surprising only in circumstance. This is the largest block of memory-work in the course, and it runs one planetary pair at a time.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 1 and 2: the five sources and their standing, and the anatomy of the figures cold (the four lines head to feet, active and passive points, entering and going-out, mobile and stable). All of that structure now starts to pay off.

You will also need the reading. Cattan's chapter of qualities (Book III, chapter 27, assigned above) is the closest thing the Renaissance tradition has to a master-table of the figures' good and ill, and the ranking section of this lesson leans on it throughout. It is nearly unintelligible on a first reading and nearly obvious on a second.

The figure pages of this site ([Via](/figures/via/), [Populus](/figures/populus/) and the rest) collect every quotation in the corpus for each figure, and you will live in them for years. The pages give you everything; this lesson gives you the *order* in which everything makes sense.

## Where the characters come from

A geomantic figure gets its character from three places.

First, and above all, from its **planet**. The tradition assigned the sixteen figures to the seven planets of ancient astronomy (the Sun, the Moon, Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter and Saturn) and to the two nodes of the Moon, the Dragon's Head and Tail. Each planet receives exactly two figures, and the two divide their patron's nature between them: one takes the planet at its best, the other at its worst, or one its bright face and the other its dark. This is the most important organizing fact about the figures, and it is why this lesson is arranged in planetary pairs rather than in any of the orders the old books use. The pairs are half the system: each figure's partner tells you, by contrast, what the figure itself is.

Second, from its **element and sign**. Each figure belongs to one of the four elements and to a sign of the zodiac, and these refine the planetary character: they tell you whether the figure's nature runs hot or cold, quick or slow, and they matter technically later, when figures are compared by element and dignity. The pseudo-Agrippa derives the signs directly from the planetary rulerships, assigning each figure to a house of its own planet:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/signs" />

This is the system this site takes as its default, and the one printed at the top of every figure page: the two Fortunes in Leo, Via and Populus in Cancer, Acquisitio in Pisces and Laetitia in Sagittarius, Puella in Taurus and Amissio in Libra, Conjunctio in Virgo and Albus in Gemini, Puer and Rubeus in Scorpio, Carcer in Capricorn and Tristitia in Aquarius, the Dragon's Head with Capricorn and the Tail with Scorpio. (Note the printer's slip in the passage: "Puella and Rubeus have for their sign Scorpio" should read *Puer* and Rubeus, as the editorial note on the quotation records; Puella was assigned to Taurus a line earlier.) The elements in the course's default system follow Agrippa's "natures of the planets and figures themselves," which you will meet below in the section on variants. **The sources do not agree on the elements and signs**, and a later section takes this up at length. Cattan's assignments differ from Agrippa's, Gerard's differ from both, and the Golden Dawn rearranged everything again. This course states one system as its default (the one in the figure pages' headline attributions) and treats the others as recorded variants, because that is what they are.

Third, from its **structure**. Lesson 2's anatomy now bears fruit. A figure with more points above than below is entering and inclines to good; more below than above, going out and inclining to ill; the elemental line where a figure carries its single, active points tells you where its energy lives. Laetitia is a single fire point over three passive lines (all its force flung upward, joy rising like flame), and its counterpart Tristitia is the same figure overturned, a single earth point dragging everything down. Acquisitio's points hang like full purses; reverse it and you get Amissio, the open bags of loss. The tradition read these shapes physiognomically, and the form is a reliable guide to the meaning.

Each figure also has a **mobility**: eight figures are stable, inclined to endure and to fix matters where they stand, and eight are mobile, inclined to motion, change and quick passage. The stable figures in this course's system are Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Puella, Albus, Tristitia, Carcer and Caput Draconis; the mobile are Via, Fortuna Minor, Puer, Rubeus, Amissio, Laetitia, Conjunctio and Cauda Draconis. Mobility matters most in questions of time and travel, and you will use it constantly from Lesson 5 onward.

Now the sixteen. For each pair: the planet's nature, the division of its significations between the two figures, and a portrait of each, the sources speaking first and commentary after. The portraits here are the skeleton; each figure's reference page holds the full flesh of quotation.

## The Sun's pair: Fortuna Major and Fortuna Minor

The Sun in traditional astrology is the planet of life, honour, victory, kings and gold, the most fortunate of lights when well placed, and arrogant, scorching and exposed when ill. Its two figures divide the solar nature not into good and bad (both Fortunes are fortunate) but into two *kinds* of good fortune, and the pseudo-Agrippa states the division with his usual compression:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/fortuna" />

The day side and the night side: the Sun in its full dignity, and the Sun labouring at less strength. Everything else about these two figures unfolds from that single distinction.

### Fortuna Major

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 1, 1]} name="Fortuna Major" size={4} />

[Fortuna Major](/figures/fortuna-major/), the Greater Fortune (Sun, Leo, fire, stable), is by common consent the best figure in geomancy. Its shape tells its story: two passive lines above, two active below, a figure whose strength is in its foundations. This is not luck that arrives; it is power that was already there. The tradition consistently reads it as success that comes slowly, with effort at the start and unshakable security at the end: victory in any matter where the querent has real strength to draw on. Crowley's index entry catches the older keywords exactly:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-major-meaning" />

"Safeguard entering": protection that is on its way in and will stay. Heydon's first-house delineation shows what the figure makes of a person:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/fortuna-major" />

This goodness has a particular texture: regal and a little overbearing ("a high mighty man given to command, coveting to bring men into subjection to him"), and it has exactly one listed weakness: secrecy. A figure of the Sun cannot hide; everything it touches comes to light. Where the question is keeping a thing concealed, the best figure in the art is "a token of small surety." The human type is the magnate, the commander, the person whose position does not depend on anyone's permission. Fortuna Major returns at full length in the worked example; the formula is slow to start, impossible to stop.

### Fortuna Minor

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 2, 2]} name="Fortuna Minor" size={4} />

[Fortuna Minor](/figures/fortuna-minor/), the Lesser Fortune (Sun, Leo, air, mobile), is the same Sun in its nocturnal weakness: success still, but borrowed, hasty and fading. Its shape is Fortuna Major overturned: strength worn on the outside, two active lines at the head and nothing beneath them. The tradition reads it as outward strength: help that comes from others, from patronage, from a burst of speed, rather than from one's own foundation. Cattan's first-house portrait is all velocity and heat:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/fortuna-minor" />

Choler, hastiness, swiftness; force and boldness of heart; victory, and then the characteristic solar leak: "the matter shall be disclosed." Like its greater sibling, Fortuna Minor cannot keep a secret, and unlike it, it cannot hold a position. Crowley's keywords give the mirror of the Greater Fortune's:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-minor-meaning" />

"Safeguard going out" against "safeguard entering": protection on the way out the door. The practical rule the sources converge on: Fortuna Minor is excellent wherever speed is the point (a quick sale, a swift journey, a war that must be won now) and unreliable wherever endurance is. Note that Cattan's chapter of qualities even lists it among the figures "signifying evill" and among the figures of loss: not because it is malefic, but because what it gives, it gives briefly. The human type is the brilliant lieutenant, the meteor, the person carried by momentum and favour. Of any success it promises, the real question is whether it will still be here in a year, and Fortuna Minor does not answer that.

## The Moon's pair: Via and Populus

The Moon is the fastest of the planets and the emptiest: she shines by reflection, gathers and scatters the tides, and governs travel, crowds, change and all things that wax and wane. Her two figures are the two extreme shapes of the whole system (all single points, and all double) and Agrippa derives them from her phases:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/via-populus" />

The new Moon increasing and moving; the full Moon, a face crowded with light. One figure is pure motion, the other pure multitude, and neither has much character of its own, which is precisely their character.

### Via

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 1, 1]} name="Via" size={4} />

[Via](/figures/via/), the Way (Moon, Cancer, water, mobile), is one active point on every line: the whole figure walking. It is the figure of the road, the journey, change itself, and the tradition gives it a peculiar double reputation. In its own province (travel, movement, letters, going from place to place) it is excellent. Everywhere else it is a solvent: it changes whatever it touches, and most questions are asked by people who want things to stay as they are. Cattan states both halves in one breath:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/via" />

A figure which "breaketh and spoileth al the goodnes of the others," strong language, and Crowley transmits the same doctrine in nearly the same words three centuries later:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/via-general" />

The interpretive key is that Via is not evil; it is *indifferent*, and indifference to the existing state of things is bad news for the contented and good news for the desperate. Ask whether the sickness will change: Via says yes. Ask whether the marriage will hold: Via says things move. Cattan's qualities chapter accordingly lists it among figures good "to have libertie, and to come out of prison" and good for amendment of sickness, every case where the querent's hope *is* the change. The human type is the traveller, the restless one, the person already halfway out the door. When Via appears, ask first: in this question, is change the cure or the disease?

### Populus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 2, 2]} name="Populus" size={4} />

[Populus](/figures/populus/), the People (Moon, Cancer, water, stable), is the opposite extremity: every line doubled, eight points, the fullest and most passive figure in the art. It is the crowd: an assembly of people gathered "for one thing or other," and like a crowd it has no will of its own. The doctrine every source repeats is that Populus takes its goodness or badness entirely from its company: good with good figures, evil with evil, a mirror held up to the rest of the chart. Heydon's first-house portrait gives the figure at its most vivid:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/populus" />

A figure of renown, assembly, water-journeys, and rain, and mud; the Moon governs the weather of the question as well as its crowds. Cattan's quality-chapter summary is the rule you will actually use:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/populus" />

"Sometime good and sometime bad" is not a confession of ignorance; it is the figure's meaning. Populus signifies the many, the public, the gathered (congregations, markets, armies, audiences) and the moral colour of a crowd is whatever the figures around it supply. In practice you will read Populus as an amplifier: it doubles the testimony of its neighbours and adds the flavour of multitude, publicity and water. The human type is the public itself, or the person who goes with it. Of the two lunar figures: Via is the one who leaves the crowd; Populus is the crowd.

## The pair of Mars: Puer and Rubeus

Mars is the lesser malefic: heat, iron, war, courage, lust and blood. No tradition pretends Mars is comfortable, but the older astrology distinguished sharply between Mars *used* (the soldier, the surgeon, the defender) and Mars *loose* (the arsonist). The two geomantic figures of Mars are exactly that distinction, and Agrippa draws the line in eight words:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/puer-rubeus" />

Mars benevolent and Mars malevolent. One of these figures is difficult; the other is the most feared figure in the art.

### Puer

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 2, 1]} name="Puer" size={4} />

[Puer](/figures/puer/), the Boy (Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile), is Mars benevolent, which is to say Mars at its least harmful, not Mars made gentle. The figure is all single points but for the passive water line: a fighter with everything active except his depths. The old keyword is "yellow, beardless," a young man, armed, rash, and not yet wise. Heydon's first-house delineation does not flatter him:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/puer" />

Clamorous, seditious, deceitful; soldiers and officers "that care neither for God nor man." And yet read on: good in love, good in war, "a meetly swiftness" for voyages, "good enough" for marriage. Crowley compresses the whole doctrine into a sentence:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/puer-general" />

Evil in most demands, *except those relating to war and love*, the two arenas where rashness, heat and forwardness are qualifications rather than defects. That exception is the figure. Puer ruins questions that need patience, prudence or peace, and carries questions that need daring. He brings combat into everything: a Puer marriage will be stormy and passionate, a Puer lawsuit aggressive, a Puer illness feverish and fast. The human type is the young soldier, the duellist, the lover who climbs the wall. Cattan's Book II portrait adds feasting and banqueting with ladies, theft, and "gaine in play." Judge him as you would such a man: superb in a charge, a liability in a negotiation.

### Rubeus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 2, 2]} name="Rubeus" size={4} />

[Rubeus](/figures/rubeus/), the Red (Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile), is Mars malevolent: passion without an object, violence without a cause, the colour of fire and spilled blood. Structurally it is the inversion of Albus, the white figure of peace: turn the quiet figure upside down, and the single active point moves from the still water line to the air line, one hot point burning in an otherwise smothered figure, the cup of wisdom overturned and spilling. The tradition's judgment is unanimous and severe. Cattan's chapter of qualities:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/rubeus" />

"Ill in all good things, and good in all ill things, and many times signifyeth death." And note the clause we will return to: *she is never found in the first house to make a judgement.* Crowley's general meaning transmits the same formula:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/rubeus-general" />

That formula (evil in good, good in evil) is subtler than simple malefice. Ask "will my business prosper?" and Rubeus is a disaster. Ask "is my partner cheating me?" and Rubeus says *yes, and drunkenly*; it is "good" for the question in the sense that it confirms and describes the vice asked about. Rubeus signifies the passions in their vicious form: drunkenness, debauchery, fraud, bloodshed; Heydon's first-house entry runs simply "Rogues, Whores, Theeves, Robbers, Murderers." It is the one figure that some authorities refuse to judge at all when it rises in the first house, a rule with its own section below, and the full multi-source portrait is the first half of this lesson's worked example. In short: Rubeus is the worst figure in the art for any honest question, and an honest answer about dishonest things.

## The pair of Venus: Puella and Amissio

Venus is the lesser benefic: love, beauty, pleasure, music, gardens, the bonds between people. Her gifts are real and her weaknesses are her gifts carried too far: indulgence, vanity, expense. Her two figures split her into the beloved and the price paid:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/puella-amissio" />

The first fortunate; the other "as it were retrograde, or combust": Venus burnt up in the Sun's beams, love turned to loss.

### Puella

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 1, 1]} name="Puella" size={4} />

[Puella](/figures/puella/), the Girl (Venus, Taurus, air, stable), is Venus whole and happy. The figure's shape was read as a woman's body, the single passive line at the neck like a mirror held up; its nature is harmony, pleasure, and peace. Heydon's portrait is one of the most charming passages in the corpus:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/puella" />

To sing and dance, to be well clothed and neat, a lover of gardens, and, the steel under the silk, "good in case of Love, and in all things but in War." Cattan's quality-chapter summary makes the rule general:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/puella" />

Very good in *all* demands, especially those of women. Puella is, after the Greater Fortune and the figures of Jupiter, among the most reliably fortunate figures in the system, and her one consistent failure is conflict: a figure of concord cannot fight. Her goodness also has a Venusian texture you should not sand off: it is pleasure-loving, a little vain, "voluptuous and given to vain pleasures" in Heydon's words, and in questions of long labour and discipline she promises more delight than endurance. The human type: the beloved, the artist, the peacemaker, the well-dressed friend everyone is glad to see. In a chart she softens what she touches; whether the question needs softening is the thing to weigh.

### Amissio

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 1, 2]} name="Amissio" size={4} />

[Amissio](/figures/amissio/), Loss (Venus, Libra, fire, mobile), is Venus combust: desire that costs. The figure is Acquisitio overturned, the moneybags emptied out, and its plain meaning is exactly what its name says: loss, expense, things slipping out of one's hands. Cattan's first-house delineation is grim and thorough:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/amissio" />

Loss of inheritance and possessions, ill beginning and ill issue ("ill in all demaundes that can be propounded"), *except for prisoners*, for whom escape is the loss the prison suffers. Crowley preserves the same structure of exceptions and adds the one that makes the figure Venusian:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/amissio-general" />

"Sometimes for love": because love, in the old moral physics, is a loss willingly suffered: you give yourself away. Amissio is the figure of every transaction where something leaves: terrible for gain, fitting for paying debts, giving gifts, spending on a wedding; good for losing what you want to lose: a sickness, a captivity, a bad habit. The double aptness is the lesson here: no figure is simply "bad" until you know what loss would mean in the question. The human type is the spendthrift, the lover who gives too much, the seller rather than the buyer. Where Puella is love enjoyed, Amissio is love paid for.

## The pair of Mercury: Conjunctio and Albus

Mercury is the planet of mind: speech, writing, commerce, calculation, science, and the connecting of one thing to another. It is traditionally neither benefic nor malefic but convertible (coloured by its company) and both its figures inherit that conditional quality. Both are good, says Agrippa, but not equally:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/conjunctio-albus" />

The first the more fortunate: Mercury active and Mercury contemplative, the broker and the scholar.

### Conjunctio

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 1, 2]} name="Conjunctio" size={4} />

[Conjunctio](/figures/conjunctio/), Conjunction (Mercury, Virgo, air, mobile), is Mercury in motion: meeting, assembly, the joining of separate things. The shape draws its own meaning: points converging at the waist, two lines reaching toward each other. Cattan gives the figure's two great significations in one sentence:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/conjunctio" />

Good with good and ill with ill, like Populus; but where Populus merely reflects, Conjunctio *connects*: "a recoverment and restitution of thinges scattered or lost." It is the classic figure for recovering stolen goods, reuniting separated parties, making contracts and marriages (Cattan's qualities chapter calls it the best of all figures for marriage). Heydon's first-house portrait shows the mental side:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/conjunctio" />

Graceful speech, good memory, mathematics and merchandising, many languages: Mercury's gifts entire. But note how much of Heydon's delineation is conditional: good aspects of one figure give knowledge, bad aspects of another give knavery and theft. Conjunctio has no moral substance of its own; it is a copula, a hyphen between the figures around it. The practical rule: Conjunctio promises that things will *come together*, and you must look at the rest of the chart to learn whether what comes together is the lost ring and its owner or the conspirators and their plot. The human type is the broker, the matchmaker, the interpreter, the scholar of many tongues.

### Albus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 1, 2]} name="Albus" size={4} />

[Albus](/figures/albus/), the White (Mercury, Gemini, earth, stable), is Mercury at rest: the contemplative mind, wisdom, peace, clear thought. Its single active point sits on the water line, a still spring under a quiet surface; the tradition read it as the figure of the philosopher and named it for the colour of purity and of old men's hair. Heydon, who loved this figure, gives it his most affectionate portrait:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/albus" />

A noble person who "scornes base actions," honest to the point of being hated for it, a scholar, a philosopher, and good for profit, law-suits, messages and marriage, "but it is naught for War, and good for Peace." Cattan's quality-chapter line gives the practical core:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/albus" />

Good for gain hoped for, and good "to have entrie into any place": beginnings, admissions, enterings-in. Albus is a quietly fortunate figure, the gentlest in the system after Puella, but its goodness is the goodness of stillness: it favours study, agreements, healing and peace, and it fails wherever force is wanted. A general with Albus for his significator should sue for terms. Set it always against its inversion Rubeus: white against red, the cool spring against the burning cup, wisdom against passion. The human type: the scholar, the counsellor, the honest clerk, the white-haired adviser whose advice is better than his sword-arm.

## The pair of Jupiter: Acquisitio and Laetitia

Jupiter is the greater benefic: abundance, justice, faith, expansion, and the kind of fortune that arrives as increase rather than victory. The Sun's good fortune is glory; Jupiter's is *more*: more money, more health, more friends, more life. His two figures divide the increase between the purse and the heart:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/acquisitio-laetitia" />

Jupiter the greater fortune in the first, the lesser in the second, "but without detriment," a gentle ranking inside an entirely benefic pair.

### Acquisitio

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 2, 1]} name="Acquisitio" size={4} />

[Acquisitio](/figures/acquisitio/), Gain (Jupiter, Pisces, water, stable), is the figure of obtaining: profit, acquisition, success in any matter where something is to be gotten. Generations of geomancers saw its shape as two tied money-bags, full and hanging. Cattan's first-house delineation is the figure's biography:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/acquisitio" />

Goodness, happiness, quiet life, "the accomplishment of the persons owne harts desire"; voyages that depart late but return glad; good for love and marriage and "merveilous good" in church causes, a figure firm, entering and good for all things *but war*, where a figure of increase has nothing to offer. Crowley's summary keeps the essential:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/acquisitio-general" />

Generally good for profit or gain, and "generally" is fair, for the qualities chapter records a few shadows: Acquisitio appears among the figures ill for coming out of prison (what it grasps, it keeps, even a prisoner) and, ominously, among figures of death in sickness questions, where the thing gained is the disease. These exceptions teach the method: the figure's core motion is *toward the querent, accumulating*, and what accumulation means depends on the question. Mostly it means money, and Acquisitio is the first figure to hope for in any question of property, business or increase. The human type: the merchant, the fortunate buyer, the person whose estate grows in their sleep. Acquisitio is also this course's standing example of disputed attributions, as you will see in the variants section below.

### Laetitia

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 2, 2]} name="Laetitia" size={4} />

[Laetitia](/figures/laetitia/), Joy (Jupiter, Sagittarius, air, mobile), is Jupiter's increase felt rather than counted: gladness, health, good fortune of the spirit. Its shape is the best mnemonic in the system: one active fire point at the head over three passive lines, a tower, a fountain, a flame going straight up; the figure literally rises. Heydon's portrait is all health and sunlight:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/laetitia" />

Long and prosperous life, a strong and healthful body, peace, amity, honesty, good in everything except war, with one Jupiterian flaw in matters of love: the person "will promise much, and perform but little." Joy overpromises. Cattan's quality-chapter line broadens it:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/laetitia" />

Good for joy "as well present as to come," good almost in all houses. Laetitia is the figure of every question whose real subject is happiness: health, children, feasts, deliverances, good news. Being mobile and airy, its fortune is quicker and lighter than Acquisitio's (it lifts rather than fills) and the old books note it is better for the spirit than the purse; Cattan's tables even list it among figures *ill* for recovering a thing stolen, for joy does not chase. The human type: the bringer of good news, the healthy and the merry, the friend whose arrival improves the room. Set Laetitia always against its overturning, Tristitia: the fountain and the stake, the same figure pointed at heaven and at the grave.

## The pair of Saturn: Carcer and Tristitia

Saturn is the greater malefic: cold, dry, slow, old; the planet of limits, walls, burdens, time and grief. But Saturn's evils are the evils of *structure*, and structure has its uses: what imprisons also defends, what weighs down also holds fast. Both his figures are evil, says Agrippa, but not equally:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/carcer-tristitia" />

The first (Carcer) of the greater detriment. The prison is worse than the sorrow, because sorrow can move.

### Carcer

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 2, 1]} name="Carcer" size={4} />

[Carcer](/figures/carcer/), the Prison (Saturn, Capricorn, earth, stable), is enclosure itself: the figure's points form a closed ring, active lines at head and feet sealing two passive lines inside. It signifies restriction, delay, binding, imprisonment in every literal and figurative sense. Cattan's first-house delineation is a study in Saturnine misery:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/carcer" />

Melancholy, heaviness, envy, anger, "great paine and travaile," ill in all things *except fortification*: "rampiers, forts, and other fortifications of Townes, and to defende them well." The exception is the figure's soul. A wall is evil to the one outside it and salvation to the one behind it. Crowley's one-line summary:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/carcer-general" />

Delay, binding, stay, bar, restriction: in a question of progress, the worst news short of Rubeus; in a question of keeping (will my tenant stay? will the secret hold? will the fortress stand?), a grim kind of yes. Carcer answers every question with *nothing moves*, and your work as a judge is to decide who profits from immobility. It is also, fittingly, the figure that breaks the entering-and-exiting rule of structure: with equal points above and below it should be mean, but Cattan excepts it by name: the prison is not neutral. The human type: the jailer and the jailed, the miser, the solitary, the keeper of walls. Slow, hard, lasting: everything it gives, it gives late and keeps long.

### Tristitia

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 2, 1]} name="Tristitia" size={4} />

[Tristitia](/figures/tristitia/), Sorrow (Saturn, Aquarius, earth, stable), is grief: the single active point on the earth line, everything above it passive and pressing down. The traditional images are a stake driven into the ground and a tower fallen; it is Laetitia overturned, joy inverted into mourning. Heydon's portrait does not spare the reader:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/tristitia" />

Troubles, melancholy perturbations of mind and body, an ill beginning, and then, characteristically, the Saturnine exceptions: good "to search treasure in the Earth," good "in matters of Fortifications and buildings." Sorrow digs; what goes down can also found. Crowley's verdict is the shortest in his handbook:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/tristitia-general" />

Evil in almost all things, but the "almost" earns its place. In questions of building, of land, of mines and buried things, of anything that must be sunk deep and endure, Tristitia's downward fixity becomes a virtue; Cattan's chapter of qualities even lists it, to every student's surprise, among the good figures for the year and the figures of gain: sorrow's harvest, slow crops from heavy soil. Treat those listings as the figure's earthy exception, not its character; its character is grief, condemnation, and descent. The human type: the mourner, the penitent, the digger of foundations and graves. When Tristitia appears, something is going down; the judgment turns on whether the question wanted roots or feared the fall.

## The Dragon's Head and Tail

The last pair belongs to no planet. Caput Draconis and Cauda Draconis are assigned to the nodes of the Moon, the two points where the Moon's path crosses the Sun's, where eclipses happen: the Dragon's Head in the north, where the Moon climbs, and the Dragon's Tail in the south, where she descends. In astrology the Head increases and the Tail diminishes; the geomantic figures inherit exactly that. Agrippa's note on the pair is a single, almost dismissive line:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/dragons-head-tail" />

They "follow their own natures": the Head swallowing in, the Tail casting out. Both are threshold figures, and both take their moral colour from their company more than any figures except Populus and Conjunctio: good with good and evil with evil, an entrance and an exit.

### Caput Draconis

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 1, 1]} name="Caput Draconis" size={4} />

[Caput Draconis](/figures/caput-draconis/), the Dragon's Head (the North Node, Capricorn, earth, stable), is the upper threshold: the doorway in. Its shape is all activity below a closed head, a figure stepping over a sill. It signifies beginnings, entrances, and the increase of whatever it joins. Heydon's first-house delineation is generous:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/caput-draconis" />

Honours, riches, favours of great persons, "good for all things that ye can demand," except war, for a threshold is where you welcome, not where you fight; and good for marriage, "but it will be long in doing," since thresholds are crossed slowly. Crowley preserves the conditional rule that the older sources attach to both nodes:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/caput-draconis-general" />

Good with good, evil with evil, but with "a good issue for gain," because the Head's own bias is toward increase. In practice Caput Draconis is read as a fortunate figure slightly dependent on its neighbours: among benefics it is nearly as good as the Fortunes; among malefics it holds the door open for them. It favours all beginnings (new ventures, foundations, plantings, contracts entered) and Cattan adds building houses and labouring the earth. The human type: the founder, the heir, the one arriving into fortune. The rule of thumb the tradition hands you: the Head blesses what is starting.

### Cauda Draconis

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 1, 2]} name="Cauda Draconis" size={4} />

[Cauda Draconis](/figures/cauda-draconis/), the Dragon's Tail (the South Node, Scorpio, water, mobile), is the lower threshold: the way out. The shape is the Head overturned, a figure stepping down and away. Everything the Head gives entering, the Tail gives leaving: endings, departures, expulsions, the diminishing side of the dragon. Cattan's quality-chapter summary holds the whole doctrine:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/cauda-draconis" />

"Very good with the ill, and very ill with the good" (note the chiasmus against the Head), good "in matter of losse... and to passe out of an affaire," and otherwise a robber on every road. Crowley transmits it intact:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/cauda-draconis-general" />

Good for loss and for passing out of an affair: if your question is how to end something (leave a post, dissolve a partnership, escape a danger) the Tail is your figure. For everything you wish to keep or begin, it is among the worst figures in the art, second only to Rubeus, with whom it shares the dubious honour of the first-house rule discussed below. Where Rubeus corrupts, Cauda *terminates*: its evil is rupture, ruin, the burning of what was built; Heydon's first-house entry allows it nothing but the ruin and burning of countries by wars and treasons. The human type: the exile, the deserter, the one already leaving, the solitary man "loving woods, and abstaining from company" of Cattan's nativity chapter. When the Tail appears, something is over. Judge whether the querent wanted it over.

That is the alphabet entire. The rest of this lesson, and the rest of the course, assumes these sixteen portraits are becoming reflexes.

## When the sources disagree

You have now met, in passing, several moments where a quotation's attributions did not match this site's headline attributions: Cattan calling Acquisitio a figure of the air in Aries while the figure page says water and Pisces, or placing Amissio in the sign of Scorpio where the default says Libra. These mismatches are not errors in the site or the tradition. **The sources genuinely disagree about the elements and signs of the figures, and the disagreement is part of the tradition you are inheriting.**

The planetary attributions are nearly unanimous: every source in this corpus gives the two Fortunes to the Sun, Via and Populus to the Moon, and so on through the seven pairs, with only the nodes attracting dissent (Cattan, in his first-house chapter, makes the Head a figure "of ♃ and ♀ according to the opinion of some men: but by mine opinion of ♃ and ☿," Jupiter and Mercury, and gives the Tail to Mars and Saturn, where later usage simply says North and South Node). Hear him weighing the opinions himself:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/caput-draconis" />

That sentence (*some men say, but by mine opinion*) catches the epistemology of the art: the Renaissance authors were not transmitting a fixed revelation but comparing systems and choosing.

The elements and signs are where the systems multiply. The pseudo-Agrippa himself reports more than one. He gives a "vulgar order" of the triplicities, and then immediately offers a better one of his own:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/triplicities" />

This second arrangement (fiery Fortuna Major, Rubeus, Puer and Amissio; airy Fortuna Minor, Puella, Laetitia and Conjunctio; watery Acquisitio, Cauda Draconis, Via and Populus; earthy Carcer, Tristitia, Albus and Caput Draconis) is the elemental system this site takes as its default, because it is derived, as Agrippa says, "according to the natures of the planets, and figures themselves." And for the signs, alongside the planetary-house system you met earlier, Agrippa also records a wholly different distribution that gives each sign of the zodiac roughly one figure:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/zodiacal-distribution" />

Acquisitio to Aries, Laetitia to Gemini, Albus to Leo, Via to Virgo, almost nothing in common with the default system. Gerard of Cremona's astronomical geomancy uses yet another set, the signs placed in the ascendant for each figure; Cattan's Book II attributions differ again; and the Golden Dawn, whose system Crowley transmits, rebuilt the signs from the planetary rulerships with different choices about which planet's two houses go to which figure.

Take **Acquisitio** as the concrete case: it is the most disputed figure on this site, and Exercise 6 will quiz you on it. The course default says Jupiter, Pisces, water. The variants recorded on [its figure page](/figures/acquisitio/): Cattan makes it air, in Aries; the "vulgar order" reported by Agrippa also makes it air; Agrippa's alternative zodiacal distribution and Gerard both give it Aries; and the Golden Dawn gives it Sagittarius and fire. One figure, three elements, three signs, and every authority is "right" within his own system. **Albus** runs the same way: earth in Gemini by the course default, water in Cancer for Cattan and the vulgar triplicities, Leo in Agrippa's alternative distribution, Cancer for Gerard, air in Gemini for the Golden Dawn. Even **Fortuna Major**, the most stable figure in meaning, is earth and Aquarius for Cattan, Aquarius again in Gerard's ascendants, Taurus in Agrippa's alternative distribution, and fire in Leo for the default and the Golden Dawn.

What should a student do with this? Three things. First, the reason it happens: elements and signs are derived layers, reasoned out from the planets and the shapes by different methods, and different methods give different results; the figures' meanings, which all sources broadly share, came first. Second, the disagreement almost never touches the judgments: Cattan's Acquisitio in Aries and Crowley's in Sagittarius both mean *gain*. Third, course policy: **pick one system and be absolutely consistent with it.** This course, and this site's figure pages, use one default: the planetary attributions and the element-and-sign system stated at the top of each figure page. Every technique taught later that uses elements or signs assumes that system. The variants are recorded on each figure's page, with their sources, because flattening them would falsify the tradition; but a working geomancer who mixes systems mid-chart is not honouring the breadth of the tradition, merely contradicting himself. The disagreement is part of the inheritance. Incoherence is optional.

## The order of fortune

The portraits gave each figure its character; the working judge also needs them in *rank*, because half of geomantic judgment is the simple question: is this figure good or ill for this matter? Cattan's chapter of qualities (your assigned reading) opens with a structural rule you already possess from Lesson 2, now made moral:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

Entering figures good, exiting figures evil, mean figures mean, with three exceptions Cattan names: Tristitia (entering but evil), Laetitia (exiting but good), Carcer (mean in shape, but not in nature). Read alongside the chapter's long tables of figures good for love, for war, for voyages, for marriage, and alongside the figure-by-figure verdicts you have been quoting all lesson, a stable hierarchy emerges, the course's working ranking, from best to worst:

1. **Fortuna Major**: good in all things; the best figure in the art.
2. **Acquisitio**: Jupiter the greater fortune; good for nearly everything that can be gained.
3. **Caput Draconis**: good in almost all demands, conditional on company.
4. **Laetitia**: good almost in all houses; Jupiter "without detriment."
5. **Puella**: very good in all demands, weakest in conflict.
6. **Albus**: gentle, wise, gainful; useless in war.
7. **Conjunctio**: good with good, ill with ill; the best conditional figure.
8. **Populus**: pure mirror; takes everything from its company.
9. **Via**: breaks the goodness of others; excellent in its own province of movement.
10. **Fortuna Minor**: real but fading good; appears in Cattan's evil lists for its instability.
11. **Puer**: evil save in war and love.
12. **Amissio**: evil save where loss is the goal.
13. **Tristitia**: evil save in the deep and the buried.
14. **Carcer**: evil, "of the greater detrement" of Saturn's two; binds everything.
15. **Cauda Draconis**: ruin and rupture; good only for endings.
16. **Rubeus**: the worst: vice, violence, and the figure that stops the chart.

The ranking is firm at the ends and loose in the middle. The extremes are unanimous across the corpus: nobody disputes that Fortuna Major is best, and nobody disputes that Rubeus and Cauda Draconis are the figures of dread. The middle is honest interpretation, and the sources would quarrel with details: Agrippa's "without detriment" arguably lifts Laetitia above Caput Draconis; Cattan's tables put Tristitia among the good figures for the year and for gain, which would flatter it past Amissio in questions of property; Fortuna Minor outranks half the list in any question where speed is the whole matter. That is not a defect of the ranking but the lesson of it: the order of fortune is a default, and the question always has the last word. A drill at the end of this lesson will make you reconstruct the ranking and its caveats from memory.

## The figure that stops the chart

One doctrine remains, and it concerns the bottom of the ranking. Crowley's handbook, transmitting the practice of the Golden Dawn, gives an instruction found nowhere else in this corpus in so many words:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/destroy-rule" />

Destroy it instantly; wait two hours; cast again. Taken as written, this is a Golden Dawn rule, and you should learn it as one: the mechanical formula (both figures, instant destruction, the two-hour quarantine) appears in no Renaissance source on this site. But the kernel of it is far older, and you have already met it twice. Cattan reports that the "Egyptians and a great part of the Latines" held that a chart with Rubeus in the first house "should not be judged. But that figure must be broken and make an other," and he sides with them, on experience; his chapter of qualities states flatly that Rubeus "is never found in the first house to make a judgement." For Cauda Draconis he reports the same opinion with less conviction. And Heydon, a century later, knows the custom and rejects it:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/cauda-draconis" />

Some old writers, he reports, would not have this figure or Rubeus judged in the first house: "but I am of the opinion that the Judgment herein should not be delayd." Judge it, says Heydon; it simply means the matter is very bad. So the lineages divide cleanly. One line (Cattan's Egyptians and Latins, Cattan himself, and the Golden Dawn after him) treats Rubeus rising (and, for the Golden Dawn, Cauda too) as a corrupted casting: the dragon got into the chart, burn it. The other (Cattan's Indians, Chaldeans and Hebrews, and Heydon) treats it as a terrible answer, which is still an answer. This course follows the first line as practice (when you begin casting in Lesson 4, a chart with Rubeus or Cauda Draconis in the first house goes in the journal, marked unjudged, and the question rests for a time), while expecting you to know, as a matter of learning, that the rule is lineage-specific and that Heydon's dissent is on the record. A geomancer should know which of his habits are the tradition and which are *a* tradition.

## Worked example

The exercises will quiz you on portraits like these: readings that gather what the sources say about one figure, weigh them, and end in a judgment of the figure's character in plain words. Here is the method performed twice, on the worst figure and the best.

### Rubeus, read across four sources

Begin always with the structural facts, because they anchor everything: Rubeus, "the Red," points 2-1-2-2, the single active point on the air line; Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile in the course system; counterpart and inversion of Albus. Then let the sources speak in order of authority, oldest doctrine first.

The pseudo-Agrippa, you recall from the Mars pair, needs only a clause: Puer has Mars benevolent, Rubeus malevolent. That is the genus. For the species, Cattan (and here we take his fullest statement, the first-house chapter rather than the quality tables already quoted in the portrait):

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/rubeus" />

This passage does three things at once. It catalogues the figure's significations: craft, fraud, treason, war, discord, "spilling and shedding of bloud... all the wickednesse in the world." It reveals that the tradition was already plural in antiquity: Indians, Chaldeans and Hebrews judging the figure, Egyptians and Latins breaking the chart. And it shows Cattan reasoning like a practitioner, not a copyist: he sides with the Egyptians "by experience." Heydon, normally an expansive writer, is driven to his tersest delineation in the whole first-house chapter:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/rubeus" />

"A deadly figure, and not fit to be judged," though remember that the same Heydon, under Cauda Draconis two pages later, argues against delaying judgment; even within one author the doctrine is under tension. Last, the modern comparison. Crowley's general rule, which you met in the portrait and which belongs in its place in the evidence:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/rubeus-general" />

and, in his chapter of method, the destroy-rule you have just studied: the Golden Dawn hardening Cattan's practice into a mechanism, and extending it to Cauda Draconis.

Now weigh. Where all sources agree, you stand on rock: Rubeus is the malevolent face of Mars; its matter is vice, violence and deceit; it is evil in every honest demand and "good" only where the question is itself about evil: there it confirms and describes. Where they diverge (judge or destroy) you note the divergence *as part of your knowledge of the figure*, choose the practice of your lineage, and never mistake your choice for the whole tradition. A portrait written this way ends with a synthesis sentence of your own, in plain words. For instance: Rubeus is passion with the lid off; it answers honest questions with disaster and dishonest questions with the truth, and one strong line of the tradition holds that when it rises in the first house, the proper judgment is to make none.

### Fortuna Major, read the same way

The same method on the opposite pole. Structure: 2-2-1-1, the active points below, strength in the foundation; Sun, Leo, fire, stable; counterpart Fortuna Minor. Agrippa's pair-doctrine gives the genus: the Sun diurnal, in his dignities, solar fortune at full strength. Cattan's first house gives the species:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/fortuna-major" />

Good will, good heart, loyalty, prosperity, the friendship of the great, good in all things, with the single exception of secrecy, the Sun being constitutionally unable to hide. Heydon's parallel passage, quoted in the portrait above, adds the human type: the "high mighty man given to command," heroic, magnificent, unbeatable; Heydon expands Cattan here as he usually does, and seeing the two side by side teaches you their literary relationship (Heydon's *Theomagia* absorbs whole stretches of Cattan, then decorates). Cattan's chapter of qualities adds the figure's characteristic *tempo*:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/fortuna-major" />

Good for gain "where a person hath hope to winne," where real strength already exists. And the tables of chapter 27 fill in the fine grain, including the surprises a portrait should mention: Fortuna Major appears not only among the figures of life, loyalty, peace and victory, but also among figures of *covetousness*, and for sickness it promises amendment "but the sickenesse will be long." Even its healing is slow. Crowley, finally, transmits the core unchanged:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-major-general" />

Note what the comparison yields: across three and a half centuries and two lineages, Fortuna Major's meaning is essentially undisputed; the variant attributions touched its element and sign (earth and Aquarius for Cattan, Aquarius for Gerard, fire and Leo here), but never its nature. A closing synthesis in your own words might run: Fortuna Major is strength that was already in the house before the question was asked; it gives victory to those with a position to win from, slowly, openly, and for keeps, and the only thing it cannot do is keep quiet.

That is the form of a portrait, and the form the quizzes below test: structure first, sources in order with citations, agreement weighed against divergence, synthesis last, in plain words.

If your answer used that distribution consistently instead, it is not wrong, but the course works in the default, and so should you from here on.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={6} title="The Acquisitio problem" kind="quiz">
  The record first, then the discipline.

  1. In the course default, what are Acquisitio's planet, sign and element?
  2. Which attribution do all the sources share?
  3. Name two variant systems and the sign or element each gives Acquisitio.
  4. The variants exist because:
     - a) the manuscripts are corrupt and the original attribution is lost
     - b) elements and signs are derived layers, reasoned from the planets and the shapes by different methods
     - c) the figure's meaning drifted between 1558 and 1909
  5. What never moves, across every system?
  6. Why does mixing systems mid-chart break the instrument?
  7. What does the course ask of a student who prefers Cattan's system, or the Golden Dawn's?

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. Jupiter, Pisces, water: Pisces from the planetary-house system the pseudo-Agrippa calls the "infallable comparisons of the figures," water from his triplicities constituted "according to the natures of the planets, and figures themselves" (Agrippa (attrib.), *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655)).
    2. Jupiter. The planet is universal; everything below it varies.
    3. Any two of: Cattan, air and Aries ("a figure of the Ayre and of ♃... in the signe of ♈," Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 1); the "vulgar order" of the triplicities, also airy; Agrippa's alternative zodiacal distribution, Aries; Gerard, Aries in the ascendant; the Golden Dawn, Sagittarius and fire.

    5. The meaning: Aries-air Acquisitio and Pisces-water Acquisitio both signify gain, in 1558 and in 1909.
    6. Because elements and signs do technical work (figures compared by triplicity, strengthened and weakened by sign), and those operations are consistent within one system and meaningless across two. <Exercise n={7} title="The order of fortune" kind="drill">
  From memory, rank the sixteen figures from most to least fortunate as this course teaches the ranking, and annotate at least three places where the sources would complicate the order. Then check against the model.
  <ModelAnswer>
    The course ranking: **1.** Fortuna Major, **2.** Acquisitio, **3.** Caput Draconis, **4.** Laetitia, **5.** Puella, **6.** Albus, **7.** Conjunctio, **8.** Populus, **9.** Via, **10.** Fortuna Minor, **11.** Puer, **12.** Amissio, **13.** Tristitia, **14.** Carcer, **15.** Cauda Draconis, **16.** Rubeus.

    The required caveats, any three of these (or comparable ones, honestly sourced), earn the marks:

    - **Laetitia vs. Caput Draconis.** Agrippa calls Laetitia Jupiter's lesser fortune "but without detriment," while Caput Draconis is good *conditionally*: "good with good, evil with evil" (Cattan, Book III, ch. 2). In benefic company the Head may outrank Joy; alone, Joy is the safer figure. The order of 3 and 4 is defensible either way.
    - **The conditional figures (7–9) resist ranking altogether.** Conjunctio, Populus and Via are not "moderately good"; they are good or ill *by company and question*. Cattan calls Populus "sometime good and sometime bad" and Via a figure that "breaketh and spoileth al the goodnes of the others" yet is "very good" for journeys (Book III, ch. 2). In a travel question Via leaps half the table.
    - **Fortuna Minor is two figures wearing one coat.** Cattan's chapter 27 lists it among the "Evill Figures signifying evill," the figures of false love and of loss; yet Book III, ch. 2 calls it "good in any affaire, wherein a person would goe quickely." Rank it tenth for endurance, third for speed.
    - **Tristitia above Amissio is arguable.** Cattan's tables put Tristitia among the good figures for the year and the figures of gain, but also call it "a very ill figure in all the houses" save the eighth and twelfth; in a property question Tristitia may beat Amissio, in a love question Amissio (good "sometimes for love," Crowley, ch. V) beats Tristitia.
    - **Carcer below Tristitia** rests on a single clause (Agrippa's "the first of the greater detrement") and on Carcer's universal note of binding; no source ranks the pair explicitly beyond that.
    - **The bottom two.** Rubeus is placed last because the breaking of the chart attaches to it most anciently and firmly (Cattan: "never found in the first house to make a judgement," Book III, ch. 2), but the Golden Dawn rule treats Rubeus and Cauda Draconis identically, and in questions of endings Cauda is not ill at all: "good for loss, and for passing out of an affair" (Crowley, ch. V). The extremes of the ranking are unanimous; the precise order within the bottom two, like everything else in this list, bends to the question.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>
