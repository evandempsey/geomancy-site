Lesson 2 gave the figures as shapes. This lesson gives their meanings: planet, sign, element, character, people signified, and place in the order of fortune. Do not reduce a figure to one keyword. Learn the range of each figure, one planetary pair at a time.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 1 and 2: the sources and their standing, and the anatomy of the figures from memory. Cattan's chapter of qualities is the assigned reading; the order-of-fortune section relies on it. The figure pages ([Via](/figures/via/), [Populus](/figures/populus/) and the rest) collect the corpus quotations for each figure and are the standing reference.

## Where the characters come from

A geomantic figure gets its character from three places.

First, and above all, from its **planet**. The sources assign the sixteen figures to the seven planets and to the Moon's two nodes, the Dragon's Head and Tail. Each planet receives two figures. The pair divides the planet's nature: one figure usually carries the more favorable form, the other the more difficult form. This is why the lesson is arranged by planetary pairs.

Second, from its **element and sign**. Each figure belongs to one of the four elements and to a sign of the zodiac, and these refine the planetary character: they tell you whether the figure's nature runs hot or cold, quick or slow, and they matter technically later, when figures are compared by element and dignity. The pseudo-Agrippa derives the signs directly from the planetary rulerships, assigning each figure to a house of its own planet:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/signs" />

This is the system this site takes as its default, and the one printed at the top of every figure page: the two Fortunes in Leo, Via and Populus in Cancer, Acquisitio in Pisces and Laetitia in Sagittarius, Puella in Taurus and Amissio in Libra, Conjunctio in Virgo and Albus in Gemini, Puer and Rubeus in Scorpio, Carcer in Capricorn and Tristitia in Aquarius, the Dragon's Head with Capricorn and the Tail with Scorpio. The elements in the course's default system follow Agrippa's "natures of the planets and figures themselves," covered below.

**The sources do not agree on the elements and signs.** This course states one system as its default, the one in the figure pages' headline attributions. Use that system throughout the course unless a lesson explicitly tells you to use Gerard's separate table. The variants stay on the figure pages for reference; they are not a memory task.

Third, from its **structure**. A figure with more points above than below is entering and tends toward good; more below than above is going out and tends toward ill. The active lines also help explain the meaning. Laetitia has one active fire line and is read as joy. Tristitia is its inversion, with one active earth line, and is read as sorrow. Acquisitio and Amissio likewise oppose each other by structure. The form is a useful guide to the meaning.

Each figure also has a **mobility**: eight figures are stable, inclined to endure and to fix matters where they stand, and eight are mobile, inclined to motion, change and quick passage. The stable figures in this course's system are Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Puella, Albus, Tristitia, Carcer and Caput Draconis; the mobile are Via, Fortuna Minor, Puer, Rubeus, Amissio, Laetitia, Conjunctio and Cauda Draconis. Use mobility especially in questions of time and travel. You will use it constantly from Lesson 5 onward.

Now the sixteen. For each pair, the lesson gives the planet's nature, how the two figures divide that nature, and a short reading of each figure. The figure pages hold the fuller source record.

## The Sun's pair: Fortuna Major and Fortuna Minor

The Sun in older astrology is the planet of life, honour, victory, kings and gold. Its two figures divide the solar nature not into good and bad (both Fortunes are fortunate) but into two *kinds* of good fortune:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/fortuna" />

Fortuna Major is the Sun by day, in full dignity; Fortuna Minor is the Sun by night, at less strength. Everything else about these two figures follows from that distinction.

### Fortuna Major

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 1, 1]} name="Fortuna Major" size={4} />

[Fortuna Major](/figures/fortuna-major/), the Greater Fortune (Sun, Leo, fire, stable), is by common consent the best figure in geomancy. Its shape is two passive lines above and two active below: strength in the foundations, power the querent already has rather than luck that arrives. The sources read it as success that comes slowly and holds. Crowley's index entry catches the older keywords:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-major-meaning" />

"Safeguard entering": protection that is on its way in and will stay. Heydon's first-house entry shows what the figure makes of a person:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/fortuna-major" />

The goodness is commanding: "a high mighty man given to command." Its one listed weakness is secrecy: where the question concerns keeping something hidden, even this best figure is "a token of small surety."

### Fortuna Minor

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 2, 2]} name="Fortuna Minor" size={4} />

[Fortuna Minor](/figures/fortuna-minor/), the Lesser Fortune (Sun, Leo, air, mobile), is the same Sun in its nocturnal weakness: success still, but borrowed, hasty and fading. Its shape is Fortuna Major overturned: two active lines at the head and nothing beneath them. The sources read it as outward strength, help from others and from speed rather than from one's own foundation. Cattan's first-house entry stresses speed and heat:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/fortuna-minor" />

Choler, hastiness, swiftness, force, boldness, victory, and the same solar weakness: "the matter shall be disclosed." Like Fortuna Major, Fortuna Minor cannot keep a secret; unlike it, it cannot hold a position. Crowley's keywords give the contrast:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-minor-meaning" />

"Safeguard going out" against "safeguard entering": protection that does not last. Fortuna Minor is good when speed is needed and unreliable when endurance is needed; Cattan even lists it among figures of evil and loss, because what it gives is brief.

## The Moon's pair: Via and Populus

The Moon is the fastest planet and governs travel, crowds, and change. Her two figures are the two extreme shapes of the system: all single points and all double points. Agrippa derives them from her phases:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/via-populus" />

One figure is motion; the other is multitude. Neither has much fixed character of its own, which is part of its meaning.

### Via

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 1, 1]} name="Via" size={4} />

[Via](/figures/via/), the Way (Moon, Cancer, water, mobile), is one active point on every line. It signifies road, journey, and change. In its own province, travel and movement, it is excellent. In questions that need stability, it is difficult because it changes the matter. Cattan states both sides:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/via" />

A figure which "breaketh and spoileth al the goodnes of the others," strong language, and Crowley gives the same rule in nearly the same words three centuries later:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/via-general" />

Via is not simply evil; it is indifferent to the present state of things. Cattan lists it as good for liberty, coming out of prison, and amendment of sickness, because in those questions change is desired. When Via appears, ask whether change helps or harms the question.

### Populus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 2, 2]} name="Populus" size={4} />

[Populus](/figures/populus/), the People (Moon, Cancer, water, stable), is the opposite extreme: every line doubled, eight points, the most passive figure in the system. It signifies a crowd or assembly. Every source repeats the same rule: Populus takes its goodness or badness from company, good with good figures and evil with evil. Heydon's first-house portrait gives the details:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/populus" />

Cattan's quality-chapter summary gives the rule you will use:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/populus" />

"Sometime good and sometime bad" is the figure's rule. Populus signifies the many: congregations, markets, armies, public matters. In practice, read it as taking its quality from nearby figures.

## The pair of Mars: Puer and Rubeus

Mars is the lesser malefic: heat, iron, war, courage, lust and blood. Older astrology distinguishes Mars used well from Mars uncontrolled, and the two figures of Mars follow that distinction:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/puer-rubeus" />

Mars benevolent and Mars malevolent. Puer is difficult; Rubeus is the most feared figure in the system.

### Puer

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 2, 1]} name="Puer" size={4} />

[Puer](/figures/puer/), the Boy (Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile), is Mars benevolent, which is to say Mars at its least harmful, not Mars made gentle. The figure is all single points except the passive water line. The old keyword is "yellow, beardless," a young man, armed, rash, and not yet wise. Heydon's first-house entry does not flatter him:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/puer" />

Clamorous, seditious, deceitful; soldiers and officers "that care neither for God nor man." Yet read on: good in love, good in war, "good enough" for marriage. Crowley compresses the rule into a sentence:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/puer-general" />

Evil in most demands, *except those relating to war and love*. Puer harms questions that need patience or peace and helps questions that need boldness: a Puer marriage is passionate and unstable, a Puer lawsuit aggressive, a Puer illness feverish and fast.

### Rubeus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 2, 2]} name="Rubeus" size={4} />

[Rubeus](/figures/rubeus/), the Red (Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile), is Mars malevolent: passion, violence, drunkenness, fraud, and bloodshed. Structurally it is the inversion of Albus, the white figure of peace. The sources agree, and they are severe. Cattan's chapter of qualities:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/rubeus" />

"Ill in all good things, and good in all ill things, and many times signifyeth death." And note the line we will return to: *she is never found in the first house to make a judgement.* Crowley's general meaning transmits the same formula:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/rubeus-general" />

That formula, evil in good and good in evil, is more precise than simple maleficence: in a prosperity question Rubeus is disastrous, while in a question about vice it can confirm the vice asked about. Heydon's first-house entry runs simply "Rogues, Whores, Theeves, Robbers, Murderers." Some sources refuse to read the chart at all when Rubeus rises in the first house, a rule treated below.

## The pair of Venus: Puella and Amissio

Venus is the lesser benefic: love, beauty, pleasure, the bonds between people. Her two figures split her into the beloved and the price paid:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/puella-amissio" />

The first is fortunate; the other is Venus "as it were retrograde, or combust": desire turning into loss.

### Puella

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 1, 1]} name="Puella" size={4} />

[Puella](/figures/puella/), the Girl (Venus, Taurus, air, stable), is Venus in her favorable form. Its nature is harmony, pleasure, and peace. Heydon's portrait gives the social and bodily details:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/puella" />

To sing and dance, to be well clothed and neat, a lover of gardens, and "good in case of Love, and in all things but in War." Cattan's quality-chapter summary makes the rule general:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/puella" />

Very good in *all* demands, especially those of women. Puella is among the more reliable fortunate figures, but it is weak in conflict, and in questions of long labor it promises more ease than endurance. It softens what it touches; whether softening helps depends on the question.

### Amissio

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 1, 2]} name="Amissio" size={4} />

[Amissio](/figures/amissio/), Loss (Venus, Libra, fire, mobile), is Venus turned to loss. The figure is Acquisitio overturned, and its plain meaning is loss, expense, and things leaving possession. Cattan's first-house entry is severe:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/amissio" />

Loss of inheritance and possessions, ill beginning and ill issue ("ill in all demaundes that can be propounded"), *except for prisoners*, because for a prisoner the loss is the prison's. Crowley preserves the same structure of exceptions and adds the one that makes the figure Venusian:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/amissio-general" />

"Sometimes for love": because love may involve willing loss. Amissio is the figure of things leaving: bad for gain, fitting for paying debts or giving gifts, and good for losing what should be lost, such as sickness or captivity.

## The pair of Mercury: Conjunctio and Albus

Mercury is the planet of mind: speech, writing, commerce, and the connecting of one thing to another. It is neither benefic nor malefic but convertible, coloured by its company, and both its figures inherit that conditional quality. Both are good, says Agrippa, but not equally:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/conjunctio-albus" />

The first the more fortunate: Mercury active and Mercury contemplative, the broker and the scholar.

### Conjunctio

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 1, 2]} name="Conjunctio" size={4} />

[Conjunctio](/figures/conjunctio/), Conjunction (Mercury, Virgo, air, mobile), is Mercury in motion: meeting, assembly, the joining of separate things. The shape suggests the meaning: the points converge at the middle. Cattan gives the figure's two great significations in one sentence:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/conjunctio" />

Good with good and ill with ill, like Populus; but where Populus merely reflects, Conjunctio *connects*: "a recoverment and restitution of thinges scattered or lost." It is the classic figure for recovering stolen goods, reuniting parties, and making contracts and marriages. Heydon's first-house portrait shows the mental side:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/conjunctio" />

Graceful speech, good memory, mathematics, and many languages: Mercury's gifts. But the entry is conditional: good company gives knowledge, bad company knavery and theft. Conjunctio joins things; the rest of the chart decides whether the joining helps.

### Albus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 1, 2]} name="Albus" size={4} />

[Albus](/figures/albus/), the White (Mercury, Gemini, earth, stable), is Mercury at rest: the contemplative mind, wisdom, peace, clear thought. Its single active point is on the water line. The sources read it as the figure of the philosopher and named it for the colour of purity and of old men's hair. Heydon gives it a strongly favorable portrait:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/albus" />

A noble person who "scornes base actions," a scholar, good for profit, law-suits, messages and marriage, "but it is naught for War, and good for Peace." Cattan's quality-chapter line gives the practical core:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/albus" />

Good for hoped-for gain, and good "to have entrie into any place." Albus is a gentle fortunate figure: it favors study, agreements, healing, and peace, and fails where force is needed.

## The pair of Jupiter: Acquisitio and Laetitia

Jupiter is the greater benefic: abundance, justice, expansion, fortune as increase. His two figures divide that increase between material gain and joy:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/acquisitio-laetitia" />

Jupiter the greater fortune in the first, the lesser in the second, "but without detriment." Both are benefic, but Acquisitio is stronger.

### Acquisitio

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 2, 1]} name="Acquisitio" size={4} />

[Acquisitio](/figures/acquisitio/), Gain (Jupiter, Pisces, water, stable), is the figure of obtaining: profit, acquisition, success in any matter where something is to be gotten. Generations of geomancers saw its shape as two tied money-bags, full and hanging. Cattan's first-house entry is the figure's biography:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/acquisitio" />

Goodness, happiness, quiet life, "the accomplishment of the persons owne harts desire"; good for love and marriage and "merveilous good" in church causes; good for all things *but war*. Crowley's summary keeps the essential:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/acquisitio-general" />

Generally good for profit or gain. The exceptions matter: Acquisitio is ill for coming out of prison, because what it grasps it keeps, and it can be ill in sickness questions when the thing gained is the disease.

### Laetitia

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 2, 2]} name="Laetitia" size={4} />

[Laetitia](/figures/laetitia/), Joy (Jupiter, Sagittarius, air, mobile), is Jupiter's increase as gladness, health, and good fortune of the spirit. Its shape is one active fire point above three passive lines, so it is easy to remember as an upward figure. Heydon's portrait is strongly favorable:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/laetitia" />

Long and prosperous life, health, peace, amity, good in everything except war, with one flaw in matters of love: the person "will promise much, and perform but little." Cattan's quality-chapter line broadens it:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/laetitia" />

Good for joy "as well present as to come," and good almost in all houses. Laetitia favors health, children, feasts, deliverance, and good news; being mobile and airy, its fortune is quicker and lighter than Acquisitio's.

## The pair of Saturn: Carcer and Tristitia

Saturn is the greater malefic: cold, slow, old; the planet of limits, walls, burdens and grief. But Saturn's evils have their uses: what imprisons also protects. Both his figures are evil, says Agrippa, but not equally:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/carcer-tristitia" />

The first (Carcer) of the greater detriment. The prison is worse than the sorrow, because sorrow can move.

### Carcer

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 2, 1]} name="Carcer" size={4} />

[Carcer](/figures/carcer/), the Prison (Saturn, Capricorn, earth, stable), is enclosure: the points form a closed ring, active lines at head and feet around two passive lines. It signifies restriction, delay, binding, imprisonment in every literal and figurative sense. Cattan's first-house entry is grim:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/carcer" />

Melancholy, heaviness, envy, anger, "great paine and travaile," ill in all things *except fortification*: "rampiers, forts, and other fortifications of Townes, and to defende them well." The exception shows the figure's logic: a wall is bad for the one outside it and protection for the one behind it. Crowley's one-line summary:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/carcer-general" />

Delay, binding, stay, bar, restriction. In a question of progress, Carcer is the worst news short of Rubeus. In a question of keeping (will my tenant stay, will the secret hold, will the fortress stand), it is a grim kind of yes. Carcer says that nothing moves, and the reader must decide who profits from that. It also breaks the entering-and-exiting rule: with equal points above and below it should be mean, but Cattan excepts it by name. Its people are the jailer and the jailed, the miser, the solitary. Everything it gives, it gives late and keeps long.

### Tristitia

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 2, 1]} name="Tristitia" size={4} />

[Tristitia](/figures/tristitia/), Sorrow (Saturn, Aquarius, earth, stable), is grief: the single active point on the earth line, everything above it passive. The older books picture it as a stake driven into the ground; it is Laetitia overturned. Heydon's entry is severe:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/tristitia" />

Troubles, melancholy perturbations of mind and body, an ill beginning, and then the Saturnine exceptions: good "to search treasure in the Earth," good "in matters of Fortifications and buildings." Crowley's summary is the shortest in his handbook:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/tristitia-general" />

Evil in almost all things, with specific exceptions: in questions of building, land, mines, and buried things, Tristitia's fixity can help. Its usual meanings are grief, condemnation, and descent.

## The Dragon's Head and Tail

The last pair belongs to no planet. Caput Draconis and Cauda Draconis are assigned to the nodes of the Moon, the points where the Moon's path crosses the Sun's: the Head in the north, where the Moon climbs, the Tail in the south, where she descends. In astrology the Head increases and the Tail diminishes, and the figures inherit exactly that. Agrippa's note on the pair is one line:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/dragons-head-tail" />

They "follow their own natures": the Head swallowing in, the Tail casting out. Both are threshold figures, and both take their moral colour from their company more than any figures except Populus and Conjunctio: good with good and evil with evil, an entrance and an exit.

### Caput Draconis

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 1, 1]} name="Caput Draconis" size={4} />

[Caput Draconis](/figures/caput-draconis/), the Dragon's Head (the North Node, Capricorn, earth, stable), is the upper threshold: the doorway in. Its shape is all activity below a closed head. It signifies beginnings, entrances, and the increase of whatever it joins. Heydon's first-house entry is generous:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/caput-draconis" />

Honours, riches, favours of great persons, "good for all things that ye can demand" except war; good for marriage, "but it will be long in doing." Crowley preserves the conditional rule attached to both nodes:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/caput-draconis-general" />

Good with good, evil with evil, but with "a good issue for gain," because the Head tends toward increase. It favors beginnings: new ventures, foundations, contracts.

### Cauda Draconis

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 1, 2]} name="Cauda Draconis" size={4} />

[Cauda Draconis](/figures/cauda-draconis/), the Dragon's Tail (the South Node, Scorpio, water, mobile), is the way out. It is the Head overturned. Where the Head begins and increases, the Tail ends and diminishes. Cattan's quality-chapter summary gives the rule:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/cauda-draconis" />

"Very good with the ill, and very ill with the good": the reverse of the Head's rule. It is good "in matter of losse... and to passe out of an affaire," and harmful nearly everywhere else. Crowley transmits the rule intact:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/cauda-draconis-general" />

Good for loss and for passing out of an affair: if the question is how to end something, leave a post, dissolve a partnership, or escape danger, the Tail can help. For anything you want to keep or begin, it is among the worst figures. When the Tail appears, ask whether the querent wanted an ending.

That is all sixteen. The rest of this lesson, and the rest of the course, assumes these portraits are becoming automatic.

## When the sources disagree

You have now met, in passing, several moments where a quotation's attributions did not match this site's headline attributions: Cattan calling Acquisitio a figure of the air in Aries while the figure page says water and Pisces, or placing Amissio in the sign of Scorpio where the default says Libra. These mismatches are not errors in the site or the source record. **The sources genuinely disagree about the elements and signs of the figures, and the disagreement is part of what you are learning.**

The planetary attributions are nearly unanimous across the sources, with only the nodes attracting dissent: Cattan makes the Head a figure "of ♃ and ♀ according to the opinion of some men: but by mine opinion of ♃ and ☿," and gives the Tail to Mars and Saturn, where later usage simply says North and South Node. Here is Cattan comparing the opinions himself:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/caput-draconis" />

That sentence (*some men say, but by mine opinion*) shows how the sources think: the Renaissance authors were not transmitting a fixed revelation but comparing systems and choosing.

The elements and signs are where the systems multiply. The pseudo-Agrippa himself reports more than one. He gives a "vulgar order" of the triplicities, and then immediately offers a better one of his own:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/triplicities" />

This second arrangement (fiery Fortuna Major, Rubeus, Puer and Amissio; airy Fortuna Minor, Puella, Laetitia and Conjunctio; watery Acquisitio, Cauda Draconis, Via and Populus; earthy Carcer, Tristitia, Albus and Caput Draconis) is the elemental system this site takes as its default, because it is derived, as Agrippa says, "according to the natures of the planets, and figures themselves." And for the signs, alongside the planetary-house system you met earlier, Agrippa also records a wholly different distribution that gives each sign of the zodiac roughly one figure:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/zodiacal-distribution" />

Acquisitio to Aries, Laetitia to Gemini, Albus to Leo, Via to Virgo, almost nothing in common with the default system. Gerard of Cremona's astronomical geomancy uses yet another set, the signs placed in the ascendant for each figure; Cattan's Book II attributions differ again; and the Golden Dawn, whose system Crowley transmits, rebuilt the signs from the planetary rulerships with different choices about which planet's two houses go to which figure.

Take **Acquisitio** as the concrete case. The course default says Jupiter, Pisces, water, and that is the attribution used in course exercises. Its figure page records other systems, but the working rule is simple: do not mix them into the same chart.

What should a student do with this? Three things.

The figures' meanings came first. The signs and elements refine those meanings and matter technically when figures are compared by element or dignity. So follow the course policy: **use one system consistently.** This course uses the default stated at the top of each figure page, and every later technique that uses elements or signs assumes it.

## The order of fortune

The portraits gave each figure its character; the working reader also needs a default order, because a reading often begins with a simple question: is this figure good or ill for this matter? Cattan's chapter of qualities (your assigned reading) opens with a structural rule you already possess from Lesson 2, now applied to fortune:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

Entering figures good, exiting figures evil, mean figures mean, with three exceptions Cattan names: Tristitia (entering but evil), Laetitia (exiting but good), Carcer (mean in shape, but not in nature). Read the rule beside the chapter's long tables of figures good for love, war, voyages, and marriage, and beside the figure-by-figure summaries above. The course uses this working order, from best to worst:

1. **Fortuna Major**: good in all things; the best figure in the system.
2. **Acquisitio**: Jupiter the greater fortune; good for nearly everything that can be gained.
3. **Caput Draconis**: good in almost all demands, conditional on company.
4. **Laetitia**: good almost in all houses; Jupiter "without detriment."
5. **Puella**: very good in all demands, weakest in conflict.
6. **Albus**: gentle, wise, gainful; useless in war.
7. **Conjunctio**: good with good, ill with ill; the best conditional figure.
8. **Populus**: pure mirror; takes everything from its company.
9. **Via**: breaks the goodness of others; excellent in its own province of movement.
10. **Fortuna Minor**: real but fading good; appears in Cattan's evil lists for its instability.
11. **Puer**: evil save in war and love.
12. **Amissio**: evil save where loss is the goal.
13. **Tristitia**: evil save in the deep and the buried.
14. **Carcer**: evil, "of the greater detrement" of Saturn's two; binds everything.
15. **Cauda Draconis**: ruin and rupture; good only for endings.
16. **Rubeus**: the worst: vice, violence, and the figure that stops the chart.

The order is firm at the ends and looser in the middle. The sources agree that Fortuna Major is best and that Rubeus and Cauda Draconis are among the worst. The middle depends more on question and context: Laetitia may be read above Caput Draconis in some benefic questions, Tristitia may improve in matters of land or buried things, and Fortuna Minor improves when speed is the main need. The order of fortune is a default, not a substitute for the question.

## When Rubeus rises

One rule remains, and it concerns the worst figures. Crowley's handbook, transmitting the practice of the Golden Dawn, gives an instruction found nowhere else in this corpus in so many words:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/destroy-rule" />

Destroy it instantly; wait two hours; cast again. Taken as written, this is a Golden Dawn rule, and you should learn it as one: the mechanical formula (both figures, instant destruction, the two-hour quarantine) appears in no Renaissance source on this site. But the kernel of it is far older, and you have already met it twice. Cattan reports that the "Egyptians and a great part of the Latines" held that a chart with Rubeus in the first house "should not be judged. But that figure must be broken and make an other," and he sides with them, on experience; his chapter of qualities states flatly that Rubeus "is never found in the first house to make a judgement." For Cauda Draconis he reports the same opinion with less conviction. And Heydon, a century later, knows the custom and rejects it:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/cauda-draconis" />

Some old writers, he reports, would not have this figure or Rubeus judged in the first house: "but I am of the opinion that the Judgment herein should not be delayd." Read it, says Heydon; it simply means the matter is very bad.

So the sources divide. Cattan's preferred practice, later repeated in the Golden Dawn, treats Rubeus rising, and in the Golden Dawn Cauda too, as a reason not to read the chart. Heydon treats the same condition as a terrible answer, but still an answer. This course follows Cattan's practice: a chart with Rubeus or Cauda Draconis in the first house is set aside unjudged, and the question rests for a time. Know that Heydon dissents.

## Worked example

The exercises quiz portraits like these: start with structure, read the relevant source passages, and end with the figure's character in plain words. Here is the method performed twice, on the worst figure and the best.

### Rubeus, read across four sources

Begin always with the structural facts, because they anchor everything: Rubeus, "the Red," points 2-1-2-2, the single active point on the air line; Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile in the course system; counterpart and inversion of Albus. Then let the key passages speak, oldest rule first.

The pseudo-Agrippa needs only a line: Puer has Mars benevolent, Rubeus malevolent. That is the general rule. For the detail, here is Cattan's fullest statement, the first-house chapter:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/rubeus" />

The passage catalogues the figure's significations (craft, fraud, treason, war, "all the wickednesse in the world"), shows that the source set was already plural in antiquity (Indians, Chaldeans and Hebrews reading the figure, Egyptians and Latins breaking the chart), and shows Cattan siding with the Egyptians "by experience." Heydon's entry is his tersest in the whole first-house chapter:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/rubeus" />

"A deadly figure, and not fit to be judged," though the same Heydon, under Cauda Draconis two pages later, argues against delaying the reading. Last, the modern comparison:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/rubeus-general" />

and, in his chapter of method, the destroy-rule you have just studied: the Golden Dawn hardening Cattan's practice into a mechanism, and extending it to Cauda Draconis.

Now put the notes together. The sources agree that Rubeus is the malevolent form of Mars: vice, violence, and deceit, evil in honest demands and useful only when the question itself concerns evil. Where the sources differ on whether to read or destroy the chart, record the variant and choose your practice. A plain synthesis: Rubeus signifies uncontrolled passion and vice; it harms honest questions, describes dishonest ones, and Cattan's practice refuses to read the chart when Rubeus rises in the first house.

### Fortuna Major, read the same way

The same method on the opposite pole. Structure: 2-2-1-1, the active points below, strength in the foundation; Sun, Leo, fire, stable; counterpart Fortuna Minor. Agrippa's pair-rule gives the genus: the Sun diurnal, in his dignities, solar fortune at full strength. Cattan's first house gives the species:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/fortuna-major" />

Good will, loyalty, prosperity, friendship of the great, and good in all things, with the single exception of secrecy. Heydon's parallel passage, quoted above, adds the commanding person and expands Cattan in his usual way. Cattan's chapter of qualities adds the figure's usual tempo:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/fortuna-major" />

Good for gain "where a person hath hope to winne," where real strength already exists. The tables of chapter 27 add the surprises a portrait should mention: Fortuna Major appears among figures of *covetousness* as well as of life and victory, and for sickness it promises amendment "but the sickenesse will be long." Crowley transmits the core unchanged:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-major-general" />

Across three and a half centuries and two lineages, Fortuna Major's meaning is essentially undisputed; the variant attributions touched its element and sign, never its nature. A closing synthesis might run: Fortuna Major is strength the querent already has; it gives lasting victory to those with a position to win from, and its one weakness is secrecy.

That is the form of a portrait, and the form the quizzes below test: structure first, important limits, and a plain synthesis.

## Exercises

Do these in order, in writing, before opening any model answer. The first four quizzes test the figure-pairs taught in this lesson. Take them with the figure pages closed, then check each answer against the model answer and the figure pages. A few words per question is enough. Add a note only when it changes how the figure is read.

<Exercise n={1} title="The Moon's pair in questions" kind="quiz">
  Via and Populus, tested as working figures; a few words each.

  1. A chart asks whether a prisoner will get out. Which of Via's qualities helps the question?
  2. A chart asks whether a partnership will stay firm. Why is Via a warning sign?
  3. Populus appears in an important house with strong neighbors. How should you read it?
  4. Populus appears in an important house with mixed or hostile neighbors. What changes?
  5. Which of the two Moon figures is better for movement?
  6. Which is more likely to mirror its surroundings?

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. Motion, passage, and release. The question wants movement out of confinement.
    2. Via moves. A partnership question usually wants steadiness, so Via can show change, departure, or inconstancy.
    3. As a mirror or crowd figure strengthened by its surroundings. Populus takes much of its practical color from the company and house around it.
    4. The same mirroring works against the matter. Populus does not supply a firm nature of its own to correct the surrounding trouble.
    5. Via.
    6. Populus.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={2} title="The Venus pair in questions" kind="quiz">
  Puella and Amissio, with attention to the kind of thing the querent wants.

  1. Which figure is easier in love, peace, ornament, and social agreement?
  2. Which figure is harder in questions of gain and keeping property?
  3. A prisoner asks about release and Amissio appears. Why may loss help?
  4. A merchant asks about profit and Amissio appears. Why is the same figure difficult?
  5. What is Amissio's counterpart?
  6. Write one sentence that tells a beginner when loss is beneficial.

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. Puella.
    2. Amissio.
    3. In a release question, losing the prison or losing the bond is the desired outcome.
    4. Profit asks for keeping and increase. Amissio removes, spends, or lets go.
    5. Acquisitio.
    6. Loss helps when the question asks to be rid of something; it harms when the question asks to gain or keep.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={3} title="The Jupiter pair in questions" kind="quiz">
  Acquisitio and Laetitia by use.

  1. Which figure is stronger for gain, profit, and obtaining?
  2. Which figure is lighter, quicker, and more joyful?
  3. A question asks whether money will be obtained. Which figure is the cleaner help?
  4. A question asks whether a feast, pleasure, or celebration goes well. Which figure is naturally at home?
  5. Why can Laetitia still disappoint in a promise or love question?
  6. What does Laetitia become when inverted?

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. Acquisitio.
    2. Laetitia.
    3. Acquisitio.
    4. Laetitia.
    5. It can promise more happiness than it performs. Joy is not the same thing as durable completion.
    6. Tristitia.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={4} title="The Saturn pair in questions" kind="quiz">
  Carcer and Tristitia are not simply "bad." Read what the question wants.

  1. Which figure is stronger for binding, imprisonment, walls, and delay?
  2. Which figure more often shows sorrow, heaviness, and fixity?
  3. A question asks whether a fortification will hold. Why can Saturn help?
  4. A question asks whether grief will lift soon. Why is Tristitia difficult?
  5. A question asks about hidden treasure in the earth. Why is Tristitia less out of place there?
  6. Write one sentence that tells a beginner when a Saturn figure can be useful.

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. Carcer.
    2. Tristitia.
    3. The question wants containment and strength against release. A figure of binding can do that work.
    4. Tristitia fixes and weighs down. The question wants relief and movement.
    5. The matter is low, hidden, and fixed in earth. Tristitia's heaviness suits that kind of matter better than a question that wants speed or joy.
    6. A Saturn figure helps when the question wants something to stay, bind, endure, contain, or be protected by a limit.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>
