Lesson 2 gave the figures as shapes. Lesson 3 teaches their meanings. Each figure has a planet, a sign, an element, a character, the people it signifies, and a place in the order of fortune. Do not reduce a figure to one keyword. Learn the range of each figure, one planetary pair at a time.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 1 and 2. You should know the sources and their standing, and you should know the anatomy of the figures from memory. Cattan's chapter of qualities is the assigned reading, and the order-of-fortune section relies on it. The figure pages ([Via](/figures/via/), [Populus](/figures/populus/) and the rest) collect the source quotations for each figure. Use them as your standing reference.

## Where the characters come from

A geomantic figure gets its character from three places: its planet, its element and sign, and its structure.

The first and most important source is the **planet**. The sources assign the sixteen figures to the seven planets and to the Moon's two nodes, the Dragon's Head and Tail. Each planet receives two figures. The pair divides the planet's nature. One figure usually carries the more favorable form, the other the more difficult form. This is why the lesson is arranged by planetary pairs.

The second source is the **element and sign**. Each figure belongs to one of the four elements and to a sign of the zodiac. These refine the planetary character. They tell you whether the figure's nature runs hot or cold, quick or slow. They also matter technically later, when figures are compared by element and dignity. The pseudo-Agrippa derives the signs directly from the planetary rulerships, assigning each figure to a house of its own planet:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/signs" />

This is the system this site takes as its default, and the one printed at the top of every figure page. The two Fortunes go in Leo, Via and Populus in Cancer, Acquisitio in Pisces and Laetitia in Sagittarius, Puella in Taurus and Amissio in Libra, Conjunctio in Virgo and Albus in Gemini, Puer and Rubeus in Scorpio, Carcer in Capricorn and Tristitia in Aquarius, the Dragon's Head with Capricorn and the Tail with Scorpio. The elements in the course's default system follow Agrippa's "natures of the planets and figures themselves," covered below.

**The sources do not agree on the elements and signs.** This course states one system as its default, the one in the figure pages' headline attributions. Use that system throughout the course unless a lesson explicitly tells you to use Gerard's separate table. The variants stay on the figure pages for reference. They are not a memory task.

The third source is the **structure**. A figure with more points above than below is entering, and it tends toward good. A figure with more below than above is going out, and it tends toward ill. The active lines also help explain the meaning. Laetitia has one active fire line and is read as joy. Tristitia is its inversion, with one active earth line, and is read as sorrow. Acquisitio and Amissio also oppose each other by structure. The form is a useful guide to the meaning.

Each figure also has a **mobility**. Eight figures are stable, inclined to endure and to fix matters where they stand. Eight are mobile, inclined to motion, change and quick passage. The stable figures in this course's system are Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Puella, Albus, Tristitia, Carcer and Caput Draconis. The mobile figures are Via, Fortuna Minor, Puer, Rubeus, Amissio, Laetitia, Conjunctio and Cauda Draconis. Use mobility especially in questions of time and travel. You will use it constantly from Lesson 5 onward.

For each pair below, the lesson gives the planet's nature, how the two figures divide that nature, and a short reading of each figure. The figure pages hold the fuller source record.

## The Sun's pair: Fortuna Major and Fortuna Minor

The Sun in older astrology is the planet of life, honour, victory, kings and gold. Both Fortunes are fortunate, so its two figures do not divide the solar nature into good and bad. They divide it into two kinds of good fortune:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/fortuna" />

Fortuna Major is the Sun by day, in full dignity. Fortuna Minor is the Sun by night, at less strength. The rest of what these two figures mean follows from that distinction.

### Fortuna Major

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 1, 1]} name="Fortuna Major" size={4} />

[Fortuna Major](/figures/fortuna-major/), the Greater Fortune (Sun, Leo, fire, stable), is widely held to be the best figure in geomancy. Its shape is two passive lines above and two active below. The strength is in the foundations. It signifies power the querent already has, not luck that arrives. The sources read it as success that comes slowly and holds. Crowley's index entry catches the older keywords:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-major-meaning" />

By "safeguard entering" Crowley means protection that is on its way in and will stay. Heydon's first-house entry shows what the figure makes of a person:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/fortuna-major" />

Heydon makes the person commanding, "a high mighty man given to command." The figure's one listed weakness is secrecy. Where the question concerns keeping something hidden, even this best figure is "a token of small surety."

### Fortuna Minor

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 2, 2]} name="Fortuna Minor" size={4} />

[Fortuna Minor](/figures/fortuna-minor/), the Lesser Fortune (Sun, Leo, air, mobile), is the same Sun by night, in its weaker state. It still signifies success, but success that is borrowed, hasty and fading. Its shape is Fortuna Major overturned, with two active lines at the head and nothing beneath them. The sources read it as outward strength, help from others and from speed rather than from one's own foundation. Cattan's first-house entry stresses speed and heat:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/fortuna-minor" />

Cattan gives choler, hastiness, swiftness, force, boldness, and victory, along with the same solar weakness about secrecy: "the matter shall be disclosed." Fortuna Minor cannot keep a secret, and unlike Fortuna Major it cannot hold a position either. Crowley's keywords give the contrast:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-minor-meaning" />

Crowley's "safeguard going out" stands against Fortuna Major's "safeguard entering." It means protection that does not last. Fortuna Minor is good when speed is needed and unreliable when endurance is needed. Cattan even lists it among figures of evil and loss, because what it gives is brief.

## The Moon's pair: Via and Populus

The Moon is the fastest planet and governs travel, crowds, and change. Her two figures are the two extreme shapes of the system, one with all single points and one with all double points. Agrippa derives them from her phases:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/via-populus" />

Via signifies motion and Populus signifies a multitude. Neither has much fixed character of its own, and that lack is part of its meaning.

### Via

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 1, 1]} name="Via" size={4} />

[Via](/figures/via/), the Way (Moon, Cancer, water, mobile), is one active point on every line. It signifies road, journey, and change. In questions of travel and movement it is excellent. In questions that need stability it is difficult, because it changes the matter. Cattan states both sides:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/via" />

Cattan says Via "breaketh and spoileth al the goodnes of the others." Crowley gives the same rule in nearly the same words three centuries later:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/via-general" />

Via is not simply evil. It is neutral about the present state of things, and it tends to change them. Cattan lists it as good for liberty, coming out of prison, and amendment of sickness, because in those questions change is wanted. When Via appears, ask whether change helps or harms the question.

### Populus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 2, 2]} name="Populus" size={4} />

[Populus](/figures/populus/), the People (Moon, Cancer, water, stable), is the opposite extreme. Every line is doubled, for eight points in all, and it is the most passive figure in the system. It signifies a crowd or assembly. Every source repeats the same rule. Populus takes its goodness or badness from its company, good with good figures and evil with evil. Heydon's first-house portrait gives the details:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/populus" />

Cattan's quality-chapter summary gives the rule you will use:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/populus" />

Cattan's rule for the figure is "sometime good and sometime bad." Populus signifies the many, such as congregations, markets, armies, and public matters. In practice, read it as taking its quality from nearby figures.

## The pair of Mars: Puer and Rubeus

Mars is the lesser malefic. It signifies heat, iron, war, courage, lust and blood. Older astrology distinguishes Mars used well from Mars uncontrolled, and the two figures of Mars follow that distinction:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/puer-rubeus" />

One figure is Mars benevolent and the other is Mars malevolent. Puer is difficult, and Rubeus is the most feared figure in the system.

### Puer

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 2, 1]} name="Puer" size={4} />

[Puer](/figures/puer/), the Boy (Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile), is Mars benevolent. This means Mars at its least harmful, not Mars made gentle. The figure is all single points except the passive water line. The old keyword is "yellow, beardless." It describes a young man, armed, rash, and not yet wise. Heydon's first-house entry does not flatter him:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/puer" />

Heydon makes him clamorous, seditious, and deceitful, with soldiers and officers "that care neither for God nor man." The same entry adds the favorable side: good in love, good in war, and "good enough" for marriage. Crowley compresses the rule into a sentence:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/puer-general" />

Crowley makes Puer evil in most demands, except those relating to war and love. Puer harms questions that need patience or peace, and it helps questions that need boldness. A Puer marriage is passionate and unstable. A Puer lawsuit is aggressive. A Puer illness is feverish and fast.

### Rubeus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 2, 2]} name="Rubeus" size={4} />

[Rubeus](/figures/rubeus/), the Red (Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile), is Mars malevolent. It signifies passion, violence, drunkenness, fraud, and bloodshed. Structurally it is the inversion of Albus, the white figure of peace. The sources agree on its meaning, and they treat it severely. Cattan's chapter of qualities puts it plainly:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/rubeus" />

Cattan says it is "ill in all good things, and good in all ill things, and many times signifyeth death." Note the line we will return to later: she "is never found in the first house to make a judgement." Crowley's general meaning transmits the same formula:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/rubeus-general" />

That formula, evil in good and good in evil, is more exact than calling Rubeus simply bad. In a prosperity question Rubeus is disastrous. In a question about vice it can confirm the vice asked about. Heydon's first-house entry runs simply "Rogues, Whores, Theeves, Robbers, Murderers." Some sources refuse to read the chart at all when Rubeus rises in the first house, a rule treated below.

## The pair of Venus: Puella and Amissio

Venus is the lesser benefic. It signifies love, beauty, pleasure, and the bonds between people. Her two figures split that nature between the thing loved and the loss it can cost:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/puella-amissio" />

Puella is fortunate. Amissio is Venus "as it were retrograde, or combust," which is desire turning into loss.

### Puella

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 1, 1]} name="Puella" size={4} />

[Puella](/figures/puella/), the Girl (Venus, Taurus, air, stable), is Venus in her favorable form. Its nature is harmony, pleasure, and peace. Heydon's portrait gives the social and bodily details:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/puella" />

To sing and dance, to be well clothed and neat, a lover of gardens, and "good in case of Love, and in all things but in War." Cattan's quality-chapter summary makes the rule general:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/puella" />

Cattan makes it very good in all demands, especially those of women. Puella is among the more reliable fortunate figures. It is weak in conflict, and in questions of long labor it promises more ease than endurance. It eases what it touches, and whether that ease helps depends on the question.

### Amissio

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 1, 2]} name="Amissio" size={4} />

[Amissio](/figures/amissio/), Loss (Venus, Libra, fire, mobile), is Venus turned to loss. The figure is Acquisitio overturned. Its plain meaning is loss, expense, and things leaving possession. Cattan's first-house entry is severe:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/amissio" />

Cattan gives loss of inheritance and possessions, ill beginning and ill issue, "ill in all demaundes that can be propounded." The exception is the prisoner, because for a prisoner the loss is the loss of the prison. Crowley keeps the same exceptions and adds the one that ties the figure to Venus:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/amissio-general" />

Crowley's "sometimes for love" is there because love can involve a willing loss. Amissio is the figure of things leaving. It is bad for gain. It is fitting for paying debts or giving gifts. It is good for losing what should be lost, such as sickness or captivity.

## The pair of Mercury: Conjunctio and Albus

Mercury is the planet of mind. It signifies speech, writing, commerce, and the connecting of one thing to another. It is not fixed as benefic or malefic. Its nature shifts with whatever it stands beside, and both its figures share that conditional quality. Both are good, says Agrippa, but not equally:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/conjunctio-albus" />

Conjunctio is the more fortunate of the two. It is Mercury in action, while Albus is Mercury in thought.

### Conjunctio

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 1, 2]} name="Conjunctio" size={4} />

[Conjunctio](/figures/conjunctio/), Conjunction (Mercury, Virgo, air, mobile), is Mercury in motion. It signifies meeting, assembly, and the joining of separate things. The shape suits the meaning, since the points converge toward the middle. Cattan gives the figure's two main significations in one sentence:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/conjunctio" />

It is good with good and ill with ill, like Populus. The difference is that Populus only reflects its company, while Conjunctio joins things together. Cattan gives it "a recoverment and restitution of thinges scattered or lost." It is the usual figure for recovering stolen goods, reuniting parties, and making contracts and marriages. Heydon's first-house portrait shows the mental side:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/conjunctio" />

Heydon gives graceful speech, good memory, mathematics, and many languages, which are Mercury's gifts. The entry is conditional. Good company gives knowledge, and bad company gives knavery and theft. Conjunctio joins things, and the rest of the chart decides whether the joining helps.

### Albus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 1, 2]} name="Albus" size={4} />

[Albus](/figures/albus/), the White (Mercury, Gemini, earth, stable), is Mercury at rest. It signifies the contemplative mind, wisdom, peace, and clear thought. Its single active point is on the water line. The sources read it as the figure of the philosopher, and they named it for the colour of purity and of old men's hair. Heydon gives it a strongly favorable portrait:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/albus" />

Heydon gives a noble person who "scornes base actions," a scholar, good for profit, law-suits, messages and marriage, "but it is naught for War, and good for Peace." Cattan's quality-chapter line gives the practical core:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/albus" />

Cattan makes it good for hoped-for gain, and good "to have entrie into any place." Albus is a gentle fortunate figure. It favors study, agreements, healing, and peace, and it fails where force is needed.

## The pair of Jupiter: Acquisitio and Laetitia

Jupiter is the greater benefic. It signifies abundance, justice, expansion, and fortune as increase. His two figures divide that increase between material gain and joy:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/acquisitio-laetitia" />

Acquisitio is Jupiter the greater fortune, and Laetitia is the lesser, "but without detriment." Both are benefic, and Acquisitio is the stronger of the two.

### Acquisitio

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 2, 1]} name="Acquisitio" size={4} />

[Acquisitio](/figures/acquisitio/), Gain (Jupiter, Pisces, water, stable), is the figure of obtaining. It signifies profit, acquisition, and success in any matter where something is to be gotten. Generations of geomancers saw its shape as two tied money-bags, full and hanging. Cattan's first-house entry sets out the figure in full:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/acquisitio" />

Cattan gives goodness, happiness, a quiet life, and "the accomplishment of the persons owne harts desire." He makes it good for love and marriage, "merveilous good" in church causes, and good for all things but war. Crowley's summary keeps the core:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/acquisitio-general" />

Crowley makes it generally good for profit or gain. There are two exceptions to note. Acquisitio is ill for coming out of prison, because it holds on to what it gains. It can also be ill in sickness questions, when the thing gained is the disease.

### Laetitia

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 2, 2]} name="Laetitia" size={4} />

[Laetitia](/figures/laetitia/), Joy (Jupiter, Sagittarius, air, mobile), is Jupiter's increase as gladness, health, and good fortune of the spirit. Its shape is one active fire point above three passive lines, so it is easy to remember as an upward figure. Heydon's portrait is strongly favorable:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/laetitia" />

Heydon gives long and prosperous life, health, peace, and amity, and makes it good in everything except war. The one flaw is in matters of love, where the person "will promise much, and perform but little." Cattan's quality-chapter line broadens it:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/laetitia" />

Cattan makes it good for joy "as well present as to come," and good almost in all houses. Laetitia favors health, children, feasts, deliverance, and good news. Because it is mobile and airy, its fortune is quicker and lighter than Acquisitio's.

## The pair of Saturn: Carcer and Tristitia

Saturn is the greater malefic. It is cold, slow, and old, the planet of limits, walls, burdens and grief. Saturn's evils still have their uses, since the same wall that imprisons can also protect. Both his figures are evil, says Agrippa, but not equally:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/carcer-tristitia" />

Carcer is the worse of the two, the figure "of the greater detriment." The prison is worse than the sorrow, because sorrow can still move.

### Carcer

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 2, 1]} name="Carcer" size={4} />

[Carcer](/figures/carcer/), the Prison (Saturn, Capricorn, earth, stable), is enclosure. The points form a closed ring, with active lines at head and feet around two passive lines. It signifies restriction, delay, binding, and imprisonment, in both the literal and the figurative sense. Cattan's first-house entry is grim:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/carcer" />

Cattan gives melancholy, heaviness, envy, anger, and "great paine and travaile," and makes it ill in all things except fortification: "rampiers, forts, and other fortifications of Townes, and to defende them well." The exception shows the figure's logic. A wall is bad for the one shut outside it and good for the one it shelters. Crowley's one-line summary:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/carcer-general" />

Crowley gives delay, binding, stay, bar, and restriction. In a question of progress, Carcer is the worst news short of Rubeus. In a question of keeping, it is a grim kind of yes. For example, it can mean that a tenant stays, a secret holds, or a fortress stands. Carcer means that nothing moves, and the reader has to decide who that helps. It also breaks the entering-and-exiting rule. It has equal points above and below, so by shape it should be mean, but Cattan excepts it by name. Its people are the jailer and the jailed, the miser, and the solitary. What it gives, it gives late and holds for a long time.

### Tristitia

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 2, 1]} name="Tristitia" size={4} />

[Tristitia](/figures/tristitia/), Sorrow (Saturn, Aquarius, earth, stable), is grief. It has a single active point on the earth line, with everything above it passive. The older books picture it as a stake driven into the ground, and it is Laetitia overturned. Heydon's entry is severe:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/tristitia" />

Heydon gives troubles, melancholy perturbations of mind and body, and an ill beginning. Then come the Saturnine exceptions. It is good "to search treasure in the Earth" and good "in matters of Fortifications and buildings." Crowley's summary is the shortest in his handbook:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/tristitia-general" />

Crowley makes it evil in almost all things, with a few exceptions. In questions of building, land, mines, and buried things, Tristitia's fixity can help. Its usual meanings are grief, condemnation, and descent.

## The Dragon's Head and Tail

The last pair belongs to no planet. Caput Draconis and Cauda Draconis are assigned to the nodes of the Moon, the points where the Moon's path crosses the Sun's. The Head is in the north, where the Moon climbs, and the Tail is in the south, where she descends. In astrology the Head increases and the Tail diminishes, and the figures carry exactly that. Agrippa's note on the pair is one line:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/dragons-head-tail" />

Agrippa says they "follow their own natures." The Head takes in and the Tail casts out. Both are threshold figures. Both take their quality from their company more than any figures except Populus and Conjunctio. They are good with good and evil with evil, one an entrance and the other an exit.

### Caput Draconis

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 1, 1]} name="Caput Draconis" size={4} />

[Caput Draconis](/figures/caput-draconis/), the Dragon's Head (the North Node, Capricorn, earth, stable), is the upper threshold, the way in. Its shape is all activity below a closed head. It signifies beginnings, entrances, and the increase of whatever it joins. Heydon's first-house entry is generous:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/caput-draconis" />

Heydon gives honours, riches, and favours of great persons, "good for all things that ye can demand" except war. It is good for marriage, "but it will be long in doing." Crowley keeps the conditional rule attached to both nodes:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/caput-draconis-general" />

It is good with good and evil with evil, but with "a good issue for gain," because the Head tends toward increase. It favors beginnings, such as new ventures, foundations, and contracts.

### Cauda Draconis

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 1, 2]} name="Cauda Draconis" size={4} />

[Cauda Draconis](/figures/cauda-draconis/), the Dragon's Tail (the South Node, Scorpio, water, mobile), is the way out. It is the Head overturned. The Head begins and increases, and the Tail ends and diminishes. Cattan's quality-chapter summary gives the rule:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/cauda-draconis" />

Cattan makes it "very good with the ill, and very ill with the good," which is the reverse of the Head's rule. It is good "in matter of losse... and to passe out of an affaire," and harmful nearly everywhere else. Crowley transmits the rule intact:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/cauda-draconis-general" />

Crowley makes it good for loss and for passing out of an affair. If the question is how to end something, leave a post, dissolve a partnership, or escape danger, the Tail can help. For anything you want to keep or begin, it is among the worst figures. When the Tail appears, ask whether the querent wanted an ending.

That covers all sixteen figures. The rest of the lesson and the later lessons assume you are learning these portraits well enough to recall them quickly.

## When the sources disagree

In the portraits above, a few quotations gave attributions that do not match this site's headline attributions. For example, Cattan calls Acquisitio a figure of the air in Aries, while the figure page says water and Pisces. He also places Amissio in the sign of Scorpio, where the default says Libra. These mismatches are not errors in the site or the source record. **The sources genuinely disagree about the elements and signs of the figures, and that disagreement is part of what you are learning.**

The planetary attributions are nearly unanimous across the sources. Only the nodes draw disagreement. Cattan makes the Head a figure "of ♃ and ♀ according to the opinion of some men: but by mine opinion of ♃ and ☿," and he gives the Tail to Mars and Saturn. Later usage simply says North and South Node. Here is Cattan comparing the opinions himself:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/caput-draconis" />

His phrasing, "some men say, but by mine opinion," is worth noting. The Renaissance authors were not copying a fixed list. They were comparing systems and choosing between them.

The elements and signs are where the systems multiply. The pseudo-Agrippa himself reports more than one. He gives a "vulgar order" of the triplicities, and then immediately offers a better one of his own:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/triplicities" />

This second arrangement is the elemental system this site takes as its default, because Agrippa derives it "according to the natures of the planets, and figures themselves." It groups the figures by element as follows. The fiery figures are Fortuna Major, Rubeus, Puer and Amissio. The airy ones are Fortuna Minor, Puella, Laetitia and Conjunctio. The watery ones are Acquisitio, Cauda Draconis, Via and Populus. The earthy ones are Carcer, Tristitia, Albus and Caput Draconis. For the signs, alongside the planetary-house system given earlier, Agrippa also records a different distribution that gives each sign of the zodiac roughly one figure:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/zodiacal-distribution" />

This one assigns Acquisitio to Aries, Laetitia to Gemini, Albus to Leo, and Via to Virgo. It has almost nothing in common with the default system. Other sources go further. Gerard of Cremona's astronomical geomancy uses yet another set, with the signs placed in the ascendant for each figure. Cattan's Book II attributions differ again. The Golden Dawn, whose system Crowley transmits, rebuilt the signs from the planetary rulerships, with different choices about which planet's two houses go to which figure.

Take **Acquisitio** as the concrete case. The course default says Jupiter, Pisces, water, and that is the attribution used in course exercises. Its figure page records other systems. The working rule is simple. Do not mix the systems into the same chart.

So what should you do with all this disagreement? The figures' meanings came first. The signs and elements refine those meanings, and they matter technically when figures are compared by element or dignity. The course policy is to **use one system consistently.** This course uses the default stated at the top of each figure page, and every later technique that uses elements or signs assumes it.

## The order of fortune

The portraits give each figure its character. A reader also needs a default order, because a reading often begins by asking whether a figure is good or ill for the matter at hand. Cattan's chapter of qualities, your assigned reading, opens with the structural rule from Lesson 2, now applied to fortune:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

Entering figures are good, exiting figures are evil, and mean figures are mean. Cattan names three exceptions. Tristitia is entering but evil. Laetitia is exiting but good. Carcer is mean in shape but not in nature. Read this rule alongside the chapter's long tables of figures good for love, war, voyages, and marriage, and alongside the figure-by-figure summaries above. The course uses this working order, from best to worst:

1. **Fortuna Major**: good in all things; the best figure in the system.
2. **Acquisitio**: Jupiter the greater fortune; good for nearly everything that can be gained.
3. **Caput Draconis**: good in almost all demands, conditional on company.
4. **Laetitia**: good almost in all houses; Jupiter "without detriment."
5. **Puella**: very good in all demands, weakest in conflict.
6. **Albus**: gentle, wise, gainful; useless in war.
7. **Conjunctio**: good with good, ill with ill; the best conditional figure.
8. **Populus**: takes its whole quality from its company.
9. **Via**: breaks the goodness of others; excellent in questions of movement.
10. **Fortuna Minor**: real but fading good; appears in Cattan's evil lists for its instability.
11. **Puer**: evil save in war and love.
12. **Amissio**: evil save where loss is the goal.
13. **Tristitia**: evil save in the deep and the buried.
14. **Carcer**: evil, "of the greater detrement" of Saturn's two; binds everything.
15. **Cauda Draconis**: loss and ending; good only for endings.
16. **Rubeus**: the worst; vice and violence, and the figure that stops the chart.

The order is firm at the ends and looser in the middle. The sources agree that Fortuna Major is best and that Rubeus and Cauda Draconis are among the worst. The middle depends more on the question and context. Laetitia may be read above Caput Draconis in some benefic questions. Tristitia may improve in matters of land or buried things. Fortuna Minor improves when speed is the main need. Treat the order of fortune as a default, and let the question adjust it.

## When Rubeus rises

One more rule concerns the worst figures. Crowley's handbook, passing on the practice of the Golden Dawn, gives an instruction that no other source on this site states so plainly:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/destroy-rule" />

The rule is to destroy the chart at once, wait two hours, and cast again. Taken as written, this is a Golden Dawn rule, and you should learn it as one. The exact formula, covering both figures, immediate destruction, and the two-hour wait, appears in no Renaissance source on this site. The core of the idea is older, though, and the lesson has already touched it twice. Cattan reports that the "Egyptians and a great part of the Latines" held that a chart with Rubeus in the first house "should not be judged. But that figure must be broken and make an other." He sides with them, on experience, and his chapter of qualities states flatly that Rubeus "is never found in the first house to make a judgement." For Cauda Draconis he reports the same opinion with less conviction. Heydon, a century later, knows the custom and rejects it:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/cauda-draconis" />

Some old writers, he reports, would not have this figure or Rubeus judged in the first house, "but I am of the opinion that the Judgment herein should not be delayd." Heydon says to read it, since it simply means the matter is very bad.

So the sources divide. Cattan's preferred practice, later repeated in the Golden Dawn, treats Rubeus rising as a reason not to read the chart, and the Golden Dawn extends this to Cauda Draconis. Heydon treats the same condition as a terrible answer, but still an answer. This course follows Cattan's practice. A chart with Rubeus or Cauda Draconis in the first house is set aside unjudged, and the question rests for a time. Note that Heydon disagrees.

## Worked example

The exercises quiz portraits like these. The method is to start with structure, read the relevant source passages, and end with the figure's character in plain words. Here is that method worked twice, once on the worst figure and once on the best.

### Rubeus, read across four sources

Begin with the structural facts. Rubeus is "the Red," points 2-1-2-2, with the single active point on the air line. In the course system it is Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile. It is the counterpart and inversion of Albus. Then work through the key passages, oldest rule first.

The pseudo-Agrippa needs only a line. Puer has Mars benevolent, and Rubeus has Mars malevolent. That is the general rule. For the detail, here is Cattan's fullest statement, the first-house chapter:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/rubeus" />

The passage lists the figure's significations: craft, fraud, treason, war, and "all the wickednesse in the world." It also shows that the sources were already plural in antiquity. The Indians, Chaldeans and Hebrews read the figure, while the Egyptians and Latins broke the chart, and Cattan sides with the Egyptians "by experience." Heydon's entry is his tersest in the whole first-house chapter:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/rubeus" />

He calls it "a deadly figure, and not fit to be judged," though the same Heydon, under Cauda Draconis two pages later, argues against delaying the reading. Last, the modern comparison:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/rubeus-general" />

Crowley's chapter of method also gives the destroy-rule you have just studied. There the Golden Dawn turns Cattan's practice into a fixed procedure and extends it to Cauda Draconis.

Now put the notes together. The sources agree that Rubeus is the malevolent form of Mars: vice, violence, and deceit. It is evil in honest demands and useful only when the question itself concerns evil. Where the sources differ on whether to read or destroy the chart, record the variant and choose your practice. A plain synthesis runs like this. Rubeus signifies uncontrolled passion and vice. It harms honest questions and describes dishonest ones, and Cattan's practice does not read the chart when Rubeus rises in the first house.

### Fortuna Major, read the same way

Now the same method on the best figure. Begin with structure. The points are 2-2-1-1, with the active points below, so the strength is in the foundation. It is Sun, Leo, fire, stable, and its counterpart is Fortuna Minor. Agrippa's pair-rule gives the general nature: the Sun by day, in his dignities, solar fortune at full strength. Cattan's first house gives the detail:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/fortuna-major" />

Cattan gives good will, loyalty, prosperity, and friendship of the great, and makes it good in all things except secrecy. Heydon's parallel passage, quoted above, adds the commanding person and expands Cattan in his usual way. Cattan's chapter of qualities adds the figure's usual pace:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/fortuna-major" />

It is good for gain "where a person hath hope to winne," where real strength already exists. The tables of chapter 27 add two details worth keeping in a portrait. Fortuna Major appears among figures of covetousness as well as of life and victory, and for sickness it promises amendment "but the sickenesse will be long." Crowley keeps the core unchanged:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-major-general" />

Across three and a half centuries and two source lines, Fortuna Major's meaning is largely undisputed. The variant attributions changed its element and sign, but not its nature. A plain synthesis runs like this. Fortuna Major is strength the querent already has. It gives lasting victory to those who have a position to win from, and its one weakness is secrecy.

That is the form of a portrait, and it is what the quizzes below test. Give the structure first, then the important limits, then a plain synthesis.

<Exercise n={1} title="The Moon's pair in questions" kind="quiz">
  Via and Populus, tested as working figures; a few words each.

  1. A chart asks whether a prisoner will get out. Which of Via's qualities helps the question?
  2. A chart asks whether a partnership will stay firm. Why is Via a warning sign?
  3. Populus appears in an important house with strong neighbors. How should you read it?

<Exercise n={2} title="The Venus pair in questions" kind="quiz">

  2. Which figure is harder in questions of gain and keeping property?

  4. A merchant asks about profit and Amissio appears. Why is the same figure difficult?

  6. Write one sentence that tells a beginner when loss is beneficial.

  <ModelAnswer>

    2. Amissio.
    3. In a release question, losing the prison or losing the bond is the desired outcome.
    4. Profit asks for keeping and increase. Amissio removes, spends, or lets go.

    6. Loss helps when the question asks to be rid of something; it harms when the question asks to gain or keep.
  </ModelAnswer>

    4. Laetitia.

<Exercise n={4} title="The Saturn pair in questions" kind="quiz">

  3. A question asks whether a fortification will hold. Why can Saturn help?
  4. A question asks whether grief will lift soon. Why is Tristitia difficult?

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. Carcer.
    2. Tristitia.
    3. The question wants containment and strength against release. A figure of binding can do that work.
    4. Tristitia fixes and weighs down. The question wants relief and movement.
    5. The matter is low, hidden, and fixed in earth. Tristitia's heaviness suits that kind of matter better than a question that wants speed or joy.
    6. A Saturn figure helps when the question wants something to stay, bind, endure, contain, or be protected by a limit.
  </ModelAnswer>
