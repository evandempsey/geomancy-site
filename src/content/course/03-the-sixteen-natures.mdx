Lesson 2 gave the figures as shapes. This lesson gives their meanings: planet, sign, element, character, people signified, and rank in the order of fortune. Do not reduce a figure to one keyword. Learn the range of each figure, one planetary pair at a time.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 1 and 2: the sources and their standing, and the anatomy of the figures from memory: four lines, active and passive points, entering and going out, mobile and stable.

You will also need the reading. Cattan's chapter of qualities is the closest Renaissance source to a master table of the figures' good and ill, and the ranking section relies on it.

The figure pages ([Via](/figures/via/), [Populus](/figures/populus/) and the rest) collect the corpus quotations for each figure. Use them as the reference. This lesson gives the order in which to learn them.

## Where the characters come from

A geomantic figure gets its character from three places.

First, and above all, from its **planet**. The sources assign the sixteen figures to the seven planets and to the Moon's two nodes, the Dragon's Head and Tail. Each planet receives two figures. The pair divides the planet's nature: one figure usually carries the more favorable form, the other the more difficult form. This is why the lesson is arranged by planetary pairs.

Second, from its **element and sign**. Each figure belongs to one of the four elements and to a sign of the zodiac, and these refine the planetary character: they tell you whether the figure's nature runs hot or cold, quick or slow, and they matter technically later, when figures are compared by element and dignity. The pseudo-Agrippa derives the signs directly from the planetary rulerships, assigning each figure to a house of its own planet:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/signs" />

This is the system this site takes as its default, and the one printed at the top of every figure page: the two Fortunes in Leo, Via and Populus in Cancer, Acquisitio in Pisces and Laetitia in Sagittarius, Puella in Taurus and Amissio in Libra, Conjunctio in Virgo and Albus in Gemini, Puer and Rubeus in Scorpio, Carcer in Capricorn and Tristitia in Aquarius, the Dragon's Head with Capricorn and the Tail with Scorpio. (Note the printer's slip in the passage: "Puella and Rubeus have for their sign Scorpio" should read *Puer* and Rubeus, as the editorial note on the quotation records; Puella was assigned to Taurus a line earlier.) The elements in the course's default system follow Agrippa's "natures of the planets and figures themselves," which you will meet below in the section on variants. **The sources do not agree on the elements and signs**, and a later section takes this up at length. Cattan's assignments differ from Agrippa's, Gerard's differ from both, and the Golden Dawn rearranged everything again. This course states one system as its default (the one in the figure pages' headline attributions) and treats the others as recorded variants, because that is what they are.

Third, from its **structure**. A figure with more points above than below is entering and tends toward good; more below than above is going out and tends toward ill. The active lines also help explain the meaning. Laetitia has one active fire line and is read as joy. Tristitia is its inversion, with one active earth line, and is read as sorrow. Acquisitio and Amissio likewise oppose each other by structure. The form is a useful guide to the meaning.

Each figure also has a **mobility**: eight figures are stable, inclined to endure and to fix matters where they stand, and eight are mobile, inclined to motion, change and quick passage. The stable figures in this course's system are Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Puella, Albus, Tristitia, Carcer and Caput Draconis; the mobile are Via, Fortuna Minor, Puer, Rubeus, Amissio, Laetitia, Conjunctio and Cauda Draconis. Mobility matters most in questions of time and travel, and you will use it constantly from Lesson 5 onward.

Now the sixteen. For each pair, the lesson gives the planet's nature, how the two figures divide that nature, and a short reading of each figure. The figure pages hold the fuller source record.

## The Sun's pair: Fortuna Major and Fortuna Minor

The Sun in older astrology is the planet of life, honour, victory, kings and gold, the most fortunate of lights when well placed, and arrogant, scorching and exposed when ill. Its two figures divide the solar nature not into good and bad (both Fortunes are fortunate) but into two *kinds* of good fortune, and the pseudo-Agrippa states the division with his usual compression:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/fortuna" />

The day side and the night side: the Sun in its full dignity, and the Sun labouring at less strength. Everything else about these two figures unfolds from that single distinction.

### Fortuna Major

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 1, 1]} name="Fortuna Major" size={4} />

[Fortuna Major](/figures/fortuna-major/), the Greater Fortune (Sun, Leo, fire, stable), is by common consent the best figure in geomancy. Its shape tells its story: two passive lines above, two active below, a figure whose strength is in its foundations. This is not luck that arrives; it is power that was already there. The sources consistently read it as success that comes slowly, with effort at the start and unshakable security at the end: victory in any matter where the querent has real strength to draw on. Crowley's index entry catches the older keywords exactly:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-major-meaning" />

"Safeguard entering": protection that is on its way in and will stay. Heydon's first-house entry shows what the figure makes of a person:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/fortuna-major" />

This goodness is regal and commanding: "a high mighty man given to command, coveting to bring men into subjection to him." Its listed weakness is secrecy. Where the question concerns keeping something hidden, even this best figure is "a token of small surety." Fortuna Major usually means strong success, slow at first and firm at the end.

### Fortuna Minor

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 2, 2]} name="Fortuna Minor" size={4} />

[Fortuna Minor](/figures/fortuna-minor/), the Lesser Fortune (Sun, Leo, air, mobile), is the same Sun in its nocturnal weakness: success still, but borrowed, hasty and fading. Its shape is Fortuna Major overturned: strength worn on the outside, two active lines at the head and nothing beneath them. The sources read it as outward strength: help that comes from others, from patronage, from a burst of speed, rather than from one's own foundation. Cattan's first-house portrait is all velocity and heat:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/fortuna-minor" />

Choler, hastiness, swiftness, force, boldness, victory, and the same solar weakness: "the matter shall be disclosed." Like Fortuna Major, Fortuna Minor cannot keep a secret; unlike it, it cannot hold a position. Crowley's keywords give the contrast:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-minor-meaning" />

"Safeguard going out" against "safeguard entering": protection that does not last. Fortuna Minor is good where speed matters: a quick sale, a swift journey, a war that must be won now. It is unreliable where endurance matters. Cattan even lists it among figures of evil and loss, not because it is wholly malefic, but because what it gives is brief.

## The Moon's pair: Via and Populus

The Moon is the fastest planet and governs travel, crowds, change, and things that wax and wane. Her two figures are the two extreme shapes of the system: all single points and all double points. Agrippa derives them from her phases:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/via-populus" />

One figure is motion; the other is multitude. Neither has much fixed character of its own, which is part of its meaning.

### Via

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 1, 1]} name="Via" size={4} />

[Via](/figures/via/), the Way (Moon, Cancer, water, mobile), is one active point on every line. It signifies road, journey, and change. In its own province, travel and movement, it is excellent. In questions that need stability, it is difficult because it changes the matter. Cattan states both sides:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/via" />

A figure which "breaketh and spoileth al the goodnes of the others," strong language, and Crowley gives the same rule in nearly the same words three centuries later:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/via-general" />

Via is not simply evil. It is indifferent to the present state of things. Ask whether a sickness will change, and Via helps. Ask whether a marriage will hold, and Via is troubling. Cattan lists it as good for liberty, coming out of prison, and amendment of sickness, because in those questions change is desired. When Via appears, ask whether change helps or harms the question.

### Populus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 2, 2]} name="Populus" size={4} />

[Populus](/figures/populus/), the People (Moon, Cancer, water, stable), is the opposite extreme: every line doubled, eight points, the most passive figure in the system. It signifies a crowd or assembly. Every source repeats the same rule: Populus takes its goodness or badness from company, good with good figures and evil with evil. Heydon's first-house portrait gives the details:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/populus" />

A figure of renown, assembly, water-journeys, rain, and mud. Cattan's quality-chapter summary gives the rule you will use:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/populus" />

"Sometime good and sometime bad" is the figure's rule. Populus signifies the many: congregations, markets, armies, audiences, and public matters. In practice, read it as an amplifier that takes much of its quality from nearby figures.

## The pair of Mars: Puer and Rubeus

Mars is the lesser malefic: heat, iron, war, courage, lust and blood. Older astrology distinguishes Mars used well, as in soldier or surgeon, from Mars uncontrolled. The two geomantic figures of Mars follow that distinction, and Agrippa draws the line in eight words:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/puer-rubeus" />

Mars benevolent and Mars malevolent. One of these figures is difficult; the other is the most feared figure in the system.

### Puer

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 2, 1]} name="Puer" size={4} />

[Puer](/figures/puer/), the Boy (Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile), is Mars benevolent, which is to say Mars at its least harmful, not Mars made gentle. The figure is all single points but for the passive water line: a fighter with everything active except his depths. The old keyword is "yellow, beardless," a young man, armed, rash, and not yet wise. Heydon's first-house entry does not flatter him:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/puer" />

Clamorous, seditious, deceitful; soldiers and officers "that care neither for God nor man." And yet read on: good in love, good in war, "a meetly swiftness" for voyages, "good enough" for marriage. Crowley compresses the whole rule into a sentence:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/puer-general" />

Evil in most demands, *except those relating to war and love*. Puer harms questions that need patience, prudence, or peace, and helps questions that need boldness. A Puer marriage is passionate and unstable; a Puer lawsuit aggressive; a Puer illness feverish and fast. Cattan's Book II portrait adds feasting, banqueting with ladies, theft, and "gaine in play."

### Rubeus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 2, 2]} name="Rubeus" size={4} />

[Rubeus](/figures/rubeus/), the Red (Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile), is Mars malevolent: passion, violence, drunkenness, fraud, and bloodshed. Structurally it is the inversion of Albus, the white figure of peace. The sources agree, and they are severe. Cattan's chapter of qualities:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/rubeus" />

"Ill in all good things, and good in all ill things, and many times signifyeth death." And note the line we will return to: *she is never found in the first house to make a judgement.* Crowley's general meaning transmits the same formula:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/rubeus-general" />

That formula, evil in good and good in evil, is more precise than simple maleficence. In a prosperity question, Rubeus is disastrous. In a question about vice or deceit, it can confirm the vice asked about. Heydon's first-house entry runs simply "Rogues, Whores, Theeves, Robbers, Murderers." Some sources refuse to read the chart at all when Rubeus rises in the first house, a rule treated below. In short: Rubeus is the worst figure for an honest good, and a strong sign when the question itself concerns wrongdoing.

## The pair of Venus: Puella and Amissio

Venus is the lesser benefic: love, beauty, pleasure, music, gardens, the bonds between people. Her gifts are real and her weaknesses are her gifts carried too far: indulgence, vanity, expense. Her two figures split her into the beloved and the price paid:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/puella-amissio" />

The first is fortunate; the other is Venus "as it were retrograde, or combust": desire turning into loss.

### Puella

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 1, 1]} name="Puella" size={4} />

[Puella](/figures/puella/), the Girl (Venus, Taurus, air, stable), is Venus in her favorable form. Its nature is harmony, pleasure, and peace. Heydon's portrait gives the social and bodily details:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/puella" />

To sing and dance, to be well clothed and neat, a lover of gardens, and, the steel under the silk, "good in case of Love, and in all things but in War." Cattan's quality-chapter summary makes the rule general:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/puella" />

Very good in *all* demands, especially those of women. Puella is among the more reliable fortunate figures, but it is weak in conflict: a figure of concord does not fight well. It is also pleasure-loving and can be vain. In questions of long labor or discipline, it promises more ease than endurance. In a chart it softens what it touches; whether softening helps depends on the question.

### Amissio

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 1, 2]} name="Amissio" size={4} />

[Amissio](/figures/amissio/), Loss (Venus, Libra, fire, mobile), is desire that costs. The figure is Acquisitio overturned, and its plain meaning is loss, expense, and things leaving possession. Cattan's first-house entry is severe:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/amissio" />

Loss of inheritance and possessions, ill beginning and ill issue ("ill in all demaundes that can be propounded"), *except for prisoners*, for whom escape is the loss the prison suffers. Crowley preserves the same structure of exceptions and adds the one that makes the figure Venusian:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/amissio-general" />

"Sometimes for love": because love may involve willing loss. Amissio is the figure of transactions where something leaves: bad for gain, fitting for paying debts, giving gifts, or spending on a wedding, and good for losing what should be lost, such as sickness, captivity, or a bad habit. No figure is simply bad until the question says what its action means.

## The pair of Mercury: Conjunctio and Albus

Mercury is the planet of mind: speech, writing, commerce, calculation, science, and the connecting of one thing to another. In older astrology it is neither benefic nor malefic but convertible (coloured by its company), and both its figures inherit that conditional quality. Both are good, says Agrippa, but not equally:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/conjunctio-albus" />

The first the more fortunate: Mercury active and Mercury contemplative, the broker and the scholar.

### Conjunctio

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 1, 2]} name="Conjunctio" size={4} />

[Conjunctio](/figures/conjunctio/), Conjunction (Mercury, Virgo, air, mobile), is Mercury in motion: meeting, assembly, the joining of separate things. The shape draws its own meaning: points converging at the waist, two lines reaching toward each other. Cattan gives the figure's two great significations in one sentence:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/conjunctio" />

Good with good and ill with ill, like Populus; but where Populus merely reflects, Conjunctio *connects*: "a recoverment and restitution of thinges scattered or lost." It is the classic figure for recovering stolen goods, reuniting separated parties, making contracts and marriages (Cattan's qualities chapter calls it the best of all figures for marriage). Heydon's first-house portrait shows the mental side:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/conjunctio" />

Graceful speech, good memory, mathematics, merchandising, and many languages: Mercury's gifts are present. But Heydon's entry is conditional. Good company gives knowledge; bad company gives knavery and theft. Conjunctio joins things, but the rest of the chart decides whether the joining is helpful or harmful.

### Albus

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 1, 2]} name="Albus" size={4} />

[Albus](/figures/albus/), the White (Mercury, Gemini, earth, stable), is Mercury at rest: the contemplative mind, wisdom, peace, clear thought. Its single active point is on the water line. The sources read it as the figure of the philosopher and named it for the colour of purity and of old men's hair. Heydon gives it a strongly favorable portrait:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/albus" />

A noble person who "scornes base actions," honest to the point of being hated for it, a scholar, a philosopher, and good for profit, law-suits, messages and marriage, "but it is naught for War, and good for Peace." Cattan's quality-chapter line gives the practical core:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/albus" />

Good for hoped-for gain, and good "to have entrie into any place": beginnings and admissions. Albus is a gentle fortunate figure. It favors study, agreements, healing, and peace, and fails where force is needed. Set it against its inversion Rubeus: wisdom and peace against passion and disorder.

## The pair of Jupiter: Acquisitio and Laetitia

Jupiter is the greater benefic: abundance, justice, faith, expansion, and fortune as increase rather than victory. His two figures divide that increase between material gain and joy:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/acquisitio-laetitia" />

Jupiter the greater fortune in the first, the lesser in the second, "but without detriment," a gentle ranking inside an entirely benefic pair.

### Acquisitio

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 2, 1]} name="Acquisitio" size={4} />

[Acquisitio](/figures/acquisitio/), Gain (Jupiter, Pisces, water, stable), is the figure of obtaining: profit, acquisition, success in any matter where something is to be gotten. Generations of geomancers saw its shape as two tied money-bags, full and hanging. Cattan's first-house entry is the figure's biography:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/acquisitio" />

Goodness, happiness, quiet life, "the accomplishment of the persons owne harts desire"; voyages that depart late but return glad; good for love and marriage and "merveilous good" in church causes, a figure firm, entering and good for all things *but war*, where a figure of increase has nothing to offer. Crowley's summary keeps the essential:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/acquisitio-general" />

Generally good for profit or gain. The exceptions matter: Acquisitio is ill for coming out of prison because what it grasps, it keeps, and it can be ill in sickness questions when the thing gained is the disease. Its core action is accumulation, and whether accumulation helps depends on the question. It is usually excellent for property, business, and increase. It is also the course's main example of disputed attributions, as the variants section shows.

### Laetitia

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 2, 2]} name="Laetitia" size={4} />

[Laetitia](/figures/laetitia/), Joy (Jupiter, Sagittarius, air, mobile), is Jupiter's increase as gladness, health, and good fortune of the spirit. Its shape is one active fire point above three passive lines, so it is easy to remember as an upward figure. Heydon's portrait is strongly favorable:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/laetitia" />

Long and prosperous life, a strong and healthful body, peace, amity, honesty, good in everything except war, with one Jupiterian flaw in matters of love: the person "will promise much, and perform but little." Joy overpromises. Cattan's quality-chapter line broadens it:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/laetitia" />

Good for joy "as well present as to come," and good almost in all houses. Laetitia favors health, children, feasts, deliverance, and good news. Being mobile and airy, its fortune is quicker and lighter than Acquisitio's. The old books note it is better for spirit than purse; Cattan even lists it as ill for recovering stolen goods. Set Laetitia against its inversion, Tristitia.

## The pair of Saturn: Carcer and Tristitia

Saturn is the greater malefic: cold, dry, slow, old; the planet of limits, walls, burdens, time and grief. But Saturn's evils are the evils of *structure*, and structure has its uses: what imprisons also protects, what weighs down also holds fast. Both his figures are evil, says Agrippa, but not equally:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/carcer-tristitia" />

The first (Carcer) of the greater detriment. The prison is worse than the sorrow, because sorrow can move.

### Carcer

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 2, 2, 1]} name="Carcer" size={4} />

[Carcer](/figures/carcer/), the Prison (Saturn, Capricorn, earth, stable), is enclosure itself: the figure's points form a closed ring, active lines at head and feet sealing two passive lines inside. It signifies restriction, delay, binding, imprisonment in every literal and figurative sense. Cattan's first-house entry is a study in Saturnine misery:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/carcer" />

Melancholy, heaviness, envy, anger, "great paine and travaile," ill in all things *except fortification*: "rampiers, forts, and other fortifications of Townes, and to defende them well." The exception is the figure's soul. A wall is evil to the one outside it and salvation to the one behind it. Crowley's one-line summary:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/carcer-general" />

Delay, binding, stay, bar, restriction: in a question of progress, the worst news short of Rubeus; in a question of keeping (will my tenant stay? will the secret hold? will the fortress stand?), a grim kind of yes. Carcer answers every question with *nothing moves*, and the reader must decide who profits from immobility. It is also the figure that breaks the entering-and-exiting rule of structure: with equal points above and below it should be mean, but Cattan excepts it by name: the prison is not neutral. The human type: the jailer and the jailed, the miser, the solitary, the keeper of walls. Slow, hard, lasting: everything it gives, it gives late and keeps long.

### Tristitia

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 2, 2, 1]} name="Tristitia" size={4} />

[Tristitia](/figures/tristitia/), Sorrow (Saturn, Aquarius, earth, stable), is grief: the single active point on the earth line, everything above it passive and pressing down. The older images are a stake driven into the ground and a tower fallen; it is Laetitia overturned, joy inverted into mourning. Heydon's portrait does not spare the reader:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/tristitia" />

Troubles, melancholy perturbations of mind and body, an ill beginning, and then, characteristically, the Saturnine exceptions: good "to search treasure in the Earth," good "in matters of Fortifications and buildings." Sorrow digs; what goes down can also found. Crowley's summary is the shortest in his handbook:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/tristitia-general" />

Evil in almost all things, but the "almost" matters. In questions of building, land, mines, buried things, and anything that must endure underground, Tristitia's fixity can help. Cattan even lists it among figures good for the year and for gain. Treat those as earthy exceptions, not the figure's general character. Its usual meanings are grief, condemnation, and descent.

## The Dragon's Head and Tail

The last pair belongs to no planet. Caput Draconis and Cauda Draconis are assigned to the nodes of the Moon, the two points where the Moon's path crosses the Sun's, where eclipses happen: the Dragon's Head in the north, where the Moon climbs, and the Dragon's Tail in the south, where she descends. In astrology the Head increases and the Tail diminishes; the geomantic figures inherit exactly that. Agrippa's note on the pair is a single, almost dismissive line:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/dragons-head-tail" />

They "follow their own natures": the Head swallowing in, the Tail casting out. Both are threshold figures, and both take their moral colour from their company more than any figures except Populus and Conjunctio: good with good and evil with evil, an entrance and an exit.

### Caput Draconis

<FigureGlyph dots={[2, 1, 1, 1]} name="Caput Draconis" size={4} />

[Caput Draconis](/figures/caput-draconis/), the Dragon's Head (the North Node, Capricorn, earth, stable), is the upper threshold: the doorway in. Its shape is all activity below a closed head, a figure stepping over a sill. It signifies beginnings, entrances, and the increase of whatever it joins. Heydon's first-house entry is generous:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/caput-draconis" />

Honours, riches, favours of great persons, "good for all things that ye can demand," except war, for a threshold is where you welcome, not where you fight; and good for marriage, "but it will be long in doing," since thresholds are crossed slowly. Crowley preserves the conditional rule that the older sources attach to both nodes:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/caput-draconis-general" />

Good with good, evil with evil, but with "a good issue for gain," because the Head tends toward increase. In practice Caput Draconis is usually fortunate but depends partly on company. It favors beginnings: new ventures, foundations, plantings, contracts, building houses, and laboring the earth.

### Cauda Draconis

<FigureGlyph dots={[1, 1, 1, 2]} name="Cauda Draconis" size={4} />

[Cauda Draconis](/figures/cauda-draconis/), the Dragon's Tail (the South Node, Scorpio, water, mobile), is the way out. It is the Head overturned. Where the Head begins and increases, the Tail ends and diminishes. Cattan's quality-chapter summary gives the rule:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/cauda-draconis" />

"Very good with the ill, and very ill with the good" (note the chiasmus against the Head), good "in matter of losse... and to passe out of an affaire," and otherwise a robber on every road. Crowley transmits it intact:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/cauda-draconis-general" />

Good for loss and for passing out of an affair: if the question is how to end something, leave a post, dissolve a partnership, or escape danger, the Tail can help. For anything you want to keep or begin, it is among the worst figures. Where Rubeus corrupts, Cauda terminates. When the Tail appears, ask whether the querent wanted an ending.

That is the alphabet entire. The rest of this lesson, and the rest of the course, assumes these sixteen portraits are becoming reflexes.

## When the sources disagree

You have now met, in passing, several moments where a quotation's attributions did not match this site's headline attributions: Cattan calling Acquisitio a figure of the air in Aries while the figure page says water and Pisces, or placing Amissio in the sign of Scorpio where the default says Libra. These mismatches are not errors in the site or the source record. **The sources genuinely disagree about the elements and signs of the figures, and the disagreement is part of what you are learning.**

The planetary attributions are nearly unanimous: every source in this corpus gives the two Fortunes to the Sun, Via and Populus to the Moon, and so on through the seven pairs, with only the nodes attracting dissent (Cattan, in his first-house chapter, makes the Head a figure "of ♃ and ♀ according to the opinion of some men: but by mine opinion of ♃ and ☿," Jupiter and Mercury, and gives the Tail to Mars and Saturn, where later usage simply says North and South Node). Hear him weighing the opinions himself:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/caput-draconis" />

That sentence (*some men say, but by mine opinion*) shows how the sources think: the Renaissance authors were not transmitting a fixed revelation but comparing systems and choosing.

The elements and signs are where the systems multiply. The pseudo-Agrippa himself reports more than one. He gives a "vulgar order" of the triplicities, and then immediately offers a better one of his own:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/triplicities" />

This second arrangement (fiery Fortuna Major, Rubeus, Puer and Amissio; airy Fortuna Minor, Puella, Laetitia and Conjunctio; watery Acquisitio, Cauda Draconis, Via and Populus; earthy Carcer, Tristitia, Albus and Caput Draconis) is the elemental system this site takes as its default, because it is derived, as Agrippa says, "according to the natures of the planets, and figures themselves." And for the signs, alongside the planetary-house system you met earlier, Agrippa also records a wholly different distribution that gives each sign of the zodiac roughly one figure:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/zodiacal-distribution" />

Acquisitio to Aries, Laetitia to Gemini, Albus to Leo, Via to Virgo, almost nothing in common with the default system. Gerard of Cremona's astronomical geomancy uses yet another set, the signs placed in the ascendant for each figure; Cattan's Book II attributions differ again; and the Golden Dawn, whose system Crowley transmits, rebuilt the signs from the planetary rulerships with different choices about which planet's two houses go to which figure.

Take **Acquisitio** as the concrete case: it is the most disputed figure on this site, and Exercise 6 will quiz you on it. The course default says Jupiter, Pisces, water. The variants recorded on [its figure page](/figures/acquisitio/): Cattan makes it air, in Aries; the "vulgar order" reported by Agrippa also makes it air; Agrippa's alternative zodiacal distribution and Gerard both give it Aries; and the Golden Dawn gives it Sagittarius and fire. One figure, three elements, three signs, and each source is "right" within its own system. **Albus** runs the same way: earth in Gemini by the course default, water in Cancer for Cattan and the vulgar triplicities, Leo in Agrippa's alternative distribution, Cancer for Gerard, air in Gemini for the Golden Dawn. Even **Fortuna Major**, the most stable figure in meaning, is earth and Aquarius for Cattan, Aquarius again in Gerard's ascendants, Taurus in Agrippa's alternative distribution, and fire in Leo for the default and the Golden Dawn.

What should a student do with this? Three things. First, the reason it happens: elements and signs are derived layers, reasoned out from the planets and the shapes by different methods, and different methods give different results; the figures' meanings, which all sources broadly share, came first. Second, the disagreement almost never touches the reading: Cattan's Acquisitio in Aries and Crowley's in Sagittarius both mean *gain*. Third, course policy: **pick one system and be absolutely consistent with it.** This course, and this site's figure pages, use one default: the planetary attributions and the element-and-sign system stated at the top of each figure page. Every technique taught later that uses elements or signs assumes that system. The variants are recorded on each figure's page, with their sources, because flattening them would misstate the sources. A working geomancer who mixes systems mid-chart is not being broad-minded; he is contradicting himself. The disagreement is real. Incoherence is optional.

## The order of fortune

The portraits gave each figure its character; the working reader also needs them in *rank*, because half of geomantic reading is the simple question: is this figure good or ill for this matter? Cattan's chapter of qualities (your assigned reading) opens with a structural rule you already possess from Lesson 2, now made moral:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

Entering figures good, exiting figures evil, mean figures mean, with three exceptions Cattan names: Tristitia (entering but evil), Laetitia (exiting but good), Carcer (mean in shape, but not in nature). Read alongside the chapter's long tables of figures good for love, for war, for voyages, for marriage, and alongside the figure-by-figure summaries you have been quoting all lesson, a stable order emerges, the course's working ranking, from best to worst:

1. **Fortuna Major**: good in all things; the best figure in the system.
2. **Acquisitio**: Jupiter the greater fortune; good for nearly everything that can be gained.
3. **Caput Draconis**: good in almost all demands, conditional on company.
4. **Laetitia**: good almost in all houses; Jupiter "without detriment."
5. **Puella**: very good in all demands, weakest in conflict.
6. **Albus**: gentle, wise, gainful; useless in war.
7. **Conjunctio**: good with good, ill with ill; the best conditional figure.
8. **Populus**: pure mirror; takes everything from its company.
9. **Via**: breaks the goodness of others; excellent in its own province of movement.
10. **Fortuna Minor**: real but fading good; appears in Cattan's evil lists for its instability.
11. **Puer**: evil save in war and love.
12. **Amissio**: evil save where loss is the goal.
13. **Tristitia**: evil save in the deep and the buried.
14. **Carcer**: evil, "of the greater detrement" of Saturn's two; binds everything.
15. **Cauda Draconis**: ruin and rupture; good only for endings.
16. **Rubeus**: the worst: vice, violence, and the figure that stops the chart.

The ranking is firm at the ends and looser in the middle. The sources agree that Fortuna Major is best and that Rubeus and Cauda Draconis are among the worst. The middle depends more on question and context: Laetitia may outrank Caput Draconis in some benefic questions, Tristitia may rise in matters of land or buried things, and Fortuna Minor rises when speed is the main need. The order of fortune is a default, not a substitute for the question.

## The figure that stops the chart

One rule remains, and it concerns the bottom of the ranking. Crowley's handbook, transmitting the practice of the Golden Dawn, gives an instruction found nowhere else in this corpus in so many words:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/destroy-rule" />

Destroy it instantly; wait two hours; cast again. Taken as written, this is a Golden Dawn rule, and you should learn it as one: the mechanical formula (both figures, instant destruction, the two-hour quarantine) appears in no Renaissance source on this site. But the kernel of it is far older, and you have already met it twice. Cattan reports that the "Egyptians and a great part of the Latines" held that a chart with Rubeus in the first house "should not be judged. But that figure must be broken and make an other," and he sides with them, on experience; his chapter of qualities states flatly that Rubeus "is never found in the first house to make a judgement." For Cauda Draconis he reports the same opinion with less conviction. And Heydon, a century later, knows the custom and rejects it:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/cauda-draconis" />

Some old writers, he reports, would not have this figure or Rubeus judged in the first house: "but I am of the opinion that the Judgment herein should not be delayd." Read it, says Heydon; it simply means the matter is very bad. So the source lines divide cleanly. One line (Cattan's Egyptians and Latins, Cattan himself, and the Golden Dawn after him) treats Rubeus rising (and, for the Golden Dawn, Cauda too) as a corrupted casting: the chart is discarded. The other (Cattan's Indians, Chaldeans and Hebrews, and Heydon) treats it as a terrible answer, which is still an answer. This course follows the first line as practice (when you begin casting in Lesson 4, a chart with Rubeus or Cauda Draconis in the first house goes in the journal, marked unjudged, and the question rests for a time), while expecting you to know, as a matter of learning, that the rule is source-specific and that Heydon's dissent is on the record. A geomancer should know which habits come from the broad source set and which come from one source line.

## Worked example

The exercises will quiz you on portraits like these: readings that gather what the sources say about one figure, weigh them, and end with the figure's character in plain words. Here is the method performed twice, on the worst figure and the best.

### Rubeus, read across four sources

Begin always with the structural facts, because they anchor everything: Rubeus, "the Red," points 2-1-2-2, the single active point on the air line; Mars, Scorpio, fire, mobile in the course system; counterpart and inversion of Albus. Then let the sources speak in source order, oldest rule first.

The pseudo-Agrippa, you recall from the Mars pair, needs only a line: Puer has Mars benevolent, Rubeus malevolent. That is the genus. For the species, Cattan (and here we take his fullest statement, the first-house chapter rather than the quality tables already quoted in the portrait):

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/rubeus" />

This passage does three things at once. It catalogues the figure's significations: craft, fraud, treason, war, discord, "spilling and shedding of bloud... all the wickednesse in the world." It reveals that the source set was already plural in antiquity: Indians, Chaldeans and Hebrews reading the figure, Egyptians and Latins breaking the chart. And it shows Cattan reasoning like a practitioner, not a copyist: he sides with the Egyptians "by experience." Heydon, normally an expansive writer, is driven to his tersest entry in the whole first-house chapter:

<Quote id="heydon/house-01/rubeus" />

"A deadly figure, and not fit to be judged," though remember that the same Heydon, under Cauda Draconis two pages later, argues against delaying the reading; even within one author the rule is under tension. Last, the modern comparison. Crowley's general rule, which you met in the portrait and which belongs in its place in the evidence:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/rubeus-general" />

and, in his chapter of method, the destroy-rule you have just studied: the Golden Dawn hardening Cattan's practice into a mechanism, and extending it to Cauda Draconis.

Now weigh. The sources agree that Rubeus is the malevolent form of Mars and signifies vice, violence, and deceit. It is evil in honest demands and useful only when the question itself concerns evil, because then it confirms and describes the vice. Where the sources differ on whether to read or destroy the chart, record the divergence, choose your practice, and do not call that choice the whole source record. A plain synthesis would be: Rubeus signifies uncontrolled passion and vice; it harms honest questions, describes dishonest ones, and one strong source line refuses to read the chart when it rises in the first house.

### Fortuna Major, read the same way

The same method on the opposite pole. Structure: 2-2-1-1, the active points below, strength in the foundation; Sun, Leo, fire, stable; counterpart Fortuna Minor. Agrippa's pair-rule gives the genus: the Sun diurnal, in his dignities, solar fortune at full strength. Cattan's first house gives the species:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-01/fortuna-major" />

Good will, loyalty, prosperity, friendship of the great, and good in all things, with the single exception of secrecy. Heydon's parallel passage, quoted above, adds the commanding person and expands Cattan in his usual way. Cattan's chapter of qualities adds the figure's usual tempo:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/fortuna-major" />

Good for gain "where a person hath hope to winne," where real strength already exists. And the tables of chapter 27 fill in the fine grain, including the surprises a portrait should mention: Fortuna Major appears not only among the figures of life, loyalty, peace and victory, but also among figures of *covetousness*, and for sickness it promises amendment "but the sickenesse will be long." Even its healing is slow. Crowley, finally, transmits the core unchanged:

<Quote id="crowley/figures/fortuna-major-general" />

Note what the comparison yields: across three and a half centuries and two lineages, Fortuna Major's meaning is essentially undisputed; the variant attributions touched its element and sign (earth and Aquarius for Cattan, Aquarius for Gerard, fire and Leo here), but never its nature. A closing synthesis in your own words might run: Fortuna Major is strength that was already in the house before the question was asked; it gives victory to those with a position to win from, slowly, openly, and for keeps, and the only thing it cannot do is keep quiet.

That is the form of a portrait, and the form the quizzes below test: structure first, sources in order with citations, agreement weighed against divergence, synthesis last, in plain words.
