Geomancy has an alphabet of sixteen letters, and you must know them cold before you can read a chart or even cast one. This lesson teaches them not as symbols but as objects: what a geomantic figure *is*, before anyone has said a word about what it means. The meanings wait for Lesson 3.

What it is, is this: four lines, stacked one above another, each line holding either one point or two. Nothing else: no third state, no fifth line. Four lines of two states each yield exactly sixteen figures, no more, and the whole art rests on them. Geomancy is a combinatorial art, a complete, closed system of binary objects worked out in sand and ink centuries before anyone spoke of binary anything.

A figure's structure is the reason its meanings are what they are. When Cattan tells you that a six-pointed figure in the first house signifies six hours, six days, six months or six years, he is reading structure. When the tradition insists that the Judge of a chart must be an even figure or the whole chart is false, it is reading structure.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lesson 1. If any of it has gone soft, return there first.

The assigned reading comes first. Crowley's chapter of attributions is admitted, as always, as a modern comparison: its tables descend from the Golden Dawn's systematization of the art in the late nineteenth century, and where this lesson leans on them it says so. Cattan's chapter of qualities (Book III, chapter 27) is the older and weightier text. Most of it concerns meanings you are not yet equipped to use; what matters here is its opening paragraph, where Cattan divides the figures by the disposition of their points, and its closing prose, where he counts points to measure a life. The site's [table of the sixteen figures](/figures/) sets out every column this lesson teaches except the meanings.

## The anatomy of a figure

A geomantic figure is four lines (in the older English texts, four "ranks" or rows of points), stacked vertically. The lines are counted from the top: first, second, third, fourth. Each line contains either a single point or a pair of points, and the pair is drawn spread apart so that the eye can never confuse the two states. That is the entire anatomy. A figure is a column of four binary choices.

Why one point or two, and never three or none? This comes from how the lines are made (the full casting procedure belongs to Lesson 4). To generate one line of a figure, the geomancer makes a row of points (in sand, on paper, with a wand or a pen) rapidly and without counting, while holding the question in mind. The row might contain nine points or fourteen or twenty-three; the number is deliberately not controlled. Then the points are counted off in pairs. If a point remains unpaired at the end, the line is *odd*, and is recorded as a single point. If the points pair off exactly, the line is *even*, and is recorded as two points. The mark you write down is not the row itself but the row's *parity*: the one fact about the row that survives when everything accidental has been cancelled away. A row is either odd or even, so a line is single or double, and nothing else can appear. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa describing exactly this, in the passage on projecting the figures:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-mothers" />

The phrase that matters is "according to the even or uneven marking every several line." This is the engine of the whole art. Geomancy does not divine from the points the hand sets down; it divines from what remains of them after the pairing, from parity, the irreducible residue of a random act. The four lines are four such residues, taken one after another, and the figure is their stack.

The two states of a line have names, traditional and modern. The old texts say what Agrippa says: a line is *uneven* (one point) or *even* (two points). Modern writers say *active* for the single point and *passive* for the double; this course adopts the terms, with the caution that they are interpretive glosses, not source vocabulary. The intuition behind the gloss: a single point stands alone, unresolved, charged, pushing outward; a pair of points is settled, completed, at rest. You will see in Lesson 3 how far the tradition's actual meanings bear that intuition out, and where they ignore it.

The four positions also carry associations. The historical texts in this library speak positionally and spatially: Cattan, in the chapter you have read, divides figures by whether they have "more pointes on high" or "more pointes below" (the top and the bottom of the figure) and Crowley's table calls Caput Draconis "the upper threshold" and Cauda Draconis "the threshold lower." The top of a figure is its head, the bottom its feet; that much is implicit everywhere in the tradition's language of entering and going out, rising and descending. The Golden Dawn school went further and assigned each line an element (fire to the first line, air to the second, water to the third, earth to the fourth) making the figure a ladder from the subtlest element down to the densest. That elemental ladder is a systematization you will not find spelled out in Cattan or Agrippa, and this course flags it accordingly; but it is coherent, it is now standard, and this site's own glyphs and data follow it. A later section returns to it.

A word on drawing. The figures are drawn from the top line downward, and by old custom the points within each row are set down from right to left, a habit inherited from the Arabic practice in sand, which Agrippa preserves in the passage above ("from the right hand towards the left"). For a finished figure the direction no longer shows: what survives on the page is one dot centered in the line, or two dots spread apart. Practice drawing a few figures now, large and deliberate. A single point should sit in the middle of the line; a double should straddle it symmetrically. Sloppy figures cause real misreadings: a cramped double taken for a single changes one figure into another, and in a chart that error propagates.

Throughout this course, a figure's pattern will sometimes be written in a compact notation: the four lines from top to bottom, single as 1 and double as 2. Acquisitio is 2·1·2·1; Puella is 1·2·1·1; Via is 1·1·1·1. This notation is the site's, not the tradition's (the old books drew dots and nothing but dots) but it is handy for checking your work, and it matches the data tables that drive this site's figure pages.

## Sixteen and only sixteen

How many figures can the rule produce? Each line is one of two states, and there are four lines, so the count is two multiplied by itself four times: sixteen. The first line can be single or double: two possibilities. For each of those, the second line can be single or double, giving four. The third doubles the count again, to eight, and the fourth doubles it once more: sixteen, exactly, with every combination realized and named. There is no seventeenth figure, and there is no combination of four binary lines that lacks a name. The system is *complete* in the strict sense: whatever parities the casting produces, the result is one of the sixteen, every time.

Geomancy's sixteen are not a canon that grew but a space that was exhausted: the medieval texts enumerate the figures, they do not curate them. Because the set is closed, the art could build tables (of witnesses and judges, of figure against figure) that cover every possible case, as Cattan's and Heydon's great tables do.

The order of a figure-list is a convention, and the sources do not agree on one. Crowley's chapter runs through the zodiac, because the Golden Dawn organized everything zodiacally. Cattan groups the figures by planet. The order above is this site's own, chosen so that structural relatives sit side by side: Via next to Populus, each Fortune beside the other, Acquisitio beside Amissio, and so on down the column. Whatever order you meet them in, the names are stable across the whole tradition, and they are Latin, with an English gloss alongside. Caput Draconis and Cauda Draconis, the Dragon's Head and Tail, are the old names for the lunar nodes, the crossing-points of the Moon's path with the Sun's.

Many of the names arrive already paired: Gain and Loss; Joy and Sorrow; Boy and Girl; White and Red; Greater and Lesser Fortune; Head and Tail. The pairing is no accident: the pairs in the words correspond to pairs in the dots.

## Counting points

Add up the points in a figure (one for each single line, two for each double) and you get its point total. The smallest possible total is four: every line single, which is Via, 1·1·1·1, four points in a vertical stroke down the page. The largest is eight: every line double, which is Populus, 2·2·2·2, a full double column. Every other figure falls between. A figure's total is fixed by how many of its four lines are double: no doubles gives four points, one double gives five, two doubles six, three doubles seven, four doubles eight. Count the ways of choosing which lines are double and you have counted the figures in each class. There is one figure with no double lines (Via) and one with four (Populus). There are four figures with exactly one double line (Caput Draconis, Puella, Puer, and Cauda Draconis, the five-point figures) because the lone double can sit in any of four positions. Symmetrically, there are four figures with exactly one *single* line: Laetitia, Rubeus, Albus, and Tristitia, the seven-point figures. And there are six figures with two doubles and two singles, the six-point figures: Fortuna Major, Fortuna Minor, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer, because two positions can be chosen out of four in six ways. One, four, six, four, one: sixteen.

The totals matter to the tradition. You have already met one use in the assigned reading: at the close of his chapter of qualities, Cattan measures out a life from a point-count, telling you that a figure of six points found in the first house "signifyeth that a man shall live sixe houres, sixe dayes, sixe moneths, sixe yeares, or 72," and that a five-point figure gives five of each, "and likewise of other figures according to the number of the points which they have" (Cattan, *The Geomancie*, Book III, ch. 27). Whatever you make of that as biography, it presupposes that the point total is a property a geomancer reads as fluently as the figure's name. Timing techniques throughout the tradition lean on these numbers.

But the deepest use of the totals is coarser than the number itself: it is the number's parity. Eight of the sixteen figures have an even total: Via with four; Fortuna Major, Fortuna Minor, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, and Carcer with six; Populus with eight. The other eight are odd: the four five-point figures and the four seven-point figures. This split into even and odd halves governs the validity of every chart, through a rule you will meet in full in Lesson 4. At the summit of a geomantic chart stand two figures called the Witnesses, and from them is derived a final figure called the Judge, which delivers the verdict on the whole question. And of the Judge, Cattan says this:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-04-of-the-judge-chap" />

"The which judge ought alwayes of necessitie to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false." Only the eight even figures can ever stand as Judge; if an odd figure appears in that place, the chart has been miscast and must be done again. The rule is the geomancer's checksum, an error-detecting code built into the art four hundred years before anyone coined the term. Why the Judge *must* come out even (why the arithmetic of the chart makes an odd Judge impossible rather than merely forbidden) is a short proof, deferred to Lesson 4, where you will have the chart-construction in front of you. For now, learn the eight even figures as a set: Via, Populus, the two Fortunes, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer.

## Entering, exiting, and the doctrine of stability

What matters now is not how many points a figure has but where they sit: whether its weight hangs in the head or the feet. The tradition considered exactly this. At the opening of the chapter of qualities you read for this lesson, Cattan lays down a rule for sorting the whole alphabet by the disposition of its points:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

The figure's upper half (the first and second lines) weighs against its lower half (the third and fourth). Figures with more points above than below are "entring in," and Cattan calls them good; figures with more points below than above are "going out," and evil; figures equally weighted are "meanes," middling. Each class carries one exception, which Cattan names: Tristitia is top-heavy but not good, Laetitia is bottom-heavy but not evil, and Carcer is balanced but not middling. The exceptions are about goodness, not geometry; the sorting itself is pure arithmetic. Five figures are top-heavy, entering: Fortuna Major (four points above, two below), Tristitia (four above, three below), Albus (four above, three below), Puella (three above, two below), and Caput Draconis (three above, two below). Five are bottom-heavy, exiting: Fortuna Minor, Laetitia, Rubeus, Puer, and Cauda Draconis, each the top-to-bottom reflection of one of the first five. Six are balanced: Via, Populus, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer, which are, you will notice, six of the eight even figures, and the only figures whose halves *can* balance.

The image behind the words is the figure as a body standing at a threshold. A figure heavy in the head leans in toward you, arriving, bringing its matter with it; a figure heavy in the feet is already turning away, leaving, taking its matter out the door. Crowley's table preserves the same picture in its names for the two nodes: Caput Draconis is "the upper threshold," the entering one, and Cauda Draconis "the threshold lower, or going out," visible in the dots: Caput (2·1·1·1) carries its double at the head and Cauda (1·1·1·2) drags its double at the feet.

This old doctrine lines up with the last column of the master table, the one this site's figure pages call *mobility*. Eight figures are classed as stable: Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Tristitia, Puella, Albus, Carcer, Caput Draconis. Eight are mobile: Via, Fortuna Minor, Amissio, Laetitia, Puer, Rubeus, Conjunctio, Cauda Draconis. The stable/mobile division is how the later tradition speaks of what Cattan's entering/exiting doctrine gropes toward, and the two line up neatly: every one of the five top-heavy, entering figures is stable, and every one of the five bottom-heavy, exiting figures is mobile. A figure whose weight is in its head stands; a figure whose weight is in its feet walks. Only the six balanced figures are left for the doctrine to decide case by case, and there the tradition split them by nature rather than by geometry: Populus, Acquisitio and Carcer are stable (the crowd that stays, the gain that is kept, the prison that holds) while Via, Amissio and Conjunctio are mobile: the road, the loss that departs, the meeting that disperses.

What does the classification do in practice? In judgment (this is anticipating Lesson 3 and beyond, but the structural point belongs here) a stable figure signifies a matter that endures, settles, resists change: slow to arrive, slow to leave, fixed in its place. A mobile figure signifies a matter in motion: quick, transient, apt to change or escape. The same quality is good or bad depending entirely on the question. Ask whether a sickness will pass, and you want mobility; ask whether a marriage will hold, and you want stability; ask after a prisoner, and Carcer's immovable balance is the worst possible news. Even Cattan's blunt sorting of entering figures as "good" and exiting as "evill" is best read this way: a matter arriving is, for most questions, better news than a matter departing. You will learn in Lesson 3 to refine that bluntness figure by figure; for now, learn the two eights as firmly as you learned the even and the odd. The two divisions cut across each other: even Amissio is mobile while even Acquisitio is stable; odd Laetitia is mobile while odd Tristitia is stable. Parity and mobility are independent coordinates, and a figure is located by both.

## Mirrors and counterparts

The sixteen figures form a structure riddled with symmetry. Two operations generate the symmetries that matter, and both are simple enough to do in your head.

The first is *inversion*: turn the figure upside down, so that the first line becomes the fourth and the second becomes the third. In the compact notation, reverse the string. Invert Fortuna Major, 2·2·1·1, and you get 1·1·2·2, which is Fortuna Minor. Invert Laetitia, 1·2·2·2, and you get 2·2·2·1, which is Tristitia. Invert Puer, 1·1·2·1, and you get 1·2·1·1, which is Puella. Invert Albus and you get Rubeus; invert Caput Draconis and you get Cauda Draconis; invert Acquisitio, 2·1·2·1, and you get 1·2·1·2, which is Amissio. Six pairs of figures exchange identities under inversion. The remaining four figures are palindromes (they read the same downward as upward) and so inversion leaves them exactly where they stood: Via (1·1·1·1), Populus (2·2·2·2), Conjunctio (2·1·1·2), and Carcer (1·2·2·1). The four self-inverse figures are all even, and not by accident: a palindrome's halves weigh the same, so its total is twice its upper half.

Inversion flips the names, too. Greater Fortune flips into Lesser Fortune. Joy flips into Sorrow. Boy into Girl, White into Red, the Dragon's Head into the Dragon's Tail, Gain into Loss. The rhyming pairs you noticed in the names are precisely the inversion pairs in the dots. The tradition's semantic opposites are the geometry's mirror images. Whoever first named these figures (and the names were fixed before the Latin tradition began) named them structurally. The site's figure data records this pairing under the name *counterpart*, and on this site the counterpart of every figure is exactly its inversion: each of the six mirror-pairs is a counterpart-pair, and each of the four palindromes is its own counterpart. When a figure page tells you that the counterpart of Albus is Rubeus, it is telling you something you can verify by turning Albus upside down.

The second operation is *complementation*: leave the figure right side up, but exchange the state of every line, so that every single becomes a double, every double a single. In the notation, swap every 1 for 2 and every 2 for 1. The complement of Via is Populus: the all-single figure and the all-double figure, each the other's photographic negative. The complement of Puer (1·1·2·1) is 2·2·1·2, which is Albus, not Puella. The complement of Laetitia (1·2·2·2) is 2·1·1·1, which is Caput Draconis, not Tristitia. The complement of Conjunctio (2·1·1·2) is 1·2·2·1, which is Carcer, and so the two figures that inversion leaves alone are swept into a pair by complementation. Indeed complementation can leave nothing alone, since no line can equal its own opposite; it sorts all sixteen figures into eight clean pairs: Via with Populus, Fortuna Major with Fortuna Minor, Acquisitio with Amissio, Laetitia with Caput Draconis, Tristitia with Cauda Draconis, Puer with Albus, Puella with Rubeus, Conjunctio with Carcer. A complement-pair's totals always sum to twelve (each line contributes one point to one figure and two to the other) which is why four-point Via partners eight-point Populus and the five-point figures partner the seven-point ones.

For most figures the two operations disagree: inversion sends Puer to Puella, complementation sends him to Albus. But for two pairs the operations coincide, and they are the two pairs at the commercial heart of the alphabet: the Fortunes, and Acquisitio with Amissio. Invert Acquisitio or complement it, and either way you get Amissio. Gain and loss are opposites twice over, upside down *and* inside out. The meanings match the geometry. ## The elemental reading of the lines

So far the four lines have been mere positions: first, second, third, fourth. The Golden Dawn school (whose tables you read in Crowley's chapter) gave them elements, and the assignment runs down the ladder of subtlety: the first line is fire, the second air, the third water, the fourth earth. The caution from earlier holds: nothing in Cattan or the pseudo-Agrippa assigns fire to the top line of a figure. The line-elements are a systematization, imposed on the art in the nineteenth century by people who wanted geomancy to interlock with their alchemy, their tarot and their Qabalah. But it is a *good* systematization, tidy, expressive, consistent with the tradition's spatial instincts (the figure's fiery head above, its earthy feet below), and it has become the lingua franca of modern practice. This site adopts it as its glyph convention: when a figure's dots are given as 2·1·2·1, the four numbers are its fire, air, water and earth lines, in that order.

The fire line, at the head, is the figure's spirit and will: drive, intention, the matter's animating push. The air line, second, is its mind and speech: thought, communication, the social and intellectual weather. The water line, third, is its heart: emotion, appetite, the currents of feeling. The earth line, at the feet, is its body: matter, money, the physical ground of the affair and its lasting result. A single point in a line is read as that element *active* in the figure: present, expressed, discharging itself outward. A double point is the element *latent*: settled, contained, withheld from expression. Again, "active" and "latent" are modern interpretive vocabulary, not source diction; the whole apparatus is a disciplined mnemonic, no more.

The apparatus organizes the alphabet well. Consider the four seven-point figures, each with exactly one single line. The single line names the figure's one active element, and the four figures take the four elements in turn. Laetitia, 1·2·2·2, is active in fire alone: one point flying at the head of three settled pairs, pure upward will, and the figure means joy. Rubeus, 2·1·2·2, is active in air alone, and means the boiling over of passion and speech, the red figure. Albus, 2·2·1·2, is active in water alone, and means the white stillness of wisdom and peace. Tristitia, 2·2·2·1, is active in earth alone: a single point sunk at the feet of the figure, all its life driven downward, and it means sorrow, but also the fixity of foundations, which is why Cattan's chapter can list so dour a figure among those good "for the yeare" and for matters of earth and permanence. The four five-point figures run the same wheel in negative: each has exactly one *double* line, one latent element among three active. Caput Draconis is latent in fire, Puella in air, Puer in water, Cauda Draconis in earth: Puer all act and no reflection in the heart, Puella all grace with thought held in reserve. The six six-point figures balance two active lines against two latent, in all six possible distributions: Fortuna Major active below (water and earth, strength founded and possessed), Fortuna Minor active above (fire and air, strength spent as fast as it shines), Acquisitio active in air and earth, Amissio active in fire and water, Conjunctio active in the middle lines, Carcer active at the extremes, its fire and earth sealed around a latent heart and mind, the prison's walls.

The line-elements are distinct from a second elemental doctrine, one that *is* in the old sources: the assignment of a whole figure to an element. Cattan does this throughout (you saw him close his note on figure-qualities with the remark that for the fruitfulness of the year "the figures entring into the elements of water and earth be good, and those which be of fire and ayre be ill") and his per-figure chapters say things like "of the element of the fire" of Amissio. The pseudo-Agrippa arranges the sixteen into four elemental triplicities:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/triplicities" />

Crowley's table carries an element column of its own, derived from each figure's zodiacal sign; and it disagrees with Agrippa and Cattan as often as not, assigning Acquisitio to fire by way of Sagittarius where the older scheme gives it to water by way of Pisces. The disagreements are real and the site's figure pages record every variant; do not paper over them. For this course: the *figure-element* is an attribution, a matter of testimony, on which the sources differ and must be cited; the *line-elements* are a fixed modern convention, the same in every book that uses them at all, because they follow the geometry. When this course says "Amissio's fire line is single," that is a statement about dots, checkable by counting. When it says "Amissio is a figure of fire," that is Cattan's testimony, and another source may say otherwise.

## Worked example

Now every tool from this lesson, on the type-case pair: Acquisitio and Amissio.

<FigureGlyph dots={[2,1,2,1]} name="Acquisitio" size={4} /> <FigureGlyph dots={[1,2,1,2]} name="Amissio" size={4} />

In notation, Acquisitio is 2·1·2·1: double fire, single air, double water, single earth. Amissio is 1·2·1·2: single fire, double air, single water, double earth. Stacked one above the other, the two strings disagree in every line. Where Acquisitio is double, Amissio is single; where Acquisitio is single, Amissio is double. They are complements. Reversed, Acquisitio's string 2·1·2·1 becomes 1·2·1·2, Amissio again. They are also inversions of each other, one of only two pairs in the alphabet (with the Fortunes) for which the two mirror-operations agree. Gain and loss are each other's reflection in both mirrors: perfect opposites.

Count the points. Acquisitio: two, one, two, one, giving six. Amissio: one, two, one, two, giving six. Both even, both therefore members of the eight-figure family from which alone a Judge can come; when either stands at the foot of a chart as Judge, the chart is at least validly cast. Their evenness has the same source: each figure alternates single and double down its four lines, the only two figures in the alphabet that do, and an alternating figure must have two of each.

Weigh the halves. Acquisitio's upper half holds three points (two and one), and so does its lower (two and one): balanced, one of Cattan's "meanes," and the same is true of Amissio. Cattan's threshold-doctrine cannot separate this pair (their opposition lives in the alternation of their lines, not in top-heaviness) and so the tradition assigned their mobility by nature instead. Acquisitio is stable: what is gained is held, banked, kept; the figure of profit sits still so the profit can accumulate. Amissio is mobile: loss is precisely the failure of a thing to stay; the figure of leaving leaves. Same points, same parity, same balance: opposite repose.

Read the lines elementally, single as active, double as latent. Acquisitio is active in air and earth: the mind that calculates and the substance that accrues, plan above, payoff below, with its fire and water, will and appetite, banked quietly inside. It is a serviceable portrait of getting: not the hot chase but the cool arithmetic of acquisition, ending in an active earth line, the matter landing in the hand. Amissio is the exact negative, active in fire and water: will and desire blazing and flowing freely (spending, wanting, pouring out) while air and earth lie latent, the calculation unmade and the substance unsecured. It ends in a double earth line, the ground closed, nothing kept. These portraits are mnemonic, not scripture: the line-elements are the Golden Dawn's ladder. But the testimony bears them out: Crowley's "generally good for profit or gain" against "good for loss of substance, and sometimes for love, but very bad for gain"; Agrippa's Acquisitio holding Jupiter "the greater fortune" while Amissio holds Venus "retrograde, or combust"; Cattan listing Acquisitio among his figures of gain and Amissio among his figures of loss, war, and false love; and once a figure's structure has been read, its meanings cost little.

Now the extreme pair, more briefly.

<FigureGlyph dots={[1,1,1,1]} name="Via" size={4} /> <FigureGlyph dots={[2,2,2,2]} name="Populus" size={4} />

Via, 1·1·1·1, is the alphabet's minimum: four points, the only four-point figure, every line single, every element active, all spirit and motion with nothing held in reserve, and fittingly the figure means the road, the journey, change itself. It is mobile, of course; it is the *idea* of mobility. Populus, 2·2·2·2, is the maximum: eight points, every line double, every element latent, all reserve and no initiative, the crowd that goes nowhere of itself and takes the character of whatever moves through it; the tradition reads it as good with good and evil with evil, a mirror to its company. It is stable; it is the idea of stability. Each is a palindrome, its own inversion and its own counterpart, while under complementation each is the other's negative, the emptiest figure against the fullest. Both totals are even, so both can stand as Judge. And the tradition binds the two poles into one planet, the Moon increasing and the Moon at full, in the Agrippa passage you read above. The two figures between them bracket the whole alphabet: every other figure is a mixture, partway from Via to Populus, its character set by which lines have settled into pairs and which still stand alone.

That is a complete structural dissection, doable on any figure in about a minute: notation, point total, parity, balance of halves, mobility, inversion, complement, counterpart, active and latent lines. Before the exercises, drill the flashcards below (glyph to name) until you have made two consecutive passes through all sixteen without a single error or hesitation. Not one pass: two, clean, in a row. Recognition must run ahead of reflection from now on, because every chart you ever read will begin with fifteen acts of recognition performed before the thinking starts.

<Exercise n={5} title="The standing and the walking" kind="drill">
  From memory, sort the sixteen figures into stable and mobile. Then answer in a few sentences each: first, what Cattan's doctrine of entering and exiting figures (Book III, ch. 27) has to do with the division, including the three exceptions he names; second, what stability and mobility imply for the speed and permanence of a matter in judgment, with one example of a question in which a mobile figure would be welcome and one in which it would not.
  <ModelAnswer>
    Stable: Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Tristitia, Puella, Albus, Carcer, Caput Draconis. Mobile: Via, Fortuna Minor, Amissio, Laetitia, Puer, Rubeus, Conjunctio, Cauda Draconis.

    Cattan's doctrine sorts figures by weighing the upper two lines against the lower two: "all the fygures which have more pointes on high then belowe, be entring in and good, saving Tristitia. And those which have more pointes below then on hie, be going out and evill, saving Lætitia. And those which have as many above as beneath be meanes, saving Carcer" (Cattan, *The Geomancie*, Book III, ch. 27, p. 215). The five top-heavy, entering figures (Fortuna Major, Tristitia, Albus, Puella, Caput Draconis) are exactly the stable figures that are not balanced; the five bottom-heavy, exiting figures (Fortuna Minor, Laetitia, Rubeus, Puer, Cauda Draconis) are exactly the unbalanced mobile ones. The six balanced "means" (Via, Populus, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer) the tradition divided by nature: the crowd, the kept gain and the prison stand; the road, the departing loss and the dissolving meeting move. Cattan's three exceptions concern goodness, not geometry: Tristitia enters but is not good, Laetitia exits but is not evil, Carcer is balanced but not middling.

    In judgment, a stable figure signifies a matter that endures and resists change: slow to arrive, slow to leave, fixed; a mobile figure signifies a matter in motion: quick, transient, apt to change or escape. Welcome mobility: ask "will this illness pass quickly?" and a mobile figure promises movement and release (compare Cattan's listing of Via, Cauda Draconis and Amissio for amendment of sickness, Book III, ch. 27). Unwelcome mobility: ask "will this marriage endure?" or "will I keep this position?" and there one wants the matter to stand, and a mobile figure warns that it will not stay as it is.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

## You are ready to continue when…

- you can draw all sixteen figures from memory, correctly, in under five minutes, and name any figure on sight without counting its lines;
- you can state any figure's point total and parity at once, and recite the eight even figures (the only possible Judges) as a single set;
- you can sort the sixteen into stable and mobile from memory, and explain the division by way of Cattan's entering-and-exiting doctrine, exceptions included;
- you can produce any figure's inversion and complement in your head, say which is its counterpart, and name the two pairs for which the operations coincide;
- you can give the elemental reading of any line of any figure, and say, accurately, which parts of that reading are Renaissance testimony and which are Golden Dawn systematization;
- you have passed the flashcard drill sixteen out of sixteen, twice in a row.

Treat the list as a gate. Every item on it is load-bearing: Lesson 3 will hang a tradition's worth of meaning on these structures, and Lesson 4 will build charts out of them at speed.

You now hold the alphabet: sixteen figures, four lines each, one point or two, and the whole quiet architecture of pairs and parities that binds them. What you do not yet hold is the literature: what Acquisitio promises in a question of money, why Rubeus was held so evil that some geomancers destroyed the chart when it rose first, what the Girl and the Boy do in matters of love. That is the work of the next lesson, where the sources speak figure by figure and you will find, as you have already glimpsed with gain and loss, the road and the crowd, that what they say grows out of what you can now see in the dots.
