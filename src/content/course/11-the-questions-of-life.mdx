This lesson runs the one machine you now possess through the territories where the old practice lived: the questions of life, money and marriage, sickness and suits, journeys, offices, things lost and persons absent, each class with its houses, its habits, its traps, and its own way of stressing the six clauses. It also keeps three promises Lesson 5 made against this lesson by name: multi-house questions and the sickness of another person, the thief-question, and the deepest of them, the *turning* of the chart, by which the tradition finds a third party's matter from the third party's house. The turning is the one doctrine here where the modern books and the Renaissance sources genuinely part company, and it is taught honestly, the sources' actual warrant measured out layer by layer.

This lesson is the mirror of Lesson 10's: there, almost nothing was new except the weighing; here, almost nothing is new except the *terrain*. The method does not change by one clause: Step 0 through Step 6, the nine rules of weighing, the six clauses of the finished judgment (answer, manner, end, timing, counsel, reservation) exactly as Lesson 10 fixed them. What changes is everything around the method. Three of the four course charts asked seventh-house questions, and Chart D's tenth-house office was the course's only excursion; tonight the wheel opens whole. A money question and a marriage question run the same six steps and stress them entirely differently (the money question's significators can never behold one another, the marriage question's can never escape one another), and a judge who has only ever judged the seventh house knows the method the way a sailor who has only crossed one strait knows the sea. The classes are the sea. And they bring with them the one genuinely new discipline this lesson teaches, stated in the next section and enforced in every judgment after it: the art of reading Cattan's Book II (the placement series, the figure-in-house delineations you have consulted since Lesson 5) as *testimony inside the method*, ranked, weighed, and selected by the question's own register, rather than as a drawer of pre-written answers.

Until now every chart you have judged had one querent and one matter. The questions of life are not so tidy: the sick father, the friend's loyalty, the brother's money, the thief who is somebody's servant. Life keeps asking about *third parties*, and the tradition's machinery for them is this lesson's central doctrinal section. That machinery is smaller than the modern books suggest and larger than nothing: a core the sources state, a move they perform without naming, a chain they assemble for theft, and a whole systematic apparatus they never built at all: four layers, each labeled, so that you always know, when you turn a chart, whose authority you are turning it on.

## The delineation as testimony

Here is the lesson's one new discipline. Cattan's Book II gives, for every house, first a chapter-head listing the demands that house owns, and then sixteen delineations, each figure's verdicts for that house's matters. You have used these entries since Lesson 5, and Lesson 8 canonized the series as "the primary dignity instrument: blunt, citable, and independent of every attribution quarrel." But there is a way of using them that destroys judgment, and it is the way every beginner instinctively reaches for: look up the quesited's figure in the quesited's house, read off the sentence that matches the question, and call it the answer. That is the fortune-cookie method, and Lesson 10's flawed-judgment exercise already convicted its cousin once. Against it, the course's rule, in one sentence: **the delineation is testimony, not verdict; it enters at Step 5, speaks with the authority of the quesited house's own department, and is weighed by the nine rules like everything else.** The grant still belongs to the scan and to nothing else (Rule 1). The end still belongs to the bench (Rule 2). What the Book II entry contributes is the richest single piece of departmental testimony in the art: how the matter *stands* in its own house, in the tradition's own long-practised words.

Three corollaries, each load-bearing in the judgments below. First, **the entry is a column, not a sentence.** Any delineation read whole answers a dozen different demands (Puella in the seventh speaks in one breath about the enemy, the suit, the thief, the fugitive, the marriage, the gamester, the maid, and the married woman's fidelity) because the house owns a dozen demands and the entry serves them all. The question selects its clause; the rest of the column is *other questions' answers*, filed and left. This is Rule 9 working at the level of a single paragraph, and the worked examples below will make you perform the selection in writing. Second, **the entries carry cross-references, and the cross-references are method.** Cattan's delineations repeatedly refuse to answer alone: Tristitia in the sixth kills the patient only "so that the viij. doe consent"; Fortuna Major in the eighth spares him "so that the fourth agree with this house," and lengthens the sickness "if the tenth doe give consent"; Amissio in the sixth sends you flatly to "the first and eight houses" before judging life or death. The placement series is not a drawer of verdicts; it is a web of conditional testimony whose conditions the rest of the chart resolves: the strongest internal evidence the sources offer that the tradition itself read delineations as testimony and never as oracle. Third, **know your witnesses' pedigrees.** Three parallel placement series survive in this course's corpus, and they are not three independent voices. Cattan's Book II is the primary: the richest, the most conditional, the most question-aware. The pseudo-Agrippa's per-figure house entries are real but thin: character sketches more than casebooks, a clause or two per house, vivid and unconditional. And Heydon's house entries, as the editorial notes on his quotes attest placement after placement, reprint the pseudo-Agrippa nearly verbatim: Lesson 6 taught you the phrase for this and the lesson below will use it: one witness in a second coat. When Cattan and the pseudo-Agrippa diverge, you have two witnesses and a genuine divergence to adjudicate; when Heydon sides with Agrippa, you still have two, because Heydon's vote was cast in 1655 by somebody else's pen. The worked examples stage exactly such divergences (including one, in the sickness chart, where the two traditions flatly contradict each other about death), and Exercise 5 quizzes the adjudication step by step.

## The classes, one by one

What follows is the map of the territory: each class of question, its primary house, its secondary houses, its native geometry, and how the parallel sources treat it. Each section ends with errands in the placement lookup, chosen to seed the worked examples and exercises. One structural instrument first, because it changes how every class reads, and it is the course's own observation, flagged as such, built directly on Lesson 8's geometry: **the wheel's fixed frame hands every class a native channel.** Lesson 8 noticed that the significators of a two-party question hold exactly one aspect, the opposition, forever, because the first and seventh houses sit six apart by construction. Generalize it around the wheel and each class inherits a fixed relation between asker and asked: the second house is the first's *companion* and holds no aspect to it; the fifth and ninth behold the first by **trine**, the warmest channel; the tenth by **square**, the channel of effort; the eleventh by **sextile**, imperfect amity in the house of friends, the wheel's own quiet joke; and the sixth, eighth and twelfth hold **no regard to the first at all**: the disease, the grave and the hidden enemy stand where the querent has no line of sight. None of this is in the sources as doctrine; all of it follows from arithmetic the sources state; and the worked examples will show you what it means in practice: a journey question whose significators are born holding hands, and a sickness question whose parties can never see each other.

### Substance and things lost: the second house

The second is "properly the house of gaine and profite": gain to come and how it comes ("with the travaile and sweate of his bodie, or by succession, or by guift"), movable goods, charges and expenses, the promise of a lord, the messenger's news, and, for the theft chain, "the place where the thing lost was stolen" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 2, quoted whole in Lesson 5). Its native geometry: no aspect to the first is possible, but the 1·2 company pair is the wheel's own bond between a man and his money: in substance questions the company mode is the native channel, and an empty 1·2 pair is itself a finding. The secondary houses: the **tenth**, which claims "any thing lost or stolen" outright; the **fourth**, holding "the place where it is thought that treasure is hidden"; the **eighth**, other men's goods: the loan, the usurer, the thing given to keep; and in every money question the **Part of Fortune**, by the modern comparison's standing instruction: "Consider also always in money questions where the Part of Fortune falls" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. VII), an instruction Cattan's own Lord-of-Tays case performs avant la lettre. The parallels: pseudo-Agrippa's second-house entries are short and absolute where Cattan's are circumstantial; you will see the gap at its widest in Exercise 5, where the two traditions read Carcer in this house in opposite directions. Errands: look up **Fortuna Major in the second** and **Amissio in the second** (the bright and dark poles of the class), then **Carcer in the second**, and hold what you find for the theft chain.

### Children and pleasures: the fifth house

The fifth, "the good fortune," owns the child (stature, birth, legitimacy, nature), the woman with child and the child's sex, promises and whether they are performed, messengers and what the letters contain, friends, the earth's fruits, the book's contents, and "all pleasures, as to kisse, coll, sing, daunce, banquet" (Book II, ch. 5). Its native channel is the trine: the asker's own house beholds his children's by perfect amity, by construction. One entry in the chapter-head matters for the turning section below: §12 asks "If the sonne be sicke, dead, or taken prisoner": the son's sickness, death and prison, three departments' worth of matter, all absorbed into the son's *own* house. That is evidence in the turning argument, on the side most students do not expect. The bench's tables carry this class's own row ("woman with child," with verdicts as particular as *a sonne* and *daughter*), and the pseudo-Agrippa's fifth-house entries are at their best here, full of children's faces. Errands: **Puella in the fifth**, **Rubeus in the fifth**, and the figure the bench just named: any chart where "woman with child" gives a sex is a chart whose table-row you should read against the fifth's figure.

### Sickness: the sixth house

The class that teaches the method most, because the wheel withholds the most. Here is the house's own commission:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-06/significations" />

Sickness and servants share a house (subjection is the common thread), and the sickness clauses are the most procedurally demanding in Book II: the disease's length, its humour, the patient's danger, the physician's skill, whether to take physic. The geometry first: the first and sixth hold no regard, and the first and eighth none either: **the sick man can behold neither his disease nor his death**; the only aspect inside the triangle is the sextile the sixth and eighth hold to each other, sickness and the grave conferring where the patient cannot see; so this class cannot be judged by the joining of significators in the ordinary way. The tradition's actual practice judges it as a state of affairs, exactly as Cattan judged the Novelaire case in Lesson 10's models: the quality of the figures in the triangle's three houses, their conditions resolved against each other, the bench's row, the end-houses. And the triangle itself is *attested twice over, independently*. Cattan's entries build it from inside, with the cross-references quoted in the doctrine section: the sixth deferring to the eighth, the eighth to the fourth and tenth, Amissio's entry sending you to "the first and eight houses" by name. And Gerard of Cremona, the medieval source whose method is Lesson 12's, states it from outside in his sixth-house chapter: "see if *Saturn,* or *Mars,* or the *Dragons Tail* be in the first" (the patient's own house first) "...but if good Planets shall be in the first, sixth and eighth, and likewise in the Angles... then the sick person shall live and recover his health" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy* (trans. Turner, 1655), the sixth house). What the course flags: Gerard is reading the **sick person in the first**, the disease in the sixth, death in the eighth (the same triangle), but his instruments are *planets and lords*, the astrological machinery this course defers, entire and unplundered, to Lesson 12. Tonight the course takes from him only the synthesis the figures can carry: first, sixth, eighth, read together, with the angles around them. The secondary houses: the **tenth** for the physician "and his ordinance," the **fourth** and the bench for the end, the **twelfth** when the sickness is of the incurable kind the twelfth owns. Errands: **Tristitia in the sixth**, **Conjunctio in the sixth**, **Caput Draconis in the eighth**, **Puer in the eighth**: the four placements the worked example and Exercise 2 turn on; read each whole, and mark every conditional clause.

### Love, marriage, suits and theft: the seventh house

The crowded angle, and the one class you already know from the inside: every course chart but D lives here. The seventh owns the contrary party in every register, "the contrary of the demaund of any person": the open enemy, "all debates, sutes in lawe, theeverie and fugitives," the accord of marriage "whether it shall take effect or not," the maid and her lover, war and peace, the agreement and whether it endures (Book II, ch. 7, quoted whole in Lesson 5). The native opposition is the class's signature: asker and asked confront each other by construction, and Lesson 8 taught you to read the confrontation's *kinship*: Chart C's bare opposition was enmity; the worked example below holds a fully kinned one, and the difference is the difference between a lawsuit and a wedding. Secondary houses: the **eighth** as the seventh's companion, its relation to the seventh the turning doctrine's best fossil; the **fifth** for the pleasures of love; the **tenth**, in suits, for the judges, by the Clermont rule of Lesson 10 (Rule 7); and in theft the whole chain (second, seventh, tenth, sixth) assembled in its own right in the third worked example. For marriage the standing precedents are Chart A (granted by approach, priced by the table) and Chart B (denied flat); for suits, Chart C, the master case. Errands: **Puella in the seventh** and **Tristitia in the seventh**, both read whole (one carries the worked marriage below, the other the worked theft), and as you read, practice the column discipline: list which clauses answer which of the chapter-head's sixteen demands.

### Death and inheritance: the eighth house

"The house of death" owns the death of any man, its when and its manner ("whether he shall die within a day, month, or yeare," "a good death, or else be slaine"), the inheritance and who takes it, and a stranger cluster the tradition filed here and the course reports as it stands: the absent person ("how the person doth that is from home," "when he that is from hence shall returne"), the substance of the other party, fear and its issue, the thing given in trust, the usurer's gain (Book II, ch. 8). No regard joins the first to the eighth; death is judged, like sickness, as a state of affairs, and most death questions arrive *as* sickness questions and resolve in the sixth-class triangle. What is proper to the eighth class is the register of manner and aftermath: a good death or ill, the estate and its heirs, the absent man's condition. This is the first of the two dire classes, and the pastoral doctrine of Lesson 10 (the counsel clause as the humane instrument, the reservation as honesty's clause) does its heaviest work here; Exercise 7 is this class's quiz, and Exercise 2 will make you deliver this class's answer in full. Errands: **Caput Draconis in the eighth** against **Puer in the eighth**, then the same two placements in the pseudo-Agrippa's entries: you will find, in one of them, the lesson's starkest divergence between the traditions, and the worked sickness example adjudicates it.

### Journeys: the ninth house

The ninth owns the long voyage ("long or shorte, good and profitable, and whether it be as good by Sea as by land"), ships, fleets, ports and shipwreck, and with them the house of God's quieter freight: priests, benefices, scholars and their books, the dream, and "if the common brute be true or not" (Book II, ch. 9). The native channel is the trine: a journey question's significators behold one another warmly by construction, and the class's grants therefore often arrive by the aspect line, the weakest join in the scan, fittingly, for a question that asks not for a meeting but a going. The secondary houses are unusually scattered, and Lesson 5's drills already walked them: the **seventh** holds "which way he goeth that taketh his journey"; the **fourth** the safe arrival of the ship; the **second** "if the voyage that he would take in hande, shall be profitable"; the **eighth** "the gaine or profite to be doone in a straunge Countrey," the profit abroad sitting in the house of other men's goods, which is either a coincidence or another fossil, and the turning section will say which. The standing precedent is Lesson 10's first exercise: the journey granted in the prison's manner, late and bound. Errands: **Fortuna Minor in the ninth** in both Cattan and the pseudo-Agrippa: the two entries pull against each other in temperature, and Exercise 3's chart hangs on reconciling them.

### Office and honour: the tenth house

The tenth owns honour and praise, kings, princes, magistrates and their decrees, the office sued for, the king's justice and his voyages, and three lodgers no modern instinct expects: the **mother** ("Upon the mother, grandmother, wife or Lemman"), the **physician** "and his ordinance," and "any thing lost or stolen" (Book II, ch. 10, quoted whole in Lesson 5). The native square between the first and tenth is the class's emblem: office is won by effort, and the kinship or bareness of that square is the first thing to read (under Chart D's kinned square, in Lesson 10's words, "the ladder is climbed, not stormed"), and Exercise 4 below supplies the bare-square mirror. Chart D is this class's worked precedent in full: occupation of a good figure, the bench's *worship: good*, the dark fortune-point pricing the revenues. Exercise 4 below is built to be set beside it. In suits, remember, the tenth doubles as the judges' bench (Rule 7); in sickness, as the physician's house; in theft, as the recovery house: the tenth is the wheel's busiest secondary, and the question's register selects its office every time. Errands: **Fortuna Major in the tenth**, Cattan and Agrippa both: the brightest placement in the series, and Exercise 4 will teach you what its brightness does and does not promise.

### Friends and hopes: the eleventh house

"The good Angle": the friend and the hope ("the thing ye love, and whereof ye hope to have consolation, ayde and profite"), the secret companion, the faithful or traitorous friend ("if thy friend be faithfull or a traytor to thée"), the year's plenty and dearth, and a clause to hold for the turning section: "over the mother and her treasure, and of all the treasures, rents and revenewes of the king" (Book II, ch. 11). The native sextile (imperfect amity, in the house of friends) sets this class's expectations honestly: eleventh-house grants are gentle and eleventh-house denials rarely catastrophic; the class's real trap is Lesson 5's old division between the eleventh's friend (a support and a hope) and the seventh's (a counterpart in a two-party bond), which Exercise 6 drills again. Secondary: the **third** for the near companion and neighbour, the **fifth** for the friend of pleasure, the **second** for what the friendship costs or yields. Errand: **Fortuna Major in the eleventh** and **Tristitia in the eleventh**: hope at its two temperatures.

### Prison and hidden enemies: the twelfth house

The last class, and the second dire one. The twelfth owns the prison and the prisoner ("whether the prisoner shall parte out of prison, and when"), desolation and mourning, the incurable sickness, "a privie ennemy rather then upon an open," the traitor and "théefe of an house," banishment, debts and coming poverty, and the great beasts, "oxen, cammels, dromodaries... horses, mules, asses," the last being why Cattan's own horse-buying case, which Lessons 8 and 9 walked, kept consulting this house (Book II, ch. 12). The geometry is the doctrine: the twelfth holds no regard to the first (*the hidden enemy stands where the querent has no line of sight*), and a privy-enemy question is therefore judged almost wholly by department: the twelfth's figure, its springings, its company, the fortune-point's address. Prison questions carry the tradition's strange mercies, planted since Lesson 3: Amissio in the first "signifieth deliverance and escape out of prison": loss is freedom when the thing lost is a chain; no placement teaches Rule 9 better. The dire-answer protocol governs the class's delivery, and Exercise 7's quiz covers both this house and the eighth. Errands: **Carcer in the twelfth** (the prison in the prison, the wheel's grimmest rhyme) and **Puer in the twelfth**, which the theft chain below will give you reason to remember.

## The third parties, and the turning of the chart

Now the central doctrine, begun with the same candor Lesson 9 brought to the point of intention. Any modern manual of geomancy carries a complete apparatus for third-party questions: every house may be taken as a new first house and the wheel counted around from it, so that the father's money is the second-from-the-fourth (the fifth), the partner's illness the sixth-from-the-seventh (the twelfth), the father's father the fourth-from-the-fourth (the seventh), and so on through chains of any depth. The apparatus is called *turning the chart*, it is tidy, complete, and genuinely useful, and **it is not attested in this course's sources.** It is horary astrology's doctrine of derived houses, transposed onto geomancy by the modern books exactly as the aspects were transposed by the Renaissance ones, and a student who learns it as "the traditional method" has been taught a history that did not happen. What *did* happen is subtler and more interesting: the practice exists in the sources in embryo (stated in one layer, performed but unnamed in a second, assembled into working chains in a third), and only the fourth layer, the full system, is a modern import. Here are the four layers, labeled, with the evidence for each; this labeling is the lesson's heart, and Exercise 6's taxonomy and the capstone both assume you can reproduce it.

**Layer 1, the attested core: the third party has a house, and the question lives there.** This needs no flag; it is the sources' own loud practice. Cattan's chapter-heads assign every person of the querent's world a seat (brothers and kin in the third, the father in the fourth, children in the fifth, servants in the sixth, the wife and the adversary in the seventh, the friend in the eleventh) and then write the third party's questions *directly into that house's list*. The fourth's commission entire: the standard case, with the next layer hiding inside it:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-04/significations" />

"Whether the Father be dead or shall die quickly." "Whether of the father or the sonne shall die first." The father's death (an eighth-house matter, were it the querent's) is asked and answered *in the fourth*, the father's own seat; and the eighth's own list does the same work from the other side, asking "Whether shall die first, the father or brother of the querant" among its death questions (Book II, ch. 8). The fifth, as the class section noted, absorbs the son's sickness, death and prison into the son's house; the tenth absorbs the mother's secrets into hers. This is Layer 1, and it is *not* turning. The tradition's first instinct, on the record across all twelve chapter-heads, is to let the person's own house carry the person's whole story (sickness, death, prison and all) exactly as the first house carries the querent's. Where the modern system would send you counting around the wheel, Cattan's lists mostly stay home. When the worked sickness example below reads the sick father, this layer does the work: the father is the fourth, his figure is his condition, and Cattan's own fourth-house delineations answer "shall he die" in so many words.

**Layer 2, the single turn: a move Cattan performs twice and never names.** The fourth's list, clause 6: "the questions which may be made touching the silver of the brother or sister of the querant." The brother lives in the third. His silver is asked *in the fourth*: the second house counted from the third, the brother's own house of substance. There is no doctrine attached, no rule stated, no name given; the derived house simply sits in the list as if it had always been there. And it is not alone. Cattan's own hands in a live judgment: you met this chapter in Lesson 8 for its aspect doctrine and in Lesson 10 for the Trivulsio case; the course quotes it a third time for one clause you had no reason to mark before:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-15-of-the-tryne-aspect-chapt" />

"Because the second is gone into the eight, **which is the house of the substance of the Emperour**, I saide that the king should loose much of his substance." The Emperor is the seventh: the adverse party in a question of peace between two princes. The eighth (the seventh's second) is, in Cattan's own casual relative clause, *the Emperor's house of substance*: the second-from-the-seventh, turned, used, and never remarked upon. The same fossil sits in his eighth chapter-head ("the goods and substance of a friend" lodged in the house after the friend's) and in the eleventh's, where "the treasures, rents and revenewes of the king" sit one house after the king's tenth:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-11/significations" />

The mother and her treasure; the king and his rents: each treasure one house on from its owner's seat. Count the fossils: the brother's silver in the fourth, the Emperor's and the friend's substance in the eighth, the mother's and the king's in the eleventh, every single one a **second-from**, the substance of a third party found one house beyond the party's own. On this evidence the course states its doctrine, and flags it precisely: *the single turn (taking the house after a third party's house as that party's substance, and by measured extension, the nth house from a party's house as that party's nth matter) is a generalization the course makes of a thing Cattan does repeatedly and never names. The course names it, and says plainly that it has done so.* Used at one remove, for one step, it stands on the sources' practice; the worked sickness example will use it exactly so, taking the sixth-from-the-fourth for the father's disease while the father himself stays in his own fourth, and will tell you, when it does, which layer it is standing on.

**Layer 3, the chains: theft, and the springing identification.** For one class of question the sources go further than the single turn: they read a *set* of houses in sequence, as a connected investigation. Theft is the great case. The chapter-heads distribute it (the goods and the place of the theft in the second, the thief and his description in the seventh ("who did the robberie be of that house, or be a stranger, and of what sort or clothing he is"), the recovery and "any thing lost or stolen" in the tenth, the stolen beasts and the household's own suspects in the sixth, the "théefe of an house" in the twelfth) and Cattan's own practice assembles them. His robbery case, which Lesson 9 quoted for the way of the point, reads four houses in order like a constable working a house: the first not good; the sixth, "wherein the demaunds of servants be made," holding Cauda Draconis, "the which doth signifie robbery," whence "I judged that the servant was a theefe"; the seventh confirming; and then the second, "which signifieth the substance of the master," *going into* another house and convicting the springing itself: "the Maister had beene robbed by the servant before his saide Maister did knowe thereof" (Book III, ch. 20). The instrument inside the chain is one you have owned since Lesson 7 (the springing of figures), sharpened here into **identification**: where the goods' figure reappears, there the goods' fate is written. The pseudo-Agrippa's tradition states the rule in exactly one worked sentence, and Heydon carries it forward in the reprint you read in Lesson 9; the course quotes it again here because tonight it is no longer a curiosity about the Index but a working tool:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/index-multiplication" />

"If a question shall be propounded of the second house concerning a Thief, and the figure of the second house shall be found in the sixth, it declareth the Thief to be some of ones own houshold or servants." The second's figure springing to the sixth: the goods walking into the servants' quarters. Crowley compresses the same doctrine to a clause in his method, the second-house figure found again in the sixth showing "the thief to be a servant in the house" (Crowley, ch. VII): three centuries, three sources, one test, and the third worked example below runs it on a live chart. Layer 3's warrant, then: the chains are source-direct for theft, and the springing identification is attested in both branches of the tradition; what the course adds, flagged, is only the orderly protocol for walking the chain inside Lesson 10's method.

**Layer 4, the full systematic turning: the modern import, flagged.** Last, the whole-wheel apparatus itself: every house a possible ascendant, derived houses counted from any seat, chains of derivation ("the fourth-from-the-fourth") as deep as the question requires. State its pedigree exactly: it is the derived-house doctrine of horary astrology, fully systematic in that art since the medieval astrologers, and the modern geomantic manuals imported it wholesale, reasonably, for it answers a real need, and without warrant in the Renaissance geomantic sources, which, as Layers 1 and 2 show, mostly *refuse* the move the system multiplies: where full turning would send the son's prison to the fourth-from-the-fifth, Cattan's fifth simply asks "if the sonne be... taken prisoner" and answers from the son's own house. The course's position, for your own method: the full system is *usable* (it is coherent, and a practitioner who adopts it with open eyes practices a defensible hybrid), but it may never be claimed for Cattan, Agrippa, Heydon or Crowley, and within this course it is reserved for the rare question Layers 1 through 3 cannot reach, and always flagged in the journal when used. And where the genuinely astrological appetite for it leads, the course goes next: Gerard of Cremona, whose sixth-house chapter you read tonight, *redistributes the whole wheel by fiat* when his question demands it ("give the first house of the Physician, the tenth to the sick, the seventh to his diseases, and the fourth to the medicines"): houses reassigned wholesale, the astrological habit of mind working at full strength in a medieval geomantic text. That is the branch of the tradition where derived and reassigned houses are truly at home, and it is Lesson 12's.

One paragraph of protocol before the worked examples, to fix the layers in practice. **Third-party questions run Lesson 10's method with exactly two amendments.** First, Step 1 assigns the question to the third party's house, and the party's figure becomes the quesited; or, where the question concerns a matter *of* the third party (his money, his disease), Step 1 names both the party's house and the turned house, with the layer written in the journal: *father 4th (Layer 1); his disease 9th = 6th-from-4th (Layer 2, course generalization)*. Second, Step 4's scan runs from the first house to the primary quesited as always (the querent still asks, and the houses still grant or refuse *him* the thing he asked) while the turned houses testify at Step 5 with the rest of the departments. Everything else stands unchanged: the bench keeps the end, the nine rules keep their stations, the six clauses close the judgment.

Two traditions, the same two figures in the same two houses, and verdicts that cannot both stand: Cattan's conditional web resolves to *life*; the pseudo-Agrippa's flat sentences read *death twice over*. The adjudication, which Exercise 5 will test: the course follows Cattan here, for three reasons stated in rank. The end: gain, the Judge Acquisitio rules the issue, and the fourth house, the end of the matter, holds Via: the disease *passes*, the figure of the road in the house of conclusions. The timing: slow, the bench's mobile Witnesses promise turns for better and worse before the stable end arrives; no date. The counsel: nurse him and spare him, the chart speaks ill of physic and physician alike for this disease, so let the regimen be rest, patience and the long game the figures themselves are playing; and let him not despair at the length, for the length is the manner of the cure, not the approach of death. The reservation: the parallel tradition, read flat, gives this man death twice over, and this judgment has weighed that testimony and overruled it on stated grounds, recorded here so the record is honest; and the judgment answers *this disease*: the eighth's mercy, "he shall have no other disease," is its own clause, and relapse, new infirmity, and the diseases of other years are other questions.

**The third-party variant: the sick father, by the layers.** Suppose the same querent had asked instead: *"Shall my father recover of his disease?"* Lesson 5 promised this case to this lesson, and the layers answer it in order. Layer 1, attested, does the main work: the question lives with the father, in the **fourth**, and the fourth holds **Via**. Cattan's fourth-house column answers the father-register directly, in the words his chapter-head promised: Via in the fourth gives "the father is of long life" (Book II, ch. 4): Layer 1 alone, no turning at all, already answers the death-fear from the father's own house, exactly as "Whether the Father be dead or shall die quickly" is answered there. For the *disease's particulars* (its nature and length, which the father's own house does not detail) the course permits the single turn, Layer 2, flagged in the journal as the course's generalization: the father's sixth is the sixth-from-the-fourth, the **ninth**, which holds Tristitia, the same Saturn sorrow, long and heavy, that this chart shows wherever it shows sickness; and his death-house, the eighth-from-the-fourth, is the **eleventh**, Fortuna Minor, a fortune, minor but clean, concording with his own house's "long life." The scan, by the protocol: it still runs from the first to the *fourth* (the querent asks, and the houses grant or refuse *him* the comfort he asks about) and the turned houses testify at Step 5 among the departments. Write the layer beside every turned house, every time: the habit costs a word and preserves, in your own journal, the boundary between what the sources practiced and what this course built.

(The pseudo-Agrippa's entry for this exact placement says nearly the opposite, poverty, the thief taken, the thing regained, "no treasure to be hid," and Exercise 5 returns to that adjudication; the short form is that one conditional, circumstantial witness plus a concordant chart outweighs one flat sentence reprinted twice.) The **way of the point**: the Judge Amissio carries a single point above (the perfect single way, "for when they be two it is not so perfite as when they be found in one") and the Right Witness matches (the Left, double-topped Populus, does not); of the ninth and tenth the ninth (Fortuna Minor, single-topped) matches; thence to the first, which dies double-topped, and the second: **the way completes in house 2, on Carcer**. The **Part of Fortune**: seventy-four points; six twelves are seventy-two; remainder **two**, **house 2, on Carcer**. Springing, way and fortune-point: three instruments, three independent derivations, one address: the goods themselves, wearing the Prison. Rule 8's same-rank counting, used exactly where counting belongs: the chart's every subtle testimony concentrates on the substance, locked.

**Step 6, the six clauses.** **Denied: the goods are not restored to him.** The manner: no robbery from without, the thief is of his own household (the goods sprung into the servants' house by kinned trine; the seventh's "the theefe is in the house"; the twelfth's house-thief register glowing Mars-red beside the friends), and the thing itself has not left his walls: "the thing is therein hidden," kept and shut, the chart's three instruments unanimous on the lock. The end: loss, the Judge is Amissio over the Witness Amissio, the table's row *not found*; but loss absorbed, not ruin: the householder's own seat wears Gain, angular and stable, and the estate stands. The timing: nothing moves, stable significators, a sitting thief, a hidden thing at rest; this denial does not ripen into recovery by waiting. The counsel (the humane instrument, working a domestic wound): call off the search abroad, for the chart locates everything within; search the house's own dark corners quietly (the hiding-place's figure passes into the recovery house: what can be found will be found by looking *down*, not out); and govern the household rather than convulse it: an accusation without proof, in a house the chart calls young and trusted, would cost more than the goods are worth. Lock what remains; the figure on the goods is, after all, the Prison, and a lock is the one counsel it gives freely. The reservation: this judgment answers the having-again, and answers it no; it does not name the thief (*who exactly*, and *whether to accuse*, and *whether to dismiss* are each their own question, their own house, their own cast) and the parallel tradition's entry for the goods' figure promises the opposite of this verdict, recorded here as the minority it is, with its adjudication standing in Exercise 5 for any reader to weigh again.

## Where you stand

Part 3 closes here, and with it the course's claim on the word *method*. Lesson 10 gave you the machine; this lesson drove it across the whole territory the old practice served (the purse and the sickbed, the altar and the harbour, the court, the household and the cell) and the machine did not change, which was the point: nine classes, seven fresh charts between the examples and the exercises, and the same Step 0, the same nine rules, the same six clauses every time, stressed differently by every class and bent by none. Along the way you took on the two disciplines that turn a methodical student into a judge of *questions*: the placement series read as a court reads witnesses (selected by register, weighed by pedigree, overruled on the record when the chart outvotes it) and the third-party machinery taken honestly, in four layers, so that you can do everything the tradition did, something the tradition almost did, and nothing the tradition never did without saying so. The promises are paid: Lesson 5's sick father has been judged from his own house, its stolen ring traced around the chain it scattered on, and the turning of the chart (promised by name, twice) has been taught the only way this course teaches anything: with the sources' warrant measured to the clause.

What remains is Part 4, and it is two different endings. Lesson 12 turns to the road this course has pointed at every time Gerard of Cremona's name appeared: **astrological geomancy**, the older, stranger branch in which the sixteen figures do not fill a wheel of houses but erect a celestial chart, judged by lords, planets, signs and the whole apparatus this lesson kept flagging and deferring; you have seen its silhouette in his sixth-house chapter, and next lesson you meet it whole, as a second system with its own integrity rather than a flavour of the first. And Lesson 13 is the capstone, the practicum, where the course stops teaching and starts witnessing: three complete written judgments against staged master solutions, the synthesis quiz that gathers what your Exercise 5s and 7s have tested, and the thirty-day journal that turns all of this from a course you took into a practice you keep. Both will be published at [the course index](/course/); go there when your checklist is clean and your taxonomy card is taped where the worksheet lives. You came to this lesson a judge of charts. You leave it a judge of questions, and the questions, from here on, are life's own.
