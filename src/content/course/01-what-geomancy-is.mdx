Every divination system carries a picture of the world. Geomancy's is this: the heavens act on the earth and on people; and when a person moved by a serious question pricks rows of points without counting them, the sources say those points can record the question in a readable form. That is the claim this lesson explains.

By the end you'll understand the sources on their own terms, which is what the rest of the course builds on.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes you've read [How to Study This Course](/course/how-to-study/). Its two assigned readings are seventeenth-century primary texts. The worked example at the end walks through the opening of the pseudo-Agrippa sentence by sentence, so don't worry if not everything is clear on first reading.

The readings will show you tables of figures, schemes of houses, and rules for reading a chart that this lesson doesn't explain. Lessons 2 through 6 teach those things in order, at a reasonable pace.

## What geomancy is

Geomancy answers questions. Not moods, not themes, not invitations to reflection: questions, posed one at a time, in plain words: will I recover the money; is the absent man alive; should I take this journey; will the suit go in my favour. The method receives the question, generates a chart by a procedure partly random and wholly rule-bound, and returns an answer: yes or no, well or ill, and with practice a good deal of circumstance besides. The Renaissance manuals treat it as a serious tool for serious matters, and the range of questions they entertain (illness, theft, marriage, imprisonment, war, the faith of a wife, the fortune of a prince) is a portrait of what people actually needed to know.

The procedure begins with points. The diviner, holding the question firmly in mind, makes sixteen rows of points (pricked in sand or soil in the older practice, set down with a pen in the later one) without counting as the hand moves. Each row is then counted after the fact and reduced to its remainder: an odd row yields one point, an even row two. Four rows make a figure of four lines, so the sixteen rows yield four figures, which the sources call the Mothers. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa describing exactly this:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-mothers" />

A geomantic figure, then, is a stack of four lines, each line either single or double. Four positions, two states each: sixteen possible figures, no more and no fewer. The method uses these figures throughout. Each has a Latin name and a settled character. <FigureGlyph dots={[1,1,1,1]} name="Via" /> Via, the Way, is the only figure of four single points; <FigureGlyph dots={[2,2,2,2]} name="Populus" /> Populus, the People, the only figure of four doubles; between these extremes stand fourteen others, such as <FigureGlyph dots={[2,1,2,1]} name="Acquisitio" /> Acquisitio, Gain, and <FigureGlyph dots={[1,2,1,2]} name="Amissio" /> Amissio, Loss. Lesson 2 is devoted entirely to these sixteen figures, their names, natures, and attributions.

Built how? By derivation. The four Mothers are recombined by fixed rules to produce four Daughters; Mothers and Daughters are added together in pairs (odd plus odd makes even, the arithmetic is no harder than that) to produce four more figures; those four yield two Witnesses; and the two Witnesses yield a single final figure, the Judge, which gives the overall answer to the question. The pseudo-Agrippa again, compressing the whole derivation into a paragraph:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-daughters" />

The completed diagram of fifteen figures (Mothers, Daughters, the four derived figures usually called Nieces, Witnesses, and Judge) is the shield chart, named for the shape of the frame the old books draw it in. Only the first four figures involve chance. Everything after the Mothers follows from them by rule, the way the last digit of a long sum follows from the numbers above it. A geomantic casting is one moment of hazard followed by pure consequence, the one point where, on the sources' account, something other than chance is at work. The figures are also distributed into the twelve astrological houses (the divisions of the heavens that give every question its place, money to the second house, brothers to the third, marriage to the seventh) and judged there according to their natures. House-by-house entries fill most of all five sources, and they will fill most of this course.

Every step after the first sixteen rows is teachable, checkable, and entirely mechanical. The sources are emphatic on this point: geomancy is not free-form intuition over evocative symbols. It is a rule-bound method, and two competent geomancers given the same chart and question should reach much the same answer.

## The daughter of astrology

The Renaissance manuals call geomancy the daughter of astrology. From its mother it inherits nearly all of its interpretive machinery: the twelve houses and their significations, the seven planets, the twelve signs, the aspects by which figures regard one another across the chart, the dignities by which they are strong or weak in their places. A geomantic figure is, in the sources' understanding, a planetary nature in a compressed notation. The pseudo-Agrippa assigns each figure to a planet and through the planet to a sign; Gerard's method, as you will have seen in the assigned reading, goes further and uses the first figure to erect what is effectively a horoscope, placing a zodiac sign on the Ascendant and distributing the planets through the houses by counts of points.

Full medieval astrology demanded the casting of an exact figure of the heavens for an exact moment: tables, ephemerides, tools, arithmetic, and years of training before a reading could even be attempted. Gerard (we will come to the question of who "Gerard" was) opens his treatise with exactly this complaint. Astronomy, he says, is "so transcendant and subtil an art in itself" that a man must attend to so many things before attaining true judgment that "the eye of the understanding will not pierce unto the half thereof," and few doctors of the age were ever experienced enough to judge by it soundly. His remedy is the treatise in hand, composed so that the student may "judge with less labour and study," because "in this present science it is not requisite to behold neither the Ascendant, nor the hour in a Table, as it is in Astrology" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), introduction).

Astrology computes the actual heavens. Geomancy builds a chart from the points and then reads it with astrology's apparatus. It needs no clock or table. The moment of the question is recorded through the hand that pricks the points. Gerard's own first move shows how literally he means it. ## Why the sources said it works

The sources have a theory of why it works, and the whole structure of the method follows from it. You can't practise geomancy intelligently (you can't even see why the rules forbid asking the same question twice) without knowing it.

The background is the layered cosmos that every educated person of the twelfth through the seventeenth century carried in his head. At the top, the divine mind, source of all order. Below it, the heavens (the planets and fixed stars) which receive that order and transmit it downward as influence: not as metaphor but as physics, the same physics by which the sun warms and the moon moves the tides. At the bottom, the elemental world of earth and everything on it, continuously worked upon by the heavens above. And running through the whole, binding the layers into one living thing, the principle the philosophers called the soul of the world, *anima mundi*. The genuine writings of Henry Cornelius Agrippa, the *Three Books of Occult Philosophy* (1533), teach this architecture at length: the virtues of things below derive from things above, and the human soul, being itself of celestial origin, participates in both worlds at once. The geomantic treatise printed under Agrippa's name does not pause to argue any of this. It assumes it, the way a modern engineering text assumes gravitation.

What the *Of Geomancy* does argue (its opening page, which the worked example below reads line by line) is how that cosmos gets into the points. The text says geomancy "can declare or shew forth nothing of verity, unless it shall be radical in some divine virtue," and reports that the authors of the science found that root to be twofold. The first root is religious and ceremonial: the points are made in earth, geomancy belongs to the element of earth, and the hand of the projector is "most powerfully moved, and directed to the terrestrial spirits," whom the older practitioners invoked with "certain holy incantations and deprecations." On this account geomancy is a regulated contact with terrestrial spirits, related to the other elemental divinations the text names, pyromancy and hydromancy. The second root (and the text plainly prefers it) needs no incantation at all:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/great-egress" />

This passage gives the second account of why the method works. When the diviner genuinely wants to know, geomancy "hath a natural obedience to the soul itself." The text says the unplanned motions of the hand are governed by the soul, and the points record that motion. The author then draws the practical conclusion: "neither matters it where or how those points are projected." Sand, soil, paper, and pen can all serve. The first root depends on religious ceremony and earth as the medium. The second root depends on the state of the person making the points. This is why the later sources can use pen and paper without treating the change of medium as a change of method.

The same sentence connects geomancy with horary astrology. Geomancy, says the text, "hath the same radix with the art of Astrological questions: which also can no otherwise be verified, unless with a constant and excessive affection of the Querent himself." Horary astrology (the astrology of questions, in which a chart is cast for the moment a question is asked) rests on the rule that a serious question has a meaningful moment. Geomancy has the same root. The difference is the recording tool: astrology reads the moment from the sky, while geomancy records it through the points.

Gerard's treatise, though it spends no paragraphs on philosophy, presupposes this theory and adds the practical rules that go with it: when the casting can be trusted. That is the subject of the next section. The theory is not psychological: nothing in these texts suggests the points reveal the contents of your own mind, or that the figures are prompts for intuition. It is not mechanical in the gambler's sense: no source imagines that the odds of the figures carry information. The claim is metaphysical and specific: a soul moved by real desire is, at that moment, in contact with the order of the cosmos, and its least deliberate motions can be read. Every rule you will learn in the coming lessons (the insistence on a well-formed question, the prohibition on idle or repeated castings, the requirement of attention at the moment of pricking) is built around that theory.

## Conditions for casting

If the truth of the figure depends on the state of the asker, the diviner, and the moment of casting, then some conditions can invalidate the figure. Gerard names them at the close of his introduction:

<Quote id="gerard/casting-caution" />

First, the weather: no question in a rainy, cloudy, or very windy season. The text does not explain the rule. The simplest reading is that, in a cosmos of continuous celestial influence, violent weather was thought to disturb the conditions of casting. This is only an inference (Gerard legislates, he does not justify), and the later manuals do not all repeat the rule. Second, the diviner's own state: not when you are angry, not when your mind is "busied with many affairs." The positive form of this rule is the pseudo-Agrippa's "great egress of desire" and Crowley's curt modern echo, "Think fixedly of the demand": the casting requires attention to the question.

Third, the querent: not "for tempters or deriders." Geomancy is not to be demonstrated to someone who asks in order to mock, or tested by someone who asks in order to trap. On the sources' theory the figure answers an earnest desire to know. Where no such desire exists, the casting has no proper question. Fourth, and most practically: do not "renew and reiterate the same Question again under the same figure or form; for that is error." One question, one casting. The moment of asking is, on the theory, the moment at which the matter is legible. If you ask again because you disliked the answer, you have changed the act from inquiry to repetition. When you begin your own practice in Lesson 4, this rule will be the first entry in your casting journal's code of conduct.

None of the four conditions concerns arithmetic. The derivation of the chart works the same way in any weather and any mood. The conditions concern the sixteen rows, because that is the only part of the procedure not determined by rule. The sources protect that first act because, on their account, it is where the question enters the chart.

## Short History

Geomancy's documented history begins not in Europe but in the Islamic world, where it is called *ʿilm al-raml*, the science of the sand. By the twelfth century it was mature there: a settled set of sixteen figures with Arabic names, the derivation of a full scheme from four randomly generated figures, manuals for reading charts, and a frame of legend that traced geomancy back through the prophet Idris (identified with Enoch and with Hermes) to instruction by the angel Jibril. The historical origins behind the legend are genuinely obscure; respectable arguments have been made for development within the Arabic-speaking world and for older roots, and this course will not pretend the question is settled. What is certain is that the system the Latin West received was an Arabic system, received whole: the European figures are the Arabic figures, the European derivation is the Arabic derivation, and several of the Latin names are straight translations. Beyond this course's scope, the same family of figures lives on in West African divination and in the sikidy of Madagascar: geomancy spread south as well as north.

The crossing into Latin happened in the twelfth century, in the great age of translation from Arabic, and geomancy travelled in the same baggage as Aristotle, Euclid, and Ptolemy. Around the middle of the century Hugh of Santalla produced his *Ars geomantiae*, among the earliest Latin geomancies, and other translations and adaptations followed quickly; by the thirteenth century original Latin treatises were multiplying, and geomancy had begun its long career as the most practised divination system of medieval Europe after astrology itself: taught in the textbook of pseudo-Pietro d'Abano, condemned by preachers, copied into the notebooks of physicians and princes. It is in this world that our oldest assigned text belongs: the *Astronomical Geomancy* that circulates under the name of Gerard of Cremona.

Gerard of Cremona (c. 1114–1187) was the most prolific of all the Toledo translators, the man through whose Latin Europe received Ptolemy's *Almagest* and much of Arabic medicine and astronomy. Whether he wrote, or translated, the *Astronomical Geomancy* is quite another matter. The attribution rests on the say-so of later manuscripts and printers, and scholars have long suspected confusion with a lesser figure (Gerard of Sabbioneta, a thirteenth-century astrologer, is the usual candidate) or simple pseudepigraphy, the medieval habit of crediting a useful text to a famous name. The truth is not recoverable on present evidence. This site and this course therefore cite the text as "attributed," use "Gerard" as a label of convenience, and ask you to remember that the label names a book, not a certainty about its author. The book itself is plainly medieval, plainly translated from the Arabic tradition, and none the worse as evidence for being anonymous.

The printing press carried geomancy into its Renaissance flowering, and the works this course is built on now enter one by one. In 1558 there appeared at Paris *La Géomancie* of Christofe de Cattan, an Italian gentleman writing in French, a complete, orderly, opinionated manual of the shield-chart method in three books. It was the most influential geomancy of its century, and in 1591 Francis Sparry's English translation, *The Geomancie of Maister Christopher Cattan*, gave geomancy its first great English textbook. Cattan teaches the figures, the houses, the witnesses and Judge, the aspects and the movements of figures about the chart, with worked examples from his own practice; when this course teaches you the method of reading charts in Part 2, it will be Cattan's method more than anyone else's.

Four years before Cattan's French printing, in 1555 (and posthumously, for the author had died in 1535) there had begun to circulate in Latin a *Fourth Book of Occult Philosophy* under the name of Henry Cornelius Agrippa, completing, as the title implied, his genuine *Three Books*. With it travelled a compact treatise, *Of Geomancy*. The attribution was challenged almost at once: Johann Weyer, who had been Agrippa's own pupil and secretary, denounced the Fourth Book as a forgery, and modern scholarship has agreed with him about the Fourth Book while remaining genuinely uncertain about the geomancy. Agrippa did write a geomancy (he says so himself in his sceptical *De vanitate*, with the sour remark that his own was no less superstitious than the rest) but whether the printed treatise is that work, a reworking of it, or another man's text sailing under his flag, cannot now be proven. This course follows the site's usage and cites it as "the *Of Geomancy* attributed to Agrippa," or "the pseudo-Agrippa" for short, the prefix marking doubt, not condemnation. Doubtful paternity does not make it a lesser text; its opening pages contain the clearest theoretical statement of why geomancy works in the whole literature, which is exactly why this lesson's worked example is built on them. In 1655 Robert Turner Englished the Fourth Book and its companions, binding the pseudo-Agrippa and the Gerard between the same covers; his translation is the form in which both texts have been read ever since, and the form in which this site presents them.

In 1663-4 came the largest English treatise geomancy would ever receive: John Heydon's *Theomagia, or the Temple of Wisdome*, a sprawling three-part compendium dressed in Rosicrucian costume. Heydon was a compiler, and an unacknowledged one. Great stretches of the *Theomagia* are Cattan's English text and Turner's pseudo-Agrippa reprinted nearly verbatim, with names changed, angels added, and no credit given. This site's quotation apparatus marks the borrowings wherever they occur, and the next section shows one. Yet the compilation has real value: Heydon preserves, consolidates, and occasionally completes his sources, and his house-by-house entries are the most exhaustive in English.

After Heydon, geomancy sank with the cosmos that had carried it. The eighteenth and nineteenth centuries kept geomancy alive mainly in chapbooks and curiosities, until the occult revival of the late nineteenth century took it up again. The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn, founded in London in 1888, made geomancy part of its standard curriculum, recasting the old method in its own ceremonial idiom (ruling spirits, sigils, invoking pentagrams) while keeping the sixteen figures, the shield chart, and the astrological houses. In 1909 Aleister Crowley printed a terse handbook of this Golden Dawn geomancy in his journal *The Equinox*, as *Liber Gaias: A Handbook of Geomancy*. It is the fifth source of this course, and it is admitted on special terms: as a modern comparison, never as a source with the same weight as the older texts. Where Crowley agrees with the Renaissance texts he shows continuity; where he differs he shows what the revival changed; in neither case does he outvote Cattan. The course will always flag him as modern when he is assigned, and you should acquire the habit of doing the same.

## The five sources, and why this course teaches disagreements

Everything in this course is grounded in five public-domain texts, all reproduced or quoted at length on this site, every quotation cited and traceable.

Cattan's *Geomancie* (1591) is the backbone: the fullest Renaissance manual in English, systematic from the casting of points to the most detailed techniques of reading, and the course's primary source for method. Heydon's *Theomagia* (1663) is the great storehouse: derivative in its teaching but unmatched in sheer coverage, and indispensable for its exhaustive entries for every figure in every house. The *Of Geomancy* attributed to Agrippa (1655) is the theorist and the second voice on placement entries: its opening supplies the theory of geomancy, its body a complete and frequently independent set of house readings. Gerard of Cremona's *Astronomical Geomancy* (1655 English) is the medieval source: a different and older branch of geomancy, valuable both for its horoscope-style method and for preserving rules (the casting cautions above among them) that the Renaissance manuals assume rather than state. Crowley's *Handbook of Geomancy* (1909) is the modern comparison: a compressed record of what the Golden Dawn revival kept, dropped, and invented, always flagged as a comparison and never counted as an older source.

Five sources spanning five centuries don't speak with one voice, and this course teaches the disagreements rather than flattening them. Learn that habit before you learn any single fact. Two examples show it.

First, sources disagree with each other, and sometimes with themselves. The pseudo-Agrippa assigns each figure a zodiac sign through its planet: Acquisitio, a figure of Jupiter, receives Pisces, a house of Jupiter.

The exercises in this course will repeatedly ask you not just *what* the sources say but *how many* of them are really speaking.

## How this course works

Every lesson has the same shape. Assigned readings stand at the top, and they are assignments, not suggestions: the lessons comment on the texts, they do not replace them. The body of the lesson teaches; a worked example then shows the lesson's skills exercised on real material at full length; exercises follow, each with a model answer folded shut beneath it; and a mastery checklist ends the lesson and gates the next. The estimated hours on each lesson (five to eight for this one) assume the readings are read twice and the exercises written out in full before the model answers are opened. Nothing polices this but you; the [study guide](/course/how-to-study/) explains why self-graded honesty is the only assessment a correspondence course without a correspondent can have, and why it is enough.

Part 1 of the course, Foundations, runs from this lesson through the casting of your first charts. The next lesson teaches the sixteen figures (the alphabet of the earth) and expects you to learn them cold: names, forms, planetary natures, and the contested attributions discussed above. The lesson after that teaches the houses; the one after that, the casting procedure entire, and there your casting journal begins. From Lesson 4 to the end of the course the journal is a standing requirement: every chart you cast, recorded with its question, its moment, your full reading written before the outcome is known, and the outcome entered later in a different ink, figuratively or literally. The final exercise of this lesson has you prepare the journal now, three lessons early, for a reason: deciding in advance what you will record is the cheapest protection against two common failures, the vague question and the answer improved after the fact.

From this first lesson onward, written answers must cite the sources in the site's standard forms (author, title, year, locator). The discipline will feel pedantic for about two lessons. It is, in fact, the whole difference between knowing the sources and remembering something like them; and in a method whose sources disagree as instructively as these five do, the citation is often where the real answer lives.

## Worked example

The skill this lesson teaches is reading a primary text closely enough to extract its theory, including the theory it assumes without stating. Here we take the opening of the *Of Geomancy* attributed to Agrippa, the most theoretically explicit page in the literature, sentence by sentence. The [full text](/library/agrippa/of-geomancy/) is in the library.

> GEOMANCY is an art of divination, whereby the judgment may be rendered by lot, or destiny to every question of every thing whatsoever.
>
> — Agrippa (attrib.), *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), opening

Three claims are packed into the definition. Here *art* means a teachable discipline with rules, not a gift or a frenzy; the word places geomancy among grammar and medicine, things with textbooks. Its mechanism is "lot, or destiny," and the pairing is deliberate, because the whole question of geomancy is whether the falling of the points is mere lot or legible destiny; the author will spend the next two sentences arguing that, under the right conditions, the lot *is* the destiny. And its scope is "every question of every thing whatsoever": a maximal claim, matched by the practice of the manuals, which entertain questions from lost spoons to the fates of princes. Equally telling is what is absent: no restriction to the future, no restriction to the spiritually prepared, no hedging about "tendencies." Geomancy gives an answer.

> but the art hereof consisteth especially in certain points, whereof certain figures are deducted according to the reason or rule of equallity or inequallity, likeness or unlikeness; which figures are also reduced to the caelestial figures, assuming their natures and properties, according to the course and forms of the signs and planets.

This sentence gives the basic technical sequence, and it turns on two verbs. The figures are *deducted* (derived, drawn down) from the points "according to the reason or rule of equallity or inequallity": that is the odd-and-even arithmetic you met above, and calling it "reason or rule" insists that everything after the points is logic, not inspiration. The figures are then *reduced* to the celestial figures, referred upward, mapped onto the signs and planets, "assuming their natures and properties." This is the daughter-of-astrology rule, as plain as it ever gets: the figures do not have meanings of their own invention; they borrow their natures from the heavens. First the points are reduced by rule into figures. Then the figures are mapped by correspondence onto signs and planets.

> Notwithstanding this in the first place we are to consider, that this kind of art can declare or shew forth nothing of verity, unless it shall be radical in some divine virtue; and this the authors of this science have demonstrated to be too-fold.

"Notwithstanding" is the hinge of the whole page. Having described the mechanism, the author immediately denies that the mechanism suffices: geomancy "can declare or shew forth nothing of verity" (nothing true at all) "unless it shall be radical in some divine virtue." *Radical* here means rooted, from *radix*; the same word horary astrologers use to this day for a chart fit to read. Arithmetic done on noise yields elaborate noise; the points must be rooted in something above themselves or the figures deduced from them are decoration. The appeal to "the authors of this science" shows how the writer presents himself: as a reporter of the sources' self-understanding, not an innovator. The sources, he says, found the root twofold.

> the one whereof consists in religion and cerimonies; and therefore they will have the projecting of the points of this art to be made with signs in the earth: wherefore the art is appropriated to this element the earth, even as pyromancy to the fire, and hydromancy to the element of water… then whereas they judged the hand of the projector or worker to be most powerfully moved, and directed to the terrestrial spirits; and therefore they first used certain holy incantations and deprecations, with other rites and observations, provoking and alluring spirits of this nature hereunto.

The first root is ceremony and spirits. On this older account the points must be "signs in the earth," because geomancy belongs to the element of earth as pyromancy belongs to fire. The agency is external: terrestrial spirits, formally invoked, move the projector's hand. Divination here depends on ritual correctness. The tense and the distance are deliberate: *they* will have it so, *they* judged, *they* first used. The author is describing other men's practice, older practice, and edging away from it even as he records it, because he has something better to offer.

> Another power there is that doth direct and rule this lot or fortune, which is in the very soul itself of the projector, when he is carried to this work with some great egress of his own desire, for this art hath a natural obedience to the soul itself, and of necessity hath efficacy and is moved to that which the soul itself desires; and this way is by far more true and pure: neither matters it where or how those points are projected.

The second root is the desiring soul. The directing power is now *internal* ("in the very soul itself of the projector") and conditional on a state, not a rite: it operates "when he is carried to this work with some great egress of his own desire." *Egress* is a going-out: the desire must move toward the question strongly enough to carry the diviner. Under that condition geomancy "hath a natural obedience to the soul" and the ceremonial apparatus of the first account becomes unnecessary: "neither matters it where or how those points are projected." No earth, no incantation, no spirits are required; pen and paper at a desk work as well as sand, provided the desire is real. The author ranks the two accounts plainly ("this way is by far more true and pure"). In this account, the rule concerns what moves the hand, not where the hand moves.

> therefore this art hath the same radix with the art of Astrological questions: which also can no otherwise be verified, unless with a constant and excessive affection of the Querent himself.

The conclusion drawn ("therefore") is the family claim: geomancy and horary astrology share one root, *radix* again, and that root is the earnest question itself. Even astrology, the text says, "can no otherwise be verified" (made true, made readable) without "a constant and excessive affection of the Querent": constant, because a flicker of curiosity is not a question; excessive, because the desire must overflow the asker. The ardour of the querent is not decorative; it is a condition of geomancy. The opening has a clear shape: a definition (answer by lot on any question), a mechanism (points ruled into figures, figures referred to heaven), a denial that mechanism suffices (nothing of verity unless radical), two candidate roots (ceremony and spirits; the desiring soul), a choice between them (the soul, by far), and a genealogy (one root shared with astrology). Six sentences, and the metaphysics, the method, and the ethics of asking are all in place. Everything you met earlier in this lesson (Gerard's prohibition on casting in anger or for deriders, the rule against repeating a question, the irrelevance of medium, the borrowed astrological machinery) is already here in this page. That is what it means to read a source closely: geomancy's rules stop being arbitrary and become consequences of its theory.

<Exercise n={1} title="Why does geomancy work?" kind="quiz">
  Answer from the sources, not from yourself; a few words each, and cite where asked.

  1. The pseudo-Agrippa concedes that geomancy's machinery proves nothing by itself. What must it be "radical in" before it can declare anything of verity?
  2. Name the two roots that "the authors of this science" found for geomancy.
  3. Which root does the text prefer, and in what words?
  4. Because the root is the mover and not the medium, the text concludes that:
     - a) the points must still be made with signs in the earth
     - b) the place and manner of projection are indifferent
     - c) the incantations remain necessary but the earth does not
  5. What single condition verifies an astrological question, on the text's account, and what does the shared radix add to geomancy's claim? Cite the passage.
  6. For Gerard, what does the casting of points spare the practitioner that astrology requires?

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. "Some divine virtue": geomancy "can declare or shew forth nothing of verity, unless it shall be radical in some divine virtue" (Agrippa (attrib.), *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), opening).
    2. Religion and ceremonies, with invoked terrestrial spirits moving the hand; and the soul of the projector itself, carried by a "great egress of his own desire" (same place).
    3. The second, the soul: "this way is by far more true and pure" (same place).
    4. b). Under genuine desire geomancy "hath a natural obedience to the soul itself," so sand, paper, ceremony and location are all indifferent: "neither matters it where or how those points are projected" (same place).
    5. "A constant and excessive affection of the Querent himself" (same place). The shared radix attaches geomancy to the prestige of horary astrology and names the common root precisely: the earnest question, asked at a significant moment.
    6. Beholding the Ascendant and the hour in a table: "it is not requisite to behold neither the Ascendant, nor the hour in a Table, as it is in Astrology" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), introduction). The points replace the ephemeris.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={2} title="Three stances toward astrology" kind="quiz">
  The pseudo-Agrippa, Gerard, and Crowley all relate geomancy to astrology, but not in the same way. A few words each; cite where asked.

  1. Which author offers geomancy as a labour-saving substitute, so the student may judge "with less labour and study"?
     - a) the pseudo-Agrippa
     - b) Gerard of Cremona
     - c) Crowley
  2. In the pseudo-Agrippa, what do the figures borrow from the heavens, and by what operation? Cite the words.
  3. What does Gerard's method do with the first figure of a cast that makes his geomancy a quasi-horoscope?
  4. In Crowley's handbook, what has moved into the place the heavens once held as the source of the operation's power?
  5. Crowley's re-ceremonialised method stands closest to:
     - a) the pseudo-Agrippa's first root, ceremony and spirits
     - b) the pseudo-Agrippa's second root, the desiring soul
     - c) Gerard's astronomical method
  6. Which of the three is admitted to this course as a modern comparison only, carrying no older-source weight?

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. b). Astronomy is "so transcendant and subtil an art" that few can judge by it, so Gerard offers the same readings with fewer tools (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), introduction).
    2. Their natures and properties: the figures are "reduced to the caelestial figures, assuming their natures and properties, according to the course and forms of the signs and planets" (Agrippa (attrib.), *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), opening).
    3. It converts the first figure into a rising sign and erects a chart from it: "If *Acquisitio* arise from the heads of those four lines, let *Aries* be placed in the Ascendant" (Gerard, same work, introduction).
    4. Ceremonial procedure: virgin paper, the invoking pentagram, the planetary sigil, attention fixed on sigil and demand (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. II).
    5. a). Where the pseudo-Agrippa demoted ceremony in favour of the desiring soul, the Golden Dawn line restores ceremonial procedure, closer to the first root than the second.
    6. Crowley. The four old texts carry the main source weight; his handbook is quoted the way a modern editor is quoted in a critical edition.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

    The correct order, with dates:

    | Order | Event | Date |
    |---|---|---|
    | 1 | ʿIlm al-raml mature in the Islamic world | by the 12th century |
    | 2 | First Latin translations (Hugh of Santalla among the earliest) | mid-12th century |
    | 3 | Cattan's *La Géomancie* printed at Paris | 1558 |
    | 4 | Sparry's English Cattan, *The Geomancie* | 1591 |

Second, the medieval *Astronomical Geomancy* attributed to Gerard belongs by composition to the 12th–13th century, immediately after the first translations, even though you read it in a 1655 English dress, a reminder that every text in this course has two dates, composition and translation, and a citation names the edition, not the era.

<Exercise n={4} title="The conditions of asking" kind="quiz">
  Work from the casting-caution passage quoted in this lesson; a few words each, and cite where asked.

  1. List Gerard's four conditions on the asking of a question.
  2. Which of the four comes with no rationale anywhere in the sources?
     - a) the weather rule
     - b) the rule against anger and a busied mind
     - c) the rule against tempters and deriders
     - d) the rule against reiteration
  3. What positive requirement of the pseudo-Agrippa supplies the rationale for the rule against anger and distraction?
  4. Why is a figure cast for a tempter or derider void, on the sources' own theory?
  5. Why is renewing the same question "error"?
  6. What is Crowley's compressed modern echo of the positive condition? Cite it.

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. No casting in a rainy, cloudy or very windy season; none in anger or with a mind busied with many affairs; none for tempters or deriders; and no renewing the same question "under the same figure or form; for that is error" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), introduction).
    2. a). The weather rule stands bare; any rationale (rough weather troubling the channel) is reconstruction, not citation, and the Renaissance manuals do not uniformly repeat it.
    3. Geomancy obeys the soul only when the projector "is carried to this work with some great egress of his own desire" (Agrippa (attrib.), *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), opening). Anger and distraction are competing motions of the soul, and a soul moved by several things at once writes an illegible record.
    4. The scoffer has no earnest desire to know, so there is nothing for geomancy's "natural obedience to the soul" to obey; the figure is void, not merely rude.
    5. The moment of earnest asking is the moment the matter is legible. A second casting against a disliked answer is an appeal against the heavens, and the theory has no mechanism by which it could be valid: the desire attached to the question has already been answered.
    6. "Think fixedly of the demand" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. II), fixity of attention standing in for the old "constant and excessive affection." Flagged as a modern comparison.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={5} title="Prepare your journal" kind="practice">
  This exercise has no model answer; the journal itself is the answer.

  Prepare your casting journal now, three lessons before its first entry. Obtain a notebook (paper follows older practice and cannot be quietly revised) and rule its first page as a template for every casting to come. Decide now, while you have no chart to be tempted by, what every entry will record.

  Your journal is ready when:

  - it is a single dedicated notebook, not loose sheets, with your name and the start date inside the cover
  - your entry template records, in order: the date, time, and place of the casting; the question, in its exact final wording; the state in which you asked it, checked against Gerard's conditions: weather, your own mood and attention, the earnestness of the querent
  - the template reserves space for the full chart, every figure from Mothers to Judge
  - the template separates, unmistakably, the reading written before the outcome is known from the outcome and later notes
  - the inside cover carries the rule of Gerard you will be most tempted to break, copied out in full: the same question is not to be renewed and reiterated under the same figure or form
</Exercise>
