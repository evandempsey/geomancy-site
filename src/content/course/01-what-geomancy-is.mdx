Geomancy is a method of divination. The diviner holds a question in mind, pricks rows of points without counting them, and derives a chart of figures from the points by fixed rules. The sources say the points can record the question in a readable form, because the heavens act on the earth and on the person asking. This lesson explains that claim, gives the history of the method, and introduces the five sources the course is built on.

## Before you begin

They will show you tables of figures, schemes of houses, and rules for reading a chart that this lesson does not explain. ## What geomancy is

Geomancy answers questions. The questions are posed one at a time, in plain words: will I recover the money, is the absent man alive, will the suit go in my favour. The method takes the question, generates a chart by a procedure that is partly random and otherwise rule-bound, and returns an answer: yes or no, well or ill, and with practice a good deal of detail besides. The Renaissance manuals treat geomancy as a serious tool for serious matters: illness, theft, marriage, imprisonment, war, the faith of a wife, the fortune of a prince.

The procedure begins with points. The diviner, holding the question firmly in mind, makes sixteen rows of points without counting as the hand moves. In the older practice the points were pricked in sand or soil; later practice uses a pen. Each row is then counted: an odd row yields one point, an even row two. Four rows make a figure of four lines, so the sixteen rows yield four figures, called the Mothers. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa describing exactly this:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-mothers" />

A geomantic figure is a stack of four lines, and each line is either single or double. Four positions with two states each gives sixteen possible figures, no more and no fewer. Each figure has a Latin name and a settled character. <FigureGlyph dots={[1,1,1,1]} name="Via" /> Via, the Way, is the only figure of four single points. <FigureGlyph dots={[2,2,2,2]} name="Populus" /> Populus, the People, is the only figure of four doubles. Fourteen others stand between, such as <FigureGlyph dots={[2,1,2,1]} name="Acquisitio" /> Acquisitio, Gain, and <FigureGlyph dots={[1,2,1,2]} name="Amissio" /> Amissio, Loss. Lesson 2 teaches all sixteen, with their names, natures, and attributions.

The rest of the chart is built by derivation. The four Mothers are recombined by fixed rules to produce four Daughters. Mothers and Daughters are added together in pairs (simple parity: odd plus odd makes even) to produce four more figures. Those four yield two Witnesses, and the two Witnesses yield a single final figure, the Judge, which gives the overall answer. The pseudo-Agrippa compresses the whole derivation into a paragraph:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-daughters" />

The completed diagram of fifteen figures is called the shield chart, named for the shape of the frame the old books draw it in. It holds the Mothers, the Daughters, the four derived figures usually called Nieces, the Witnesses, and the Judge. Only the Mothers involve chance. Everything after them follows by rule, and on the sources' account the first sixteen rows are the one point where something other than chance is at work.

The figures are also distributed into the twelve astrological houses, the divisions that give every question its place: money to the second house, brothers to the third, marriage to the seventh. The figures are judged in their houses according to their natures. House-by-house entries fill most of all five sources, and they will fill most of this course.

Every step after the first sixteen rows is teachable, checkable, and mechanical. The sources are emphatic on this point. Geomancy is not free-form intuition over evocative symbols. It is a rule-bound method, and two competent geomancers given the same chart and question should reach much the same answer.

## The daughter of astrology

The Renaissance manuals call geomancy the daughter of astrology. From astrology it inherits nearly all of its interpretive tools: the twelve houses and their significations, the seven planets, the twelve signs, the aspects by which figures regard one another across the chart, and the dignities by which they are strong or weak in their places. In the sources' understanding, a geomantic figure is a planetary nature in a compressed notation. The pseudo-Agrippa assigns each figure to a planet, and through the planet to a sign. Gerard's method goes further: it uses the first figure to erect what is effectively a horoscope, placing a zodiac sign on the Ascendant and distributing the planets through the houses by counts of points.

Full medieval astrology required casting an exact figure of the heavens for an exact moment: tables, ephemerides, arithmetic, and years of training. Gerard opens his treatise with exactly this complaint. Astronomy, he says, is "so transcendant and subtil an art in itself" that few doctors of the age were experienced enough to judge by it soundly. His remedy is the treatise in hand, composed so that the student may "judge with less labour and study," because "in this present science it is not requisite to behold neither the Ascendant, nor the hour in a Table, as it is in Astrology" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), introduction).

So astrology computes the actual heavens, while geomancy builds a chart from the points and reads it with astrology's apparatus. It needs no clock or table; the moment of the question is recorded through the hand that pricks the points. Gerard means this literally. His first move converts the figure cast from the first four rows directly into a rising sign:

You don't need to learn that table now. This course teaches the shield chart of the main source line, not Gerard's horoscope method, which is a distinct and older branch of geomancy. The point to take is that, for the medieval geomancer, the chart of points was a figure of heaven and was read as one.

## Why the sources said it works

The sources have a theory of why geomancy works, and the rules of the method follow from it. You cannot practise geomancy intelligently without knowing the theory. For example, the rule that forbids asking the same question twice makes no sense without it.

The background is the layered cosmos that educated people of the twelfth through the seventeenth century took for granted. At the top is the divine mind, the source of all order. Below it are the heavens, which receive that order and transmit it downward as influence. This influence was understood as physics, not metaphor: the same physics by which the sun warms and the moon moves the tides. At the bottom is the elemental world of earth, continuously worked on by the heavens above. Binding the layers together is the principle the philosophers called the soul of the world, *anima mundi*. The genuine writings of Henry Cornelius Agrippa, the *Three Books of Occult Philosophy* (1533), teach this picture at length: the virtues of things below derive from things above, and the human soul, being itself of celestial origin, participates in both worlds at once. The geomantic treatise printed under Agrippa's name does not argue any of this. It assumes it, the way a modern engineering text assumes gravitation.

What the *Of Geomancy* does argue, on its opening page, is how that cosmos gets into the points. The text says geomancy "can declare or shew forth nothing of verity, unless it shall be radical in some divine virtue," and reports that the authors of the science found that root to be twofold. The first root is religious and ceremonial: the points are made in earth, geomancy belongs to the element of earth, and the hand of the projector is "most powerfully moved, and directed to the terrestrial spirits," whom the older practitioners invoked with incantations. The second root needs no incantation at all, and the text plainly prefers it:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/great-egress" />

On this second account, when the diviner genuinely wants to know, geomancy "hath a natural obedience to the soul itself." The unplanned motions of the hand are governed by the soul, and the points record that motion. The author draws the practical conclusion himself: "neither matters it where or how those points are projected." Sand, soil, paper, and pen all serve. The first root depends on ceremony and on earth as the medium; the second depends on the state of the person making the points. This is why the later sources can use pen and paper without treating the change of medium as a change of method.

The same sentence connects geomancy with horary astrology, the astrology of questions, in which a chart is cast for the moment a question is asked on the rule that a serious question has a meaningful moment. Geomancy has "the same radix," says the text. The difference is the recording tool: astrology reads the moment from the sky, geomancy records it through the points. The worked example below reads the whole opening page line by line.

Note what the theory is not. It is not psychological: nothing in these texts suggests the points reveal the contents of your own mind, or that the figures are prompts for intuition. It is not statistical: no source imagines that the odds of the figures carry information. The claim is metaphysical and specific. A soul moved by real desire is, at that moment, in contact with the order of the cosmos, and its least deliberate motions can be read. Every rule in the coming lessons is built around that theory: the well-formed question, the ban on idle or repeated castings, the requirement of attention at the moment of pricking.

## Conditions for casting

If the truth of the figure depends on the state of the asker, the diviner, and the moment of casting, then some conditions can invalidate the figure. Gerard names them at the close of his introduction:

<Quote id="gerard/casting-caution" />

There are four conditions.

First, the weather: no question in a rainy, cloudy, or very windy season. The text does not explain this rule. The simplest reading is that violent weather was thought to disturb the conditions of casting, but that is an inference, and the later manuals do not all repeat the rule.

Second, the diviner's own state: not when you are angry, and not when your mind is "busied with many affairs." The positive form of this rule is the pseudo-Agrippa's "great egress of desire," and Crowley's short modern version, "Think fixedly of the demand." The casting requires attention to the question.

Third, the querent: not "for tempters or deriders." On the sources' theory the figure answers an earnest desire to know. Where no such desire exists, the casting has no proper question.

Fourth, and most practical: do not "renew and reiterate the same Question again under the same figure or form; for that is error." One question, one casting. The moment of asking is the moment at which the matter is legible, and asking again because you disliked the answer is no longer inquiry. When you begin your own practice in Lesson 4, this rule will be the first entry in your casting journal's code of conduct.

None of the four conditions concerns arithmetic; the derivation works the same way in any weather and any mood. The conditions concern the sixteen rows, the only part of the procedure not determined by rule. On the sources' account, that first act is where the question enters the chart, so that is the act the conditions protect.

## Short history

Geomancy's documented history begins in the Islamic world, where it is called *ʿilm al-raml*, the science of the sand. By the twelfth century it was mature there: a settled set of sixteen figures with Arabic names, the derivation of a full chart from four randomly generated figures, manuals for reading, and a legend tracing the science back through the prophet Idris (identified with Enoch and with Hermes) to instruction by the angel Jibril. The origins behind the legend are obscure. Arguments have been made both for development within the Arabic-speaking world and for older roots, and this course will not pretend the question is settled. What is certain is that the Latin West received an Arabic system whole: the European figures are the Arabic figures, the derivation is the Arabic derivation, and several Latin names are straight translations. The same family of figures also lives on in West African divination and in the sikidy of Madagascar, though those branches are beyond this course's scope.

Geomancy crossed into Latin in the twelfth century, in the great age of translation from Arabic, alongside Aristotle, Euclid, and Ptolemy. Around mid-century Hugh of Santalla produced his *Ars geomantiae*, among the earliest Latin geomancies, and others followed quickly. By the thirteenth century original Latin treatises were multiplying, and geomancy became the most practised divination system of medieval Europe after astrology itself: taught in the textbook of pseudo-Pietro d'Abano, condemned by preachers, copied into the notebooks of physicians and princes. Our oldest assigned text belongs to this world: the *Astronomical Geomancy* circulating under the name of Gerard of Cremona.

Gerard of Cremona (c. 1114–1187) was the most prolific of the Toledo translators; Europe received Ptolemy's *Almagest* and much of Arabic medicine and astronomy through his Latin. Whether he wrote or translated the *Astronomical Geomancy* is another matter. The attribution rests on later manuscripts and printers. Scholars have long suspected either confusion with Gerard of Sabbioneta, a thirteenth-century astrologer, or simple pseudepigraphy, the medieval habit of crediting a useful text to a famous name. The truth cannot be recovered. This course cites the text as "attributed," uses "Gerard" as a label of convenience, and asks you to remember that the label names a book, not a certainty about its author. The book itself is plainly medieval, plainly drawn from the Arabic source line, and no worse a source for being anonymous.

The printing press carried geomancy to its Renaissance peak, and the works this course is built on now enter one by one. In 1558 *La Géomancie* of Christofe de Cattan, an Italian gentleman writing in French, appeared at Paris: a complete, orderly, opinionated manual of the shield-chart method in three books, and the most influential geomancy of its century. In 1591 Francis Sparry's English translation, *The Geomancie of Maister Christopher Cattan*, gave geomancy its first great English textbook. Cattan teaches the figures, the houses, the Witnesses and Judge, the aspects, and the movements of figures about the chart, with worked examples from his own practice. When this course teaches chart reading in Part 2, the method will be Cattan's more than anyone else's.

In 1555, twenty years after Agrippa's death, a Latin *Fourth Book of Occult Philosophy* began to circulate under the name of Henry Cornelius Agrippa, presented as completing his genuine *Three Books*. With it travelled a compact treatise, *Of Geomancy*. The attribution was challenged almost at once: Johann Weyer, Agrippa's own pupil and secretary, denounced the Fourth Book as a forgery. Modern scholarship agrees about the Fourth Book but remains uncertain about the geomancy. Agrippa did write a geomancy; he says so in his sceptical *De vanitate*, adding sourly that his own was no less superstitious than the rest. Whether the printed treatise is that work, a reworking of it, or another man's text under his name cannot be proven. This course cites it as "the *Of Geomancy* attributed to Agrippa," or "the pseudo-Agrippa" for short. The prefix marks doubt, not condemnation: its opening pages contain the clearest statement of why geomancy works in the whole literature, which is why this lesson's worked example is built on them. In 1655 Robert Turner translated the Fourth Book and its companions into English, binding the pseudo-Agrippa and the Gerard between the same covers. His translation is the form in which both texts have been read ever since, and the form this site presents.

In 1663–4 came the largest English treatise geomancy would ever receive: John Heydon's *Theomagia, or the Temple of Wisdome*, a sprawling three-part compendium presented as Rosicrucian. Heydon was a compiler, and an unacknowledged one. Great stretches of the *Theomagia* are Cattan's English text and Turner's pseudo-Agrippa reprinted nearly verbatim, with names changed, angels added, and no credit given. This site's quotation apparatus marks the borrowings wherever they occur, and the next section shows one. The compilation still has value: Heydon preserves, consolidates, and occasionally completes his sources, and his house-by-house entries are the most exhaustive in English.

After Heydon, geomancy declined along with the worldview that had supported it, surviving the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries mainly in chapbooks, until the occult revival of the late nineteenth century took it up again. The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn, founded in London in 1888, made geomancy part of its standard curriculum, recasting the old method in its own ceremonial idiom (ruling spirits, sigils, invoking pentagrams) while keeping the sixteen figures, the shield chart, and the astrological houses. In 1909 Aleister Crowley printed a terse handbook of this Golden Dawn geomancy in his journal *The Equinox*, as *Liber Gaias: A Handbook of Geomancy*. It is the fifth source of this course, admitted on special terms: as a modern comparison, never with the weight of the older texts. Where Crowley agrees with the Renaissance texts he shows continuity; where he differs he shows what the revival changed. In neither case does he outvote Cattan. The course will always flag him as modern when he is assigned, and you should do the same.

## The five sources, and why this course teaches disagreements

Everything in this course is grounded in five public-domain texts, all reproduced or quoted at length on this site, with every quotation cited and traceable.

- **Cattan's *Geomancie* (1591)** is the backbone: the fullest Renaissance manual in English and the course's primary source for method.
- **Heydon's *Theomagia* (1663)** is the storehouse: derivative in its teaching but unmatched in coverage, with exhaustive entries for every figure in every house.
- **The *Of Geomancy* attributed to Agrippa (1655)** supplies the theory of geomancy in its opening and a complete, frequently independent set of house readings in its body.
- **Gerard's *Astronomical Geomancy* (1655 English)** is the medieval source: an older branch of geomancy, valuable for its horoscope-style method and for preserving rules, including the casting cautions above, that the Renaissance manuals assume rather than state.
- **Crowley's *Handbook of Geomancy* (1909)** is the modern comparison: a record of what the Golden Dawn revival kept, dropped, and invented, always flagged as a comparison.

Five sources spanning five centuries do not speak with one voice. This course teaches the disagreements rather than flattening them, and you should learn that habit before you learn any single fact. Two examples show it.

First, sources disagree with each other, and sometimes with themselves. The pseudo-Agrippa assigns each figure a zodiac sign through its planet: Acquisitio, a figure of Jupiter, receives Pisces, a house of Jupiter.

<Quote id="agrippa/general/signs" />

A page later the same treatise reports, without endorsing it, the distribution "they do likewise" use, and in that scheme Acquisitio belongs to Aries:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/zodiacal-distribution" />

Gerard's table, which you saw earlier in this lesson, agrees with the second scheme and puts Acquisitio's questioner under Aries. Crowley's Golden Dawn table assigns Acquisitio to Sagittarius. So one figure has three signs across four source claims. A course that silently picked one and taught it as "the" attribution would be tidier and falser. Lesson 2 teaches which attributions are stable across the whole source set (the planetary rulerships scarcely waver), which are contested, and how a practising geomancer chooses a scheme and stays consistent within it. Note also the editorial note on the first quotation above. It flags an error in the 1655 printing, "Puella and Rubeus" for "Puer and Rubeus," visible because the same sentence has already given Puella to Taurus. Even single sources must be read critically.

Second, sources copy each other, and you must know who is echoing whom before you count voices. Here is Heydon teaching the Judge:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/the-judge" />

That paragraph is Cattan's chapter on the Judge (*The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 4) reproduced nearly word for word, seventy years later, without acknowledgment. The editorial note beneath the quotation says so, as the site's notes always do where Heydon borrows. So when Cattan and Heydon agree, that is usually one source line, not two. Independent agreement, such as Cattan and the pseudo-Agrippa arriving at the same reading from different source lines, is strong support; an echo shows only what Heydon read. The exercises in this course will repeatedly ask not just *what* the sources say but *how many* of them are really speaking.

## Worked example

The skill this lesson teaches is reading a primary text closely enough to extract its theory, including the theory it assumes without stating. Here we take the opening of the *Of Geomancy* attributed to Agrippa, the most theoretically explicit page in the literature, sentence by sentence. The [full text](/library/agrippa/of-geomancy/) is in the library.

> GEOMANCY is an art of divination, whereby the judgment may be rendered by lot, or destiny to every question of every thing whatsoever.
>
> — Agrippa (attrib.), *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), opening

The definition packs in three claims. *Art* means a teachable discipline with rules, not a gift; the word places geomancy among grammar and medicine, things with textbooks. The mechanism is "lot, or destiny," and the pairing is deliberate: the whole question of geomancy is whether the falling of the points is mere lot or legible destiny, and the author spends the next two sentences arguing that, under the right conditions, the lot *is* the destiny. The scope is "every question of every thing whatsoever," a maximal claim matched by the manuals, which take questions from lost spoons to the fates of princes. Note also what is absent: no restriction to the future, no restriction to the spiritually prepared, no hedging about "tendencies." Geomancy gives an answer.

> but the art hereof consisteth especially in certain points, whereof certain figures are deducted according to the reason or rule of equallity or inequallity, likeness or unlikeness; which figures are also reduced to the caelestial figures, assuming their natures and properties, according to the course and forms of the signs and planets.

This sentence gives the technical sequence, and it turns on two verbs. The figures are *deducted*, that is, derived, from the points "according to the reason or rule of equallity or inequallity": the odd-and-even arithmetic you met above. Calling it "reason or rule" insists that everything after the points is logic, not inspiration. The figures are then *reduced* to the celestial figures, mapped onto the signs and planets, "assuming their natures and properties." This is the daughter-of-astrology rule stated plainly: the figures do not invent their own meanings, they borrow their natures from the heavens. So the sequence is points reduced by rule into figures, then figures mapped by correspondence onto signs and planets.

> Notwithstanding this in the first place we are to consider, that this kind of art can declare or shew forth nothing of verity, unless it shall be radical in some divine virtue; and this the authors of this science have demonstrated to be too-fold.

"Notwithstanding" is the hinge of the page. Having described the mechanism, the author immediately denies that the mechanism suffices: geomancy "can declare or shew forth nothing of verity" unless it is "radical in some divine virtue." *Radical* means rooted, from *radix*; horary astrologers still use the same word for a chart fit to read. The points must be rooted in something above themselves, or the figures derived from them are decoration. The appeal to "the authors of this science" shows how the writer presents himself: as a reporter of the sources, not an innovator. The sources, he says, found the root twofold.

> the one whereof consists in religion and cerimonies; and therefore they will have the projecting of the points of this art to be made with signs in the earth: wherefore the art is appropriated to this element the earth, even as pyromancy to the fire, and hydromancy to the element of water… then whereas they judged the hand of the projector or worker to be most powerfully moved, and directed to the terrestrial spirits; and therefore they first used certain holy incantations and deprecations, with other rites and observations, provoking and alluring spirits of this nature hereunto.

The first root is ceremony and spirits. On this older account the points must be "signs in the earth," because geomancy belongs to the element of earth as pyromancy belongs to fire. The agency is external: terrestrial spirits, formally invoked, move the projector's hand, so divination depends on ritual correctness. Note the distance in the writing: *they* will have it so, *they* judged, *they* first used. The author is describing other men's older practice, and he has a better account to offer.

> Another power there is that doth direct and rule this lot or fortune, which is in the very soul itself of the projector, when he is carried to this work with some great egress of his own desire, for this art hath a natural obedience to the soul itself, and of necessity hath efficacy and is moved to that which the soul itself desires; and this way is by far more true and pure: neither matters it where or how those points are projected.

The second root is the desiring soul. The directing power is now internal, "in the very soul itself of the projector," and it depends on a state rather than a rite: it operates "when he is carried to this work with some great egress of his own desire." *Egress* is a going-out; the desire must move toward the question strongly enough to carry the diviner. Under that condition geomancy "hath a natural obedience to the soul," and the ceremonial apparatus becomes unnecessary: "neither matters it where or how those points are projected." Pen and paper at a desk work as well as sand, provided the desire is real. The author ranks the two accounts plainly: "this way is by far more true and pure."

> therefore this art hath the same radix with the art of Astrological questions: which also can no otherwise be verified, unless with a constant and excessive affection of the Querent himself.

The conclusion ("therefore") is the family claim: geomancy and horary astrology share one root, *radix* again, and that root is the earnest question itself. Even astrology, the text says, "can no otherwise be verified" without "a constant and excessive affection of the Querent": constant, because a flicker of curiosity is not a question; excessive, because the desire must overflow the asker.

The opening as a whole has a clear shape: a definition (answer by lot on any question), a mechanism (points ruled into figures, figures referred to heaven), a denial that mechanism suffices (nothing of verity unless radical), two candidate roots (ceremony and spirits; the desiring soul), a choice between them (the soul, by far), and a genealogy (one root shared with astrology). Six sentences, and the metaphysics, the method, and the ethics of asking are all in place. Everything you met earlier in this lesson is already here: Gerard's prohibition on casting in anger or for deriders, the rule against repeating a question, the irrelevance of medium, and the borrowed astrological apparatus. Read a source this closely and geomancy's rules stop being arbitrary; they become consequences of its theory.

  6. For Gerard, what does the casting of points spare the practitioner that astrology requires?

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. "Some divine virtue": geomancy "can declare or shew forth nothing of verity, unless it shall be radical in some divine virtue" (Agrippa (attrib.), *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), opening).
    2. Religion and ceremonies, with invoked terrestrial spirits moving the hand; and the soul of the projector itself, carried by a "great egress of his own desire" (same place).
    3. The second, the soul: "this way is by far more true and pure" (same place).
    4. b). Under genuine desire geomancy "hath a natural obedience to the soul itself," so sand, paper, ceremony and location are all indifferent: "neither matters it where or how those points are projected" (same place).
    5. "A constant and excessive affection of the Querent himself" (same place). The shared radix attaches geomancy to the standing of horary astrology and names the common root precisely: the earnest question, asked at a significant moment.
    6. Beholding the Ascendant and the hour in a table: "it is not requisite to behold neither the Ascendant, nor the hour in a Table, as it is in Astrology" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), introduction). The points replace the ephemeris.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={3} title="Source order" kind="drill">
  Closed book, arrange the following in chronological order, with an approximate date for each: Sparry's English Cattan; Crowley's *Handbook of Geomancy*; the maturity of ʿilm al-raml in the Islamic world; Heydon's *Theomagia*; the first Latin translations of geomancy; Turner's English translation of the pseudo-Agrippa and Gerard; Cattan's French *Géomancie*; the founding of the Golden Dawn. Then, open book, check yourself against the lesson and note any event you misplaced by more than a step.

  <ModelAnswer>
    The correct order, with dates:

    | Order | Event | Date |
    |---|---|---|
    | 1 | ʿIlm al-raml mature in the Islamic world | by the 12th century |
    | 2 | First Latin translations (Hugh of Santalla among the earliest) | mid-12th century |
    | 3 | Cattan's *La Géomancie* printed at Paris | 1558 |
    | 4 | Sparry's English Cattan, *The Geomancie* | 1591 |
    | 5 | Turner's English pseudo-Agrippa and Gerard | 1655 |
    | 6 | Heydon's *Theomagia* | 1663–4 |
    | 7 | Golden Dawn founded | 1888 |
    | 8 | Crowley's *A Handbook of Geomancy* in *The Equinox* | 1909 |

    The drill has two traps. First, the Latin *Fourth Book* attributed to Agrippa began circulating in the 1550s, but the *English* texts of the pseudo-Agrippa and Gerard date from Turner's 1655 translation, a full lifetime after Sparry's 1591 Cattan. If you placed "Turner" before "Sparry" because Agrippa died (1535) before Cattan published, you confused the dates of authors with the dates of the books this course uses. Second, the *Astronomical Geomancy* belongs by composition to the 12th–13th century, even though you read it in Turner's 1655 English. Every text in this course has two dates, composition and translation, and a citation names the edition, not the era.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={4} title="The conditions of asking" kind="quiz">
  Work from the casting-caution passage quoted in this lesson; a few words each, and cite where asked.

  1. List Gerard's four conditions on the asking of a question.
  2. Which of the four comes with no rationale anywhere in the sources?
     - a) the weather rule
     - b) the rule against anger and a busied mind
     - c) the rule against tempters and deriders
     - d) the rule against reiteration
  3. What positive requirement of the pseudo-Agrippa supplies the rationale for the rule against anger and distraction?
  4. Why is a figure cast for a tempter or derider void, on the sources' own theory?
  5. Why is renewing the same question "error"?
  6. What is Crowley's compressed modern echo of the positive condition? Cite it.

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. No casting in a rainy, cloudy or very windy season; none in anger or with a mind busied with many affairs; none for tempters or deriders; and no renewing the same question "under the same figure or form; for that is error" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), introduction).
    2. a). The weather rule stands bare; any rationale (rough weather disturbing the conditions of casting) is reconstruction, not citation, and the Renaissance manuals do not uniformly repeat it.
    3. Geomancy obeys the soul only when the projector "is carried to this work with some great egress of his own desire" (Agrippa (attrib.), *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner (London, 1655), opening). Anger and distraction are competing motions of the soul, and a soul moved by several things at once writes an illegible record.
    4. The scoffer has no earnest desire to know, so there is nothing for geomancy's "natural obedience to the soul" to obey; the figure is void, not merely rude.
    5. The moment of earnest asking is the moment the matter is legible. A second casting against a disliked answer repeats a question that has already been answered, and the theory has no mechanism by which the repetition could be valid.
    6. "Think fixedly of the demand" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. II), fixity of attention standing in for the old "constant and excessive affection." Flagged as a modern comparison.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={5} title="Prepare your journal" kind="practice">
  This exercise has no model answer; the journal itself is the answer.

  Prepare your casting journal now, three lessons before its first entry. Get a notebook (paper follows older practice and cannot be quietly revised) and rule its first page as a template for every casting to come. Decide now, while you have no chart to be tempted by, what every entry will record.

  Your journal is ready when:

  - it is a single dedicated notebook, not loose sheets, with your name and the start date inside the cover
  - your entry template records, in order: the date, time, and place of the casting; the question, in its exact final wording; the state in which you asked it, checked against Gerard's conditions: weather, your own mood and attention, the earnestness of the querent
  - the template reserves space for the full chart, every figure from Mothers to Judge
  - the template separates, unmistakably, the reading written before the outcome is known from the outcome and later notes
  - the inside cover carries the rule of Gerard you will be most tempted to break, copied out in full: the same question is not to be renewed and reiterated under the same figure or form
</Exercise>
