This lesson teaches the whole operation of casting: from framing a question, through sixteen lines of points made without counting, to the fifteen figures of the shield chart. You will perform it by hand until it is automatic. Everything the rest of the course does is done *to* a cast chart.

This lesson is procedure, and procedure is learned in the hands, not the head: you can read the rules for deriving a Daughter in two minutes, but you will derive several dozen before your hand does it unsupervised, and the exercises are built accordingly. It also opens your casting journal, the record of real charts for real questions you will keep to the end of the course. The journal begins here because casting begins here; the judgment of what you cast begins in Lesson 5.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes complete command of Lessons 1 through 3: you can name any figure from its dots and draw any figure from its name without hesitation, you know each figure's planet, element, mobility and character, and you hold Lesson 1's doctrine of why a handful of points should answer at all. If any of that has gone soft, repair it first. The casting procedure is a chain of small operations performed on figures, and a student who must pause to remember whether Puer has its two points on the water line will make errors at every link.

You will also want the physical tools settled before you start: a pen, unlined or lightly lined paper, and the notebook that will become your journal. The tradition cast in sand or earth (the art is named for it) but paper has served geomancers for centuries and will serve you. What matters is not the medium but the conditions.

## The question

A geomantic chart is cast *about something*. The something is the question, and the question is not a formality to be got through on the way to the interesting part. It is half the operation. The figures that fall will be read against the question; a vague question produces a chart that cannot be read against anything, and the fault will lie not in the figures but in the asking.

A well-formed geomantic question has three properties. It is **single**: it asks one thing, not a braid of things. "Shall I take the position, and will the money be good, and will my family be content there?" is three questions wearing one question mark; each must be cast separately or not at all. It is **concrete**: it concerns a particular matter with a particular outcome (a marriage, a journey, a lawsuit, a sum of money) rather than a mood or an abstraction. "Will things get better?" gives the chart nothing to grip. And it is **earnest**: the querent actually wants to know, and is prepared to hear either answer. The doctrine of Lesson 1 is not decoration here but mechanism. The tradition held that the chart is true because the soul of the querent, moved by real desire, moves the hand that makes the points; a question asked idly, or in mockery, or to test the art, severs the very connection the art depends on. The pseudo-Agrippa says plainly that the whole science "can declare or shew forth nothing of verity" unless it is rooted in a divine virtue, and that the surer of its two roots is the soul of the projector carried to the work by a great egress of desire. No desire, no root; no root, no verity.

To these the sources add cautions about the occasion. Re-read the opening of Gerard of Cremona's *Astronomical Geomancy*, which is in this lesson's assigned reading; the heart of it is this:

<Quote id="gerard/casting-caution" />

The weather conditions (rain, cloud, strong wind) belong to the medieval picture in which the heavens stamp the moment, and a troubled sky is a troubled stamp; you may take them literally or take them as emblems of disturbance, but the tradition recorded them and this course reports what the tradition recorded. The inward conditions are not negotiable on any reading: do not cast when angry, and do not cast when the mind is "busied with many affairs," because a hand moved by agitation is being moved by the agitation and not by the question. Do not cast "for tempters or deriders" (for people who ask in order to mock, or to catch the art out) because their asking has no earnestness in it. And do not ask the same question twice "under the same figure or form, for that is error." This last is the one modern students break most often. A chart that gives an unwelcome answer is not a malfunction to be retried; asking again is not persistence, it is the refusal to hear, and the tradition is unanimous that repeated castings of one question yield noise. If circumstances genuinely change (new facts, a new stage of the matter) a new question may be framed about the new situation. But the same question, re-asked in the hope of a better answer, is forbidden, and the prohibition is as old as the art.

There is one further caution, which belongs to the casting stage though it is applied after the chart is made. The Golden Dawn line of the tradition, which you meet in Crowley's handbook, holds that certain charts announce their own unfitness:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/destroy-rule" />

Whether you adopt this rule is a question for the judgment lessons, where it will be weighed with the other tests of a chart's fitness; the tradition contains it, and "destroy it and wait" was a live instruction, not a metaphor. The general principle beneath all of these rules is the same: the chart is only as sound as the moment of its casting, and the geomancer's first skill is knowing when not to cast.

When the question is framed, write it down (in full, in the exact words you are asking) together with the date, the time, and the place. Then hold it in your mind, and begin.

## The sixteen lines and the four Mothers

The mechanical heart of the art is almost absurdly simple, and the sources describe it in a few sentences. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-mothers" />

Unpacked into modern instructions: you will make **sixteen rows of points** on the paper, working each row **from right to left**, one row beneath another. The right-to-left direction is constant across the sources (the art came through Arabic, and kept the Arabic hand) and you should keep it, because a fixed ritual of movement helps the hand run unsupervised. For each row, set down a series of points (jabs of the pen, dashes, marks in sand, it does not matter) **without counting them**, the mind held on the question and not on the marks. More than four points and fewer than about sixteen is the practical range; what matters is only that you genuinely do not know, while making the row, whether its count is odd or even. When sixteen rows stand on the page, the random work is done and the rest of the operation is pure rule.

Why sixteen rows made blind? Because each row will contribute exactly one piece of information (whether its count is odd or even) and four figures of four lines each require sixteen such pieces. The blindness is the doctrine of Lesson 1 in practice: the answer enters through the unguarded hand, so the hand must actually be unguarded. A geomancer who half-counts while marking, steering rows toward odd or even, is recording his own preferences, not an impression. Crowley's compressed instruction (think fixedly of the demand, and do not lift the hand from the paper until the lines are complete) says in one sentence what the whole tradition assumes: attention on the question, none on the points.

Now count. Take each row in order and count its points; you care only about the parity. **An odd count yields one point; an even count yields two.** Each row therefore collapses into a single geomantic line. The sixteen rows, read in groups of four from the top, yield four figures of four lines each: the first four rows give the first figure (first row its fire line, second its air line, third its water line, fourth its earth line), rows five through eight the second figure, and so on. These four figures are the **Mothers**, and Agrippa's word for what they do is exact: they "bring forth the rest." Every one of the remaining eleven figures of the chart is computed from the Mothers by fixed rules. The randomness, or, in the tradition's terms, the celestial influence, enters the chart in the sixteen rows and nowhere else. Everything after this point is arithmetic, and arithmetic must be done *correctly*, because an error in derivation is not a different answer, it is no answer.

Write the four Mothers side by side, and (this is another habit worth fixing early) write them **from right to left**, the First Mother at the far right, the Fourth at the far left. The shield chart is laid out this way in every source, and the habit will save you confusion when you begin reading historical examples.

## The Daughters: the Mothers turned on their side

The next four figures cost nothing new from the heavens; they are the Mothers' own lines redistributed. Agrippa again:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-daughters" />

The rule is a transposition: the Mothers read sideways. Take the **head lines** (the fire lines) of the four Mothers in order: First Mother's head, Second Mother's head, Third, Fourth. Stack these four lines downward in that order and they form a new figure: the **First Daughter**. The four **air lines** of the Mothers, stacked the same way, form the Second Daughter; the four water lines the Third Daughter; the four earth lines the Fourth Daughter. Nothing is added, nothing combined: every line of every Daughter *is* a line of some Mother, relocated. If you imagine the four Mothers written side by side as a four-by-four grid of lines, the Daughters are simply that grid read in rows instead of columns.

Take an example before the worked chart. Suppose the four Mothers are Puer, Amissio, Albus and Populus, the same four the structural diagrams on this site use. Their head lines, in order, are one point, one point, two points, two points; stacked downward that is the figure with lines 1, 1, 2, 2 (Fortuna Minor) and that is the First Daughter. With four figures written side by side, the eye runs across the top line of all four Mothers and reads off a figure, across the second another, and so on. Two practical consequences of the transposition are worth noticing now. First, the Daughters contain exactly the same sixteen lines as the Mothers (the same points, rearranged) so the total number of points among the Daughters always equals the total among the Mothers. That fact matters later in this lesson. Second, an error in one line of one Mother corrupts exactly one line of one Daughter, a precision we will exploit when we study how mistakes propagate.

The Daughters take their place in the shield to the *left* of the Mothers, continuing the right-to-left order: positions five through eight.

## The Nieces: figures added together

With the Daughters the chart stops copying and starts combining. The next four figures, which the sources call by various names (this course follows the common English usage and calls them the **Nieces**, Agrippa's *nepotes*, sometimes Englished as Nephews), are made by **adding figures together in pairs**, and the addition rule is the most-used operation in geomancy.

To add two figures, take them line by line: the two fire lines together, the two air lines, the two water lines, the two earth lines. For each pair of lines, count the combined points. **If the combined count is even, the resulting line has two points; if odd, one point.** Since each line holds either one or two points, only three cases exist:

| First line | Second line | Combined points | Resulting line |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ● | ● | 2, even | ● ● |
| ● ● | ● ● | 4, even | ● ● |
| ● | ● ● | 3, odd | ● |

In other words: **two like lines give a double line; two unlike lines give a single line.** That compressed form is how you will actually compute; underneath it is the tradition's way: you are pooling the points of the two lines and asking whether the pool is even or uneven, the same operation you performed on the sixteen rows. The whole chart is parity from top to bottom: rows collapse to lines by oddness and evenness, and figures combine by oddness and evenness. A modern reader will recognise addition modulo two; a medieval geomancer simply saw the same rule of "even or uneven" applied at every stage, and both descriptions are exactly right.

The four Nieces are formed from adjacent pairs, in the order the figures stand in the shield: the **First Niece** is the First Mother added to the Second; the **Second Niece** is the Third Mother added to the Fourth; the **Third Niece** is the First Daughter added to the Second; the **Fourth Niece** is the Third Daughter added to the Fourth. They occupy positions nine through twelve, written in a row beneath the Mothers and Daughters, each Niece sitting under the pair that produced it. Agrippa describes precisely this pairing in the passage above (out of the first and second figures the ninth, out of the third and fourth the tenth, and so on) "by the combination or joining together of two figures, according to the rule of the even or uneven number."

Practise the addition until it is a reflex. Take any two figures from your Lesson 2 flashcards and add them; check by re-deriving. You will quickly notice useful small facts: adding any figure to itself gives Populus (every pair of lines is alike); adding any figure to Populus gives the figure back again (Populus's double lines never change a parity); adding any figure to Via inverts every line. These are not curiosities: Populus and Via behave this way *because* they are pure evenness and pure oddness, and the behaviour will matter when you come to judge charts in which they appear.

## Witnesses and Judge

The same addition, applied twice more, finishes the shield. The First Niece added to the Second yields the **Right Witness**; the Third Niece added to the Fourth yields the **Left Witness**; and the Right Witness added to the Left yields the **Judge**, the fifteenth and final figure, which stands alone at the foot of the chart. Cattan, whose account of these three figures the later English tradition reprints nearly word for word, explains what they are for:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-03-of-the-two-witnesses-chap" />

Note Cattan's structural point, because it explains the names: the Right Witness descends, through the Nieces, from the four Mothers, the right-hand side of the shield, the side of the querent and the question's origin; the Left Witness descends from the four Daughters, the left side, the side of the thing asked about. The two Witnesses are "no houses, neither natural figures, but be only accidentals": they do not signify places or persons the way the twelve figures above them do, but summarise their respective sides so that the Judge may weigh them. And of the Judge itself:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-04-of-the-judge-chap" />

"If the Judge be good, the signification of the demand shall come to a good end, and if he be ill, it shall come to an ill end." That is the Judge's office, and it is why the figure earned its name. The full doctrine of reading Witnesses and Judge belongs to the judgment lessons; what belongs to *this* lesson is one clause of Cattan's that most students skim past: "The which Judge ought always of necessity to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false." That clause gets its own section below; first, the finished structure.

## The shield assembled

Fifteen figures now stand on the page, and the traditional arrangement of them is called the **shield chart**. The four Mothers fill the top row's right half, the four Daughters its left half, all eight running right to left in order of birth. The four Nieces form the second row, each beneath its parents. The two Witnesses form the third row, and the Judge sits centred at the bottom. The structure is a tournament of additions, every figure below the top row standing directly under the two figures that made it:

<ShieldChart />

From the top down, the diagram shows the entire derivation: sixteen rows of points collapse into four Mothers; the Mothers transpose into four Daughters; pairs collapse into Nieces, Nieces into Witnesses, Witnesses into the Judge. Fifteen figures from one act of the hand. The shield is the working document of the geomancer (historical manuscripts are full of them) and you should draw the frame freehand in your journal for every cast until it is second nature.

## Why the Judge is always even

Cattan says the Judge "ought always of necessity to be even" (that is, the Judge's four lines always contain an even number of points in total) and that an uneven Judge means "the figure should be false." This is not an observation about most charts but a theorem about all of them, and it is the first piece of real structure the shield has shown. The argument is worth following in full.

Begin with one small fact about the addition rule. When two figures are added, look at any single line of the result and ask how its points relate to the points of the two parent lines beneath it. If the parents held one and one, the child holds two, and one plus one is two. If the parents held two and two, the child holds two, and two plus two is four. If the parents held one and two, the child holds one, and one plus two is three. In every case the child line's count is even exactly when the parents' combined count is even, and odd exactly when it is odd. The child does not preserve the parents' *number* of points, but it always preserves their *evenness or oddness*, line by line, and therefore in total. **A figure made by addition has an even number of points precisely when its two parents, taken together, have an even number of points.** That is the only tool the proof needs.

Now apply it up the shield, starting at the bottom. The Judge is made by adding the two Witnesses; so the Judge's count is even exactly when the two Witnesses together hold an even count. Each Witness is made by adding two Nieces; so the Witnesses together hold an even count exactly when the four Nieces together do. Each Niece is made by adding two of the figures in the top row; so the four Nieces together are even exactly when all eight top-row figures (the four Mothers and the four Daughters) together hold an even number of points. The whole question of the Judge's parity has been pushed up to the top of the shield: **the Judge is even if, and only if, the Mothers and Daughters together carry an even number of points.**

And they always do, for the reason you noted when you learned the transposition. The Daughters are the Mothers' own sixteen lines, rearranged: every point in the four Mothers appears in exactly one Daughter, and every point in the Daughters came from exactly one Mother. So the Daughters' total is the *same number* as the Mothers'. The top row of the shield therefore contains every point of the four Mothers **exactly twice**: once where it stands in its Mother, and once where it stands relocated in a Daughter. Whatever the Mothers' total may be, odd or even, twice that total is even. The top row's combined count is always even; therefore, by the chain just built, so is the Judge's. There is no chart, among the 65,536 possible sets of sixteen rows, whose Judge is odd. The points of the Mothers are counted twice in the Judge's ancestry, and a doubled count cannot be uneven.

You can watch the theorem operate in any chart by tallying points down the shield. In the worked example below, the four Mothers carry twenty-one points (an odd total, which is perfectly permissible) and the four Daughters, being the same lines rearranged, carry twenty-one as well: forty-two together, even. The four Nieces carry twenty-two: a different number, but even, as the chain requires. The two Witnesses carry fourteen; the Judge carries six. Even, even, even, all the way down, not because the example is special but because no example can do otherwise.

Only eight of the sixteen figures have an even point-count, so only eight can ever stand as Judge: Populus, Via, Conjunctio, Carcer, Acquisitio, Amissio, Fortuna Major and Fortuna Minor. The other eight (the seven-pointed and five-pointed figures) can appear anywhere else in the chart, but never at its foot. The four figures that are their own counterparts in the pairing you studied in Lesson 2 (Populus, Via, Conjunctio and Carcer) all stand among the eight, and the other four make up the two counterpart pairs Acquisitio–Amissio and Fortuna Major–Fortuna Minor; the art's symmetries will keep surfacing as the course proceeds.

The theorem has a thoroughly practical use, which is why the old authors state it as a rule of validity rather than a curiosity: **an odd Judge proves a miscast chart.** If you complete a shield and the Judge's points sum to five or seven, you have made an arithmetical error somewhere in the derivation (with certainty, not probability) and the chart must be checked addition by addition before anything is read from it. Cattan's "the figure should be false" is exact. The converse, however, does not hold, and this is a warning the course will repeat: an even Judge does *not* prove a correct chart. Errors can (and, as Exercise 4 will show you in detail, sometimes must) cancel themselves out of the Judge while leaving the rest of the chart corrupt. The parity check is a smoke alarm, not an audit. The only audit is re-derivation, which is why the journal protocol below has you verify every chart.

## One organism, not fifteen figures

The derivation has established something about the nature of the chart. The fifteen figures of the shield are not fifteen independent omens that happen to share a page. Eleven of them are computed from the other four; every line of every figure below the top row is determined, through the chain of transpositions and additions, by the sixteen parities of the original rows. The chart is one organism.

The tradition read it accordingly. When the same figure appears in several places in a chart, the old authors did not treat the repetition as coincidence (in a derived structure it is not coincidence) but as the chart insisting on something, carrying one signification from place to place. The pseudo-Agrippa makes the principle central to judgment itself:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/technique-springing" />

"This whole Art consisteth in the commixtures of the figures." The example Agrippa gives, the figure of the second house reappearing in the sixth and thereby naming the thief a servant, is a technique you will learn properly in Part 2 as the [springing of figures](/techniques/springing-of-figures/). Alongside it stands the [projection of points](/techniques/projection-of-points/), in which the points of the first twelve figures are counted and cast around the houses to find where the chart's weight falls. Both techniques only make sense because the chart is a single computed fabric: repetitions and totals carry information *because* nothing in the shield is independent of anything else. Neither technique is for use yet. What matters now is how often figures repeat across a shield (the worked example below repeats four of its figures): in a derived structure the repetitions are the fabric talking, not fifteen separate fortunes.

## From shield to houses

The shield is the chart as cast; for judgment, the tradition also sets the chart into the twelve **houses** of astrology, the frame of places you will study properly in Lesson 5. The allocation in the main line of the tradition (the one Agrippa describes in the casting passage you have already read, and the one this course uses throughout) is the simplest possible: the four Mothers go into houses one through four, the four Daughters into houses five through eight, and the four Nieces into houses nine through twelve, all in their order of birth. The first twelve figures of the shield, in sequence, are simply dealt around the wheel.

The Witnesses and the Judge are *not* placed in houses. Recall Cattan: they "be no houses, neither natural figures, but be only accidentals." They remain outside the wheel, where they pass sentence on what the houses contain. Twelve figures in places, three figures above the places: that division of labour is the entire architecture of geomantic judgment, and you will spend the rest of the course learning each half.

Be aware, for your reading, that the allocation is not universal: Agrippa goes on to offer an alternative "astrological" arrangement, and the Golden Dawn system in Crowley's handbook begins the first Mother elsewhere on the wheel. When you read a historical chart you must know which scheme its author used. This course states its choice once and keeps it: **Mothers in houses one to four, Daughters in five to eight, Nieces in nine to twelve.**

What the houses *mean* (that the first house is the querent, the seventh the spouse or adversary, the eighth death and legacies, and all the rest) is the business of Lesson 5, and nothing in this lesson's exercises requires it. The house chart of the worked example below shows the next step: the same fifteen figures you derived as a family tree become a map of a human situation, each figure standing in a place, each place owning a department of life. The shield answers "what came?"; the houses answer "where does it stand?"; and judgment, when you reach it, is the art of reading the two together.

## The casting journal begins now

From this lesson forward you are a practising geomancer, and a practising geomancer keeps a record. The journal is not an optional study aid; it is the instrument on which everything in Part 3 of this course depends, because the only honest test of judgment is a judgment written down before the outcome was known. Lesson 4's portion of the discipline is the casting protocol. For every chart you cast, record:

1. **The question**, written out in full, in the exact words asked, not a paraphrase made later.
2. **The date, time and place** of the casting.
3. **The sixteen rows of points themselves**, not just their parities. The rows are your raw data; if a derivation is ever in doubt, you must be able to return to them.
4. **The full shield chart**, all fifteen figures, drawn in the traditional frame, right to left.
5. **The house allocation**: the twelve figures dealt into their houses.
6. **The verification**: the Judge's parity checked, and the whole derivation re-derived once or confirmed against the casting widget below.
7. **First impressions, clearly labelled as such**: anything that strikes you about the chart: repetitions, the character of the Judge, a figure that catches the eye. Two or three sentences at most. You do not yet know how to judge a chart, and you must not pretend to; but the habit of looking before you can read is worth forming, and these notes will be instructive (sometimes humbling) when you return to them with trained eyes.

What you must *not* yet write is a judgment. No predictions, no interpretations, no answers to the question, not until Lessons 5 through 7 have given you the houses, the Witnesses and Judge, and the modes of perfection. A journal that begins with three weeks of carefully cast, scrupulously unjudged charts is a better foundation than any amount of premature fortune-telling. The restraint is temporary and the reason for it is permanent: in this art, the cast and the judgment are separate acts, and the cast must be beyond suspicion before the judgment is worth anything.

## Worked example

Now the whole procedure, from beginning to end, by hand, with nothing skipped. The question (framed, let us say, for a querent asking about a proposed match) is: **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** The question is single, concrete and earnest; the moment is calm; the question has not been asked before. We write it down, with the date and hour, and make sixteen rows of points from right to left, eyes on the question and not on the count.

### The sixteen rows

The rows are made; now, and only now, we count them. Suppose the counts come out as follows (yours, of course, will differ with every cast):

| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | 10 | even | ● ● |
| 2 | 7 | odd | ● |
| 3 | 9 | odd | ● |
| 4 | 8 | even | ● ● |
| 5 | 11 | odd | ● |
| 6 | 7 | odd | ● |
| 7 | 9 | odd | ● |
| 8 | 5 | odd | ● |
| 9 | 9 | odd | ● |
| 10 | 12 | even | ● ● |
| 11 | 7 | odd | ● |
| 12 | 10 | even | ● ● |
| 13 | 8 | even | ● ● |
| 14 | 11 | odd | ● |
| 15 | 9 | odd | ● |
| 16 | 7 | odd | ● |

Rows one through four give the First Mother: two points, one, one, two: **Conjunctio**. Rows five through eight: one, one, one, one: **Via**. Rows nine through twelve: one, two, one, two: **Amissio**. Rows thirteen through sixteen: two, one, one, one: **Caput Draconis**. The four Mothers, written right to left across the top of the shield, are Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis.

### The Daughters

Read the Mothers sideways. The head lines of Mothers one through four are: two points (Conjunctio), one (Via), one (Amissio), two (Caput Draconis). Stacked downward: 2, 1, 1, 2, the **First Daughter is Conjunctio**, the First Mother reborn in another place. The air lines: one (Conjunctio), one (Via), two (Amissio), one (Caput Draconis), giving 1, 1, 2, 1, the **Second Daughter is Puer**. The water lines: one, one, one, one, the **Third Daughter is Via**. The earth lines: two (Conjunctio), one (Via), two (Amissio), one (Caput Draconis), giving 2, 1, 2, 1, the **Fourth Daughter is Acquisitio**. Check the transposition fact: the Mothers carry 6 + 4 + 6 + 5 = 21 points, and the Daughters carry 6 + 5 + 4 + 6 = 21. The same lines, redistributed.

## The course charts

The chart you have just derived is the first of three that will accompany you for the rest of this course. Call them **Chart A**, **Chart B** and **Chart C**. Each is a complete cast with a standing question, and every later lesson returns to them: when Lesson 5 gives you the houses, you will read these charts' houses; when Lesson 7 gives you the modes of perfection, you will test these questions for perfection; when the aspect and company doctrines arrive, they will be demonstrated on these same fifteen-figure fabrics. By the end of the course you will know these three charts the way a law student knows the great cases. Enter all three in the front of your journal now.

### Chart A: "Will the marriage go ahead?"

The worked example above: Mothers **Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis**; Daughters Conjunctio, Puer, Via, Acquisitio; Nieces Carcer, Puer, Puella, Amissio; Right Witness Rubeus, Left Witness Tristitia, **Judge Acquisitio**.

What you can already see, with structure alone: a chart dense with repetition (four figures doubled) and a seventh house (the house of marriage, as Lesson 5 will confirm) holding Via, the most changeable figure of the sixteen. The Judge is a fortunate, stable figure of Jupiter; the Witnesses it was born from are two of the harshest figures in the art. A promising sentence from rough testimony: how that tension resolves is precisely what the judgment lessons exist to teach.

### Chart B: "Will the querent recover the partnership?"

A seventh-house question (partners, like spouses and adversaries, belong to the seventh) cast for a querent whose business partnership has broken and who asks whether it can be restored. The Mothers fell **Puella, Rubeus, Rubeus, Amissio**. Two identical Mothers in one cast may surprise you; it should not. With sixteen figures and four draws, repeated Mothers turn up constantly in honest practice, and a student who never sees them has been casting with a thumb on the scale. The derivation (yours to perform in Exercise 1) yields Daughters Carcer, Conjunctio, Carcer, Laetitia; Nieces Via, Cauda Draconis, Via, Tristitia; Right Witness Tristitia, Left Witness Cauda Draconis; **Judge Via**.

Structurally: the doubled Rubeus of the Mothers breeds a doubled Carcer in the Daughters and a doubled Via in the Nieces; watch, in Exercise 1, exactly how each repetition forces the next. The Judge is Via, the figure of change and movement itself, sired by the two figures of sorrow and departure. A chart about restoration whose final word is "change" will be a delicate thing to judge.

### Chart C: "Will the querent win the lawsuit?"

A lawsuit: the adversary, like the partner and the spouse, lives in the seventh house, and the question is whether the querent wins. The Mothers fell **Amissio, Amissio, Puella, Fortuna Minor**, again a doubled Mother, this time in the first two places. The derivation, which is Exercise 2, yields Daughters Via, Tristitia, Cauda Draconis, Albus; Nieces Populus, Caput Draconis, Cauda Draconis, Fortuna Minor; Right Witness Caput Draconis, Left Witness Albus; **Judge Acquisitio**.

Structurally: the two identical Mothers add to Populus in the First Niece (any figure added to itself must) and the chart doubles Cauda Draconis and Fortuna Minor elsewhere. Note one thing now and keep it: the Judge is Acquisitio, gain, among the best figures in the art, and yet the seventh house, the house of this very question, holds Cauda Draconis, one of the worst. A bright Judge over a dark seventh house is not a contradiction; it is a chart with a complicated sentence to deliver, and resolving exactly this tension is on the syllabus of the judgment lessons. If the course charts were all simple, they would teach nothing.

## Practice

A student who watches the widget derive fifty charts learns almost nothing; a student who derives twenty charts and checks them learns the procedure for life. Your target before the exercises: three consecutive hand-derived shields, from random Mothers, with no errors.

<Exercise n={3} title="Prove that the Judge is always even" kind="drill">
  Without consulting the lesson text, write out the complete argument that the Judge of any correctly cast chart must carry an even number of points. The argument must cover every step from the addition rule to the conclusion, and must make clear exactly where the transposition of the Daughters enters.
  <ModelAnswer>
    Step one: when two figures are added line by line, each line of the result is even or odd in points exactly as the two parent lines are even or odd taken together: one and one make two (even parents, even child), two and two make four (even, even), one and two make three (odd, odd). So a figure produced by addition has an even total of points precisely when its two parents together have an even total. Addition preserves evenness and oddness of the point-count.

    Step two: chain this up the shield. The Judge is the sum of the two Witnesses, so the Judge is even exactly when the Witnesses together are even. Each Witness is the sum of two Nieces, so the Witnesses together are even exactly when the four Nieces together are even. Each Niece is the sum of two figures from the top row, so the Nieces together are even exactly when the eight top-row figures (four Mothers and four Daughters) together are even. The Judge's parity is therefore the parity of the combined points of Mothers and Daughters.

    Step three: the Daughters are the Mothers transposed. Every line of every Daughter is a line of some Mother, relocated, and every line of every Mother appears in exactly one Daughter. The Daughters therefore contain exactly as many points as the Mothers, not approximately, but identically, since they are the same lines. The top row of the shield thus counts every point of the Mothers exactly twice: once in its Mother and once in its Daughter.

    Conclusion: twice any whole number is even, so the combined points of Mothers and Daughters are always even, whatever the Mothers may be; and by step two the Judge inherits that evenness. No set of sixteen rows can produce an odd Judge. Practically: a Judge with five or seven points is certain proof of an arithmetical error in the derivation, and the chart must be re-derived before use. The converse fails (an even Judge does not certify a correct chart, since errors can cancel on the way down) so the parity test detects some miscasts but never confirms a cast.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

    The pattern is exact, not accidental. A single wrong line corrupts only the air line of every figure it reaches: the error reaches the Second Daughter (which holds that very line), then one Niece on each side of the shield (the First Niece through the corrupted Mother, the Third through the corrupted Daughter) then both Witnesses, one from each Niece. At the Judge the two corrupted streams meet: the Judge's air line is the sum of two lines that have *both* flipped, and two flips cancel in parity, so the Judge's air line (and the whole Judge) comes out exactly as before. Any single-line miscount must cancel at the Judge in this way, because the transposition guarantees the error enters the Judge's ancestry exactly twice.

    The moral: the parity check, and even comparison of the Judge alone, can never certify a chart. Here is a cast with six wrong figures (both Witnesses among them) wearing a flawless and *identical* Judge. Verification means re-deriving the whole shield, which is why the journal protocol requires it.
