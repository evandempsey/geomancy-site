This lesson teaches the whole operation of casting: from framing a question, through sixteen lines of points made without counting, to the fifteen figures of the shield chart. You will perform it by hand until it is automatic. Everything the rest of the course does is done *to* a cast chart.

This lesson is procedure. You can read the rule for deriving a Daughter quickly, but you will need repeated hand practice before it becomes reliable. The exercises are built for that. Reading begins in Lesson 5; accurate casting begins here.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 1 through 3: you can name any figure from its dots, draw any figure from its name, and know each figure's planet, element, mobility and character. If any of that is uncertain, review first, because hesitation over basic figures causes casting errors.

You will also want a pen and unlined or lightly lined paper. The old method used sand or earth (geomancy is named for it), but paper has served geomancers for centuries and will serve you. Use any medium, but keep the casting conditions.

## The question

A geomantic chart is cast *about something*. The question is part of the operation: the figures are read against it, and a vague question gives the chart nothing clear to answer.

A well-formed geomantic question has three properties. It is **single**: it asks one thing. "Should I take the position, and will the money be good, and will my family be content there?" is three questions and must be separated. It is **concrete**: it concerns a particular matter with a particular outcome, such as a marriage, journey, lawsuit, or sum of money. "Will things get better?" is too vague. It is **earnest**: the querent actually wants to know and is prepared to hear either answer. Use Lesson 1 here: the sources held that the soul of the querent, moved by real desire, moves the hand that makes the points. A question asked idly, mockingly, or merely to test geomancy lacks that root.

To these the sources add cautions about the occasion. Re-read the opening of Gerard of Cremona's *Astronomical Geomancy*, which is in this lesson's assigned reading; the heart of it is this:

<Quote id="gerard/casting-caution" />

The weather conditions belong to the medieval setting. The inward conditions apply to every reading: do not cast when angry or distracted, do not cast "for tempters or deriders," and do not ask the same question twice "under the same figure or form, for that is error." If the situation genuinely changes, a new question may be asked about the new facts; the same question repeated in search of a better answer is forbidden.

One further caution belongs to the casting stage though it is applied after the chart is made. The Golden Dawn rule says that certain charts are not fit to read:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/destroy-rule" />

Whether you adopt this rule is handled in the reading lessons. The general principle is the same as above: the chart depends on the fitness of the casting, and the geomancer must know when not to cast.

When the question is framed, write it down (in full, in the exact words you are asking) together with the date, the time, and the place. Then hold it in your mind, and begin.

## The sixteen lines and the four Mothers

The mechanical core of the method is simple, and the sources describe it in a few sentences. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-mothers" />

Unpacked into modern instructions: make **sixteen rows of points**, working each row **from right to left**, one row beneath another. The right-to-left direction is constant across the sources, and a fixed movement helps the hand run without supervision.

For each row, set down a series of points **without counting them**, the mind held on the question and not on the marks. Pen jabs, dashes, or marks in sand all serve. More than four points and fewer than about sixteen is the practical range. While making the row, you must not know whether its count is odd or even. When sixteen rows stand on the page, the random work is done and the rest of the operation is pure rule.

Why sixteen rows made without counting? Each row contributes one fact, odd or even, and four figures of four lines need sixteen such facts. Lesson 1's theory requires the hand not to steer the result: a geomancer who half-counts while marking records his own preferences, not the cast.

Now count each row's points; you care only about the parity. **An odd count yields one point; an even count yields two.** The sixteen rows, read in groups of four from the top, yield four figures of four lines each. These are the **Mothers**, and Agrippa says exactly what they do: they "bring forth the rest." Every remaining figure is computed from the Mothers by fixed rules. The randomness, or in the sources' terms the celestial influence, enters the chart in the sixteen rows and nowhere else; everything after is arithmetic, and a chart with a derivation error is not a different answer, it is no answer.

Write the four Mothers side by side, and (this is another habit worth fixing early) write them **from right to left**, the First Mother at the far right, the Fourth at the far left. The shield chart is laid out this way in every source, and the habit will save you confusion when you begin reading historical examples.

## The Daughters: the Mothers turned on their side

The next four figures add nothing new; they are the Mothers' own lines redistributed. Agrippa again:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-daughters" />

The rule is a transposition: the Mothers read sideways. Take the **head lines** (the fire lines) of the four Mothers in order and stack them downward: that figure is the **First Daughter**. The four **air lines**, stacked the same way, form the Second Daughter; the water lines the Third; the earth lines the Fourth. Nothing is added: every line of every Daughter *is* a line of some Mother, relocated. If you imagine the four Mothers as a four-by-four grid of lines, the Daughters are that grid read in rows instead of columns.

For example, if the four Mothers are Puer, Amissio, Albus and Populus, their head lines are one point, one point, two points, two points; stacked downward that is 1, 1, 2, 2, Fortuna Minor, the First Daughter. Note one consequence now: the Daughters contain exactly the same sixteen lines as the Mothers, so the two sets always carry the same total of points. The even-Judge check below rests on that fact.

The Daughters take their place in the shield to the *left* of the Mothers, continuing the right-to-left order: positions five through eight.

## The Nieces: figures added together

With the Daughters the chart stops copying and starts combining. The next four figures, which this course calls the **Nieces** (Agrippa's *nepotes*), are made by **adding figures together in pairs**, and the addition rule is the most-used operation in geomancy.

To add two figures, take them line by line: the two fire lines together, the two air lines, the two water lines, the two earth lines. For each pair of lines, count the combined points. **If the combined count is even, the resulting line has two points; if odd, one point.** Since each line holds either one or two points, only three cases exist:

| First line | Second line | Combined points | Resulting line |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ● | ● | 2, even | ● ● |
| ● ● | ● ● | 4, even | ● ● |
| ● | ● ● | 3, odd | ● |

In other words: **two like lines give a double line; two unlike lines give a single line.** That compressed form is how you will actually compute; underneath it is the older way: you are pooling the points of the two lines and asking whether the pool is even or uneven, the same operation you performed on the sixteen rows. The whole chart is parity from top to bottom: rows collapse to lines by oddness and evenness, and figures combine by oddness and evenness.

The four Nieces are formed from adjacent pairs, in the order the figures stand in the shield: the **First Niece** is the First Mother added to the Second; the **Second Niece** is the Third Mother added to the Fourth; the **Third Niece** is the First Daughter added to the Second; the **Fourth Niece** is the Third Daughter added to the Fourth. They occupy positions nine through twelve, written in a row beneath the Mothers and Daughters, each Niece sitting under the pair that produced it. Agrippa describes precisely this pairing in the passage above (out of the first and second figures the ninth, out of the third and fourth the tenth, and so on) "by the combination or joining together of two figures, according to the rule of the even or uneven number."

Practise the addition until it is automatic. Take any two figures from your Lesson 2 flashcards and add them; check by re-deriving. Three small facts will save you time: a figure added to itself gives Populus; a figure added to Populus comes back unchanged; a figure added to Via has every line inverted.

## Witnesses and Judge

The same addition, applied twice more, finishes the shield. The First Niece added to the Second yields the **Right Witness**; the Third Niece added to the Fourth yields the **Left Witness**; and the Right Witness added to the Left yields the **Judge**, the fifteenth and final figure, which stands alone at the foot of the chart. Cattan, whose account of these three figures the later English sources reprint nearly word for word, explains what they are for:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-03-of-the-two-witnesses-chap" />

Note Cattan's structural point, because it explains the names: the Right Witness descends, through the Nieces, from the four Mothers, the querent's side of the shield; the Left Witness descends from the four Daughters, the side of the thing asked about. The two Witnesses are "no houses, neither natural figures, but be only accidentals": they summarise their sides so that the Judge can give the chart's final figure. And of the Judge itself:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-04-of-the-judge-chap" />

"If the Judge be good, the signification of the demand shall come to a good end, and if he be ill, it shall come to an ill end." That is the Judge's office. The full rule for reading Witnesses and Judge belongs to the reading lessons; what belongs to this lesson is the validity rule: "The which Judge ought always of necessity to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false." That rule gets its own section below; first, the finished structure.

## The shield assembled

Fifteen figures now stand on the page, and the standard arrangement of them is called the **shield chart**. The four Mothers fill the top row's right half, the four Daughters its left half, all eight running right to left in order of birth. The four Nieces form the second row, each beneath its parents. The two Witnesses form the third row, and the Judge sits centred at the bottom. Every figure below the top row stands directly under the two figures that made it:

<ShieldChart />

From the top down, the diagram shows the entire derivation: sixteen rows of points collapse into four Mothers; the Mothers transpose into four Daughters; pairs collapse into Nieces, Nieces into Witnesses, Witnesses into the Judge. The shield is the working document of the geomancer, and you should draw the frame freehand for every cast until it is second nature.

## The even-Judge check

Cattan says the Judge "ought always of necessity to be even" (its four lines must hold an even number of points in total) and that an uneven Judge means "the figure should be false." The rule holds for every chart that can exist, and the reason is short: the Daughters are the Mothers' own lines rearranged, so the top row counts every point twice, and the line-by-line additions preserve that evenness all the way down to the Judge.

Only eight of the sixteen figures have an even point-count, so only eight can ever stand as Judge: Populus, Via, Conjunctio, Carcer, Acquisitio, Amissio, Fortuna Major and Fortuna Minor. The other eight can appear anywhere else in the chart, but never at its foot.

The practical use: **an odd Judge proves a miscast chart.** If your Judge sums to five or seven, you have made an arithmetical error, with certainty, and the derivation must be checked before anything is read. The converse does not hold: an even Judge does *not* prove a correct chart, because errors can cancel out of the Judge while leaving the rest of the chart corrupt, as Exercise 3 shows. The only full check is re-derivation.

## One Derived Structure

The derivation establishes an important fact. The fifteen figures of the shield are not independent omens sharing a page. Eleven are computed from the first four. Every figure below the top row is determined by the sixteen original parities through transposition and addition. The chart is one derived structure.

The sources read it accordingly. When the same figure appears in several places in a chart, the old authors did not treat the repetition as coincidence. In a derived structure it is not coincidence. A repeated figure carries one signification from place to place. The pseudo-Agrippa makes the principle central to reading a chart:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/technique-springing" />

"This whole Art consisteth in the commixtures of the figures." Agrippa's example, the figure of the second house reappearing in the sixth and naming the thief a servant, is the technique later taught as [springing of figures](/techniques/springing-of-figures/); [projection of points](/techniques/projection-of-points/) is its companion. Neither is for use yet. For now, notice repetitions across the shield.

## From shield to houses

The shield is the chart as cast; to read it, the sources also set the chart into the twelve **houses** of astrology, the frame of places you will study properly in Lesson 5. The allocation Agrippa describes in the casting passage, and the one this course uses throughout, is the simplest possible: the four Mothers go into houses one through four, the four Daughters into houses five through eight, and the four Nieces into houses nine through twelve, all in their order of birth. The first twelve figures of the shield, in sequence, are simply dealt around the wheel.

The Witnesses and the Judge are *not* placed in houses. Recall Cattan: they "be no houses, neither natural figures, but be only accidentals." They remain outside the wheel, where they give the summary of what the houses contain. Twelve figures stand in places; three stand outside them and summarise. You will spend the rest of the course learning each half of that division.

Be aware, for your reading, that the allocation is not universal: Agrippa goes on to offer an alternative "astrological" arrangement, and the Golden Dawn system in Crowley's handbook begins the first Mother elsewhere on the wheel. When you read a historical chart you must know which scheme its author used. This course states its choice once and keeps it: **Mothers in houses one to four, Daughters in five to eight, Nieces in nine to twelve.**

What the houses *mean* is the business of Lesson 5, and nothing in this lesson's exercises requires it. The shield records what came; the houses show where each figure stands; and reading the chart means reading the two together.

## Worked example

Now the whole procedure, from beginning to end, by hand, with nothing skipped. The question (framed, let us say, for a querent asking about a proposed match) is: **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** The question is single, concrete and earnest; the moment is calm; the question has not been asked before. We write it down, with the date and hour, and make sixteen rows of points from right to left, eyes on the question and not on the count.

Rows one through four give the First Mother: two points, one, one, two: **Conjunctio**. Rows five through eight: one, one, one, one: **Via**. Rows nine through twelve: one, two, one, two: **Amissio**. Rows thirteen through sixteen: two, one, one, one: **Caput Draconis**. The four Mothers, written right to left across the top of the shield, are Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis.

### The Daughters

Read the Mothers sideways. The head lines of Mothers one through four are: two points (Conjunctio), one (Via), one (Amissio), two (Caput Draconis). Stacked downward: 2, 1, 1, 2, the **First Daughter is Conjunctio**, the First Mother's figure again. The air lines: one (Conjunctio), one (Via), two (Amissio), one (Caput Draconis), giving 1, 1, 2, 1, the **Second Daughter is Puer**. The water lines: one, one, one, one, the **Third Daughter is Via**. The earth lines: two (Conjunctio), one (Via), two (Amissio), one (Caput Draconis), giving 2, 1, 2, 1, the **Fourth Daughter is Acquisitio**. Check the transposition fact: the Mothers carry 6 + 4 + 6 + 5 = 21 points, and the Daughters carry 6 + 5 + 4 + 6 = 21. The same lines, redistributed.

### Witnesses and Judge

**Right Witness**, First Niece (Carcer) added to Second Niece (Puer): fire, 1 and 1, even, two points; air, 2 and 1, odd, one point; water, 2 and 2, even, two points; earth, 1 and 1, even, two points. Lines 2, 1, 2, 2: the **Right Witness is Rubeus**.

**Left Witness**, Third Niece (Puella) added to Fourth Niece (Amissio): fire, 1 and 1, even, two; air, 2 and 2, even, two; water, 1 and 1, even, two; earth, 1 and 2, odd, one. Lines 2, 2, 2, 1: the **Left Witness is Tristitia**.

**Judge**, Rubeus added to Tristitia: fire, 2 and 2, even, two; air, 1 and 2, odd, one; water, 2 and 2, even, two; earth, 2 and 1, odd, one. Lines 2, 1, 2, 1: the **Judge is Acquisitio**.

## The course charts

The chart you have just derived is the first of three standing course charts: **Chart A**, **Chart B** and **Chart C**. Each is a complete cast with a standing question. Later lessons return to them for houses, perfection, aspects, company, and finished reading.

### Chart A: "Will the marriage go ahead?"

The worked example above: Mothers **Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis**; Daughters Conjunctio, Puer, Via, Acquisitio; Nieces Carcer, Puer, Puella, Amissio; Right Witness Rubeus, Left Witness Tristitia, **Judge Acquisitio**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['conjunctio', 'via', 'amissio', 'caput-draconis']} caption="Chart A, Judge: Acquisitio." />

What you can already see from structure alone: four figures repeat, the seventh house holds Via, the Judge is the fortunate and stable Acquisitio, and the Witnesses are two harsh figures. The chart has mixed findings. How to resolve them belongs to the reading lessons.

### Chart B: "Will the querent recover the partnership?"

A seventh-house question, because partners belong to the seventh. The querent's business partnership has broken and he asks whether it can be restored. The Mothers fell **Puella, Rubeus, Rubeus, Amissio**. Repeated Mothers are normal in honest practice. The derivation, which you perform in Exercise 1, yields Daughters Carcer, Conjunctio, Carcer, Laetitia; Nieces Via, Cauda Draconis, Via, Tristitia; Right Witness Tristitia, Left Witness Cauda Draconis; **Judge Via**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['puella', 'rubeus', 'rubeus', 'amissio']} caption="Chart B, Judge: Via." />

Structurally, the doubled Rubeus in the Mothers produces doubled Carcer in the Daughters and doubled Via in the Nieces. The Judge is Via, the figure of change. A chart about restoring a partnership with Via as Judge will require careful reading.

### Chart C: "Will the querent win the lawsuit?"

A lawsuit: the adversary, like the partner and the spouse, belongs to the seventh house, and the question is whether the querent wins. The Mothers fell **Amissio, Amissio, Puella, Fortuna Minor**, again a doubled Mother, this time in the first two places. The derivation, which is Exercise 2, yields Daughters Via, Tristitia, Cauda Draconis, Albus; Nieces Populus, Caput Draconis, Cauda Draconis, Fortuna Minor; Right Witness Caput Draconis, Left Witness Albus; **Judge Acquisitio**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['amissio', 'amissio', 'puella', 'fortuna-minor']} caption="Chart C, Judge: Acquisitio." />

Structurally, the two identical Mothers add to Populus in the First Niece, and the chart repeats Cauda Draconis and Fortuna Minor elsewhere. Note the main tension: the Judge is Acquisitio, one of the best figures, while the seventh house holds Cauda Draconis, one of the worst. That is not a contradiction. It is mixed finding, and resolving it is part of the reading lessons.

    5. a). The Judge "ought always of necessity to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false" (Cattan, *The Geomancie*, London 1591, Book III, ch. 4); an odd Judge shows an error in the geomancer's arithmetic, not in the heavens.

<Exercise n={5} title="Why the points must be made without counting" kind="quiz">
  The rule looks procedural but rests on Lesson 1's theory; the questions test that theory.

  1. Why does counting while marking spoil a row?
  2. The casting instruction is always double. State both halves.
  3. What is the caster's attention supposed to be doing, if not counting?
  4. After the sixteen rows are set down, careful counting is:
     - a) still forbidden until the Judge is formed
     - b) mandatory, since from that point error, not influence, is the enemy
     - c) permitted but discouraged
  5. Give Crowley's two-part compression of the discipline, with the citation.
  6. In what sense is not counting the geomancer's equivalent of not being able to move the planets?

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. A counter knows at every instant whether the row stands odd or even, and cannot help completing it toward one or the other; the line then records a preference, not an impression. The deliberating mind takes over the role the sources give to the soul (Agrippa, attrib., *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner, London 1655, pp. 1-2).
    2. Make the marks blindly, and hold the question fixedly.
    3. It is redirected, not suppressed: fully occupied by the question, it has none left over for the count, and the hand is free to be moved.
    4. b). Every operation after the rows (transposition, the additions, the Witnesses, the Judge) is determined by rule. The casting is the only opening through which anything can enter the chart, and it closes when the rows are done.
    5. "Think fixedly of the demand," and "the hand should not be moved from the paper till complete" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2), London 1909, ch. II).
    6. The horary astrologer cannot steer the heavens at the moment of asking. The geomancer must achieve the same thing by self-discipline: a record the asker cannot influence.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>
