This lesson teaches the whole operation of casting. You start by framing a question. You make sixteen lines of points without counting them. From those you derive the fifteen figures of the shield chart. You will perform the operation by hand until it is automatic. The rest of the course works on a chart you have cast.

This lesson is a procedure. You can read the rule for deriving a Daughter in a minute, but you need repeated hand practice before it becomes reliable. The exercises are built for that. Reading begins in Lesson 5. Accurate casting begins here.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 1 through 3. You can name any figure from its dots, draw any figure from its name, and know each figure's planet, element, mobility and character. If any of that is uncertain, review it first. Hesitation over basic figures causes casting errors.

You will also want a pen and unlined or lightly lined paper. The old method used sand or earth, which is where geomancy gets its name. Paper has served geomancers for centuries and will serve you. Use any medium, but keep the casting conditions.

## The question

A geomantic chart is cast about something. The question is part of the operation. The figures are read against it, and a vague question gives the chart nothing clear to answer.

A well-formed geomantic question has three properties. First, it is **single**. It asks one thing. "Should I take the position, and will the money be good, and will my family be content there?" is three questions and must be separated. Second, it is **concrete**. It concerns a particular matter with a particular outcome, such as a marriage, journey, lawsuit, or sum of money. "Will things get better?" is too vague. Third, it is **earnest**. The querent actually wants to know and is prepared to hear either answer. Lesson 1 explains why this matters: the sources held that the querent's real desire is what moves the hand to make the points. A question asked idly, mockingly, or only to test geomancy lacks that.

The sources add cautions about the occasion. Re-read the opening of Gerard of Cremona's *Astronomical Geomancy*, which is in this lesson's assigned reading. The main caution is this:

<Quote id="gerard/casting-caution" />

The weather conditions belong to the medieval setting. The inward conditions apply to every reading. Do not cast when angry or distracted. Do not cast "for tempters or deriders." Do not ask the same question twice "under the same figure or form, for that is error." If the situation genuinely changes, you may ask a new question about the new facts. Repeating the same question in search of a better answer is not allowed.

One further caution belongs to the casting stage, though it is applied after the chart is made. The Golden Dawn rule says that certain charts are not fit to read:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/destroy-rule" />

Whether you adopt this rule is handled in the reading lessons. The principle is the same as above. The chart depends on a clean cast, and the geomancer must know when not to cast.

When the question is framed, write it down in full, in the exact words you are asking, together with the date, the time, and the place. Then hold it in your mind and begin.

## The sixteen lines and the four Mothers

The core of the method is simple, and the sources describe it in a few sentences. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-mothers" />

In modern terms: make **sixteen rows of points**, working each row **from right to left**, one row beneath another. The right-to-left direction is constant across the sources, and a fixed movement keeps the hand running on its own.

For each row, set down a series of points **without counting them**, with the mind on the question and not on the marks. Pen jabs, dashes, or marks in sand all serve. More than four points and fewer than about sixteen is the practical range. While making the row, you must not know whether its count is odd or even. When sixteen rows stand on the page, the random work is done and the rest of the operation is rule.

Why sixteen rows made without counting? Each row gives one fact, odd or even, and four figures of four lines need sixteen such facts. Lesson 1's theory requires the hand not to steer the result. A geomancer who half-counts while marking records his own preferences, not the cast.

Now count each row's points. You care only about the parity. **An odd count gives one point; an even count gives two.** Read the sixteen rows in groups of four from the top. Each group gives a figure of four lines. These four figures are the **Mothers**, and Agrippa says what they do: they "bring forth the rest." Every remaining figure is computed from the Mothers by fixed rules. The randomness, or in the sources' terms the celestial influence, enters the chart in the sixteen rows and nowhere else. Everything after is arithmetic. A chart with a derivation error is not a different answer. It is no answer.

Write the four Mothers side by side. Write them **from right to left**, the First Mother at the far right, the Fourth at the far left. This is another habit worth fixing early. The shield chart is laid out this way in every source, and the habit will save you confusion when you begin reading historical examples.

## The Daughters by transposition

The next four figures add nothing new. They are the Mothers' own lines redistributed. Agrippa again:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-daughters" />

The rule is a transposition. The Mothers are read sideways. Take the **head lines**, the fire lines, of the four Mothers in order and stack them downward. That figure is the **First Daughter**. The four **air lines**, stacked the same way, form the Second Daughter. The water lines form the Third. The earth lines form the Fourth. Nothing is added. Every line of every Daughter is a line of some Mother, moved to a new place. If you picture the four Mothers as a four-by-four grid of lines, the Daughters are that grid read in rows instead of columns.

For example, if the four Mothers are Puer, Amissio, Albus and Populus, their head lines are one point, one point, two points, two points. Stacked downward that is 1, 1, 2, 2, which is Fortuna Minor, the First Daughter. Note one consequence now. The Daughters contain exactly the same sixteen lines as the Mothers, so the two sets always carry the same total of points. The even-Judge check below rests on that fact.

The Daughters take their place in the shield to the *left* of the Mothers, continuing the right-to-left order. They are positions five through eight.

## The Nieces by addition

After the Daughters, the next four figures are made by combining pairs of figures. This course calls them the **Nieces** (Agrippa's *nepotes*). They are made by **adding figures together in pairs**, and addition is the operation you will use most often in geomancy.

To add two figures, take them line by line: the two fire lines together, then the two air lines, then the two water lines, then the two earth lines. For each pair of lines, count the combined points. **If the combined count is even, the resulting line has two points. If it is odd, the resulting line has one point.** Since each line holds either one or two points, only three cases exist:

| First line | Second line | Combined points | Resulting line |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ● | ● | 2, even | ● ● |
| ● ● | ● ● | 4, even | ● ● |
| ● | ● ● | 3, odd | ● |

In short: **two like lines give a double line, and two unlike lines give a single line.** That is the form you will use when computing. Underneath it is the older way of putting it. You add the points of the two lines and ask whether the total is even or uneven. This is the same test you used on the sixteen rows. The chart is built on parity throughout. The rows became lines by odd or even count, and the figures combine the same way.

The four Nieces are formed from adjacent pairs, in the order the figures stand in the shield. The **First Niece** is the First Mother added to the Second. The **Second Niece** is the Third Mother added to the Fourth. The **Third Niece** is the First Daughter added to the Second. The **Fourth Niece** is the Third Daughter added to the Fourth. They occupy positions nine through twelve, written in a row beneath the Mothers and Daughters, each Niece under the pair that produced it. Agrippa describes this pairing in the passage above, "out of the first and second figures the ninth, out of the third and fourth the tenth," and so on, "by the combination or joining together of two figures, according to the rule of the even or uneven number."

Practise the addition until it is automatic. Take any two figures from your Lesson 2 flashcards and add them, then check by re-deriving. Three small facts will save you time. A figure added to itself gives Populus. A figure added to Populus comes back unchanged. A figure added to Via has every line inverted.

## Witnesses and Judge

The same addition, used twice more, finishes the shield. The First Niece added to the Second gives the **Right Witness**. The Third Niece added to the Fourth gives the **Left Witness**. The Right Witness added to the Left gives the **Judge**, the fifteenth and final figure, which stands alone at the foot of the chart. The later English sources reprint Cattan's account of these three figures nearly word for word. He explains what they are for:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-03-of-the-two-witnesses-chap" />

Note Cattan's structural point, because it explains the names. The Right Witness comes, through the Nieces, from the four Mothers, which form the querent's side of the shield. The Left Witness comes from the four Daughters, the side of the thing asked about. The two Witnesses are "no houses, neither natural figures, but be only accidentals." They sum up their sides so that the Judge can give the chart's final figure. Cattan says this of the Judge:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-04-of-the-judge-chap" />

"If the Judge be good, the signification of the demand shall come to a good end, and if he be ill, it shall come to an ill end." That is what the Judge does. The full rule for reading Witnesses and Judge belongs to the reading lessons. This lesson covers the validity rule: "The which Judge ought always of necessity to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false." That rule gets its own section below. First, the finished structure.

## The shield assembled

Fifteen figures now stand on the page. The standard arrangement of them is called the **shield chart**. The four Mothers fill the right half of the top row, the four Daughters the left half, all eight running right to left in order of birth. The four Nieces form the second row, each beneath its parents. The two Witnesses form the third row, and the Judge sits centred at the bottom. Every figure below the top row stands directly under the two figures that made it:

<ShieldChart />

Read from the top down, the diagram shows the whole derivation. The sixteen rows of points give the four Mothers. The Mothers transpose into four Daughters. Pairs add to the Nieces, the Nieces to the Witnesses, the Witnesses to the Judge. The shield is the geomancer's working document. Draw the frame freehand for every cast until you can do it without thinking.

## The even-Judge check

Cattan says the Judge "ought always of necessity to be even," meaning its four lines must hold an even number of points in total, and that an uneven Judge means "the figure should be false." The rule holds for every chart that can exist. The reason is short. The Daughters are the Mothers' own lines rearranged, so the top row counts every point twice. The line-by-line additions keep that evenness all the way to the Judge.

Only eight of the sixteen figures have an even point-count, so only eight can ever stand as Judge: Populus, Via, Conjunctio, Carcer, Acquisitio, Amissio, Fortuna Major and Fortuna Minor. The other eight can appear anywhere else in the chart, but never at its foot.

The practical use is this. **An odd Judge proves a miscast chart.** If your Judge sums to five or seven, you have made an arithmetic error for certain, and you must check the derivation before reading anything. The reverse does not hold. An even Judge does *not* prove a correct chart, because errors can cancel out of the Judge while the rest of the chart stays wrong, as Exercise 3 shows. The only full check is to re-derive the chart.

## The chart is one structure

The derivation shows an important fact. The fifteen figures of the shield are not fifteen separate omens placed on one page. Eleven of them are computed from the first four. Every figure below the top row is fixed by the sixteen original parities through transposition and addition. The whole chart is one derived structure.

The sources read it that way. When the same figure appears in several places in a chart, the old authors did not treat the repetition as accident. In a derived structure it is not accident. A repeated figure carries the same meaning from one place to another. The pseudo-Agrippa makes this point central to reading a chart:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/technique-springing" />

"This whole Art consisteth in the commixtures of the figures." Agrippa's example is the figure of the second house reappearing in the sixth, naming the thief a servant. This is the technique later taught as [springing of figures](/techniques/springing-of-figures/), and [projection of points](/techniques/projection-of-points/) is its companion. Neither is for use yet. For now, just notice repetitions across the shield.

## From shield to houses

The shield is the chart as cast. To read it, the sources also set the chart into the twelve **houses** of astrology, which you will study properly in Lesson 5. The allocation Agrippa describes in the casting passage, and the one this course uses throughout, is the simplest one. The four Mothers go into houses one through four, the four Daughters into houses five through eight, and the four Nieces into houses nine through twelve, all in their order of birth. The first twelve figures of the shield are dealt around the wheel in sequence.

The Witnesses and the Judge are *not* placed in houses. Recall Cattan: they "be no houses, neither natural figures, but be only accidentals." They stay outside the wheel and sum up what the houses contain. So twelve figures stand in houses, and three stand outside them. You will spend the rest of the course learning each half of that division.

For your reading, note that the allocation is not universal. Agrippa goes on to offer an alternative "astrological" arrangement, and the Golden Dawn system in Crowley's handbook begins the first Mother elsewhere on the wheel. When you read a historical chart you must know which scheme its author used. This course states its choice once and keeps it: **Mothers in houses one to four, Daughters in five to eight, Nieces in nine to twelve.**

What the houses *mean* is the work of Lesson 5, and nothing in this lesson's exercises requires it. The shield records what came up. The houses show where each figure stands. To read the chart, you read the two together.

## Worked example

Here is the whole procedure, from start to finish, by hand, with nothing skipped. Say a querent asks about a proposed match. The question is: **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** It is single, concrete and earnest. The moment is calm, and the question has not been asked before. We write it down with the date and hour, then make sixteen rows of points from right to left, eyes on the question and not on the count.

The rows are made. Now, and only now, we count them. Suppose the counts come out as follows. Yours will differ with every cast.

Rows one through four give the First Mother: two points, one, one, two: **Conjunctio**. Rows five through eight: one, one, one, one: **Via**. Rows nine through twelve: one, two, one, two: **Amissio**. Rows thirteen through sixteen: two, one, one, one: **Caput Draconis**. The four Mothers, written right to left across the top of the shield, are Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis.

### The Daughters

Read the Mothers sideways. The head lines of Mothers one through four are two points (Conjunctio), one (Via), one (Amissio), two (Caput Draconis). Stacked downward, 2, 1, 1, 2, the **First Daughter is Conjunctio**, the First Mother's figure again. The air lines are one (Conjunctio), one (Via), two (Amissio), one (Caput Draconis), giving 1, 1, 2, 1, the **Second Daughter is Puer**. The water lines are one, one, one, one, the **Third Daughter is Via**. The earth lines are two (Conjunctio), one (Via), two (Amissio), one (Caput Draconis), giving 2, 1, 2, 1, the **Fourth Daughter is Acquisitio**. Check the transposition fact. The Mothers carry 6 + 4 + 6 + 5 = 21 points, and the Daughters carry 6 + 5 + 4 + 6 = 21. The same lines, in new places.

### Witnesses and Judge

**Right Witness**, First Niece (Carcer) added to Second Niece (Puer): fire, 1 and 1, even, two points; air, 2 and 1, odd, one point; water, 2 and 2, even, two points; earth, 1 and 1, even, two points. Lines 2, 1, 2, 2: the **Right Witness is Rubeus**.

**Left Witness**, Third Niece (Puella) added to Fourth Niece (Amissio): fire, 1 and 1, even, two; air, 2 and 2, even, two; water, 1 and 1, even, two; earth, 1 and 2, odd, one. Lines 2, 2, 2, 1: the **Left Witness is Tristitia**.

**Judge**, Rubeus added to Tristitia: fire, 2 and 2, even, two; air, 1 and 2, odd, one; water, 2 and 2, even, two; earth, 2 and 1, odd, one. Lines 2, 1, 2, 1: the **Judge is Acquisitio**.

## The course charts

The chart you have just derived is the first of three standing course charts: **Chart A**, **Chart B** and **Chart C**. Each is a complete cast with a fixed question. Later lessons return to them for houses, perfection, aspects, company, and written readings.

### Chart A: "Will the marriage go ahead?"

The worked example above: Mothers **Conjunctio, Via, Amissio, Caput Draconis**; Daughters Conjunctio, Puer, Via, Acquisitio; Nieces Carcer, Puer, Puella, Amissio; Right Witness Rubeus, Left Witness Tristitia, **Judge Acquisitio**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['conjunctio', 'via', 'amissio', 'caput-draconis']} caption="Chart A, Judge: Acquisitio." />

You can see this much from the structure alone. Four figures repeat. The seventh house holds Via. The Judge is Acquisitio, a fortunate and stable figure, and the two Witnesses are harsh figures. The chart has mixed findings. How to resolve them belongs to the reading lessons.

### Chart B: "Will the querent recover the partnership?"

A seventh-house question, because partners belong to the seventh. The querent's business partnership has broken and he asks whether it can be restored. The Mothers fell **Puella, Rubeus, Rubeus, Amissio**. Repeated Mothers are normal. The derivation, which you perform in Exercise 1, yields Daughters Carcer, Conjunctio, Carcer, Laetitia; Nieces Via, Cauda Draconis, Via, Tristitia; Right Witness Tristitia, Left Witness Cauda Draconis; **Judge Via**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['puella', 'rubeus', 'rubeus', 'amissio']} caption="Chart B, Judge: Via." />

The doubled Rubeus in the Mothers gives doubled Carcer in the Daughters and doubled Via in the Nieces. The Judge is Via, the figure of change. A chart about restoring a partnership with Via as Judge will need careful reading.

### Chart C: "Will the querent win the lawsuit?"

A lawsuit. The adversary, like the partner and the spouse, belongs to the seventh house, and the question is whether the querent wins. The Mothers fell **Amissio, Amissio, Puella, Fortuna Minor**, again a doubled Mother, this time in the first two places. The derivation, which is Exercise 2, yields Daughters Via, Tristitia, Cauda Draconis, Albus; Nieces Populus, Caput Draconis, Cauda Draconis, Fortuna Minor; Right Witness Caput Draconis, Left Witness Albus; **Judge Acquisitio**.

<ShieldChart mothers={['amissio', 'amissio', 'puella', 'fortuna-minor']} caption="Chart C, Judge: Acquisitio." />

The two identical Mothers add to Populus in the First Niece, and the chart repeats Cauda Draconis and Fortuna Minor elsewhere. Note the main tension. The Judge is Acquisitio, one of the best figures, while the seventh house holds Cauda Draconis, one of the worst. That is not a contradiction. It is a mixed finding, and resolving it is part of the reading lessons.

<Exercise n={4} title="Cast, postpone, or reject" kind="quiz">
  For each case, write **cast**, **postpone**, **reject**, or **repair the derivation**.

  1. The querent asks one concrete question, but is angry and distracted.
  2. A friend asks a question only to see whether the method can be mocked.
  3. The querent asks the same question again because the first answer was unwelcome.
  4. The querent asks the same question after the contract, price, or other facts have materially changed.
  5. You derive the chart and the Judge is odd.
  6. The first house holds Rubeus or Cauda Draconis under this course's practice.
  7. The weather or setting makes it hard to cast steadily.

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. **Postpone.** The question may be real, but the casting is not clean.
    2. **Reject.** A mock test is not an earnest demand.
    3. **Reject.** Repetition because of dislike is not a new question.
    4. **Cast**, if the new facts make a genuinely new question.
    5. **Repair the derivation.** An odd Judge is an arithmetic error.
    6. **Reject this cast and set it aside unjudged.** Under this course's practice, those figures rising are not read.
    7. **Postpone.** Use a time and place where the rows can be made steadily.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>
