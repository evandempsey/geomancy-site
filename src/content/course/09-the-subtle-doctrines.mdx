The modes of perfection told you whether the houses grant the thing asked; aspect and dignity taught you to weigh the testimonies. Those are the foundations of judgment, and nothing in this lesson touches them. This lesson hands you the rest of the toolroll: the small, bright, precisely ground instruments the Renaissance art kept for the finishing strokes, the way of the point, the Part of Fortune, the point of intention, the figure of figures, the declaration of places, and the doctrines of timing. Cattan calls them "much necessarie and profitable," and they are; but he puts them *after* the houses, the Witnesses and Judge, the companies and aspects, in short chapters near the end of Book III, and in his cases they arrive last, adding a detail, breaking a tie, never overturning what the foundations establish. These are refinements, not foundations. A student who can trace the way of the point but cannot scan a chart for perfection has learned to engrave before learning to draw.

Why teach them at all, then, and why now? Three reasons. First, because the sources use them, constantly and visibly: open any of Cattan's case studies (the robbed master, the Lord of Tays and his horse, the courtier and the King) and you will find the subtle techniques woven through the judgment, supplying exactly the kind of particular detail ("the servant shall be discovered by means of the goodes which he hath given unto his kinsfolkes") that the great machines of perfection and dignity are too coarse to produce. A reader of the old books who does not know these instruments cannot follow the old judgments. Second, because two of them (the way of the point and the Part of Fortune) are checkable arithmetic procedures of the kind this course loves, and you will find that the parity theorems you proved in Lessons 4 and 6 have one more delightful consequence waiting inside the way of the point. Third, because this lesson completes Part 2. When it ends, every instrument of reading this course teaches will be in your hands, and every journal chart, the three course charts above all, will carry a complete *pre-judgment dossier*: everything examined, everything recorded, nothing yet weighed. The weighing is an art of its own, and it is the whole business of Part 3.

Six instruments, each a precise procedure with a narrow legitimate use, each from its own chapter of Cattan with its own case study, each run against the three standing charts, including the casts where the instrument returns *nothing*. Then the dossier: the one-page discipline that gathers five lessons of instruments into a single sheet, and the judgment worksheet run end to end as a dry rehearsal.

## Refinements, not foundations

The hierarchy is set by the sources' own behaviour.

Cattan opens his chapter on the way of the point like this: "The Geomanciens have many Rules to judge their figures aright, and amongest others, that which they doe call the way of point, as a thing much necessarie and profitable in this Arte" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 20). *Among others*, one rule in a toolroll of many, necessary and profitable the way a fine chisel is necessary: for the work only it can do. And when he opens the Part of Fortune chapter, he gives the whole order of march in a single sentence: "The question being made, after that we have judged by the houses, figures, angles, companies, aspectes, the way of point, and by all other sortes and manners before saide, nowe resteth it to judge by the parte of fortune" (Book III, ch. 21). Read that sequence with four lessons of judgment behind you and you will recognise it: houses and figures first, the placements of Lesson 5; the angles, strength of place; companies and aspects, Lessons 7 and 8; *then* the way of the point, *then* the Part of Fortune. The subtle doctrines come at the end of the procession because they are the end of the procession.

His case studies keep the same order with their hands. In the horse-buying judgment for the Lord of Tays, which you will read entire below, the angles are weighed first and found "indifferent well"; only then do the aspects, the way of the point and the Part of Fortune come in; and because the foundations were mixed, the refinements tip the balance, all three pulling ill, and ill is the judgment. In the robbery case at the end of the way-of-point chapter, the houses convict the servant (first house, sixth house, seventh, second) and the way of the point arrives last of all to add one engraver's stroke: *how* the thief will be found out. That is the legitimate office of everything in this lesson. When the foundations speak clearly, the subtle techniques add detail and texture. When the foundations are mixed, they break ties. What they never do, anywhere in the sources this course follows, is overrule a clear answer from perfection, the significators and the bench; and a student who lets a Part of Fortune outvote a perfection has not learned a subtler art, only a vaguer one.

Take the two arithmetical instruments first; they are the sharpest.

## The way of the point

Here is the procedure in Cattan's own words, with his case study at its end. Read it slowly; the prose is dense, and the procedure inside it is exact:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-20-of-the-way-of-point-chap" />

Now extract the procedure, step by step. (One slip of the 1591 print to clear first: "then take the points of the left Judge" should read "left witnes", since the sentence makes no sense otherwise.)

1. **Take the first line of the Judge**, the top line, "be it even or odde": one point or two.
2. **Compare the Witnesses' top lines.** If the Right Witness's top line is like the Judge's, the way runs to the right; if the Left Witness's, to the left. If *neither* Witness's top line matches the Judge's, then, in Cattan's words, there is "no way of point" in the figure: the trail dies at the first step, and you record exactly that.
3. **Follow the match upward into the Nieces.** A matching Right Witness sends you to the ninth and tenth houses; look for the same top line there. A matching Left Witness sends you to the eleventh and twelfth.
4. **Follow it again into the top row.** The ninth house, if it matches, sends you to the first and second houses; the tenth to the third and fourth; the eleventh to the fifth and sixth; the twelfth to the seventh and eighth.
5. **Judge by the figure where the way ends.** "If it be good ye shal judge good, and if it be ill you shal judge ill"; the terminal figure, and the house it stands in, are the way's verdict.

The procedure is not arbitrary: the routes are the shield's own family tree, walked backward. The Judge was begotten of the two Witnesses; the Right Witness of the Nieces in houses nine and ten; the ninth house's Niece of the figures in houses one and two; the tenth's of three and four; and the left-hand routes likewise, the Left Witness from the eleventh and twelfth, the eleventh from the fifth and sixth, the twelfth from the seventh and eighth. Every step of the way of the point retraces one generation of the derivation you have performed by hand since Lesson 4. The technique takes the Judge's top line (the first line of the chart's final sentence) and asks: *where did this come from?* It follows the line back through Witness and Niece to the house figure whose top line seeded it, and judges the matter by what it finds at the root. The site's technique page compresses it: the houses where the trail ends show the hidden root of the matter.

The parity arithmetic of Lesson 4 has one more consequence here. Cattan closes the procedure with a sentence that reads like a connoisseur's aside: "ye must understand that the way of point intendeth properly unto one point, for when they be two it is not so perfite as when they be found in one." The way of the point *belongs*, properly, to the single point: a Judge whose top line is one point makes a more perfect way than a Judge whose top line is two. Why? The Judge's top line is the parity-sum of the Witnesses' top lines. If the Judge's top is a *single* point, odd, then the Witnesses' tops sum odd, which means one is single and one double: **exactly one Witness matches, always.** And the same logic repeats at every generation, because every figure's top line is the parity-sum of its two parents' tops: a single-pointed trail figure always has exactly one matching parent. So a single-pointed way never fails and never forks: it threads one unbranched path from the Judge to a unique terminal house, every time, in every chart that can exist. But if the Judge's top is a *double* point, even, the Witnesses' tops are alike: both single or both double. Both match, or neither does. The double-pointed way either dies on the spot or splits in two, and at every later generation each branch dies or splits again. It can never "intend unto one point." Cattan's sentence is not an aside; it is the arithmetic speaking in Elizabethan dress.

In his robbery case, the houses do the convicting: an ill first house; Cauda Draconis in the sixth, the house of servants, "the which doth signifie robbery"; an ill seventh; the second house's figure, the master's substance, springing into the house "which containeth the demaundes which be upon substance," the stolen goods moving through the chart exactly as Lesson 7 taught you figures pass. The aspects refine: trine and sextile regards from figures of Mercury and the Sun, so the servant "should be taken in doing the facte." And the way of the point arrives last, running into a figure of the Sun, to add the single most particular detail in the judgment: the thief will be discovered "by means of the goodes which he hath given unto his kinsfolkes." One instrument, one engraved stroke. That is the technique used well.

Now the three standing charts.

**Chart A.** The Judge is Acquisitio, whose first line is two points: a double-topped Judge, so expect the way to fork or fail. Both Witnesses match: Rubeus carries two points on its top line and so does Tristitia, both like the Judge, just as the arithmetic predicts for a double top (both or neither). So the way looks right, to houses nine and ten: Carcer and Puer, top lines single and single, no match. And left, to houses eleven and twelve: Puella and Amissio, single and single, no match. The way stops before reaching the upper houses: it does not complete. Record precisely that: *way of point: begins double, both Witnesses match, dies at the Nieces; no way.* The instrument returns nothing for Chart A, and nothing is the finding.

**Chart B.** The Judge is Via, first line a single point, and you now know what that promises: one Witness will match, and the way will run unbranched to its end. The Right Witness Tristitia carries a double top, no match. The Left Witness Cauda Draconis carries a single; the way runs left, to houses eleven and twelve. The eleventh holds Via, top line single: it matches; the twelfth holds Tristitia, double: it does not. From the eleventh, the way runs to houses five and six. The fifth holds Carcer, top line single: it matches; the sixth holds Conjunctio, double: it does not. **The way of the point ends in house five, on Carcer.** A perfect, single-pointed way, the kind Cattan calls the more perfite; and its verdict is the prison. Judge by the figure where the way ends: Carcer is an ill figure, the figure of binding and delay, and the way of the point therefore judges this matter ill: the hidden root of the partnership question is the bind itself. Set that beside what the dossier already holds for Chart B (perfection denied by occupation and conjunction, the blackest column on the sixteen leaves, a Judge of change over a question of restoration) and notice what the subtle instrument has done: not overturned anything, but *agreed*, adding one more dark weight to a scale already tilting dark, and adding it from an entirely independent direction. Weights, not verdicts; the weighing is Lesson 10's.

**Chart C.** The Judge is Acquisitio again, double-topped, and again both Witnesses match: Caput Draconis and Albus both carry two points above. The way looks right, to houses nine and ten: the ninth holds Populus, top line double, match; the tenth holds Caput Draconis, double, match. The double way is forking, exactly as the arithmetic says it must if it lives at all. From the ninth, the way runs to houses one and two: Amissio and Amissio, single tops, no match. From the tenth, to houses three and four: Puella and Fortuna Minor, single tops, no match. And the left branch, from Albus to houses eleven and twelve: Cauda Draconis and Fortuna Minor, single tops, no match. The way stops before reaching the upper houses: it does not complete. Chart C's trail runs deeper than Chart A's (two full generations before it dies one rank short of the top row) and still delivers nothing. Record the honest negative, with the trace: in the conflict chart, the instrument built for finding hidden roots finds none, and the conflict stands exactly where Lessons 6 and 8 left it.

One chart in three with a way at all, and that proportion is honest. The way of the point is a conditional instrument: it speaks rarely, and when it speaks (as in Chart B, with the perfect single-point way) it speaks with a clear small voice. Every "no way" is a completed application of the technique, not a failure of it.

## The Part of Fortune

The second arithmetical instrument, from the chapter that follows, with its case study and (read all the way to the end) a rejection you must learn as carefully as the rule:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-21-howe-to-knowe-the-parte-of" />

The rule first, stated cleanly. **Sum the points of the twelve house figures, the twelve and no others. Divide by twelve. The remainder names the house, and the Part of Fortune falls there; mark it with the character ☼.** A remainder of two gives the second house, four the fourth, ten the tenth; and when the division comes out even, remainder nothing, the Part falls in the twelfth, as Cattan's own worked numbers show: "if the fygure be of 72. points, or 84. or 96. or of 108. points, then the parte of fortune shall goe into the twelfth." (The remainder of twelve figure-points is always even, as Cattan's examples show: two, four, six, eight, ten, twelve, and your own below will be too.)

Then the interpretation, which is where Lesson 8's habits apply. The Part of Fortune is not a verdict; it is an *address*, the place where the chart's fortune concentrates, and Cattan's rule for reading it weighs two things together: "if the fygure and house be good (for both the one and the other must be looked uppon) you shall judge good, and if it bee evill ye shall also judge evill… But if the fygure be good, and the house ill, or contrary, the house good and the fygure ill, you shall judge the saide parte of fortune to be meane." Figure *and* house, always both: a good figure on a good house is good fortune for the question; ill on ill is ill; and every mixed case is mean: the Elizabethan *mean* you know from the tables, middling, neither. The instrument's whole interpretive doctrine is three sentences long, and its discipline is the word in parentheses: *both the one and the other must be looked uppon.*

The case study shows the instrument in its working place. The Lord of Tays wants a horse; the figure is made, and the order of the judgment is the familiar one. The first figure and its companion are good, the fourth and seventh good, "the Angles for the better parte were good": foundations first, and they lean fair: "I judged indifferent well." But the aspects were ill, the opposition mean, "the way of point went into an ill fygure: and also because the parte of fortune happened on an ill fygure and house, I judged it to be ill." Mixed foundations; the refinements break the tie, and three subtle instruments pulling the same direction is no longer a detail. And then the engraver's stroke, from the houses as always: the twelfth house (the house of horses and great beasts, as Lesson 5 taught you, and of the feet and legs in the body) holds Amissio, so "I judged that the horse had an evill foote, and was worth nothing: and so it was founde true in the Towne of Lyon." A judgment built exactly to the hierarchy this lesson opened with.

And then the last sentences of the chapter, which this course requires you to learn as doctrine. "Many doe use an other manner to finde part of fortune, in taking all the points as well of the twelve houses as of the two witnesses, and the Judge, which they do part by twelve (as is aforesaide) but because I have founde no trueth therein I will speake no more thereof." There is a rival rule (fifteen figures, Witnesses and Judge included) and Cattan reports it, tests it against his own practice, finds no truth in it, and discards it in a sentence. This course follows him: **the Part of Fortune is computed from the twelve house figures only.** But how he discards it is the model: he does not pretend the variant doesn't exist, and he does not adjudicate it by authority: he reports it, gives his ground ("I have founde no trueth therein"), and moves on. A divergence noted, a working rule chosen, a reason on the record. Exercise 2 will have you compute the rejected variant once, so that you know exactly what you are *not* doing and can recognise it when a later book does it silently.

Now the three charts; the arithmetic is yours to verify line by line.

**Chart A.** The twelve house figures and their points: Conjunctio 6, Via 4, Amissio 6, Caput Draconis 5, Conjunctio 6, Puer 5, Via 4, Acquisitio 6, Carcer 6, Puer 5, Puella 5, Amissio 6. The sum: 64. Divide by twelve: five twelves are 60, remainder **4**. The Part of Fortune falls in the **fourth house**, on **Caput Draconis**; mark ☼ beside it in your journal copy. The weighing belongs to the worked example below, where it is done in full.

**Chart B.** Puella 5, Rubeus 7, Rubeus 7, Amissio 6, Carcer 6, Conjunctio 6, Carcer 6, Laetitia 7, Via 4, Cauda Draconis 5, Via 4, Tristitia 7. The sum: 70. Five twelves are 60, remainder **10**: the **tenth house**, and the tenth house of Chart B holds **Cauda Draconis**. Weigh both, per the rule. The figure: the Dragon's Tail, the way out, one of the two figures of dread: ill. The house: the tenth, an angle, the heart of heaven, among the strong and fortunate places. A good house under an ill figure: *the Part of Fortune is mean*; and lean, if anything, toward the cold side of mean, for the fortune of this chart concentrates at the threshold of departure, in the house of standing and authority. For a question about recovering a partnership, the chart's point of fortune sits on the figure of leaving.

**Chart C.** Amissio 6, Amissio 6, Puella 5, Fortuna Minor 6, Via 4, Tristitia 7, Cauda Draconis 5, Albus 7, Populus 8, Caput Draconis 5, Cauda Draconis 5, Fortuna Minor 6. The sum: 70, the same total as Chart B, and you should check it digit by digit precisely because it is the same. Remainder **10**: the **tenth house** again; and Chart C's tenth holds **Caput Draconis**. The figure: the Dragon's Head, the fortunate threshold, the way in: good. The house: the tenth, angle, good. Both good: *the Part of Fortune is good*, and it sits in the house of honours and magistrates, for a lawsuit, the house of the judges themselves.

Charts B and C carry the *same* house-point total, seventy, and therefore the same remainder, ten, and therefore the same Part-of-Fortune house, the tenth. And the tenth house of Chart B holds Cauda Draconis while the tenth of Chart C holds Caput Draconis: the Dragon's Tail and the Dragon's Head, the two threshold figures, each the other's top-to-bottom reflection, one the way out and one the way in. Identical arithmetic, opposite figures, opposite colour: B's fortune-point mean-dark on the figure of departure, C's good on the figure of arrival. The computation and the interpretation are separate acts: the division names an address, and only the weighing of figure and house delivers a meaning.

The modern comparison, last, and brief. Here is the whole of Crowley's doctrine on the subject:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/part-of-fortune" />

One sentence, and instructive in three ways. The arithmetic is *identical* to Cattan's: the points of figures one through twelve, the Golden Dawn line, whatever else it changed, kept the twelve-figure rule and not the fifteen-figure variant Cattan rejected, a four-century agreement worth a line in your journal. The mark differs: Crowley's ⊕, the astrological sigil of Fortuna, where Cattan prescribes ☼; use either, but know whose is whose. And the *meaning* has narrowed drastically: for Crowley the Part of Fortune shows "ready money or cash belonging to the Querent" (a pointer for second-house questions, full stop) where Cattan weighs figure against house and judges the fortune of the whole question by it, in any matter whatever. The modern comparison, as so often, preserves the procedure and shrinks the doctrine; when you read a Golden Dawn chart, expect its Part of Fortune to be talking about money, and when you read by this course, expect yours to be talking about the question.

## The point of intention

The third instrument answers a stranger question than the first two: not "how does the matter end" or "where does the fortune fall," but *what is actually being asked*. Cattan gives it two chapters (one for the intent of a figure found without its question, one for the thought of another person) and you must read both with their frames intact, because the frames are half the doctrine:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-18-to-know-for-what-intent-a" />

The problem the chapter solves is real and specific: "it may happen sometimes that a person may finde a fygure, and knoweth not for what intent and ende it hath beene made, neyther findeth any inscription in the same." A chart without its question, a dead letter, by everything this course has taught, since Lesson 5 made the question the lamp that lights the houses. Cattan's rules recover the question from the chart itself, and he gives three.

The **first rule**: watch where the first house "goeth into any of the other" (where the querent's own figure springs up elsewhere in the chart) "and judge according unto the signification of the house where she goeth." The querent's figure, repeated in the fifth, betrays a fifth-house errand; in the tenth, an ambition. You have been recording springings since Lesson 4; this rule simply reads them for motive.

The **second rule** is the projection, and it is the one Cattan endorses: take "all the uneven points of all the twelve figures" (count the *single* points, the odd lines, across the twelve house figures) and deal them out one to each house in order, first, second, third, around and around the wheel "untill that all the points be bestowed." The house that receives the last point is the house of the intent: the matter the figure was made for. His case study performs it: a found figure, twenty-four uneven points, "thereby having twice run over all the fygures" (twenty-four is two full circuits of twelve) the last point falls on the twelfth, "so that I judged that the fygure had beene made uppon the imprisonment of some person." And his ranking is explicit: "that rule which is onely by the 12. houses, is the better, more sure and certaine." When this course says *the point of intention*, it means this rule.

The **third rule** adds the first and seventh figures together and judges the intent by the figure their sum produces, read against the little table that closes the chapter: Acquisitio, "the demaunde was for silver"; Puella, "of mirth, or of a woman with childe, or of mariage, or of jorny"; Carcer, "of prisonment, sickenesse, or a thing hidden." A figure-alphabet of motives.

And then the chapter's last sentence: "You must understand that these Rules be not here placed as necessarie unto Geomancie, but onely put for pleasure, and to shewe that Geomancie is a wittie Arte." Cattan himself files the intent-rules under *pleasure* (wit, virtuosity, the art showing off its reach) not under necessity. The second chapter doubles the disclaimer:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-19-to-knowe-what-an-other-thinketh" />

To know the thought of another person, cast a figure and read the first figure against the table: Fortuna Major, "of feare, fortune, or gaine by a woman"; Laetitia, "of some good and merrie thing"; Rubeus, "of bloud shead or of a red thing or of some fright." And the frame, stated before the rules this time, in Cattan's most explicit voice: placed "by the way of pleasure and recreation… not to the intent that any man give any credit or trust to it more then to the rest of the Arte, which was for none other purpose invented (as I have many times protested and said) but for the pastime and pleasure of the wittie heads and inventive." Whatever you make of the protestation (Lesson 1 discussed the Renaissance habit of armouring divination books with disclaimers) the *relative* weighting is unmistakable: of all the instruments in this lesson, the intention-rules are the ones their own author holds lightest.

So when is the point of intention legitimate? This course's answer, defended and tested in Exercise 5, is: **when the question is genuinely unavailable, and as a check, never as a substitute.** The legitimate cases are Cattan's own and their honest extensions: a figure found without inscription, where there is no question to be had and the projection is the only door in; a historical chart in an old book, recorded without its demand; and (the practitioner's case) a querent who will not, or cannot, say plainly what they are asking, where the projection serves as a *test of the stated question*: cast, project, and if the point of intention lands far from the house the querent named, you have learned to ask better questions before judging. The crutch begins where the discipline of Lesson 4 ends: a diviner who skips the framing of the question because "the chart will reveal the intent" has inverted the art's entire procedure: the question creates the chart, on every doctrine this course teaches, and a technique for recovering a lost question is not a licence to stop asking. Cattan asked his querents what they wanted. The chapter exists for the day somebody hands you a figure that cannot answer.

One comparison remains, and it changes the instrument's weight entirely as it crosses sources. The pseudo-Agrippa's treatise carries a projection of its own, the **Index**:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/technique-index" />

The same family of operation (count points, divide by twelve, project the remainder from the Ascendant around the houses) though note the differences in the procedure's grain: the pseudo-Agrippa counts "all the points which are contained in the lines of the projections" and projects *the remainder* house by house, where Cattan's second rule deals out *the odd points themselves* until exhausted; cousins, not twins. But the doctrine attached to it is of another order altogether. The Index "giveth you a competent judgment of the thing quesited"; and "if on either part they shall be equal, or ambiguous, then the index alone shall certifie you of the thing quesited." This is not pleasure and recreation. This is the secret of the art, a standing arbiter with the authority to *decide a tied chart by itself*. And Heydon carries the same apparatus forward into the seventeenth century:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/index-multiplication" />

By Lesson 6's source-critical eye, the editorial note tells you, and a clause-by-clause comparison with the pseudo-Agrippa confirms, that this is the *Of Geomancy* attributed to Agrippa reprinted nearly verbatim, one witness in a second coat, exactly like his Witnesses chapters. So the tally is: one tradition (pseudo-Agrippa, transmitted by Heydon) in which the projection-figure is a weighty standing instrument, consulted in every judgment ("beholding and diligently respecting all the figures, and the Index it self in two houses, you shall ground the face of judgment") and beside it the multiplication doctrine you already know as springing, the quesited's figure spreading itself through the houses ("if a question shall be propounded of the second house concerning a Thief, and the figure of the second house shall be found in the sixth, it declareth the Thief to be some of ones own houshold or servants"). And one tradition (Cattan) in which the cousin technique is filed under wit and pleasure. The divergence is real, and this course reports it rather than flattening it: the projection-of-points family ranges, across the sources, from a parlour flourish to the secret of the art. The course's practice follows Cattan (the point of intention is an occasional instrument with a narrow legitimate use) while requiring you to know that a geomancer of the pseudo-Agrippa's school would have computed an Index for every chart and trusted it in every deadlock. When Lesson 10 teaches the weighing of divided testimony, remember that one branch of the tradition kept a tie-breaking figure for exactly that purpose, and this course's branch did not.

## The figure of figures, and the declaration of places

Two short chapters of Cattan's Book III remain, both easy to misread as repetition; here is what they add.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-11-of-the-figure-of-figures" />

The **figure of figures** is a doctrine of *agreement between figure and place*, the chart rhyming with its own question. Cattan gives three forms of it. A significator's figure passing into the very house whose matter is asked about: Acquisitio in the first house passing to the second in a question of gain, the asker's own figure walking into the house of the thing he wants. A first-house figure whose gender agrees with the querent: a masculine figure rising for a man, a feminine for a woman: "then say wee that the Fygure dooth agree with the place." And the question's natural figure standing in the question's house: Via, the Way, in the third house for a question of ways. None of these is perfection (nothing here requires the two significators to reach one another) and none of it is mere placement either. It is a *felicity*: the chart speaking the question's own language, and the tradition counted such agreement a strengthening testimony wherever it appeared. His case study reads two such agreements at once (a feminine figure rising for a gentlewoman querent, and Laetitia, the figure of joy, standing in the fifth, the house of feasting, for a question about table-talk) and builds from them a judgment of startling particularity: a woman clothed in white, talk "of mirth, & of things of the Church," found true. The doctrine for your dossier: note every agreement of figure with place involving the significators or the question's house, by name ("figure of figures: Via (the Way) would rhyme with a journey question; here, none") and count it as one small fair weight where it occurs.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-10-the-declaration-of-the-thing-which" />

The **declaration of places** looks, at first, like Lesson 5's angles, succedents and cadents again; and its first half is exactly that, in Cattan's own Book III voice: place, house and figure "is not but all one thing"; the angles strongest, the succedents mean, the cadents weak and feeble. But the chapter's second half adds two doctrines Lesson 5 did not have. First, *manner*: the angles signify things "manifest and publike," the cadents "things privie and secret," the succedents partly each; where a testimony stands tells you not only how strong it is but how visible its matter is. Second, and for this lesson decisively, *time*: "The places finally signify the time, as the Angles time present, the Succedants to come, the Cadants past." The wheel is a clock as well as a map: angular testimony speaks of now, succedent of what is coming, cadent of what has passed or comes "very late." His case study runs the whole chart through that clock for a courtier deciding when to return to the King: good angles: go now, the present favours it; good succedents: profit shortly to come; ill cadents every one: do not linger, for the latter end of the stay turns bad. One chart, three tenses, and a piece of practical advice assembled from nothing but strength of place read as time.

## The speed of the matter

The tradition's timing doctrines are scattered, modest and honest about their own softness, and this course teaches them in exactly that spirit. You hold three of them now.

The first you have owned since Lesson 2, as structure: the entering and exiting figures.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

In short: top-heavy figures enter and are good, bottom-heavy go out and are evil, balanced figures are mean, with the three named exceptions (Tristitia top-heavy but not good, Laetitia bottom-heavy but not evil, Carcer balanced but never neutral). Applied to a question, the doctrine speaks of *direction*: a matter signified by entering figures is arriving, coming toward the querent, gathering; by exiting figures, already leaving. The second is the mobility classification that runs in the dossier's last column and has run through every lesson since the natures: stable figures signify matters that endure, settle, resist (slow to arrive and slow to leave) and mobile figures matters in motion, quick, transient, apt to change. Read together over a whole chart, and especially over the significators and the Judge, these two give you a *relative* estimate of speed and direction: a chart of mobile, entering figures is a matter arriving fast; stable, balanced figures, a matter that sits.

The third is the strangest, and you re-read it in the assigned reading, the closing material of the chapter of qualities, where Cattan judges the trades, complexions and length of life:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-time-of-life" />

Set aside the trades (the weaver, the gaoler, the "théefe and a Pirote on the sea") and take the timing kernel: a figure of six points in the first house signifies that the man shall live "sixe houres, sixe dayes, sixe moneths, sixe yeares, or 72"; the figure's point-count supplies a *number*, and the unit (hours, days, months or years) is chosen "according as the fygures be good or badde," with the whole apparatus of companies, angles, aspects, the way of point and the Part of Fortune called in to settle it, and the result then scaled again: good figures the full term, ill figures "a third parte or lesser," mean figures half. What this is: a number with a four-orders-of-magnitude unit attached, disciplined only by the judge's whole reading of the chart; and Cattan closes the passage with his broadest disclaimer in the book, that none should "give credite unto all this," it being for "recreation and pastime." This course teaches the point-count method so that you can read the sources that use it, and licenses it in your own practice only at the level the evidence supports: as a way of articulating a timing *guess* you have already formed from mobility, entrance and place, never as a calculation that outranks them.

The course's timing protocol, fixed from this lesson: estimate speed from the mobility of the significators and the Judge, coloured by entering and exiting; locate the matter in time by the places, after the declaration's clock; and state every timing estimate with its uncertainty written into the sentence. The worked example performs it on Chart A; Exercise 6 hands you Chart B. What you may never write is a date. The tradition's own instruments do not yield one, and a precision the instruments cannot support is not precision but theatre.

## The pre-judgment dossier

The gathering is itself this lesson's protocol upgrade, the last of Part 2.

Since Lesson 4 your journal has grown by accretion: the cast and its verification, then placements and significators, then the triad and its sentence, then the perfection scan and a provisional verdict, then the weighing of aspects and dignities. This lesson completes the instrument set, and with completion comes a new discipline: the **pre-judgment dossier**, one page per chart, everything in one place. From this lesson every chart in your journal gets one:

1. **The question**, exactly as asked, and its house, with the one-sentence defence.
2. **Step 0**: the first-house figure, and *proceeds*, or the entry marked unjudged, and the page ends there.
3. **The placements**: the twelve houses written out, the significators named, each classified by place, and now also by the declaration's clock: angular-present, succedent-coming, cadent-past.
4. **The springings**: every repeated figure, with its houses, and any figure of figures noted by name where figure and place agree.
5. **The bench**: the triad with points and parities, the sentence in one line, the Judge's tendency, the table's row for the question, in the column's own words.
6. **The perfection scan**: every mode tested in order, everything found recorded, the Lesson 7 provisional verdict carried forward.
7. **The weights**: the aspects the significators behold and suffer, the dignities and debilities, as Lesson 8 taught.
8. **The way of the point**: the trace, step by step, to its terminal figure, or the honest *no way*.
9. **The Part of Fortune**: the sum, the division, the house, the figure, the ☼, and the weighing, figure and house both, good, ill or mean.
10. **Timing**: the speed estimate from mobility and entrance, with its uncertainty in the sentence.
11. **The verdict line** (and here the discipline bites) written *provisional* in every case: one sentence of where the weights lean, marked pending, because the method that turns a dossier into a judgment is Lesson 10's, and not one entry in your journal anticipates it.

The judgment worksheet below embodies the spine of this form (question, bench, significators, perfection, strength, verdict) and the dossier wraps the worksheet's six steps in the full instrument set. Run the worksheet as the rehearsal; keep the dossier as the record.

Everything is on the table: nothing left unexamined, nothing yet decided.

## Worked example

Chart A, under the full suite of subtle instruments, end to end, and then the worksheet, run as the course's first complete dry rehearsal of judgment.

The standing question: **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** The chart, dealt into its houses:

**The way of the point, traced.** The Judge is Acquisitio; its first line carries two points, a double top, so before touching the chart you know the way will fork or fail, never thread. The Witnesses: Rubeus's top line, two points, like the Judge; Tristitia's top line, two points, like the Judge. Both match, as a double top requires (both or neither). The right branch: houses nine and ten, Carcer and Puer (top lines one point and one point) neither like the Judge. The left branch: houses eleven and twelve, Puella and Amissio (one point and one point) neither like the Judge. The way stops before reaching the upper houses: it does not complete. The journal line: *way of point: double top; both Witnesses match; dies at the Nieces; no way.* The instrument has nothing to say about this marriage, and the dossier records the nothing.

**The Part of Fortune, computed digit by digit.** The twelve house figures: Conjunctio 6, Via 4, Amissio 6, Caput Draconis 5, running sum 21; Conjunctio 6, Puer 5, Via 4, Acquisitio 6, sum 42; Carcer 6, Puer 5, Puella 5, Amissio 6, sum **64**. The house: the fourth, an angle, strong; and, by its office, the house of the end of the matter. (For completeness, the rejected variant: adding the Witnesses and Judge (Rubeus 7, Tristitia 7, Acquisitio 6) gives 84, which divides by twelve exactly, remainder nothing, sending the variant Part to the twelfth house. You will compute this once, properly, in Exercise 2, and then never again.)

**The point of intention, demonstrated.** Suppose this chart had reached you without its question, or suppose, more realistically, the querent had brought it to you wrapped in fog: *"It concerns… an arrangement with another person."* Apply Cattan's better rule, "onely by the 12. houses." Count the uneven points (the single-point lines) of the twelve house figures: Conjunctio 2, Via 4, Amissio 2, Caput Draconis 3, Conjunctio 2, Puer 3, Via 4, Acquisitio 2, Carcer 2, Puer 3, Puella 3, Amissio 2, thirty-two in all. (Check it the fast way: twelve figures carry forty-eight lines; the house points total 64; every double line adds one point above its line, so the doubles number 64 − 48 = 16, and the singles 48 − 16 = 32. The arithmetic audits itself, as always.) Deal the thirty-two points around the wheel, one to each house: twice round is twenty-four; the remaining eight carry the count to the **eighth house**. The point of intention falls in the eighth; and recall the eighth's tenants from Lesson 5: the substance of the other party, "the treatie of the marriage of a woman," the dowry. The projection, asked what this foggy "arrangement" really concerns, points at the marriage-treaty corner of the wheel, startlingly warm for an instrument its own author files under pleasure, though honesty requires the full report: it indicates the *treaty and portion*, one house away from the seventh-house accord itself. And the other rules? The first rule reads the querent's springing: Conjunctio passes from the first to the fifth, pleasures, feasts, children: adjacent to a marriage's concerns, but no nearer. The third rule adds the first and seventh figures (Conjunctio and Via: fire 2 and 1, odd, one point; air 1 and 1, even, two; water 1 and 1, even, two; earth 2 and 1, odd, one) **Carcer**, whose entry in the intent-table reads "of prisonment, sickenesse, or a thing hidden": for this question, a plain miss, unless one strains for the hidden negotiations, and you should not strain. Record all three results, the warm and the cold together. The instrument is approximate; Cattan said so first; and a student who reports only the projection that landed warm has begun curating evidence, which is the first step off the path this course walks.

**The timing, estimated.** The significators: Conjunctio and Via, both mobile: the parties and the matter are in motion. The wheel at large: eight of the twelve house figures mobile, a chart that moves. The Judge: Acquisitio, stable: an end that holds once reached. The entrances: the question's own house holds Via, balanced and mean (neither arriving nor departing of itself) while the Part of Fortune's Caput Draconis enters, and the fourth house, where matters end, is angular: present-tense by the declaration's clock. The estimate, with its uncertainty written in: *a swift matter: mobile significators in a mobile chart suggest the business moves quickly toward its conclusion, and the stable Judge suggests that what concludes will stay concluded; but the tradition's instruments yield comparative speed, not a calendar, and this estimate asserts no more than "quick rather than slow, and lasting rather than brief."* That is a complete, honest timing line. What it does not contain: a month, a season, a number; the point-count method of the chapter of qualities could dress this estimate in numbers (a six-point Judge: six days, six months…), and the dressing would add theatre, not knowledge.

**The dossier, assembled.** Everything this course has extracted from Chart A since Lesson 4, on one page: the question and its seventh house; Step 0: Conjunctio, proceeds; significators Conjunctio (1st, angle, mobile) and Via (7th, angle, mobile); springings: Conjunctio 1 and 5, Via 2 and 7, Amissio 3 and 12, Puer 6 and 10; the bench: Rubeus (7), Tristitia (7), Acquisitio (6), *ill begun, hard carried, ends in gain*, the table's row for the bond: *a wife: ill*; the perfection scan: no occupation, but **the chart perfects by conjunction**, the quesited's Via standing in the second house, beside the querent; the Lesson 8 weights on the significators as your worksheet recorded them; the way of the point: none; the Part of Fortune: 64, remainder 4, the fourth, Caput Draconis, ☼, *good*; timing: swift, lasting, uncertainty stated. Eleven lines, and not one of them a judgment.

**The worksheet, run dry.** Now the rehearsal. Work the six steps of the worksheet below with Chart A's dossier open. You now possess a defensible entry for every field, which was not true one lesson ago.

Step one, the question: *Will the marriage go ahead?* The seventh house, the accord between the querent and a facing party. Step two, the bench: Rubeus, Tristitia, Acquisitio; the promise: an ill beginning, a heavy course, a gainful end; the table's own row for the bond dark. Step three, the significators: Conjunctio in the first, Via in the seventh; the springings: Via again in the second, Conjunctio again in the fifth. Step four, perfection: conjunction, the quesited's figure stands beside the querent's house; mark it, with occupation tested and absent, and the company and aspect findings noted as Lesson 8 weighed them. By Cattan's rule, "if the last point remaine on the first house, it signifyeth thereby that the person hath desired to have that figure to be made upon some of the demaundes which be of the first house", and so for each house in turn (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 18). The judgment: the figure was made upon some demand of the fourth house: the father, lands and inheritances, houses and grounds, treasure hidden, or the end and issue of some matter. A fuller answer adds the honest hedge the worked example modelled: the projection names a house, not a sentence, and Populus, the mirror, standing in that house declines to particularise; "a question touching the father, or lands, or some inheritance" is as far as the instrument carries, and further is invention.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>
