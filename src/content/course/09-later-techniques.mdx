The modes of perfection tell you whether the thing asked for reaches the asker; aspect and dignity show how strongly the surrounding findings stand. Those are the foundations, and this lesson does not change them. It adds the remaining secondary techniques: the way of the point, the Part of Fortune, the point of intention, the figure of figures, the declaration of places, and timing. Cattan calls them "much necessarie and profitable," but in his cases they add detail or help with mixed cases. They do not overturn the foundations; treat them as refinements.

They matter for three reasons. First, Cattan's cases use them, and you cannot follow those cases without them. Second, the way of the point and the Part of Fortune are checkable arithmetic procedures. Third, this lesson completes Part 2's chart record: by the end, a chart has its main answer, its qualifications, and its later refinements in one place. Lesson 10 teaches how to turn that record into a clean written answer.



## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 5 through 8: the houses, significators and Step 0; the Witness–Judge triad and its summary (several techniques here begin at the Judge and work backward); the modes of perfection and the scanning discipline (the chart record has the scan at its centre); and the aspect-and-dignity review (the Part of Fortune requires figure and house together).

The assigned reading is the timing portion of Cattan's chapter of qualities, especially the entering and exiting figures and the material on reading length of life from a figure's points. Also read the three technique pages: [the way of the point](/techniques/way-of-the-point/), [the point of intention](/techniques/point-of-intention/), and [projection of points](/techniques/projection-of-points/). The last is the pseudo-Agrippa's cousin to Cattan's intention rule.

The reading worksheet can organize your notes. It walks the reading order one step at a time and assembles a draft paragraph. Use it if it helps. In Step 6, write the answer line your record supports, along with the main limits the answer must keep.

## Later techniques, not foundations

The reading order is set by the sources' own behaviour.

Cattan opens his chapter on the way of the point like this: "The Geomanciens have many Rules to judge their figures aright, and amongest others, that which they doe call the way of point, as a thing much necessarie and profitable in this Arte" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 20). It is one rule among others. When he opens the Part of Fortune chapter, he gives the order plainly: "The question being made, after that we have judged by the houses, figures, angles, companies, aspectes, the way of point, and by all other sortes and manners before saide, nowe resteth it to judge by the parte of fortune" (Book III, ch. 21). The order is: houses and figures; angles and strength of place; companies and aspects; then the way of the point and the Part of Fortune.

His case studies use the same order. In the Lord of Tays horse-buying case, the angles are read first; because the foundations are mixed, the secondary techniques help decide the case. In the robbery case, the houses identify the servant, and the way of the point adds the detail of how he is discovered. Clear foundations decide; mixed foundations may be clarified by later checks; a Part of Fortune that reverses a clear perfection is an error.

Start with the two arithmetic techniques.

## The way of the point

Here is the procedure in Cattan's own words, with his case study at its end. Read it slowly; the prose is dense, and the procedure inside it is exact:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-20-of-the-way-of-point-chap" />

Now extract the procedure, step by step. (One slip of the 1591 print to clear first: "then take the points of the left Judge" should read "left witnes", since the sentence makes no sense otherwise.)

1. **Take the first line of the Judge**, the top line, "be it even or odde": one point or two.
2. **Compare the Witnesses' top lines.** If the Right Witness's top line is like the Judge's, the way runs to the right; if the Left Witness's, to the left. If *neither* Witness's top line matches the Judge's, then, in Cattan's words, there is "no way of point" in the figure: the path stops at the first step, and you record exactly that.
3. **Follow the match upward into the Nieces.** A matching Right Witness sends you to the ninth and tenth houses; look for the same top line there. A matching Left Witness sends you to the eleventh and twelfth.
4. **Follow it again into the top row.** The ninth house, if it matches, sends you to the first and second houses; the tenth to the third and fourth; the eleventh to the fifth and sixth; the twelfth to the seventh and eighth.
5. **Judge by the figure where the way ends.** "If it be good ye shal judge good, and if it be ill you shal judge ill"; the terminal figure, and the house it stands in, are the way's answer.

The route follows the derivation of the shield backward: Judge to Witnesses, Witnesses to Nieces, Nieces to the top row. The way of the point takes the Judge's top line and asks where it came from, and the terminal house shows the underlying place of the matter.

The parity arithmetic from Lesson 4 explains Cattan's preference for a single point. He writes: "ye must understand that the way of point intendeth properly unto one point, for when they be two it is not so perfite as when they be found in one." If the Judge's top line is single, the Witnesses' top lines must differ. Exactly one Witness matches, and the same rule repeats at every generation. A single-pointed way therefore cannot fail or fork; it always reaches one terminal house. If the Judge's top line is double, the Witnesses' top lines are alike. Both match or neither does. The way either stops or forks, and each later branch can stop or fork again.

In Cattan's robbery case, the main reading comes from the houses, and the way of the point arrives last, adding one specific detail: the thief will be discovered "by means of the goodes which he hath given unto his kinsfolkes." That is the technique in its proper place.

Now the three standing charts.

**Chart A.** The Judge is Acquisitio, double-topped, so expect the way to fork or fail. Both Witnesses match, but houses nine through twelve all carry single tops: no match anywhere, and the way stops at the Nieces. Record precisely that: *way of point: begins double, both Witnesses match, dies at the Nieces; no way.* The technique returns nothing for Chart A, and nothing is the finding.

**Chart B.** The Judge is Via, first line a single point, so one Witness will match and the way will run to one end. The Right Witness Tristitia has a double top, no match. The Left Witness Cauda Draconis has a single top, so the way runs left, to houses eleven and twelve. The eleventh holds Via, top line single: it matches. The twelfth holds Tristitia, double: it does not. From the eleventh, the way runs to houses five and six. The fifth holds Carcer, top line single: it matches. The sixth holds Conjunctio, double: it does not. **The way of the point ends in house five, on Carcer.** Carcer is an ill figure of binding and delay, so the technique reads the partnership question as bound by fifth-house matters. That agrees with the rest of Chart B's chart record; it does not reverse anything. It adds one more unfavorable finding.

**Chart C.** The Judge is Acquisitio again, double-topped, and both Witnesses match. The trail runs deeper (Populus and Caput Draconis in the ninth and tenth also match) but every branch dies at the top row: **no way**. Record the negative finding with the trace.

Only one of the three standing charts has a completed way. That is normal. The way of the point is conditional: sometimes it applies, often it does not. A recorded "no way" is still a completed use of the technique.

## The Part of Fortune

The second arithmetic technique comes from the next chapter. Read the rule, the case study, and the rejection at the end.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-21-howe-to-knowe-the-parte-of" />

The rule is simple. **Sum the points of the twelve house figures, and only those twelve. Divide by twelve. The remainder names the house, and the Part of Fortune falls there; mark it with ☼.** A remainder of two gives the second house, four the fourth, ten the tenth. If the division is exact, the Part falls in the twelfth, as Cattan's examples show.

Then the interpretation. The Part of Fortune is not an answer; it is an *address*, the place where the chart's fortune concentrates, and Cattan's rule reads two things together: "if the fygure and house be good (for both the one and the other must be looked uppon) you shall judge good, and if it bee evill ye shall also judge evill… But if the fygure be good, and the house ill, or contrary, the house good and the fygure ill, you shall judge the saide parte of fortune to be meane." Figure *and* house, always both; every mixed case is mean.

The case study shows the technique in its place. The Lord of Tays wants a horse; the foundations lean favorable ("I judged indifferent well"), but the aspects are ill, the way of the point goes into an ill figure, and the Part of Fortune falls on an ill figure and house, so the later findings decide the mixed case. The twelfth house supplies the detail: Amissio, so the horse has an ill foot and is worth nothing.

The last sentences of the chapter matter. "Many doe use an other manner to finde part of fortune, in taking all the points as well of the twelve houses as of the two witnesses, and the Judge, which they do part by twelve (as is aforesaide) but because I have founde no trueth therein I will speake no more thereof." Cattan reports a rival fifteen-figure rule, tests it, rejects it, and gives his reason. This course follows him: **compute the Part of Fortune from the twelve house figures only.**

Now the three charts; the arithmetic is yours to verify line by line.

**Chart A.** The twelve house figures and their points: Conjunctio 6, Via 4, Amissio 6, Caput Draconis 5, Conjunctio 6, Puer 5, Via 4, Acquisitio 6, Carcer 6, Puer 5, Puella 5, Amissio 6. The sum: 64. Divide by twelve: five twelves are 60, remainder **4**. The Part of Fortune falls in the **fourth house**, on **Caput Draconis**; mark ☼ beside it. The worked example below reads figure and house together.

**Chart B.** Puella 5, Rubeus 7, Rubeus 7, Amissio 6, Carcer 6, Conjunctio 6, Carcer 6, Laetitia 7, Via 4, Cauda Draconis 5, Via 4, Tristitia 7. The sum: 70. Five twelves are 60, remainder **10**: the **tenth house**, and the tenth house of Chart B holds **Cauda Draconis**. Read both, per the rule. The figure is the Dragon's Tail, the way out, and is ill. The house is the tenth, an angle and a strong place. A good house under an ill figure makes the Part of Fortune mean. For a question about recovering a partnership, the chart's point of fortune sits on the figure of leaving.

**Chart C.** Amissio 6, Amissio 6, Puella 5, Fortuna Minor 6, Via 4, Tristitia 7, Cauda Draconis 5, Albus 7, Populus 8, Caput Draconis 5, Cauda Draconis 5, Fortuna Minor 6. The sum: 70, the same total as Chart B, and you should check it digit by digit precisely because it is the same. Remainder **10**: the **tenth house** again; and Chart C's tenth holds **Caput Draconis**. The figure: the Dragon's Head, the fortunate threshold, the way in: good. The house: the tenth, angle, good. Both good: *the Part of Fortune is good*, and it sits in the house of honours and magistrates, for a lawsuit, the house of the judges themselves.

Charts B and C have the same point total, the same remainder, and the same Part-of-Fortune house: the tenth. The interpretation differs because the figures differ. Chart B's tenth holds Cauda Draconis, so the Part is mixed and unfavorable in tone. Chart C's tenth holds Caput Draconis, so the Part is good. The division gives an address; the figure and house give the meaning.

The modern comparison, last, and brief. Here is Crowley's whole rule on the subject:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/part-of-fortune" />

Crowley's arithmetic is the same as Cattan's: count figures one through twelve, not the Witnesses and Judge. The mark differs: Crowley uses ⊕, while Cattan uses ☼. The meaning also differs. For Crowley the Part of Fortune shows "ready money or cash belonging to the Querent"; for Cattan it reads the fortune of the whole question by figure and house. When reading Golden Dawn material, expect the Part to refer to money. In this course, read it for the question.

## The point of intention

The third technique asks what the chart was made for. Cattan gives it two chapters: one for a figure found without its question, and one for another person's thought. Read both with their disclaimers, because the disclaimers tell you when the technique belongs in a reading:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-18-to-know-for-what-intent-a" />

The problem is specific: "it may happen sometimes that a person may finde a fygure, and knoweth not for what intent and ende it hath beene made, neyther findeth any inscription in the same." A chart without its question cannot be judged in the ordinary way. Cattan gives three rules for recovering the likely question.

The **first rule**: watch where the first house "goeth into any of the other" (where the querent's own figure springs up elsewhere in the chart) "and judge according unto the signification of the house where she goeth." The querent's figure repeated in the fifth points to a fifth-house matter; in the tenth, an ambition. The chart record's springings supply this rule's material.

The **second rule** is the projection, and it is the one Cattan endorses: take "all the uneven points of all the twelve figures" (count the *single* points, the odd lines, across the twelve house figures) and deal them out one to each house in order, first, second, third, around and around the wheel "untill that all the points be bestowed." The house that receives the last point is the house of the intent: the matter the figure was made for. His case study performs it: a found figure, twenty-four uneven points, "thereby having twice run over all the fygures" (twenty-four is two full circuits of twelve) the last point falls on the twelfth, "so that I judged that the fygure had beene made uppon the imprisonment of some person." Cattan states his preference: "that rule which is onely by the 12. houses, is the better, more sure and certaine." When this course says *the point of intention*, it means this rule.

The **third rule** adds the first and seventh figures together and judges the intent by the figure their sum produces, read against the little table that closes the chapter: Acquisitio, "the demaunde was for silver"; Puella, "of mirth, or of a woman with childe, or of mariage, or of jorny"; Carcer, "of prisonment, sickenesse, or a thing hidden." A table of motives, figure by figure.

The chapter ends with a caution: these rules are "onely put for pleasure, and to shewe that Geomancie is a wittie Arte." The second chapter repeats it:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-19-to-knowe-what-an-other-thinketh" />

For another person's thought, Cattan has you cast a figure and read the first figure against a table: Fortuna Major means "of feare, fortune, or gaine by a woman"; Rubeus, "of bloud shead or of a red thing or of some fright." He places the rule "by the way of pleasure and recreation." These are the lightest techniques in this lesson.

Use the point of intention only when the question is genuinely unavailable, or as a check on an unclear question. Legitimate uses are: a found figure with no inscription; a historical chart recorded without its demand; or a querent who cannot state the question plainly. In the last case, the projection helps you ask better questions before reading. Do not use it as a substitute for framing the question. The question creates the chart; a rule for recovering a lost question is not a reason to stop asking.

One comparison remains. The pseudo-Agrippa's treatise carries a projection of its own, the **Index**:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/technique-index" />

The operation is related but not identical. The pseudo-Agrippa counts all points in the projected lines and projects the remainder from the Ascendant; Cattan's rule deals out the odd points themselves. The status is also different. The Index "giveth you a competent judgment of the thing quesited," and if findings are equal or ambiguous, "the index alone shall certifie you." This is not recreation; in that source it is a standing tie-breaker. Heydon carries the same apparatus into the seventeenth century:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/index-multiplication" />

The practical difference is simple: the pseudo-Agrippa and Heydon treat the Index as a major tool used in every reading, while Cattan treats the point of intention as an occasional rule for found or unclear figures. This course follows Cattan. Use the related technique more heavily only if you have chosen that other method for the whole reading.

## The figure of figures, and the declaration of places

Two short chapters of Cattan's Book III remain, both easy to misread as repetition; here is what they add.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-11-of-the-figure-of-figures" />

The **figure of figures** is agreement between figure and place. Cattan gives three forms. First, a significator's figure passes into the house of the matter asked about, as Acquisitio in the first passing to the second in a question of gain. Second, the first-house figure's gender agrees with the querent. Third, the natural figure of the question stands in the question's house, such as Via in the third house for a question about ways. This is not perfection, because it does not require the significators to meet. It is a strengthening finding. In the chart record, note any such agreement involving the significators or the question's house as small favorable support.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-10-the-declaration-of-the-thing-which" />

The **declaration of places** begins with Lesson 5's angles, succedents, and cadents: angles are strongest, succedents mean, cadents weak. It then adds two points. First, manner: angles signify things "manifest and publike," cadents "things privie and secret," and succedents partly each. Second, time: "The places finally signify the time, as the Angles time present, the Succedants to come, the Cadants past." Angular finding speaks of the present, succedent finding of what is coming, and cadent finding of what has passed or comes late. Cattan's courtier case uses this clock directly: good angles say go now, good succedents promise later profit, and ill cadents warn against lingering.

## The speed of the matter

The timing rules are scattered and imprecise. Use them modestly. This lesson uses three.

The first you have owned since Lesson 2, as structure: the entering and exiting figures.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

In short: top-heavy figures enter and are generally good; bottom-heavy figures go out and are generally evil; balanced figures are mean, with the exceptions Cattan names. Applied to a question, entering figures suggest a matter arriving or gathering; exiting figures suggest a matter leaving. Mobility supplies the second measure: stable figures endure and resist change, while mobile figures move quickly and change. Read the two together, especially over the significators and Judge, for a relative estimate of speed and direction.

The third is the strangest, and you re-read it in the assigned reading, the closing material of the chapter of qualities, where Cattan judges the trades, complexions and length of life:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-time-of-life" />

Set aside the trades and take the timing rule: a figure of six points in the first house may signify "sixe houres, sixe dayes, sixe moneths, sixe yeares, or 72." The point-count supplies a number, but the unit depends on the rest of the chart. Good figures give the fuller term, ill figures reduce it, and mean figures halve it. This is a weak timing method, and Cattan warns that it is for "recreation and pastime." Learn it so you can read the sources, but in your own practice use it only to phrase a timing guess already supported by mobility, entrance, and place.

The timing procedure is: estimate speed from the mobility of the significators and Judge; color that estimate by entering and exiting; place it in time by the declaration's clock; and state uncertainty in the timing line. Do not write a date. These techniques do not support that precision.

## The chart record

The chart record completes Part 2's procedure. Assemble one page with every finding in one place:

1. **The question**, exactly as asked, and its house, with the one-sentence reason for the assignment.
2. **Step 0**: the first-house figure, and *proceeds*, or the chart set aside unjudged.
3. **The placements**: the twelve houses, the significators, each classified by place and by the declaration's clock.
4. **The springings**: every repeated figure, with its houses, and any figure of figures.
5. **The triad**: points and parities, the one-line summary, the Judge's tendency, the table's row for the question.
6. **The perfection scan**: every mode tested in order, with the answer it gives.
7. **The review**: aspects and dignities, as Lesson 8 taught.
8. **The way of the point**: the trace to its terminal figure, or *no way*.
9. **The Part of Fortune**: sum, division, house, figure, ☼, and the reading of figure and house.
10. **Timing**: the speed estimate, with its uncertainty stated.
11. **The answer line**, stated plainly, with the findings that qualify it.

If you use the reading worksheet, it covers only the main order. The chart record adds the remaining techniques around it. Chart A's completed record is the worked example below; Charts B and C are summarized in compact form.

**Chart B.** *Will the querent recover the partnership?* Seventh house; Step 0 passes. Significators Puella (1st) and Carcer (7th), angular and stable. Triad: Tristitia, Cauda Draconis, Via, with substance very ill and the thing lost not found. Perfection: denied. Way of the point: a single-pointed way ending on **Carcer in the fifth**, unfavorable. Part of Fortune: remainder 10, **tenth house on Cauda Draconis**, ill figure in good house, mean. Timing: slow, ending in change. Answer line: denied; the record strongly denies recovery.

**Chart C.** *Will the querent win the lawsuit?* Seventh house; Step 0 passes. Significators Amissio (1st) and Cauda Draconis (7th), both angular, both repeating. Triad: Caput Draconis, Albus, Acquisitio, mostly favorable. Perfection: denied. Way of the point: **no way**. Part of Fortune: remainder 10, **tenth house on Caput Draconis**, good figure in good house, good. Timing: movement with a settled end. Answer line: denied for the querent, with favorable findings around the suit's conclusion and the judges.

The chart records are complete.

## Worked example

The worked example gives Chart A the full set of secondary techniques, then shows how the worksheet can hold the main notes if you want it.

The standing question: **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** The chart, dealt into its houses:

<HouseChart mothers={['conjunctio', 'via', 'amissio', 'caput-draconis']} caption="Chart A in the twelve houses: ☼ the Part of Fortune falls in the 4th, on Caput Draconis." />

**The way of the point, traced.** The Judge is Acquisitio; its first line carries two points, a double top, so before touching the chart you know the way will fork or fail, never thread. The Witnesses: Rubeus's top line, two points, like the Judge; Tristitia's top line, two points, like the Judge. Both match, as a double top requires (both or neither). The right branch: houses nine and ten, Carcer and Puer (top lines one point and one point) neither like the Judge. The left branch: houses eleven and twelve, Puella and Amissio (one point and one point) neither like the Judge. The way stops before reaching the upper houses: it does not complete. The record line: *way of point: double top; both Witnesses match; dies at the Nieces; no way.* The technique has nothing to say about this marriage, and the chart record records the nothing.

**The Part of Fortune, computed digit by digit.** The twelve house figures: Conjunctio 6, Via 4, Amissio 6, Caput Draconis 5, running sum 21; Conjunctio 6, Puer 5, Via 4, Acquisitio 6, sum 42; Carcer 6, Puer 5, Puella 5, Amissio 6, sum **64**. Divide by twelve: five twelves are sixty, remainder **four**. The Part of Fortune falls in the **fourth house**, and the fourth house of Chart A holds **Caput Draconis**: set ☼ beside it. Now read the two together, "for both the one and the other must be looked uppon." The figure is good; the house is strong and belongs to the end of the matter. Both good: *the Part of Fortune is good.* In this marriage chart, the point of fortune falls in the house of conclusions, on an entering figure.

**The point of intention, demonstrated.** Suppose this chart arrived without its question. Apply Cattan's preferred rule, "onely by the 12. houses": the single-point lines of the twelve house figures total thirty-two. Two full circuits of the wheel make twenty-four; the remaining eight carry the count to the **eighth house**, which includes the other party's substance, the marriage treaty, and the dowry, so the projection points near the actual question. The other two rules are weaker here: the first sends Conjunctio to the fifth, the third produces **Carcer**. Record all three; the technique is approximate, and selective reporting would make it misleading.

**The timing, estimated.** Conjunctio and Via are both mobile, so the parties and the matter are in motion. Eight of the twelve house figures are mobile. The Judge, Acquisitio, is stable, so the end holds once reached. The question's house holds Via, balanced and mean; the Part of Fortune's Caput Draconis enters; and the fourth house is angular, present-tense by the declaration's clock. The timing line is: *quick rather than slow, and lasting rather than brief; mobile significators suggest movement toward conclusion, while the stable Judge suggests that the conclusion holds. This is comparative speed, not a calendar.* Do not add a date or number.

**The chart record, assembled.** Question, seventh house; Step 0: Conjunctio, proceeds; significators Conjunctio (1st) and Via (7th), both angular and mobile; springings as recorded; triad Rubeus, Tristitia, Acquisitio, with the row *a wife: ill*; perfection by conjunction; Lesson 8 review; way of the point: none; Part of Fortune: fourth house, Caput Draconis, good; timing: swift, lasting, uncertain. The record is ready to be written as an answer.

**The worksheet, as notes.** If the form helps you, use the worksheet below with Chart A's chart record open. You have a supported entry for every field.

Step one: the question, a seventh-house matter. Step two: the triad and its unfavorable row. Step three: the significators and their springings. Step four: perfection by conjunction. Step five: both significators angular and mobile, plus this lesson's refinements: good Part of Fortune in the fourth, no way of the point, a point of intention near the marriage treaty. Step six: *The marriage takes effect by conjunction; the triad qualifies it harshly and promises gain; the short answer is "the marriage takes effect, at cost."*

    The lesson is simple. Two charts can have the same total, the same remainder, and the same house. The arithmetic gives only the address. The interpretation depends on the figure standing there and the house it occupies. In Chart B the tenth holds the Dragon's Tail, so the finding is mean and unfavorable. In Chart C the tenth holds the Dragon's Head, so the finding is good. If you write only "Part of Fortune in the tenth," you have done the division but not the judgment.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={6} title="When to use the later techniques" kind="quiz">
  Put each later technique in its proper place in a reading.

  1. The scan denies recovery of lost goods, but the Part of Fortune is good. What happens to the answer?
  2. The way of the point completes on the sixth house in a theft question. What kind of information can that add?
  3. The way of the point returns no path. What should you write?
  4. A reader gives a calendar date from speed, mobility, and place alone. What is wrong?
  5. The point of intention disagrees with a clear stated question. What should the reader do?
  6. State the practical rule for all later techniques.

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. The denial stands. The Part of Fortune can qualify money, cost, help, or circumstance, but it cannot reverse the scan.
    2. It can point to a department involved in the matter, such as servants, illness, small animals, or labor. It adds detail.
    3. Write "no way" and do not force one. Silence is a finding.
    4. The method supports relative pace, not a fixed date from those factors alone.
    5. Keep the stated question as the reading's frame and note the disagreement as a caution.
    6. Foundations decide; refinements refine. Later techniques add detail, limits, pace, or support, but they do not replace houses, significators, the triad, perfection, aspects, and dignity.
  </ModelAnswer>
