The modes of perfection tell you whether the significators meet and whether the thing asked for reaches the asker. Aspect and dignity teach you how much weight to give the findings. Those are the foundations of the reading, and this lesson does not change them.

This lesson adds the remaining secondary techniques: the way of the point, the Part of Fortune, the point of intention, the figure of figures, the declaration of places, and timing. Cattan calls them "much necessarie and profitable," but he places them after the houses, the Witnesses and Judge, the companies, and the aspects. In his cases they add detail or break a tie. They do not overturn the foundations. Treat them as refinements.

They matter for three reasons. First, Cattan's cases use them, and you cannot follow those cases without them. Second, the way of the point and the Part of Fortune are checkable arithmetic procedures, so mistakes can be found. Third, this lesson completes Part 2. By the end, each chart in your journal will have a complete *pre-reading chart record*: everything examined, everything recorded, nothing yet finally weighed. Lesson 10 teaches the weighing.

The lesson covers six techniques, runs them against the three standing charts, and records even the cases where a technique returns nothing. It then gathers the results into the chart record and runs the reading worksheet as a dry rehearsal.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lessons 5 through 8: the houses, significators and Step 0; the Witness–Judge triad and its summary (several techniques here begin at the Judge and work backward); the modes of perfection and the scanning discipline (the chart record has the scan at its centre); and the weighing of aspects and dignities (the Part of Fortune will demand "weigh figure and house together").

The assigned reading is the timing portion of Cattan's chapter of qualities, especially the entering and exiting figures and the material on reading length of life from a figure's points. Also read the three technique pages: [the way of the point](/techniques/way-of-the-point/), [the point of intention](/techniques/point-of-intention/), and [projection of points](/techniques/projection-of-points/). The last is the pseudo-Agrippa's cousin to Cattan's intention rule.

The reading worksheet organizes your notes. It walks the reading order one step at a time and assembles a draft paragraph. This lesson runs it end to end as a dry run. Fill every step, but mark the Step 6 answer *provisional*, because Lesson 10 supplies the method for a finished reading.

## Later techniques, not foundations

The reading order is set by the sources' own behaviour.

Cattan opens his chapter on the way of the point like this: "The Geomanciens have many Rules to judge their figures aright, and amongest others, that which they doe call the way of point, as a thing much necessarie and profitable in this Arte" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 20). It is one rule among others. When he opens the Part of Fortune chapter, he gives the order plainly: "The question being made, after that we have judged by the houses, figures, angles, companies, aspectes, the way of point, and by all other sortes and manners before saide, nowe resteth it to judge by the parte of fortune" (Book III, ch. 21). The order is: houses and figures; angles and strength of place; companies and aspects; then the way of the point and the Part of Fortune.

His case studies use the same order. In the Lord of Tays horse-buying case, the angles are weighed first and found "indifferent well"; only then do the aspects, the way of the point, and the Part of Fortune enter. Because the foundations are mixed, the secondary techniques help decide the case. In the robbery case, the houses already identify the servant, and the way of the point adds the detail of how he will be discovered. That is the proper use of the techniques in this lesson. Clear foundations decide. Mixed foundations may be clarified by later checks. A Part of Fortune that reverses a clear perfection is an error.

Start with the two arithmetic techniques.

## The way of the point

Here is the procedure in Cattan's own words, with his case study at its end. Read it slowly; the prose is dense, and the procedure inside it is exact:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-20-of-the-way-of-point-chap" />

Now extract the procedure, step by step. (One slip of the 1591 print to clear first: "then take the points of the left Judge" should read "left witnes", since the sentence makes no sense otherwise.)

1. **Take the first line of the Judge**, the top line, "be it even or odde": one point or two.
2. **Compare the Witnesses' top lines.** If the Right Witness's top line is like the Judge's, the way runs to the right; if the Left Witness's, to the left. If *neither* Witness's top line matches the Judge's, then, in Cattan's words, there is "no way of point" in the figure: the path stops at the first step, and you record exactly that.
3. **Follow the match upward into the Nieces.** A matching Right Witness sends you to the ninth and tenth houses; look for the same top line there. A matching Left Witness sends you to the eleventh and twelfth.
4. **Follow it again into the top row.** The ninth house, if it matches, sends you to the first and second houses; the tenth to the third and fourth; the eleventh to the fifth and sixth; the twelfth to the seventh and eighth.
5. **Judge by the figure where the way ends.** "If it be good ye shal judge good, and if it be ill you shal judge ill"; the terminal figure, and the house it stands in, are the way's answer.

The route follows the derivation of the shield backward. The Judge comes from the two Witnesses. The Right Witness comes from the Nieces in houses nine and ten; those Nieces come from houses one through four. The Left Witness comes from houses eleven and twelve; those come from houses five through eight. The way of the point takes the Judge's top line and asks where that line came from. The terminal house shows the hidden root of the matter.

The parity arithmetic from Lesson 4 explains Cattan's preference for a single point. He writes: "ye must understand that the way of point intendeth properly unto one point, for when they be two it is not so perfite as when they be found in one." If the Judge's top line is single, the Witnesses' top lines must differ. Exactly one Witness matches, and the same rule repeats at every generation. A single-pointed way therefore cannot fail or fork; it always reaches one terminal house. If the Judge's top line is double, the Witnesses' top lines are alike. Both match or neither does. The way either stops or forks, and each later branch can stop or fork again.

In Cattan's robbery case, the main reading comes from the houses: an ill first house, Cauda Draconis in the sixth house of servants, an ill seventh, and the second-house figure moving through the chart. The aspects add evidence. The way of the point arrives last and adds one specific detail: the thief will be discovered "by means of the goodes which he hath given unto his kinsfolkes." That is the technique used in its proper rank.

Now the three standing charts.

**Chart A.** The Judge is Acquisitio, whose first line is two points: a double-topped Judge, so expect the way to fork or fail. Both Witnesses match: Rubeus carries two points on its top line and so does Tristitia, both like the Judge, just as the arithmetic predicts for a double top (both or neither). So the way looks right, to houses nine and ten: Carcer and Puer, top lines single and single, no match. And left, to houses eleven and twelve: Puella and Amissio, single and single, no match. The way stops before reaching the upper houses: it does not complete. Record precisely that: *way of point: begins double, both Witnesses match, dies at the Nieces; no way.* The technique returns nothing for Chart A, and nothing is the finding.

**Chart B.** The Judge is Via, first line a single point, so one Witness will match and the way will run to one end. The Right Witness Tristitia has a double top, no match. The Left Witness Cauda Draconis has a single top, so the way runs left, to houses eleven and twelve. The eleventh holds Via, top line single: it matches. The twelfth holds Tristitia, double: it does not. From the eleventh, the way runs to houses five and six. The fifth holds Carcer, top line single: it matches. The sixth holds Conjunctio, double: it does not. **The way of the point ends in house five, on Carcer.** Carcer is an ill figure of binding and delay, so the technique reads the hidden root of the partnership question as a bind. That agrees with the rest of Chart B's chart record; it does not reverse anything. It adds one more unfavorable weight. The final weighing belongs to Lesson 10.

**Chart C.** The Judge is Acquisitio again, double-topped, and both Witnesses match. The way runs to houses nine and ten, where Populus and Caput Draconis also match. From the ninth it runs to houses one and two, both single tops and no match. From the tenth it runs to houses three and four, also single tops and no match. The left branch from Albus to houses eleven and twelve also dies. The way stops before reaching the upper houses: **no way**. Record the negative finding with the trace.

Only one of the three standing charts has a completed way. That is normal. The way of the point is conditional: sometimes it applies, often it does not. A recorded "no way" is still a completed use of the technique.

## The Part of Fortune

The second arithmetic technique comes from the next chapter. Read the rule, the case study, and the rejection at the end.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-21-howe-to-knowe-the-parte-of" />

The rule is simple. **Sum the points of the twelve house figures, and only those twelve. Divide by twelve. The remainder names the house, and the Part of Fortune falls there; mark it with ☼.** A remainder of two gives the second house, four the fourth, ten the tenth. If the division is exact, the Part falls in the twelfth, as Cattan's examples show.

Then the interpretation, which is where Lesson 8's habits apply. The Part of Fortune is not an answer; it is an *address*, the place where the chart's fortune concentrates, and Cattan's rule for reading it weighs two things together: "if the fygure and house be good (for both the one and the other must be looked uppon) you shall judge good, and if it bee evill ye shall also judge evill… But if the fygure be good, and the house ill, or contrary, the house good and the fygure ill, you shall judge the saide parte of fortune to be meane." Figure *and* house, always both: a good figure on a good house is good fortune for the question; ill on ill is ill; and every mixed case is mean: the Elizabethan *mean* you know from the tables, middling, neither. The whole rule is three sentences long, and its discipline is the phrase in parentheses: *both the one and the other must be looked uppon.*

The case study shows the technique in its place. The Lord of Tays wants a horse. The first figure, its companion, the fourth, and the seventh are good, so the foundations lean favorable: "I judged indifferent well." But the aspects are ill, the opposition is mean, the way of the point goes into an ill figure, and the Part of Fortune falls on an ill figure and house. Because the foundations are mixed, these later findings decide the case. The twelfth house then supplies the concrete detail: it holds Amissio, so Cattan judges that the horse has an ill foot and is worth nothing.

The last sentences of the chapter matter. "Many doe use an other manner to finde part of fortune, in taking all the points as well of the twelve houses as of the two witnesses, and the Judge, which they do part by twelve (as is aforesaide) but because I have founde no trueth therein I will speake no more thereof." Cattan reports a rival fifteen-figure rule, tests it, rejects it, and gives his reason. This course follows him: **compute the Part of Fortune from the twelve house figures only.** Exercise 2 has you compute the rejected variant once so that you can recognize it later.

Now the three charts; the arithmetic is yours to verify line by line.

**Chart A.** The twelve house figures and their points: Conjunctio 6, Via 4, Amissio 6, Caput Draconis 5, Conjunctio 6, Puer 5, Via 4, Acquisitio 6, Carcer 6, Puer 5, Puella 5, Amissio 6. The sum: 64. Divide by twelve: five twelves are 60, remainder **4**. The Part of Fortune falls in the **fourth house**, on **Caput Draconis**; mark ☼ beside it in your journal copy. The weighing belongs to the worked example below, where it is done in full.

**Chart B.** Puella 5, Rubeus 7, Rubeus 7, Amissio 6, Carcer 6, Conjunctio 6, Carcer 6, Laetitia 7, Via 4, Cauda Draconis 5, Via 4, Tristitia 7. The sum: 70. Five twelves are 60, remainder **10**: the **tenth house**, and the tenth house of Chart B holds **Cauda Draconis**. Weigh both, per the rule. The figure is the Dragon's Tail, the way out, and is ill. The house is the tenth, an angle and a strong place. A good house under an ill figure makes the Part of Fortune mean. For a question about recovering a partnership, the chart's point of fortune sits on the figure of leaving.

**Chart C.** Amissio 6, Amissio 6, Puella 5, Fortuna Minor 6, Via 4, Tristitia 7, Cauda Draconis 5, Albus 7, Populus 8, Caput Draconis 5, Cauda Draconis 5, Fortuna Minor 6. The sum: 70, the same total as Chart B, and you should check it digit by digit precisely because it is the same. Remainder **10**: the **tenth house** again; and Chart C's tenth holds **Caput Draconis**. The figure: the Dragon's Head, the fortunate threshold, the way in: good. The house: the tenth, angle, good. Both good: *the Part of Fortune is good*, and it sits in the house of honours and magistrates, for a lawsuit, the house of the judges themselves.

Charts B and C have the same point total, the same remainder, and the same Part-of-Fortune house: the tenth. The interpretation differs because the figures differ. Chart B's tenth holds Cauda Draconis, so the Part is mixed and unfavorable in tone. Chart C's tenth holds Caput Draconis, so the Part is good. The division gives an address; the figure and house give the meaning.

The modern comparison, last, and brief. Here is Crowley's whole rule on the subject:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/part-of-fortune" />

Crowley's arithmetic is the same as Cattan's: count figures one through twelve, not the Witnesses and Judge. The mark differs: Crowley uses ⊕, while Cattan uses ☼. The meaning also differs. For Crowley the Part of Fortune shows "ready money or cash belonging to the Querent"; for Cattan it weighs the fortune of the whole question by figure and house. When reading Golden Dawn material, expect the Part to refer to money. In this course, read it for the question.

## The point of intention

The third technique asks what the chart was made for. Cattan gives it two chapters: one for a figure found without its question, and one for another person's thought. Read both with their disclaimers, because the disclaimers set the technique's rank:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-18-to-know-for-what-intent-a" />

The problem is specific: "it may happen sometimes that a person may finde a fygure, and knoweth not for what intent and ende it hath beene made, neyther findeth any inscription in the same." A chart without its question cannot be judged in the ordinary way. Cattan gives three rules for recovering the likely question.

The **first rule**: watch where the first house "goeth into any of the other" (where the querent's own figure springs up elsewhere in the chart) "and judge according unto the signification of the house where she goeth." The querent's figure, repeated in the fifth, betrays a fifth-house errand; in the tenth, an ambition. The springings already in the journal supply this rule's material.

The **second rule** is the projection, and it is the one Cattan endorses: take "all the uneven points of all the twelve figures" (count the *single* points, the odd lines, across the twelve house figures) and deal them out one to each house in order, first, second, third, around and around the wheel "untill that all the points be bestowed." The house that receives the last point is the house of the intent: the matter the figure was made for. His case study performs it: a found figure, twenty-four uneven points, "thereby having twice run over all the fygures" (twenty-four is two full circuits of twelve) the last point falls on the twelfth, "so that I judged that the fygure had beene made uppon the imprisonment of some person." And his ranking is explicit: "that rule which is onely by the 12. houses, is the better, more sure and certaine." When this course says *the point of intention*, it means this rule.

The **third rule** adds the first and seventh figures together and judges the intent by the figure their sum produces, read against the little table that closes the chapter: Acquisitio, "the demaunde was for silver"; Puella, "of mirth, or of a woman with childe, or of mariage, or of jorny"; Carcer, "of prisonment, sickenesse, or a thing hidden." A figure-alphabet of motives.

The chapter ends with a warning about rank: "You must understand that these Rules be not here placed as necessarie unto Geomancie, but onely put for pleasure, and to shewe that Geomancie is a wittie Arte." Cattan treats the rules as optional, not necessary. The second chapter repeats the caution:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-19-to-knowe-what-an-other-thinketh" />

For another person's thought, Cattan has you cast a figure and read the first figure against a table. Fortuna Major means "of feare, fortune, or gaine by a woman"; Laetitia, "of some good and merrie thing"; Rubeus, "of bloud shead or of a red thing or of some fright." He says the rule is placed "by the way of pleasure and recreation," not so that anyone should trust it more than the rest of the method. Whatever we make of that disclaimer, the relative rank is clear: these are the lightest techniques in this lesson.

Use the point of intention only when the question is genuinely unavailable, or as a check on an unclear question. Legitimate uses are: a found figure with no inscription; a historical chart recorded without its demand; or a querent who cannot state the question plainly. In the last case, the projection helps you ask better questions before reading. Do not use it as a substitute for framing the question. The question creates the chart; a rule for recovering a lost question is not a license to stop asking.

One comparison remains, and it changes the technique's weight entirely as it crosses sources. The pseudo-Agrippa's treatise carries a projection of its own, the **Index**:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/technique-index" />

The operation is related but not identical. The pseudo-Agrippa counts all points in the projected lines and projects the remainder from the Ascendant; Cattan's rule deals out the odd points themselves. The rank is also different. The Index "giveth you a competent judgment of the thing quesited," and if findings are equal or ambiguous, "the index alone shall certifie you." This is not recreation; in that source it is a standing tie-breaker. Heydon carries the same apparatus into the seventeenth century:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/index-multiplication" />

The note and the wording show that Heydon is reprinting the *Of Geomancy* attributed to Agrippa nearly verbatim. Count that as one source line transmitted twice, not as two independent sources. The result is a real divergence: the pseudo-Agrippa and Heydon treat the Index as a major tool used in every reading, while Cattan treats the point of intention as an occasional rule for found or unclear figures. This course follows Cattan, but you should know that another branch of the sources gave the related technique much more weight.

## The figure of figures, and the declaration of places

Two short chapters of Cattan's Book III remain, both easy to misread as repetition; here is what they add.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-11-of-the-figure-of-figures" />

The **figure of figures** is agreement between figure and place. Cattan gives three forms. First, a significator's figure passes into the house of the matter asked about, as Acquisitio in the first passing to the second in a question of gain. Second, the first-house figure's gender agrees with the querent. Third, the natural figure of the question stands in the question's house, such as Via in the third house for a question about ways. This is not perfection, because it does not require the significators to meet. It is a strengthening finding. In the chart record, note any such agreement involving the significators or the question's house, and count it as a small favorable weight.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-10-the-declaration-of-the-thing-which" />

The **declaration of places** begins with Lesson 5's angles, succedents, and cadents: angles are strongest, succedents mean, cadents weak. It then adds two points. First, manner: angles signify things "manifest and publike," cadents "things privie and secret," and succedents partly each. Second, time: "The places finally signify the time, as the Angles time present, the Succedants to come, the Cadants past." Angular finding speaks of the present, succedent finding of what is coming, and cadent finding of what has passed or comes late. Cattan's courtier case uses this clock directly: good angles say go now, good succedents promise later profit, and ill cadents warn against lingering.

## The speed of the matter

The timing rules are scattered and imprecise. Use them modestly. This lesson uses three.

The first you have owned since Lesson 2, as structure: the entering and exiting figures.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

In short: top-heavy figures enter and are generally good; bottom-heavy figures go out and are generally evil; balanced figures are mean, with the exceptions Cattan names. Applied to a question, entering figures suggest a matter arriving or gathering; exiting figures suggest a matter leaving. Mobility supplies the second measure: stable figures endure and resist change, while mobile figures move quickly and change. Read the two together, especially over the significators and Judge, for a relative estimate of speed and direction.

The third is the strangest, and you re-read it in the assigned reading, the closing material of the chapter of qualities, where Cattan judges the trades, complexions and length of life:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-time-of-life" />

Set aside the trades and take the timing rule: a figure of six points in the first house may signify "sixe houres, sixe dayes, sixe moneths, sixe yeares, or 72." The point-count supplies a number, but the unit depends on the rest of the chart. Good figures give the fuller term, ill figures reduce it, and mean figures halve it. This is a weak timing method, and Cattan warns that it is for "recreation and pastime." Learn it so you can read the sources, but in your own practice use it only to phrase a timing guess already supported by mobility, entrance, and place.

The timing procedure is: estimate speed from the mobility of the significators and Judge; color that estimate by entering and exiting; place it in time by the declaration's clock; and state uncertainty in the timing line. Do not write a date. These techniques do not support that precision.

## The pre-reading chart record

The chart record is the last procedure upgrade of Part 2. Since Lesson 4 your journal has added one layer at a time: cast, placements, Witness–Judge triad, perfection scan, aspects, and dignities. This lesson completes the set. From now on, every chart gets a **pre-reading chart record**, one page with all the evidence in one place:

1. **The question**, exactly as asked, and its house, with the one-sentence source note.
2. **Step 0**: the first-house figure, and *proceeds*, or the entry marked unjudged, and the page ends there.
3. **The placements**: the twelve houses written out, the significators named, each classified by place, and now also by the declaration's clock: angular-present, succedent-coming, cadent-past.
4. **The springings**: every repeated figure, with its houses, and any figure of figures noted by name where figure and place agree.
5. **The Witness–Judge triad**: the triad with points and parities, the summary in one line, the Judge's tendency, the table's row for the question, in the column's own words.
6. **The perfection scan**: every mode tested in order, everything found recorded, the Lesson 7 provisional answer carried forward.
7. **The weights**: the aspects the significators behold and suffer, the dignities and debilities, as Lesson 8 taught.
8. **The way of the point**: the trace, step by step, to its terminal figure, or *no way*.
9. **The Part of Fortune**: the sum, the division, the house, the figure, the ☼, and the weighing, figure and house both, good, ill or mean.
10. **Timing**: the speed estimate from mobility and entrance, with its uncertainty in the timing line.
11. **The answer line**, written *provisional* in every case: one sentence of where the weights lean, marked pending, because Lesson 10 supplies the final method.

The reading worksheet covers the main spine of this form: question, triad, significators, perfection, strength, and answer. The chart record adds the remaining techniques around that spine. Run the worksheet as rehearsal; keep the chart record as the record.

Chart A's completed chart record appears in the worked example below. Charts B and C are summarized here in the compact form your journal can use once the procedure is familiar.

**Chart B.** *Will the querent recover the partnership?* Seventh house; Step 0: Puella, proceeds. Significators Puella in the first and Carcer in the seventh are angular and stable. Springings: Rubeus in 2 and 3, Carcer in 5 and 7, Via in 9 and 11; no figure of figures. Triad: Tristitia, Cauda Draconis, Via: sorrowful beginning, departing course, end in change; the table gives substance very ill and the thing lost not found. Perfection: denied by occupation and conjunction. Way of the point: a single-pointed way ending on **Carcer in the fifth**, unfavorable. Part of Fortune: 70 points, remainder 10, **tenth house on Cauda Draconis**: ill figure in good house, mean. Timing: stable significators and mobile Judge suggest a slow matter ending in change. Provisional answer: the weights lean strongly against recovery.

**Chart C.** *Will the querent win the lawsuit?* Seventh house; Step 0: Amissio, proceeds. Significators Amissio in the first and Cauda Draconis in the seventh are angular; Amissio repeats in the second, Cauda Draconis in the eleventh, and Fortuna Minor in the fourth and twelfth. Triad: Caput Draconis, Albus, Acquisitio: good beginning, quiet course, end in gain; the table is mostly favorable. Perfection: denied by occupation and conjunction. Way of the point: double-topped Judge, forked trail, **no way**. Part of Fortune: 70 points, remainder 10, **tenth house on Caput Draconis**: good figure in good house, good. Timing: mobile significators and stable Judge suggest movement with a settled end. Provisional answer: the conflict remains. The triad and Part are favorable; the significator houses and perfection scan are not. Lesson 10 weighs the conflict.

The chart records are complete. The answers remain provisional.

## Worked example

The worked example gives Chart A the full set of secondary techniques, then runs the worksheet as a dry rehearsal of the finished reading.

The standing question: **"Will the marriage go ahead?"** The chart, dealt into its houses:

<HouseChart mothers={['conjunctio', 'via', 'amissio', 'caput-draconis']} caption="Chart A in the twelve houses: ☼ the Part of Fortune falls in the 4th, on Caput Draconis." />

**The way of the point, traced.** The Judge is Acquisitio; its first line carries two points, a double top, so before touching the chart you know the way will fork or fail, never thread. The Witnesses: Rubeus's top line, two points, like the Judge; Tristitia's top line, two points, like the Judge. Both match, as a double top requires (both or neither). The right branch: houses nine and ten, Carcer and Puer (top lines one point and one point) neither like the Judge. The left branch: houses eleven and twelve, Puella and Amissio (one point and one point) neither like the Judge. The way stops before reaching the upper houses: it does not complete. The journal line: *way of point: double top; both Witnesses match; dies at the Nieces; no way.* The technique has nothing to say about this marriage, and the chart record records the nothing.

**The Part of Fortune, computed digit by digit.** The twelve house figures: Conjunctio 6, Via 4, Amissio 6, Caput Draconis 5, running sum 21; Conjunctio 6, Puer 5, Via 4, Acquisitio 6, sum 42; Carcer 6, Puer 5, Puella 5, Amissio 6, sum **64**. Divide by twelve: five twelves are sixty, remainder **four**. The Part of Fortune falls in the **fourth house**, and the fourth house of Chart A holds **Caput Draconis**: set ☼ beside it. Now weigh, "for both the one and the other must be looked uppon." The figure is good; the house is strong and belongs to the end of the matter. Both good: *the Part of Fortune is good.* In this marriage chart, the point of fortune falls in the house of conclusions, on an entering figure. (For completeness, the rejected variant: adding the Witnesses and Judge (Rubeus 7, Tristitia 7, Acquisitio 6) gives 84, which divides by twelve exactly, remainder nothing, sending the variant Part to the twelfth house. You will compute this once, properly, in Exercise 2, and then never again.)

**The point of intention, demonstrated.** Suppose this chart arrived without its question, or with only a vague statement: *"It concerns… an arrangement with another person."* Apply Cattan's preferred rule, "onely by the 12. houses."   Compute the Part of Fortune for Chart B and Chart C, showing every step: the twelve figures named with their points, the running sum, the division, the remainder, the house, the figure, and then the weighing, figure and house both, per Cattan's rule in Book III, ch. 21.   <ModelAnswer>
    **Chart B.** The twelve: Puella 5, Rubeus 7, Rubeus 7, Amissio 6, sum 25; Carcer 6, Conjunctio 6, Carcer 6, Laetitia 7, sum 50; Via 4, Cauda Draconis 5, Via 4, Tristitia 7, sum **70**. Figure ill, house good: by the rule, *mean*; and the texture of the mean is worth a part: the chart's fortune concentrates in the house of office and standing, on the figure of departure.     **Chart C.** The twelve: Amissio 6, Amissio 6, Puella 5, Fortuna Minor 6, sum 23; Via 4, Tristitia 7, Cauda Draconis 5, Albus 7, sum 46; Populus 8, Caput Draconis 5, Cauda Draconis 5, Fortuna Minor 6, sum **70**. Both good: *the Part of Fortune is good*; and it falls, for a lawsuit, in the house of magistrates and judges, on the figure of fortunate entrance.

    The point is simple: two different charts can have the same total, the same remainder, and the same house. The arithmetic only gives the address. The interpretation depends on what stands there. In Chart B the tenth holds the Dragon's Tail; in Chart C it holds the Dragon's Head. Cattan's rule therefore gives opposite findings: mean and unfavorable for B, good for C. A geomancer who reports "Part of Fortune in the tenth" and stops has performed the division and skipped the weighing; the division only tells you where to weigh figure with house.

</Exercise>

<Exercise n={2} title="The rejected variant, computed once" kind="drill">
  Compute the fifteen-figure Part of Fortune for Chart A (the variant that counts the Witnesses and the Judge along with the twelve houses) showing the arithmetic in full. State where it falls, and which figure it lands on. Then explain, citing the closing sentences of Cattan's ch. 21, why this course (and Cattan) discards the variant, and say what the *manner* of his rejection teaches, beyond the rejection itself.

  <ModelAnswer>
    The twelve house figures of Chart A sum to 64, as the worked example computed. Add the court: Rubeus 7, Tristitia 7, Acquisitio 6 (fourteen for the Witnesses, six for the Judge) total **84**. Divide by twelve: seven twelves are exactly 84, remainder **nothing**; and a remainder of nothing sends the Part to the **twelfth house**, by the same convention Cattan's own examples state for the twelve-figure rule ("if the fygure be of 72. points, or 84. or 96… then the parte of fortune shall goe into the twelfth"). Chart A's twelfth house holds **Amissio**; so the variant Part of Fortune falls on Loss, in the house of prisons and privy enemies, where the twelve-figure rule put it on Caput Draconis in the fourth, the good figure on the good angle. The two rules do not refine one another; they contradict one another in the first chart you ever cast.

    Why discard the variant? Because Cattan tested it and says so: "Many doe use an other manner to finde part of fortune, in taking all the points as well of the twelve houses as of the two witnesses, and the Judge, which they do part by twelve (as is aforesaide) but because I have founde no trueth therein I will speake no more thereof" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 21). Note what the sentence is and is not. It is not a claim that the variant is irrational; both rules are coherent arithmetic. It is a practitioner's report: the variant was current ("many doe use"), he tried it against outcomes, and it did not perform. There is also a structural argument on his side that he does not state: the Witnesses and the Judge are "no houses… but bee onely accidentalles," wholly derived from the twelve. Adding their points double-counts the chart's summary and then names a house with a number partly made of figures that have no house. The useful lesson is Cattan's procedure: report the divergence, give the ground, choose the working rule, and leave the rival on the record. You have computed the variant once so that you can recognise it when a later book uses it without comment.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>
