Everything before this lesson was preparation. This lesson teaches the full method of judgment end to end, resolves Chart C's six-lesson conflict as the master case, and judges a fourth chart, Chart D, from a standing start. Its business is the questions Lesson 9 deferred: what outranks what, how many testimonies make a sentence, when a Judge overrules a house and when a house a Judge.

Almost nothing in this lesson is new. The method you will learn end to end is the method you already perform in pieces: Step 0 from Lesson 5, the bench from Lesson 6, the scan from Lesson 7, the audit from Lesson 8, the subtle instruments and the pre-judgment dossier from Lesson 9. What is new is the one act every lesson since the fourth has deferred: the *weighing*, the discipline that takes a dossier full of testimonies, some fair, some dark, some loud, some whispering, and produces from them a single finished sentence that a querent can carry out of the room. That discipline is not a feeling and not a knack. It is a short list of rules about rank, about which testimonies answer which questions, and what happens when they seem to disagree, and the whole of it can be written on one page, because the sources, read carefully, have been applying it on every page this course has quoted. The lesson's labor is to extract the rules, source each one honestly, and then use them four times: on Chart B, whose denial is now uniform across every instrument; on Chart A, whose grant has been priced since Lesson 6; on Chart C, the divided chart, the master case, held open since Lesson 4 and resolved here rule by rule; and on Chart D, which you have never seen, judged from a standing start in the worked example, the first chart of your training to be met with the complete method already in your hands.

Every verdict line in your journal reads, by Lesson 9's protocol, *provisional, pending Lesson 10's method*. By the end of this lesson the method has arrived, the master case is closed, and *provisional* retires from your journal, replaced, for every chart you have cast and every chart you will cast, by judgments that are finished, defended, and final.

## The method, end to end

Here is the whole method of judgment in its canonical order. Every step names the lesson that taught it, and the assembly follows the order Cattan gives in his own voice: "The question being made, after that we have judged by the houses, figures, angles, companies, aspectes, the way of point, and by all other sortes and manners before saide, nowe resteth it to judge by the parte of fortune" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 21), the order of march this course adopts as the spine of every judgment.

**Step 0, inspect the first house** (Lesson 5). Before anything else: Rubeus or Cauda Draconis rising, and the chart is recorded, marked unjudged, and the question rests. Anything else, and you proceed.

**Step 1, the question and its house** (Lesson 5). The question exactly as asked, the house it lives in, the one-sentence defence of the assignment. Everything downstream keys to this step, because the question always has the last word: the same chart answers a marriage question and a money question differently, and a misplaced question misplaces the entire judgment after it.

**Step 2, the bench** (Lesson 6). The Witnesses and the Judge, with points and parities verified; the sentence in one line (beginning, course, end); the table's row for the question's matter, in the column's own words; and the standing doctrines: Populus on the bench recuses the sentence to the angles, and the sentence concerns the *matter*, never yet the question's grant.

**Step 3, the significators and their springings** (Lessons 5 and 7). Querent's figure and house, quesited's figure and house, every repetition of both located, and the chart's other repetitions noted: the dossier of doubled figures that the scan is about to consume.

**Step 4, the scan** (Lesson 7). The six-step perfection scan, in order, every mode tested and every result recorded: occupation, conjunction, company, the aspect line, the conclusion. This is the hinge: the grant or the refusal. Perfection found, and the houses grant the thing asked, in the mode's manner and the figure's quality; perfection denied, and the houses refuse it; and denial is an answer, as complete an answer as a grant.

**Step 5, the audit and the instruments** (Lessons 8 and 9). The weights, in Cattan's order of march: strength of place and dignity for the significators and the testimonies that touch them; the aspects they behold and suffer, judged by the figures found in the beholding houses; then the subtle instruments: the way of the point traced or honestly absent, the Part of Fortune computed and weighed figure-with-house, the timing estimate with its uncertainty written into the sentence. Refinements, not foundations, every one, entered as weights and details, never as verdicts.

**Step 6, the weighing and the verdict** (this lesson). The act the course has deferred since the verdict column was first ruled: rank the testimonies by the nine rules below, resolve what each is entitled to decide, and write the finished judgment, a sentence with six clauses, named and taught at this lesson's end: the answer, the manner, the end, the timing, the counsel, and the reservation.

The steps map onto the worksheet exactly: fieldset one is Step 1, the question and its house; fieldset two is Step 2, the bench; fieldset three is Step 3, the significators; fieldset four is Step 4, the scan's checklist; fieldset five is Step 5, strength and quality with the subtle findings entered among the weights; fieldset six is Step 6, the verdict, no longer a draft to be marked *dry run*, but the judgment itself. The agreement is nearly total. The demand and its house first; the Witnesses and Judge in their table; the figure in the house required *with its springings* (his stolen-money example, the second-house figure found again in the sixth showing "the thief to be a servant in the house," is the springing doctrine of Lesson 7 compressed to one clause); the aspects noted around the significator; the figure in the fourth "to signify the end of the matter"; the Part of Fortune last, for money questions: Cattan's order of march, surviving three centuries and a complete change of cosmology almost untouched. But at the end. "Then add… Now consider all you have written, and according to the balance of Good and Evil, form your final judgement." *The balance of Good and Evil*: every testimony written into one of two columns, good on one side, evil on the other, and the verdict read off the heavier pile. That is a method, and an honest one, and it is not this course's method, because it is flat: in Crowley's balance a Judge's sentence, a perfection, an aspect and a fourth-house figure all weigh as entries in the same two columns, and eight small fair testimonies can outvote one great dark one. Cattan never counts like that: in every case this course has read, and in the three you will reread below, he *ranks*: certain testimonies answer certain questions, the perfection and the houses decide the grant, the bench the end, and the rest qualifies. The difference between counting and ranking is the difference between the modern comparison and the Renaissance art, and the nine rules exist to make the ranking explicit.

## The nine rules of weighing

This is the lesson's doctrine, and the course's policy governs every line of it: where a rule is the sources' own, the source is named and quoted; where a rule is the course's synthesis (a codification of what the sources *do* but never state) it is flagged as exactly that, so that you always know whether you are standing on Cattan or on this course's reading of him. Nine rules; the order is itself the hierarchy.

**Rule 1. Perfection alone grants or denies the thing asked.** The grant half is Cattan direct: the modes of perfection are his chapters 6 through 9, and his practice performs them: the Lord of Clermont's suit won "especially, for that the occupation was good," the Lord of Ferte's marriage granted from the springings of the first and seventh (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, chs. 6 and 9). The denial half you have held since Lesson 7: no perfection, no grant; denial is an answer. And the rule's sharp edge, *alone*, has a negative corollary this course states outright: **the bench cannot grant what the houses refuse, and cannot refuse what they grant.** That corollary is the course's synthesis, flagged as such: Cattan never writes the sentence, but his chapter of the Judge confines the office to "the ende of all the signification of the demaund" (Book III, ch. 4), limiting language, an end-jurisdiction and not a grant-jurisdiction, and Lesson 6 drew exactly this boundary when it taught you that the sentence concerns the matter's issue while the houses hold the parties and the grant. Chart C, below, is this rule's monument.

**Rule 2. The Judge rules the end and manner of the whole matter; the Witnesses the beginning and the course.** Source-direct, both halves: the Judge "is procreated of the two witnesses to judge the ende of all the signification of the demaund… if the Judge be good, the signification of the demaund shall come to a good ende, and if he be ill, it shall come to an ill ende" (Book III, ch. 4); the Right Witness signifies the querent's side and "the thing lately passed," the Left Witness "the thing to come, and put in question" (Book III, ch. 3). The bench's jurisdiction is *trajectory* (how the business begins, how it is carried, how it concludes) and within that jurisdiction it is supreme: no house can amend a Judge on the question of how the matter ends, any more than a Judge can amend the houses on whether the asker gets the thing asked.

**Rule 3. The quality of the perfecting figures conditions the grant.** Source-direct, from the occupation chapter, and worth quoting at the length Lesson 7 first gave it: a good figure occupying "is the best signe that may be. But if the figure be ill, it is an ill signe," and his example prices it exactly: two figures of the Fire occupying, in a question of two parties at suit, and "the accord shall bee surely, but it shall not be without fraude or deceipt" (Book III, ch. 9). Granted, and burnt in the granting. The mode answers *whether*; the figure answers *in what manner*. Chart A's grant by Via has carried this rule's signature since Lesson 7, and Chart D's grant by Acquisitio will show you its bright face.

**Rule 4. Angles outweigh succedents, succedents cadents; and strength is not goodness.** The first half is source-direct: the declaration of places gives the angles strongest, the succedents mean, the cadents "weak and feeble" (Book III, ch. 10), and Lesson 5 has had you classifying by it since the houses were new. The second half, that strength and goodness are independent axes, so that a testimony must be graded on both before it is weighed at all, is the course's articulation, flagged: it is Lesson 8's doctrine, distilled from Cattan's practice rather than his statement, and Chart C's seventh house is its standing proof: ill, ill-suited, and excellently resourced.

**Rule 5. Aspects and dignities weight testimony; they never reverse the scan's substance.** Course synthesis, flagged (this is Lesson 8's central discipline restated as a rule of rank) and defended from the sources' conduct: in every one of Cattan's worked cases, the aspects arrive after the houses and qualify what the houses have established. In the Trivulsio peace case below, an ill trine *confirms and colors* a refusal the first and seventh houses have already shaped; in the Novelaire case, the good trines and sextiles soften the timing of a death the angles have already decreed; nowhere, in any case this course has found, does a regard overturn a perfection or un-deny a denial. An aspect is a channel and a weight. It tunes the answer; it does not own one.

**Rule 6. The subtle instruments detail and break ties; they never overrule foundations.** The order is source-direct (the way of the point and the Part of Fortune come at the end of Cattan's procession by his own sentence, Book III, ch. 21, the order of march above) and the *force* of the rule, that the instruments' only deciding power is the tie-break, is the course's synthesis from the cases, flagged: in the Lord of Tays' horse-buying judgment the foundations came up mixed and three subtle instruments pulling together tipped the verdict; in the robbery case the houses convicted and the way of the point added one engraved detail. Lesson 9 gave you the formula and this rule canonizes it: refinements, not foundations; and a Part of Fortune that outvotes a perfection is not subtlety but error.

**Rule 7. Every house testifies to its own department: the fourth gives the end of the matter; in suits, the ninth and tenth are the counsellors and the judges.** The fourth-house clause is the modern comparison's most useful contribution, stated plainly in the method you just read, "add the meaning of the figure in the 4th, to signify the end of the matter" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. VII), and it is fully consonant with Cattan, whose fourth-house chapter judges inheritances, grounds and conclusions by exactly that house's figure (Book II, ch. 4). The suits clause is Cattan direct, from the Clermont judgment: "Because therefore that the ninth is a good figure and well accompanied with an other that is good, which is the tenth: this signifyeth that the Counsellours and Judges shall give sentence" (Book III, ch. 9). The rule generalizes what Lesson 5 always taught: the wheel is a map of departments, and a judgment that needs to know who profits, where the cost falls, or by whose hand the end comes, asks the house whose department it is. The fourth's "end of the matter" and the Judge's "end" are different testimonies in different registers: the Judge rules how the whole business concludes; the fourth house describes the state of things at its conclusion. They can agree, qualify one another, or price one another, and Chart D shows the pricing at full scale.

**Rule 8. Rank first, count second.** The counting method is sourced and modern: Crowley's balance of Good and Evil, the flat list (table-row, house-figure, springings, aspects good and evil, the fourth house, the Reconciler, the Part of Fortune) totted into two columns and the verdict read from the heavier (Crowley, ch. VII, flagged as the modern comparison it is). Cattan ranks and never counts: no case of his adds testimonies across ranks, and his verdicts always assign each finding to its jurisdiction before weighing anything against anything. The course's rule, **clear perfection with a concurring bench outweighs any number of subtle testimonies; counting decides only among testimonies of the same rank**, is synthesis, flagged: it is the course's codification of what Cattan does on every page and states on none. When five small fair weights face one perfection, the perfection wins, always. When two regards of the same rank pull opposite ways, count, and let the majority and the kinships decide. The balance is a tool for the bottom of the hierarchy, never a court of appeal over the top of it.

**Rule 9. The question always has the last word.** Course canon since Lesson 3, where the order of fortune ended in exactly these words, and the deepest of the nine. Every testimony in a chart is testimony *about the question asked*: Carcer is ruin in a journey question and mercy in a question of whether the prisoner's chains will hold; a dark fortune-point in the eighth house means one thing when the question is money and another when it is marriage; the same Judge Acquisitio that crowns Chart C's lawsuit would read entirely differently over a question about losing weight. The eight rules above tell you how testimonies rank against each other. This rule tells you what they are all ranked *for*; and a judgment that is impeccably weighed but answers a question the querent did not ask has failed at the only step that cannot be repaired.

Nine rules, one page. Their architecture is the answer to Lesson 9's closing riddle. *What outranks what:* Rules 1 through 7 are not a ladder of force but a map of jurisdictions (perfection owns the grant, the bench owns the trajectory, the places own strength, the departments own their circumstances) and most apparent conflicts between testimonies dissolve the moment each is confined to what it is entitled to decide. *How many testimonies make a sentence:* one, if it is the right rank (a perfection is a sentence's worth of grant all by itself); no number, if they are the wrong rank (Rule 8). *When a Judge overrules a house and when a house a Judge:* never, and never; and the question, which this course has let stand in that form for two lessons, is a category error the moment you hold Rule 1 beside Rule 2. The Judge cannot overrule the houses on the grant because the grant was never his; the houses cannot overrule the Judge on the end because the end was never theirs. A divided chart is not a quarrel to be won. It is a sentence with subordinate clauses, every clause true in its own jurisdiction; and Chart C is about to demonstrate it.

## The judge at work: three judgments entire

First the rules at work in the hands that taught them. These chapters, met in Lesson 8 for their aspect doctrine, each carry a complete worked judgment end to end (the course quotes two entire, the third by its judgment), a model of the prose a finished judgment lives in.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-15-of-the-tryne-aspect-chapt" />

The Trivulsio case: Cardinal Trivulzio come as Legate to make peace between Francis and the Emperor, and a gentleman's question: shall the peace be made? The first figure goes into the third house, a springing, read by where it stays: "they should treate of this peace," the houses establishing the matter's course (Rule 1's machinery, here showing treaty without perfection of the peace itself). The first is beheld with an ill aspect from the fifth, Tristitia, Saturn retrograde: "it signifyed that there should bee no peace made at this time," an ill figure in a friendly channel, Lesson 8's central doctrine, *coloring and confirming* the refusal (Rule 5: the aspect qualifies; it did not create the denial alone, and watch how Cattan immediately corroborates it from the houses). The ninth is good, the house of Churchmen: "the sayde Cardinall would doe what he were able," Rule 7, the department testifying to its own occupant. The seventh is ill: "the Emperour should have no peace," the other party's own house, read for the other party. The witnesses and Judge, all of Saturn with the fifth: the bench concurring in the dark trajectory (Rule 2). The second gone into the eighth, the King's substance passed into the house of the Emperor's substance: "the king should loose much of his substance," a springing read by department, the cost clause of the judgment. And the trine from the eighth to the twelfth, ill, adds the final engraved detail: the Emperor "should not long keepe the substance which he should winne." Then the verification, which Cattan never omits: Provence taken "untill Ayre," the short stay, the departure, the loss of all. The architecture: an answer (no peace), a manner (treaty attempted, the Cardinal laboring), an end (loss on both sides), a timing ("at this time"), and the texture of cost, every clause assigned to the testimony entitled to give it, nothing counted across ranks.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-17-of-the-aspect-of-opposition" />

The Novelaire case (is the Count dead or not?) is a judgment of astonishing economy. The first and seventh agree in element, water with water: "the saide Countie was not dead," the opposition's own doctrine, contrariety absent, the parties' houses concordant, so the dreadful possibility is refused *first*, from the foundations (Rule 1's logic applied to a state-of-affairs question). The fourth and tenth "be contraries and of great enmitie": "he should die of this disease," the end-houses, Rule 7, the fourth and its opposite angle decreeing the end of the matter. The trines and sextiles good: "without any doubt he should live a space after that hee was hurt," Rule 5 at its purest, the friendly regards *unable to reverse* the end the angles have decreed, able only to stretch the time of it; and Rule 6's instruments-as-detail in the same breath. Then the concurrence: other figures "did agree with the tenth which is Carcer, which alwaies in this place signifyeth death," Acquisitio in the eighth (dead men's goods) and Conjunctio for the Judge: the bench and the departments converging on the same sentence (Rule 2, Rule 7, and Rule 8's same-rank counting, used exactly where counting belongs). The verdict: he shall die; and the verification, the wound in the belly, my Lord of Tays as witness. An answer, a manner (not dead yet; dies of this hurt), an end, a timing (a space after), every clause sourced to its jurisdiction.

The third model, the Emperor's departure from Nice (Book III, ch. 16), you may reread in your own copy (Lesson 8 printed it entire) and it earns its place in this trio for a different virtue: it is the tradition's best surviving *timing* judgment. Asked which day the Emperor would depart, Cattan reads fixity against motion exactly as Lesson 9's protocol does: Acquisitio in the first, "a fygure fixed," of one planet with the fixed tenth: "I judged that the Emperour shall not part thence in sixe dayes"; the witnesses and Judge fixed figures: "he should stay there fiue or sixe daies"; and around the timing core, the departments fill in the circumstances: the seventh and eighth retrograde and ill ("his long stay… should be much to his hinderance"), the fifth for the country he would go to, the ninth and eleventh ill for borrowed money, the first springing into the house of sadness for the end of the voyage: "a short returne with dishonour, losse, and discomfyture of his men." The timing clause is delivered with a number and a hedge ("fiue or sixe daies") and verified at eight; the tradition's own best practice states pace with uncertainty inside the sentence, exactly as your protocol requires.

Three judgments, one method. In none of them does Cattan count good against evil across the whole chart; in all of them he assigns (this testimony to the grant, that to the end, this to the cost, that to the time) and the verdict assembles the assignments. That is the prose your journal is about to learn to write.

## The four charts, judged

Now the verdict column. Four standing questions; the dossiers are complete, the rules are stated, and the order of business runs from the easiest promotion to the master case, then, in the worked example, to the chart you have never seen. Each judgment below ends in the six-clause form this lesson teaches, and Exercise 7 will have you copy each into the journal over the struck-through word *provisional*.

### Chart B: the partnership, denied and done

The easiest final verdict this course will ever ask of you: nothing in Chart B's dossier needs to be re-decided, because every instrument, at every level, has said the same thing; what Lesson 9 left provisional is promoted on the dossier's own unanimity.

Rule 1: the scan found no occupation, no conjunction, no company that joins (Puella stands once in the whole chart, the querent traveling to no meeting) and no aspect between the significators softens it: perfection is denied, and the houses refuse the recovery. Rule 2: the bench (Tristitia, Cauda Draconis, Via) pronounces a sorrowful beginning, a departing course, an end in change, over a question that asked for restoration; and the table's column is among the blackest on the sixteen leaves: substance very ill, the thing lost not found. Bench and houses concur, so Rule 1's hard corollary never even engages. Rules 4 and 5: the audit found both significators angular and stable (the stalemate strong, present-tense, well-resourced) with the prison's kinned sextile to its own double in the fifth keeping the lock in good repair: strength serving the denial. Rule 6: the instruments agree from independent directions: the course's one perfect single-pointed way runs to Carcer in the fifth, the hidden root of the matter a bind; the fortune-point sits mean-dark on Cauda Draconis in the tenth, the chart's fortune concentrated on the figure of departure in the house of standing. Nothing to weigh, because nothing dissents; the only discipline this judgment demands is the humane one, held for the pastoral section below: the verdict is dark, and it must be delivered as counsel and not as doom.

The finished judgment, in the six clauses: **Denied: the partnership is not recovered.** The manner: the matter sits where it stands, stable significators in their angles, the prison in the suit's own seat, no party moving toward any meeting. The end: when it comes, it comes as change and not as restoration; Via on the bench rules the issue of the whole matter, and what is gone moves on. The timing: slow, a matter that endures in its stalemate, with an end that arrives as alteration, likely late and possibly abrupt after long stillness; no date, as always. The counsel: release the question; the chart's one bright track is that the fortune-point and the Judge agree the querent's standing survives the change; build where the change leads, not where the bond was. The reservation: this judgment denies the recovery asked about; it does not judge the partner, the money owed, or any question not asked; each of those is its own demand, its own house, its own cast, on another day.

Strike through *provisional* in Chart B's dossier and write the six clauses beneath it. That is the first promotion, and the form of all the rest.

### Chart A: the marriage, granted and priced

Chart A's judgment has been assembling itself since Lesson 6, and the weighing confirms what every lesson added: a real grant, really priced.

Rule 1: the chart perfects: the quesited's Via stands in the second house, beside the querent: conjunction, the matter coming to the asker unfetched. The houses grant the marriage. Rule 3 conditions the grant exactly as Lesson 7 first read it: granted by approach and not identity, and granted by *Via*, the most changeable figure of the sixteen carrying the most binding of contracts, a grant that is real and restless at once. Rule 2 gives the trajectory: Rubeus, Tristitia, Acquisitio, ill begun, hard carried, ends in gain; and the table's row for the bond itself reads *a wife: ill*, the priced clause this course has refused to soften since Lesson 6. Rules 4 and 5: both significators angular and mobile, the grant strong and in motion; the one place bench and wheel touch, as Lesson 7 noticed, is the money square: Acquisitio, the Judge's own figure, keeping elemental company with the match from the eighth house, the dowry's house. Rule 6: the way of the point is honestly absent; the fortune-point is good: Caput Draconis in the fourth, the entering threshold in the house where matters end: the end-house fair, the chart's fortune concentrated at the conclusion. Rule 7 reads the same fourth house as the end of the matter, and it is the chart's kindest testimony. Rule 9, last, keeps the judgment honest about what was asked: *shall it take effect*, and it shall; the question did not ask whether it would be easy, and the chart answered that anyway.

The finished judgment: **Granted: the marriage takes effect**; the matter comes to the querent rather than being fetched. The manner: ill begun and hard carried, a match made under friction, granted by the figure of motion, its one steady alliance running through the goods. The end: gain; the Judge's sentence and the fair end-house agree the conclusion is good and holds. The timing: swift rather than slow (mobile significators in a mobile chart) and lasting once concluded, under a stable Judge. The counsel: marry, knowing the price; guard the early road, where the bench puts the trouble, and treat the money (the one bright bridge between sentence and wheel) as the bond's working ground rather than its danger. The reservation: the table's *a wife: ill* is not erased by the grant; it is the grant's texture: the keeping will demand more than the getting, and the querent should hear that clause as part of the answer, not as a footnote to it.

### Chart C: the master case

Six lessons brought Chart C here: the conflict seen, located, sharpened and reserved for this lesson by name, the houses' refusal added, the denial's key witness measured and found strong, the dossier completed. Nothing remains to gather. Weigh.

<HouseChart mothers={['amissio', 'amissio', 'puella', 'fortuna-minor']} caption="Chart C in the twelve houses, the divided chart: Amissio doubled in 1 and 2, Cauda Draconis in 7 and 11, Caput Draconis in the 10th, and over it all the Judge Acquisitio." />

**First, the grant: Rule 1.** Does the querent win the lawsuit? Ask only the testimony entitled to answer. The scan, on the record since Lesson 7: no occupation, for the seventh holds the Dragon's Tail, not Amissio. No conjunction, for both significators spring, and neither springs toward the other: Amissio from the first into the second, Cauda from the seventh into the eleventh. Company, found, and it is the cruelest finding in the chart: the 1·2 pair is Amissio with Amissio, company simple of an ill figure, the querent doubled with his own Loss, the strengthening mode, joining him to nothing but his own misfortune, "much ill" by the chapter's own words. And between the significators themselves, exactly one regard: the opposition, first to seventh, unkinned, no shared element, no shared planet, nothing but the aspect of contrariety between the asker and the adversary. The houses refuse. **The querent does not prevail.** That is Rule 1's whole jurisdiction exercised, and by the rule's corollary (the course's flagged synthesis, drawn from the Judge's own limiting commission and held since Lesson 6) no brightness anywhere on the bench can amend it. The grant was never the Judge's to give.

**Second, the force of the denial: Rule 4.** Lesson 8's audit, now cashed. The Tail in the seventh is angular, doubled into the eleventh, and thrice-attended (Via's kinned sextile from the fifth, Populus's from the ninth, its own double's trine from the eleventh, the warmest aspect-attendance in the chart) and the adversary holds the strong ground, and strength is not goodness, but strength serving a denial makes the denial forceful. And on the querent's side the same audit reads the cost: Amissio doubled into the second, his person and his purse both wearing Loss, the suit draining his substance whether or not it ever reaches judgment. This denial is not a gate left unlatched. It is locked, and the lock is resourced.

**Third, the bench: Rule 2, in its own jurisdiction.** Caput Draconis, Albus, Acquisitio: well begun, quietly carried, ends in gain; the table's column fair in nearly every row. For six lessons this sentence has stood against the wheel like a contradiction. Apply the rule and the contradiction dissolves: the bench rules the end and manner of the *matter* (the suit as a business in the world) and says nothing about the grant, which was Rule 1's and is already decided. The suit concludes; concludes well; concludes in gain. The houses have just told you the gain is not the querent's victory. Both are true. The matter will be *ended*, definitely, even profitably, and not as his prevailing. A suit can end in gain that is not the querent's gain; Lesson 6 wrote that sentence as a possibility, and the weighing has just made it the finding.

**Fourth: whose gain? Rule 7: follow the fortune to its department.** The Part of Fortune sits on Caput Draconis in the tenth (good figure, good house, the chart's one unambiguously bright address) and in a lawsuit the tenth is not an abstraction: it is the house of magistrates and judges, the counsellors' bench. Cattan reads exactly this configuration in the Clermont case: the ninth and tenth good, "this signifyeth that the Counsellours and Judges shall give sentence and say judgement" (Book III, ch. 9). A bright tenth in a suit means the court is disposed to act: sentence *will* be given; the matter ends by the court's act, cleanly, not by exhaustion in the lists, and the men of the law fare well in the ending. There is the Judge's gain, located at last: the chart's fortune concentrates on the side of the court. The matter ends in gain; the gain falls to the bench that ends it.

**Fifth, the manner of it: Rules 2 and 5 on the adversary's side.** Lesson 8 left a deliberately three-horned question standing: does the Tail's strength doom the querent's cause, or carry the adversary the more surely to the ruin the Tail also signifies, or mean something neither of you has yet imagined? The weighing answers: the second, with a color of the third. Cauda Draconis strong in the suit's own house is the suit itself wearing the figure of departure: the matter consumes itself and goes out, swiftly, for both significators are mobile; finally, for the Judge is stable. The adversary prevails in the narrow sense that the querent does not; and his house wears ruin even in prevailing, for the Tail promises the going-out of the thing it sits on, and what it sits on is the suit. No one wins this lawsuit the way the querent meant the word when he asked. The court ends it; the parties absorb it; the law is paid.

**Sixth, the verdict.** Write it in the journal in full, because this is the master case and its form is the model for every divided chart you will ever judge:

**Denied: the querent does not prevail.** The matter will nonetheless be ended, by sentence or by composition from above, swiftly (the significators are mobile) and finally (the Judge is stable); it will cost him in substance, his own figure doubling Loss into the house of his goods; and the gain the Judge promises falls to the side of the court, not to him. Counsel: do not fight to judgment; compound, while the tenth is bright and disposed to end the matter fairly; the same court whose act would end the suit against him can end it for him at a price, and the chart shows the price smaller now than later. Reservation: this judgment answers the prevailing, not the merits; and the one mercy in the wheel, that the querent's losses sit doubled in houses he can still govern, holds only while he can still choose the composition over the sentence.

The master case has one thesis: **a divided chart is not a contradiction; it is a sentence with subordinate clauses.** The houses refused the grant; the bench promised a good end; the fortune-point named whose good; the audit priced the course; the instruments timed it; and not one testimony overruled another, because not one ever spoke outside its jurisdiction. The conflict this course carried for six lessons was never a conflict in the art. It was a judgment waiting for a judge who knew which question each witness was answering. You are now that judge; and the proof is that the resolution, read back, feels less like a verdict imposed on the chart than like the chart finally allowed to finish its own sentence.

## Worked example

A fourth chart, Chart D, and it is unlike every chart before it in exactly one way: you meet it with the complete method already in your hands. Charts A, B and C grew up with you, each lesson adding a layer; Chart D arrives whole, the way every chart of the rest of your practice will, and is judged here from a standing start, in real time, at full discipline. This is the lesson's true summit: not the master case, which settled an old account, but the first ordinary act of the rest of your training.

The question, framed and defended: **"Will the querent get the office they seek?"** A man seeks an appointment: an office, a dignity, a place under authority. The house: the tenth, honours, offices, magistracy, "the office or dignitie which he shall have" in Cattan's own tenth-house words; one sentence, defensible, done. (The novelty, by Rule 9: the first three charts all asked seventh-house questions, and Lesson 7 warned against letting that accident harden into a rule. Here the quesited is the *tenth*, and every test below runs first-to-tenth.)

The four Mothers, cast for this question: **Acquisitio, Fortuna Major, Via, Amissio.** The shield:

**Step 2, the bench.** Right Witness Fortuna Major; Left Witness Populus; Judge Fortuna Major. What Populus the Witness does is what Populus always does: the mirror takes the character of its company, adds no parities of its own, and lets the Right Witness's figure pass through to the Judge unchanged, which is why the Judge *is* Fortuna Major. The sentence: begun in the Greater Fortune, carried by a course that takes the shape of its beginning, ended in the Greater Fortune, the best figure in the art, on which, as Lesson 3 had it, nobody in the corpus disputes. The trajectory of this matter is its beginning writ large: as the asker enters it, so it goes. And the table's row: the question is an office, so the matter is *worship*, the tables' word for honour and standing:

Worship: good. The bench could hardly speak fairer. The sentence rules the end; the grant belongs to the houses. Scan.

**Step 3, the significators and the springings.** Querent Acquisitio in the first: angular, stable, the figure of gain in the asker's own seat. Quesited Acquisitio in the tenth: angular, stable, the same figure. **Step 4, the scan.** Occupation: is the quesited's house held by the querent's own figure? The tenth holds Acquisitio; the first holds Acquisitio. **Yes.** The querent's figure goes straight into the house of the thing demanded: the first occupation of this course, met in the wild at last, after three charts that perfected by approach or not at all. And occupation is the strongest mode there is: identity of figure, the asker's own substance standing in the matter's own seat, no second figure diluting the join. Rule 3 prices it at once, and the pricing is bright: the occupying figure is Acquisitio, good, and Cattan's doctrine for exactly this case is the strongest sentence in his perfection chapters: if the first figure "be good, and that it passe into the house of the thing demaunded, that is the best signe that may be" (Book III, ch. 9). The rest, for completeness, since a scan is complete or it is nothing: conjunction, no significator's figure stands beside the other's house; company, the 1·2 pair, Acquisitio beside Fortuna Major, shares no figure, planet or element, only the light kinship of agreeing top lines; the 11·12 pair is Carcer with Carcer, company simple of an ill figure, far from both significators and noted for Step 5. **The chart perfects by occupation, of a good figure: the houses grant the office, in the best mode, in the best manner.**

**Step 5, the audit and the instruments.** Strength first: both significator seats angular, the grant standing on the wheel's strongest ground at both ends. The regards between the seats: the first and tenth behold one another by the square, and the figure at each end of the channel is Acquisitio itself: same figure, same planet, same element, the hard aspect carried entirely by kin. Lesson 8 taught you to read exactly this: a square is the channel of effort and contest, and a kinned square is effort that *serves*; the ladder is climbed, not stormed; the office is won by labor that is the querent's own. The darker regards, recorded honestly: the tenth suffers the opposition of Amissio from the fourth, and Amissio is Acquisitio's own counterpart, gain's undoing, regarding the office from the house where matters end; the same Amissio doubles into the sixth, and Fortuna Major's fair doubling into the second and fifth answers it from the querent's money and pleasures. File the fourth-house opposition for Rule 7; it is the judgment's reservation growing in plain sight. The subtle instruments, in their place and rank: the **way of the point**: the Judge's top line is double, so the way forks or fails; both Witnesses match; through the ninth (Conjunctio, double top) the trail runs to the first and second, and both match: **the way completes, in houses 1 and 2**, the first completing way in the course, double-topped and forked, the fork that lives. The hidden root of the matter, by the instrument built for finding roots: the querent himself and his own substance; the office grows from his merit and his means, not from patronage. (The other fork-arms die: the tenth's trail finds single tops in the third and fourth; Populus's branch finds Carcer's single tops in the eleventh and twelfth. Trace it all; record it all.) The **Part of Fortune**: the twelve house figures sum to 68 (five twelves are 60, remainder **8**) the eighth house, on **Cauda Draconis**: ill figure, and the eighth among the evil houses; **the fortune-point is dark**, the chart's fortune concentrated in the house of other men's goods and dead men's substance, on the figure of going-out. In an office question, read by department: what flows to him from others' coffers (fees, dues, the perquisites of place) wears the Tail; the office's revenues will disappoint, or drain. And **Carcer doubled in the eleventh and twelfth**: company simple of the prison, in the houses of friends and of hidden things: friends slow to move, hindrances unseen; the one corner of the wheel where this bright chart binds. Timing: querent, quesited and Judge all stable, a sure matter, not a swift one; the office comes, and comes to stay, and does not come tomorrow.

**Step 6, the weighing and the verdict.** Rank, then count; and in this chart the ranking is almost ceremonial, which is itself the lesson: this is what an *undivided* grant looks like under the same method that resolved the divided one. Rule 1: the houses grant by occupation, the strongest mode. Rule 3: granted by a good figure, the best sign that may be. Rule 2: the bench concurs, begun, carried and ended in the Greater Fortune, the table's *worship: good* on the row that is this question's own. Rule 7 now earns its keep, because the one structured tension in the chart lives exactly on the line that rule draws: the Judge's end is fair, and the *fourth house* (the end of the matter, the state of things at its conclusion) holds Amissio, loss, in opposition to the office itself. These are different testimonies in different registers, and a judge who can hold them apart writes a better verdict than one who averages them: the whole business concludes in the Greater Fortune (he gets and keeps the standing) *and* the matter's conclusion carries loss in it (the keeping costs). Cattan's own fourth-house entry for Amissio is unsparing, "This figure is ill for all the demaunds which may be made in this house" (Book II, ch. 4), and the course's reference entry for the occupying figure is just as exactly on point the other way:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-10/acquisitio" />

"The man shall have the honor he pretendeth… it is good for the office or dignitie which he shall have." The placement series, the scan and the bench agree to the letter. Rule 6 keeps the instruments in their seats: the dark fortune-point and the Carcer pair *detail* the grant's price (they cannot touch the grant) and the completing way supplies the judgment's warmest particular: the root of this office is the man himself and his means. Rule 8: nothing here needs counting; every rank speaks with one voice per rank. Rule 9: the question asked for the obtaining, and the chart's strongest testimony answers precisely that.

The finished judgment, six clauses, into the journal: **Granted: the querent obtains the office he sues for.** The manner: by his own merit and his own means, the way's root in his person and his purse, the kinned square between his seat and the office's the signature of a ladder climbed by labor; his friends will be slower than his work, and some hindrances will move unseen (Carcer doubled in the eleventh and twelfth). The end: the whole business concludes in the Greater Fortune, the standing, once gained, holds (stable significators, stable Judge, worship good). The timing: sure and not swift, a stable chart moves like what it is; no date. The counsel: take the office, and enter it provisioned; let him trust the substance that won it rather than the revenues it promises, for the fortune-point is dark in the house of other men's goods, and the place will feed his standing before it feeds his purse. The reservation: the end of the *matter* carries loss, Amissio at the fourth angle, opposing the office itself, so the having will cost more than the getting; the grant is not in doubt, and its price is not either.

Chart D beside Chart C makes the symmetry: the master case was a denial under a bright Judge, a NO with a definite, even profitable, end; Chart D is a grant carrying a dark end-house and a dark fortune-point, a confident YES with reservations. The structural mirror, one of each, judged by the same nine rules without a single rule bending. That is what a method is for.

## The finished judgment: the six clauses

The verdicts above all wore the same shape, and the shape is the lesson's last piece of doctrine: the **final-verdict protocol**, the form of a finished judgment. Six clauses, in a fixed order, every one present in every judgment you write from tonight (a clause may be one word long; it may not be absent):

1. **The answer.** Granted or denied, in the question's own words, from the scan, by Rule 1; first, always, because the querent asked a question and the first words of a judgment answer it. No throat-clearing, no "the chart suggests."
2. **The manner.** How the matter begins and is carried, and in what style the grant or denial operates: the Witnesses' clauses, the quality of the perfecting figures (Rule 3), the springings read by their houses, the audit's texture. The answer's adverbs live here.
3. **The end.** The Judge's sentence on the whole matter's issue, and the fourth house's end-of-the-matter where the question wants it (Rules 2 and 7); held distinct when they differ, and the difference stated, as Chart D's verdict stated it.
4. **The timing.** Pace and direction from mobility, entrance and place, by Lesson 9's protocol, with the uncertainty written into the sentence. Never a date.
5. **The counsel.** What the querent should *do*: the one clause addressed to the will rather than the understanding. The chart almost always contains it: the bright house that shows the open road (compound while the tenth is bright), the dark one that shows the closed (enter it provisioned). A judgment without counsel is a verdict read to a prisoner.
6. **The reservation.** The qualification the chart itself demands: the table-row that stays ill inside a grant, the mercy that survives inside a denial, the question the judgment deliberately does not answer. Honesty's clause: it marks the judgment's edges so the querent cannot mistake them.

Answer, manner, end, timing, counsel, reservation. This form is not retired when the lesson ends: Lesson 11's judgments by the classes of questions are written in it, the capstone requires it, and your journal speaks it from tonight forward. Its architecture is the nine rules made into prose: clause one belongs to Rule 1, clause two to Rules 3 through 5, clause three to Rules 2 and 7, clause four to the timing protocol, and clauses five and six to the part of the art that no rule can carry alone: the judge facing the human being who asked.

## The journal, promoted

The last protocol upgrade of the course's middle act.

1. **Every provisional verdict is promoted.** For each of the four course charts and every cast in the journal: re-run Step 6 against the standing dossier (the dossier is complete; this is weighing, not gathering) and write the finished judgment in the six-clause form directly beneath the provisional line. Strike through *provisional, pending Lesson 10's method*; leave it legible. The strike-through is the record of a discipline kept.
2. **Every new cast gets a full judgment.** From this lesson forward the protocol runs: cast, verify, Step 0, dossier, weighing, finished judgment, all six clauses, the same day. The era of the deferred verdict is over; what remains permanently is its ethic: no clause the dossier cannot defend.
3. **The worksheet runs whole.** Steps 1 through 6, every chart you take to judgment, the assembled paragraph copied into the journal, no longer marked *dry run*.
4. **The verdicts are dated and final, and finality has a discipline of its own.** A finished judgment is not revised when the outcome arrives; it is *checked*. Where the matter resolves in the world, record the outcome beside the judgment, as Cattan recorded Lyon and Provence and the wound in the belly: hits and misses alike, in the same ink. The misses are the curriculum of the judge you will be in ten years.

## Delivering the ill answer

This section could not have been written until your first final denials existed: Chart B's, and Chart C's. They are written now, and someone has to say them aloud.

The geomancer's honesty was settled doctrine long before this lesson: denial is an answer; an honest negative, honestly recorded, outweighs a forced positive every time the two compete. But a journal absorbs an ill answer silently, and a querent does not. The man who asked Chart C's question is in a lawsuit he now learns he will not win; the asker of Chart B's wanted a partnership back and has been told the matter ends in change, not restoration. What the judge owes them is not softness: a softened denial is a small lie wearing kindness's coat, and the querent who acts on the soft version is injured by precisely the difference. What the judge owes them is the *whole form*. This is why the six clauses end as they do: the counsel is the humane instrument. An answer of *no*, delivered with the manner, the end, the timing, and above all the counsel the chart licenses (compound while the tenth is bright; build where the change leads) is not a blow. It is a service: the closed door named, and the open one beside it pointed out, both from the same chart, both at the same rank of honesty. Cattan's consulting rooms worked exactly so: the Lord of Tays was not told *do not buy a horse*; he was told the horse had an evil foot and was worth nothing, a denial with its reasons and its protection inside it. Say the ill answer plainly, say the counsel with it, and put the reservation last so the querent leaves knowing what was *not* judged: that is the whole pastoral art this course teaches, and it is nothing but the verdict-form, kept under pressure. The judge who pads a denial has stopped being a judge; the one who delivers it bare has stopped being of use. The six clauses are how you are both spared.

<Exercise n={3} title="The flawed judgment" kind="drill">
  A fellow student judges the chart of Exercise 2 and hands you this:

  *"The Witnesses are Acquisitio and Acquisitio (gain doubled) and the table of Acquisitio gives, for the thing lost: found. The bench therefore grants the recovery. The significator of the goods is Acquisitio, the figure of gain, which stands in the third and fifth houses, moving toward the querent.   Find and correct at least three errors, each itemized against a named rule or doctrine, and finish with the corrected verdict.

  <ModelAnswer>
    There are five, and the first three are the ones the exercise demands. **First: the bench is misread as the grant.** Even if this bench could speak, "thing lost: found" is a sentence about the matter's trajectory, not a grant of the question; Rule 1: perfection alone grants or denies, and the bench cannot grant what the houses refuse; the scan found no perfection by any mode, so the recovery is refused at the only rank entitled to refuse it. **Second: the bench could not even speak.** The Witnesses are identical, the Judge is Populus, and the doctors are unanimous: the sentence is withheld and judgment falls "by the foure angles and other rules" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 6, the advertisement; Lesson 6's standing practice). The flawed judgment quotes a recused judge. **Third: the significator is wrong.** The quesited is the figure standing in the question's house (Puella, in the second) not the figure whose *meaning* flatters the question; choosing Acquisitio "because it signifies gain" selects evidence by the desired verdict, the inversion of everything Lesson 5 taught about the question lighting the houses. (And its corollary error: Acquisitio's spring from third to fifth is two circumstance-houses talking to each other, not anything "moving toward the querent.") **Fourth: a subtle instrument is allowed to overrule a denial.** The way of the point is a refinement, by Rule 6: detail and tie-break, never foundation; its terminal on Acquisitio in the fifth names the root of the loss (the house of pleasure, where the goods went), and to read it as "confirming recovery" is to let the engraver overrule the architect. **Fifth: the timing is invented.** "Quickly" has no source in the chart (stable querent, nothing entering) and a timing clause without instruments behind it is theatre, against Lesson 9's protocol. The corrected verdict is Exercise 2's: denied, the goods are not found; gone quietly by way of pleasure and the household; the matter closes without ruin; cease the search and lock the two named doors; the bench silent, the judgment standing wholly on the houses.
