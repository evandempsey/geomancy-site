This lesson applies the method to the main kinds of questions the old books treat: money, marriage, sickness, suits, journeys, offices, lost things, absent people, death, prison, and hidden enemies. Each class has its own houses and common mistakes. It also handles the cases Lesson 5 set aside: multi-house questions, another person's sickness, theft, and the *turning* of the chart, by which a third party's matter is found from that third party's house.

The method does not change. You still use Step 0 through Step 6, the nine rules of weighing, and the six parts of the finished reading. What changes is the kind of question being read. A money question, a sickness question, and a marriage question use the same method, but the relevant houses and findings differ. The new discipline is reading Cattan's Book II as finding inside the method, not as a list of ready-made answers.

Many real questions are indirect: a sick father, a friend's debt, a brother's money, a thief who may be a servant. Turning the chart handles these cases. The sources support some of it plainly, some of it by practice, and some later manuals extend it beyond the Renaissance source. This lesson separates those layers so the journal can say what support each turn has.

## Placement entries as findings

The lesson's new discipline is placement lookup. Cattan's Book II gives each house a list of questions, then sixteen figure-in-house entries. The mistake is to look up the quesited figure, copy the matching sentence, and call it the answer. That is not a reading. The rule is: **the placement entry is finding, not answer; it enters at Step 5, speaks for the quesited house's own department, and is weighed by the nine rules like everything else.** Whether the thing reaches the asker still belongs to the perfection scan (Rule 1). The end still belongs to the triad (Rule 2). The Book II entry tells how the matter stands in its own house.

Use three corollaries in the readings below. First, **the entry is a column, not a sentence.** A full entry may answer many demands because the house owns many demands. The question selects the relevant part; the rest belongs to other questions. Second, **the entries carry cross-references, and the cross-references are method.** Cattan often makes one house depend on another: Tristitia in the sixth kills the patient only "so that the viij. doe consent"; Fortuna Major in the eighth spares him "so that the fourth agree with this house"; Amissio in the sixth sends you to "the first and eight houses" before deciding life or death. These conditions show that the entries are finding to be resolved by the chart. Third, **know the source lines.** Cattan is the primary source: fuller, more conditional, and more attentive to the question. The pseudo-Agrippa gives real but shorter entries. Heydon often reprints the pseudo-Agrippa nearly word for word, so he is not a third independent source. When Cattan and the pseudo-Agrippa differ, resolve the difference. When Heydon follows Agrippa, count that as one dependent line of transmission.

## The classes, one by one

The map below gives each question class, its primary house, its secondary houses, its usual geometry, and the relevant parallel sources. Each section ends with placement-lookup errands for the worked examples and exercises. One structural point comes first. By the wheel's fixed geometry, every class has a default relation between the first house and the house of the matter. The second is next to the first and makes no aspect. The fifth and ninth behold the first by trine. The tenth beholds it by square. The eleventh by sextile. The sixth, eighth, and twelfth have no regard to the first at all. This is the course's synthesis from the geometry in Lesson 8, not a named source rule, but it follows from the fixed house positions and is used in practice.

### Substance and things lost: the second house

The second is "properly the house of gaine and profite": gain to come and how it comes ("with the travaile and sweate of his bodie, or by succession, or by guift"), movable goods, charges and expenses, the promise of a lord, the messenger's news, and, for the theft chain, "the place where the thing lost was stolen" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 2, quoted whole in Lesson 5).

The geometry is the adjacent pair. No aspect to the first is possible, but the 1·2 company pair is the wheel's own bond between a person and their money. In substance questions, company is the natural channel; an empty 1·2 pair is itself a finding.

The secondary houses are the **tenth**, which claims "any thing lost or stolen" outright; the **fourth**, for "the place where it is thought that treasure is hidden"; and the **eighth**, for other people's goods: the loan, the usurer, and the thing given to keep. In every money question, also check the **Part of Fortune**. Crowley's instruction is explicit: "Consider also always in money questions where the Part of Fortune falls" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. VII), and Cattan's Lord-of-Tays case already does the same in practice.

Source note: the pseudo-Agrippa's second-house entries are short and absolute where Cattan's are circumstantial. Exercise 5 shows the gap at its widest, when the two sources read Carcer in this house in opposite directions. Errands: look up **Fortuna Major in the second** and **Amissio in the second** (the favorable and unfavorable poles of the class), then **Carcer in the second**, and hold what you find for the theft chain.

### Children and pleasures: the fifth house

The fifth, "the good fortune," owns the child (stature, birth, legitimacy, nature), the woman with child and the child's sex, promises and whether they are performed, messengers and what the letters contain, friends, the earth's fruits, the book's contents, and "all pleasures, as to kisse, coll, sing, daunce, banquet" (Book II, ch. 5).

Its native channel is the trine. The asker's house beholds the child's house by perfect amity, by construction.

Use one entry in the chapter-head for the turning section below: §12 asks "If the sonne be sicke, dead, or taken prisoner." The son's sickness, death, and prison, three departments' worth of matter, are all absorbed into the son's *own* house. That supports the side of the turning rule most students do not expect.

The triad's tables carry this class's own row ("woman with child," with answers as particular as *a sonne* and *daughter*), and the pseudo-Agrippa's fifth-house entries are at their best here, full of children's faces. Errands: **Puella in the fifth**, **Rubeus in the fifth**, and the figure the triad just named: any chart where "woman with child" gives a sex is a chart whose table-row you should read against the fifth's figure.

### Sickness: the sixth house

The class that teaches the method most, because the wheel withholds the most. Here is the house's own scope:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-06/significations" />

Sickness and servants share a house; subjection is the common thread. The sickness parts are the most procedurally demanding in Book II: the disease's length, its humour, the patient's danger, the physician's skill, and whether to take physic.

The geometry comes first. The first and sixth hold no regard, and the first and eighth hold none either. The sick man cannot behold either his disease or his death. The only aspect inside the triangle is the sextile between the sixth and eighth, so this class cannot be read by the ordinary joining of significators.

Read sickness as a state of affairs, just as Cattan read the Novelaire case in Lesson 10's models. Use the figures in the triangle's three houses, their conditions resolved against each other, the triad's row, and the end-houses.

The triangle has independent source support. Cattan's entries build it from inside, with the cross-references quoted above: the sixth defers to the eighth, the eighth to the fourth and tenth, and Amissio's entry sends you to "the first and eight houses" by name. Gerard states it from outside in his sixth-house chapter: "see if *Saturn,* or *Mars,* or the *Dragons Tail* be in the first" (the patient's own house first) "...but if good Planets shall be in the first, sixth and eighth, and likewise in the Angles... then the sick person shall live and recover his health" (Gerard of Cremona, *Of Astronomical Geomancy* (trans. Turner, 1655), the sixth house).

Source note: Gerard reads the **sick person in the first**, the disease in the sixth, and death in the eighth, the same triangle. His tools are *planets and lords*, which belong to Lesson 12. Here the course takes only what the figures can carry: first, sixth, eighth, read together, with the angles around them.

The secondary houses are the **tenth** for the physician "and his ordinance," the **fourth** and the triad for the end, and the **twelfth** when the sickness is of the incurable kind the twelfth owns. Errands: **Tristitia in the sixth**, **Conjunctio in the sixth**, **Caput Draconis in the eighth**, **Puer in the eighth**. These are the four placements the worked example and Exercise 2 turn on; read each whole, and mark every conditional part.

### Love, marriage, suits and theft: the seventh house

The seventh owns the contrary party: the enemy, lawsuit, thief, fugitive, marriage partner, lover, war, peace, agreement, and whether the agreement endures (Book II, ch. 7, quoted in Lesson 5). Its native aspect is opposition, because the asker and the other party face one another by construction. Ask whether that opposition has kinship: Chart C's bare opposition was hostile; the worked example below has the same figure on both sides, which is what separates a lawsuit from a marriage. Secondary houses: the **eighth** as the seventh's companion, the **fifth** for the pleasures of love, the **tenth** for judges in suits by Lesson 10's Clermont rule, and, in theft, the chain of second, seventh, tenth, and sixth. For marriage, the precedents are Chart A and Chart B; for suits, Chart C. Errands: read **Puella in the seventh** and **Tristitia in the seventh** whole, and list which parts answer which demands in the chapter-head.

### Death and inheritance: the eighth house

"The house of death" owns the death of any person, its when and its manner ("whether he shall die within a day, month, or yeare," "a good death, or else be slaine"), the inheritance and who takes it, and a stranger cluster the older texts file here and the course reports as it stands: the absent person ("how the person doth that is from home," "when he that is from hence shall returne"), the substance of the other party, fear and its issue, the thing given in trust, and the usurer's gain (Book II, ch. 8).

No regard joins the first to the eighth. Death is read, like sickness, as a state of affairs, and most death questions arrive *as* sickness questions and resolve in the sixth-class triangle.

What belongs especially to the eighth class is manner and aftermath: a good death or ill, the estate and its heirs, and the absent person's condition. This is the first of the two serious classes, so Lesson 10's advice and limits parts matter especially here. Exercise 7 is this class's quiz, and Exercise 2 will make you deliver this class's answer in full.

Errands: read **Caput Draconis in the eighth** against **Puer in the eighth**, then the same two placements in the pseudo-Agrippa's entries. One of them gives the lesson's clearest source disagreement, and the worked sickness example resolves it.

### Journeys: the ninth house

The ninth owns the long voyage ("long or shorte, good and profitable, and whether it be as good by Sea as by land"), ships, fleets, ports and shipwreck, and also priests, benefices, scholars and their books, dreams, and "if the common brute be true or not" (Book II, ch. 9).

The native channel is the trine. A journey question's significators behold one another by construction, so the class's yes answers often arrive by the aspect line. That is the weakest join in the scan, fittingly for a question that asks not for a meeting but a going.

The secondary houses are scattered: the **seventh** holds "which way he goeth that taketh his journey"; the **fourth** the safe arrival of the ship; the **second** "if the voyage that he would take in hande, shall be profitable"; the **eighth** "the gaine or profite to be doone in a straunge Countrey."

The precedent is Lesson 10's first exercise: the journey made in the prison's manner, late and bound. Errands: read **Fortuna Minor in the ninth** in both Cattan and the pseudo-Agrippa. The two entries pull against each other, and Exercise 3's chart hangs on reconciling them.

### Office and honour: the tenth house

The tenth owns honour, praise, kings, princes, magistrates, decrees, office, the king's justice, and royal journeys. It also owns three matters a modern reader may not expect: the **mother** ("Upon the mother, grandmother, wife or Lemman"), the **physician** "and his ordinance," and "any thing lost or stolen" (Book II, ch. 10, quoted in Lesson 5). Its native aspect to the first is a square, so office questions usually show effort or resistance. Chart D is the worked precedent: occupation by a good figure, the triad's *worship: good*, and the fortune-point qualifying the revenues. Exercise 4 is its opposite case. In suits, the tenth is the judges' house; in sickness, the physician's house; in theft, the recovery house. The question decides which office the tenth is serving. Errand: read **Fortuna Major in the tenth** in both Cattan and Agrippa, and notice what that good placement can and cannot promise.

### Friends and hopes: the eleventh house

"The good Angle": the friend and the hope ("the thing ye love, and whereof ye hope to have consolation, ayde and profite"), the secret companion, the faithful or traitorous friend ("if thy friend be faithfull or a traytor to thée"), the year's plenty and dearth, and a part to hold for the turning section: "over the mother and her treasure, and of all the treasures, rents and revenewes of the king" (Book II, ch. 11). The native sextile (imperfect amity, in the house of friends) sets this class's expectations honestly: eleventh-house yes answers are gentle and eleventh-house denials rarely catastrophic; the class's real trap is Lesson 5's old division between the eleventh's friend (a support and a hope) and the seventh's (a counterpart in a two-party bond), which Exercise 6 drills again. Secondary: the **third** for the near companion and neighbour, the **fifth** for the friend of pleasure, the **second** for what the friendship costs or yields. Errand: **Fortuna Major in the eleventh** and **Tristitia in the eleventh**: hope at its two temperatures.

### Prison and hidden enemies: the twelfth house

The last class, and the second serious one. The twelfth owns the prison and the prisoner ("whether the prisoner shall parte out of prison, and when"), desolation and mourning, the incurable sickness, "a privie ennemy rather then upon an open," the traitor and "théefe of an house," banishment, debts and coming poverty, and the great beasts, "oxen, cammels, dromodaries... horses, mules, asses," the last being why Cattan's own horse-buying case, which Lessons 8 and 9 walked, kept consulting this house (Book II, ch. 12). The geometry gives the rule: the twelfth has no regard to the first, so a privy-enemy question is read almost wholly by department: the twelfth's figure, its springings, its company, the fortune-point's address. Prison questions also show why register changes the reading: Amissio in the first "signifieth deliverance and escape out of prison"; in a prison question, losing the bond means release. The serious-answer procedure governs the class's delivery, and Exercise 7's quiz covers both this house and the eighth. Errands: **Carcer in the twelfth** and **Puer in the twelfth**, which the theft chain below will give you reason to remember.

## The third parties, and the turning of the chart

The central rule: modern geomancy manuals usually teach a complete method for third-party questions. Every house may be treated as a new first house and the wheel counted around from it, so that the father's money is the second-from-the-fourth (the fifth), the partner's illness the sixth-from-the-seventh (the twelfth), the father's father the fourth-from-the-fourth (the seventh), and so on through chains of any depth. This is called *turning the chart*. It is useful, but **it is not attested as a complete system in this course's Renaissance sources.** It is horary astrology's derived-house method, adapted to geomancy by modern books. Do not call it "the old method." The sources support narrower practices: direct third-party houses, single turns in specific cases, and chained house-reading in theft. Only the full systematic method is a modern import. Here are the four layers, with the evidence for each; Exercise 6's question map and the final practice assume you can reproduce them.

**Layer 1, the attested core: the third party has a house, and the question belongs there.** This needs no flag; it is the sources' own practice. Cattan's chapter-heads assign every person of the querent's world a seat (brothers and kin in the third, the father in the fourth, children in the fifth, servants in the sixth, the wife and the adversary in the seventh, the friend in the eleventh) and then write the third party's questions directly into that house's list. The fourth's scope gives the standard case, and also contains the next layer:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-04/significations" />

"Whether the Father be dead or shall die quickly." "Whether of the father or the sonne shall die first." The father's death (an eighth-house matter, were it the querent's) is asked and answered in the fourth, the father's own seat; and the eighth's own list does the same work from the other side, asking "Whether shall die first, the father or brother of the querant" among its death questions (Book II, ch. 8). The fifth, as the class section noted, absorbs the son's sickness, death and prison into the son's house; the tenth absorbs the mother's secrets into hers. This is Layer 1, and it is not turning. Across the chapter-heads, Cattan usually lets the person's own house answer questions about that person's sickness, death, prison, or secrets. When the worked sickness example below reads the sick father, this layer does the work: the father is the fourth, his figure is his condition, and Cattan's own fourth-house entries answer whether he dies.

**Layer 2, the single turn: a move Cattan performs twice and never names.** The fourth's list, part 6: "the questions which may be made touching the silver of the brother or sister of the querant." The brother belongs to the third house. His silver is asked *in the fourth*: the second house counted from the third, the brother's own house of substance. Cattan gives no explanation and names no rule. He simply places the brother's money in the brother's second house. Cattan's own worked case shows the same move. You met this chapter in Lesson 8 for its aspect rule and in Lesson 10 for the Trivulsio case; the course quotes it here for one further part:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-15-of-the-tryne-aspect-chapt" />

"Because the second is gone into the eight, **which is the house of the substance of the Emperour**, I saide that the king should loose much of his substance." The Emperor is the seventh: the adverse party in a question of peace between two princes. The eighth (the seventh's second) is, in Cattan's own relative part, the Emperor's house of substance: the second-from-the-seventh, used without being named as a rule. The same pattern appears in his eighth chapter-head ("the goods and substance of a friend" lodged in the house after the friend's) and in the eleventh's, where "the treasures, rents and revenewes of the king" sit one house after the king's tenth:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-11/significations" />

The mother and her treasure; the king and his rents: each treasure one house on from its owner's seat. Count the examples: the brother's silver in the fourth, the Emperor's and the friend's substance in the eighth, the mother's and the king's in the eleventh, every single one a **second-from**, the substance of a third party found one house beyond the party's own. On this evidence the course states its rule, and flags it precisely: *the single turn (taking the house after a third party's house as that party's substance, and by measured extension, the nth house from a party's house as that party's nth matter) is a generalization the course makes from a practice Cattan uses repeatedly and never names. The course names it, and says plainly that it has done so.* Used at one remove, for one step, it stands on the sources' practice; the worked sickness example will use it exactly so, taking the sixth-from-the-fourth for the father's disease while the father himself stays in his own fourth, and will tell you, when it does, which layer it is using.

**Layer 3, the chains: theft, and the springing identification.** For one class of question the sources go further than the single turn: they read a set of houses in sequence, as a connected investigation. Theft is the main case. The chapter-heads distribute it: the goods and the place of the theft in the second, the thief and his description in the seventh ("who did the robberie be of that house, or be a stranger, and of what sort or clothing he is"), the recovery and "any thing lost or stolen" in the tenth, the stolen beasts and the household's own suspects in the sixth, and the "théefe of an house" in the twelfth. Cattan's own practice assembles those houses. His robbery case reads four houses in order: the first not good; the sixth, "wherein the demaunds of servants be made," holding Cauda Draconis, "the which doth signifie robbery," whence "I judged that the servant was a theefe"; the seventh confirming; and then the second, "which signifieth the substance of the master," going into another house and identifying the same servant: "the Maister had beene robbed by the servant before his saide Maister did knowe thereof" (Book III, ch. 20). The chain uses the springing of figures as **identification**: where the goods' figure reappears, the chart identifies the relevant department. The pseudo-Agrippa source line states the rule in exactly one worked sentence, and Heydon carries it forward in the reprint; the course quotes it again here because it is now a working rule:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/index-multiplication" />

"If a question shall be propounded of the second house concerning a Thief, and the figure of the second house shall be found in the sixth, it declareth the Thief to be some of ones own houshold or servants." The second's figure springing to the sixth puts the goods' finding in the servants' house. Crowley compresses the same rule to a part in his method, the second-house figure found again in the sixth showing "the thief to be a servant in the house" (Crowley, ch. VII): three centuries, three sources, one test, and the third worked example below runs it on a live chart. Layer 3's source, then: the chains are source-direct for theft, and the springing identification is attested in both source branches; what the course adds, flagged, is only the orderly procedure for walking the chain inside Lesson 10's method.

**Layer 4, the full systematic turning: the modern import, flagged.** Last, the full turning system: every house may be treated as a possible first house, and derived houses may be counted from any seat as deeply as the question requires. It comes from horary astrology. Modern geomantic manuals imported it because it answers real questions, but this course's Renaissance geomantic sources do not attest it as a complete system. Layers 1 and 2 show the difference: where full turning would send the son's prison to the fourth-from-the-fifth, Cattan's fifth house itself asks "if the sonne be... taken prisoner" and answers from the son's own house. The course's position is this: the full system is usable as a labeled hybrid, but it may not be claimed for Cattan, Agrippa, Heydon, or Crowley. Within this course it is reserved for questions Layers 1 through 3 cannot reach, and it is always flagged in the journal. Gerard of Cremona is different. His sixth-house chapter explicitly reassigns houses when the question demands it ("give the first house of the Physician, the tenth to the sick, the seventh to his diseases, and the fourth to the medicines"). That makes full reassignment part of Gerard's system, not part of Cattan's shield method.

One paragraph of procedure before the worked examples, to fix the layers in practice. **Third-party questions run Lesson 10's method with exactly two amendments.** First, Step 1 assigns the question to the third party's house, and the party's figure becomes the quesited; or, where the question concerns a matter *of* the third party (his money, his disease), Step 1 names both the party's house and the turned house, with the layer written in the journal: *father 4th (Layer 1); his disease 9th = 6th-from-4th (Layer 2, course generalization)*. Second, Step 4's scan runs from the first house to the primary quesited as always, because the querent is still the asker and the third party is still the person asked about. The turned houses speak at Step 5 with the rest of the departments. Everything else stands unchanged: the triad keeps the end, the nine rules keep their stations, the six parts close the reading.

### The marriage asked again: Puella at both ends

The question, framed and checked: **"Will the querent obtain in marriage the woman he seeks?"** The accord of marriage, "whether it shall take effect or not": the seventh house, one sentence, done. The four Mothers, cast for this question: **Puella, Acquisitio, Fortuna Major, Conjunctio.**

*A wife: good*: the row sits directly on the demand, and it decides the end and trajectory, not whether the marriage takes effect. **Step 3.** Repetitions: Puella in 1 and 7; **Acquisitio in 2, 6, 10 and 12**, the figure of gain four times around the wheel; Cauda Draconis in 8 and 9. **Step 4, the scan.** Occupation: the seventh holds the querent's own figure, **yes**. Two source lines give the same two figures in the same two houses, and the answers cannot both stand. **Manner:** long and heavy, a Saturn sickness, with recovery by endurance rather than crisis.
**End:** gain. The Judge is Acquisitio, and the fourth house, the end of the matter, holds Via, so the disease passes.
**Timing:** slow, with changes before the stable end arrives; no date.
**Advice:** nurse him and spare him. The chart speaks ill of physic and physician for this disease, so the regimen is rest and patience. Do not let him despair at the length, because length is the manner of the cure, not the approach of death.
**Limits:** the parallel source gives death twice over, and this reading records that contrary finding on stated grounds. The reading answers this disease only; relapse, new infirmity, and later illness are other questions.

**The third-party variant: the sick father, by the layers.** Suppose the same querent had asked instead: *"Will my father recover from his disease?"* Lesson 5 promised this case to this lesson, and the layers answer it in order.

Layer 1, attested, does the main work. The question belongs with the father, in the **fourth**, and the fourth holds **Via**. Cattan's fourth-house column answers the father-register directly: Via in the fourth gives "the father is of long life" (Book II, ch. 4). Layer 1 alone, no turning at all, already answers the death-fear from the father's own house, exactly as "Whether the Father be dead or shall die quickly" is answered there.

For the *disease's particulars*, meaning its nature and length, the father's own house does not give enough detail. The course permits the single turn, Layer 2, flagged in the journal as the course's generalization. The father's sixth is the sixth-from-the-fourth, the **ninth**, which holds Tristitia, the same Saturn sorrow, long and heavy, that this chart shows wherever it shows sickness. His death-house, the eighth-from-the-fourth, is the **eleventh**, Fortuna Minor, a fortune, minor but clean, agreeing with his own house's "long life."

The scan still runs from the first to the *fourth*, because the querent asks and the father is the person asked about. Read the turned houses at Step 5 among the departments. Write the layer beside every turned house, every time. The note is short, and it preserves the boundary between what the sources practiced and what this course built.

The triad's summary concerns the matter's course and end, not yet whether the goods are recovered. **Step 3, the springings, which in this class are the investigation.** Carcer in 2 and **6**; Conjunctio in 4 and 10; Fortuna Minor in 8 and 9; Puer in 11 and 12. For this register: "**the theefe is in the house, and the thing is therein hidden**," and, for recovery, "the theefe or fugitive will not be found." Lock what remains. The limits: this reading answers whether the goods are recovered, and answers no. It does not name the thief, decide whether to accuse, or decide whether to dismiss anyone. The parallel source's contrary entry is recorded as the minority finding.
