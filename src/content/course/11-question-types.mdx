This lesson applies the method to the main kinds of questions the old books treat: money, marriage, sickness, suits, journeys, offices, lost things, absent people, death, prison, and hidden enemies. Each class has its own houses and common mistakes. It also handles the cases Lesson 5 set aside: multi-house questions, another person's sickness, theft, and third-party questions.

The method does not change: Step 0 through Step 6, with the reading rules in their places. What changes is the kind of question. The new discipline is reading Cattan's Book II as finding inside the method, not as a list of ready-made answers.

Many real questions are indirect: a sick father, a friend's debt, a brother's money, a thief who may be a servant. The older sources do not give one general rule for all of these. They give narrower practices. This lesson teaches those practices and tells you when the course is making a limited generalization.

## Placement entries as findings

The lesson's new discipline is placement lookup. Cattan's Book II gives each house a list of questions, then sixteen figure-in-house entries. The mistake is to look up the quesited figure, copy the matching sentence, and call it the answer. That is not a reading. The rule is: **the placement entry is finding, not answer; it enters at Step 5, applies to the quesited house's own department, and is used by the reading rules like everything else.** Whether the thing reaches the asker still belongs to the perfection scan (Rule 1). The end still belongs to the triad (Rule 2). The Book II entry tells how the matter stands in its own house.

Use two corollaries. First, **the entry is a column, not a sentence**: a full entry answers many demands, and the question selects the relevant part. Second, **the cross-references are method**: Cattan often makes one house depend on another (Tristitia in the sixth kills the patient only "so that the viij. doe consent"), which shows that entries must be read with the rest of the chart.

## The classes, one by one

The map below gives each class its primary house, secondary houses, usual geometry, and placement-lookup errands at the end of each section. One structural point first: by the wheel's fixed geometry, every class has a default relation between the first house and the house of the matter. The second makes no aspect to the first; the fifth and ninth behold it by trine, the tenth by square, the eleventh by sextile; the sixth, eighth, and twelfth have no regard to it at all. This is the course's synthesis from Lesson 8's geometry, and it is used in practice.

### Substance and things lost: the second house

The second is "properly the house of gaine and profite": gain to come and how it comes ("with the travaile and sweate of his bodie, or by succession, or by guift"), movable goods, charges and expenses, the promise of a lord, the messenger's news, and, for the theft sequence, "the place where the thing lost was stolen" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 2, quoted in full in Lesson 5).

The geometry is the adjacent pair: no aspect to the first is possible, but the 1·2 company pair is the wheel's own bond between a person and their money, and an empty pair is itself a finding. The secondary houses are the **tenth** ("any thing lost or stolen"), the **fourth** (hidden treasure), and the **eighth** (other people's goods). In every money question also check the **Part of Fortune**: "Consider also always in money questions where the Part of Fortune falls" (Crowley, ch. VII). Errands: **Fortuna Major in the second**, **Amissio in the second**, then **Carcer in the second**, held for the theft sequence.

### Children and pleasures: the fifth house

The fifth, "the good fortune," owns the child (birth, legitimacy, nature), the woman with child and the child's sex, promises, messengers, friends, the earth's fruits, and "all pleasures, as to kisse, coll, sing, daunce, banquet" (Book II, ch. 5). Its usual aspect to the first is the trine. Note one chapter-head entry for the third-party section below: §12 asks "If the sonne be sicke, dead, or taken prisoner": three departments of matter absorbed into the son's *own* house. The triad's tables carry this class's row ("woman with child," with answers as particular as *a sonne*). Errands: **Puella in the fifth** and **Rubeus in the fifth**.

### Sickness: the sixth house

This class teaches the method most, because the houses give the least direct help. Here is the house's own scope:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-06/significations" />

Sickness and servants share a house; subjection is the common thread. The sickness parts are the most procedurally demanding in Book II: the disease's length, its humour, the patient's danger, the physician's skill, and whether to take physic.

The geometry comes first: the first and sixth hold no regard, and neither do the first and eighth. The sick man cannot behold his disease or his death, so this class cannot be read by the ordinary joining of significators. Read sickness as a state of affairs, as Cattan read the Novelaire case: the figures in the triangle of the first, sixth, and eighth, their conditions read with their cross-references, the triad's row, and the end-houses.

Use the triangle as part of the sickness method. Cattan's entries build it from inside, with the cross-references quoted above; Gerard states it from outside: "see if *Saturn,* or *Mars,* or the *Dragons Tail* be in the first... but if good Planets shall be in the first, sixth and eighth, and likewise in the Angles... then the sick person shall live and recover his health" (Gerard, *Of Astronomical Geomancy* (trans. Turner, 1655), the sixth house). Gerard's tools are planets and lords, which belong to Lesson 12; here the course takes only the triangle. The secondary houses are the **tenth** for the physician, the **fourth** and the triad for the end, and the **twelfth** for the incurable kind. Errands: **Tristitia in the sixth**, **Conjunctio in the sixth**, **Caput Draconis in the eighth**, **Puer in the eighth**; read each whole, and mark every conditional part.

### Love, marriage, suits and theft: the seventh house

The seventh owns the contrary party: the enemy, lawsuit, thief, fugitive, marriage partner, lover, war, peace, agreement, and whether the agreement endures (Book II, ch. 7, quoted in Lesson 5). Its usual aspect is opposition, because the asker and the other party face one another by construction. Ask whether that opposition has kinship: Chart C's bare opposition was hostile; the worked example below has the same figure on both sides, which is what separates a lawsuit from a marriage. Secondary houses: the **eighth** as the seventh's companion, the **fifth** for the pleasures of love, the **tenth** for judges in suits by Lesson 10's Clermont rule, and, in theft, the sequence of second, seventh, tenth, and sixth. For marriage, the precedents are Chart A and Chart B; for suits, Chart C. Errands: read **Puella in the seventh** and **Tristitia in the seventh** whole, and list which parts answer which demands in the chapter-head.

### Death and inheritance: the eighth house

"The house of death" owns the death of any person, its when and manner, the inheritance, and a mixed set the older texts file here: the absent person, the other party's substance, fear, the thing given in trust, the usurer's gain (Book II, ch. 8). No regard joins the first to the eighth, so death is read, like sickness, as a state of affairs, and most death questions arrive as sickness questions. What belongs especially to the eighth is manner and aftermath: a good death or ill, the estate, the absent person's condition. This is the first serious class; Exercise 6 is its quiz and Exercise 2 delivers its answer in full. Errands: **Caput Draconis in the eighth** and **Puer in the eighth**.

### Journeys: the ninth house

The ninth owns the long voyage ("long or shorte, good and profitable, and whether it be as good by Sea as by land"), ships, ports and shipwreck, priests, scholars, dreams, and "if the common brute be true or not" (Book II, ch. 9). Its usual aspect to the first is the trine, so the class's yes answers often arrive by the aspect line, the weakest join, which suits a question that asks not for a meeting but a going. Secondary houses: the **seventh** for the route, the **fourth** for the ship's safe arrival, the **second** and **eighth** for profit at home and abroad. Errand: read **Fortuna Minor in the ninth** in Cattan.

### Office and honour: the tenth house

The tenth owns honour, praise, kings, princes, magistrates, decrees, office, the king's justice, and royal journeys. It also owns three matters a modern reader may not expect: the **mother** ("Upon the mother, grandmother, wife or Lemman"), the **physician** "and his ordinance," and "any thing lost or stolen" (Book II, ch. 10, quoted in Lesson 5). Its usual aspect to the first is a square, so office questions usually show effort or resistance. Chart D is the worked precedent: occupation by a good figure, the triad's *worship: good*, and the fortune-point qualifying the revenues. Exercise 4 is its opposite case. In suits, the tenth is the judges' house; in sickness, the physician's house; in theft, the recovery house. The question decides which office the tenth is serving. Errand: read **Fortuna Major in the tenth** in Cattan; the placement still does not decide whether this querent gets the office.

### Friends and hopes: the eleventh house

"The good Angle": the friend and the hope ("the thing ye love, and whereof ye hope to have consolation, ayde and profite"), the faithful or traitorous friend, the year's plenty and dearth, and a part to hold for the third-party section: "over the mother and her treasure, and of all the treasures, rents and revenewes of the king" (Book II, ch. 11). The usual sextile to the first sets the class's expectations: gentle yes answers, rarely catastrophic denials. The real trap is the division between the eleventh's friend (a support) and the seventh's (a counterpart in a two-party bond), which Exercise 5 drills. Errand: **Fortuna Major in the eleventh** and **Tristitia in the eleventh**.

### Prison and hidden enemies: the twelfth house

The last class, and the second serious one. The twelfth owns the prison and the prisoner ("whether the prisoner shall parte out of prison, and when"), desolation, the incurable sickness, "a privie ennemy rather then upon an open," the traitor and "théefe of an house," banishment, debts, and the great beasts, "oxen, cammels, dromodaries... horses, mules, asses" (Book II, ch. 12). The twelfth has no regard to the first, so a privy-enemy question is read almost wholly by department. Prison questions show why register changes the reading: Amissio in the first "signifieth deliverance and escape out of prison"; losing the bond means release. Errands: **Carcer in the twelfth** and **Puer in the twelfth**.

## Third parties and counted houses

Modern geomancy books often teach a broad rule for third-party questions: treat any house as a new first house and count every related matter from it. That method is useful, but this course does not teach it as Renaissance shield method. The sources used here show narrower practices. Use those practices first, and mark any course generalization in the written reading.

**First use the person's own house.** Cattan's chapter-heads assign people in the querent's world to houses, then put many questions about those people directly in those houses. The fourth gives the standard case:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-04/significations" />

"Whether the Father be dead or shall die quickly." If the father were the querent, death would belong to the eighth. Here the question is asked in the fourth, the father's own house. The fifth likewise includes the son's sickness, death, and prison, and the tenth includes the mother's secrets. So begin with the person's own house before counting from it.

**Use one counted house when the matter is a thing of that person.** The fourth's list, part 6, includes "the questions which may be made touching the silver of the brother or sister of the querant." The brother belongs to the third house. His silver is asked in the fourth: the second house counted from the third, the brother's own house of substance. Cattan gives no explanation and names no rule. He simply places the brother's money in the brother's second house. Cattan's own worked case shows the same move. You met this chapter in Lesson 8 for its aspect rule and in Lesson 10 for the Trivulsio case; the course quotes it here for one further part:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-15-of-the-tryne-aspect-chapt" />

"Because the second is gone into the eight, **which is the house of the substance of the Emperour**, I saide that the king should loose much of his substance." The Emperor is the seventh, so the eighth is the second-from-the-seventh, the Emperor's house of substance, used without being named as a rule. The same pattern appears in the eighth chapter-head ("the goods and substance of a friend") and in the eleventh's, where "the treasures, rents and revenewes of the king" sit one house after the king's tenth:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-11/significations" />

Each treasure sits one house on from its owner's seat: the brother's silver in the fourth, the Emperor's and the friend's substance in the eighth, the mother's and the king's in the eleventh. Each is a **second-from**. The course therefore allows one counted house from a person's own house, with a method note: *course generalization from Cattan's repeated second-from practice*. Use it for one remove only. Do not turn it into a complete counted-house system.

**Use the theft sequence only for theft-style questions.** For one class the sources read several houses as a connected investigation. Theft is the main case: the goods and the place of the theft in the second, the thief and his description in the seventh, the recovery in the tenth, the household suspects in the sixth, and the "théefe of an house" in the twelfth. Cattan's robbery case assembles those houses in order, judging "that the servant was a theefe" from Cauda Draconis in the sixth and confirming from the seventh and the second (Book III, ch. 20). The sequence uses springing as **identification**: where the goods' figure reappears, that repeated figure marks a relevant department. The supporting rule is short:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/index-multiplication" />

"If a question shall be propounded of the second house concerning a Thief, and the figure of the second house shall be found in the sixth, it declareth the Thief to be some of ones own houshold or servants." Crowley compresses the same rule, the second-house figure in the sixth showing "the thief to be a servant in the house" (ch. VII). The theft sequence is supported for theft. Do not make it a general rule for unrelated questions.

**Do not import a complete turned-house method into the shield method.** A system in which every house can become a new first house comes from horary astrology and later geomancy manuals. It is not shown as a complete rule in the Renaissance shield sources used here. Where that system would send the son's prison to the fourth-from-the-fifth, Cattan's fifth house itself asks "if the sonne be... taken prisoner." If a question requires repeated counted houses, narrow the question or cast a separate question. Gerard is different: his sixth-house chapter explicitly reassigns houses for the physician's question. That belongs to Gerard's astrological method, not to Cattan's shield method.

For third-party questions, keep Lesson 10's method with two changes. First, Step 1 names the person's own house as the primary house. If the question needs one counted house, write both houses and the method note: *father 4th; his disease 9th = sixth from the fourth (course generalization)*. Second, Step 4 scans from the first house to the person's own house, because the querent is still the asker and that person is the one asked about. Counted houses are Step 5 findings. Everything else stays the same: the triad keeps the end, the reading rules keep their places, and the answer says what the chart supports.

### The marriage asked again: Puella at both ends

The question, framed and checked: **"Will the querent obtain in marriage the woman he seeks?"** The accord of marriage, "whether it shall take effect or not": the seventh house, one sentence, done. The four Mothers, cast for this question: **Puella, Acquisitio, Fortuna Major, Conjunctio.**

The selection: the parts that answer the marriage register are "it is good for a woman, a leaman, marriage, and play"; "the agreement shall be made and last long"; "in all the demaundes contayned in this house this figure is good but for warre." The manner: by likeness, her figure is his figure, the parties share figure, planet, and element, and the household's gain is repeated around the wheel. The end: the Greater Fortune, the triad's *a wife: good* on the question's own row, and the fourth house, the end of the matter, holding Conjunctio, the figure of joining itself: the matter ends in the union it asks for. The timing: sure and not swift, because the chart is stable; no date. The advice: marry, and settle the matter of her goods *before* the wedding, openly, for the chart's one unfavorable address is the eighth, her substance, where its fortune sits on the figure of loss. The limits: this reading answers the marriage and prices its purse; it does not answer the keeping; and the fidelity caution should be asked as its own question, on its own day, with its own cast.

### The sick man: the triangle with no aspect in it

The question: **"Shall the sick man recover of this disease, or die thereof?"** The querent is the sick man himself; the sickness is the sixth's matter; the triangle is the class's: first, sixth, eighth, with no regard joining any of them. The four Mothers: **Laetitia, Carcer, Tristitia, Via.**

**The derivation, verified.** Mothers: Laetitia [1,2,2,2], Carcer [1,2,2,1], Tristitia [2,2,2,1], Via [1,1,1,1]. Daughters: first lines 1,1,2,1 gives **Puer**; second lines 2,2,2,1 gives **Tristitia**; third lines 2,2,2,1 gives **Tristitia**; fourth lines 2,1,1,1 gives **Caput Draconis**. Nieces: Laetitia and Carcer, 1+1 two; 2+2 two; 2+2 two; 2+1 one: **Tristitia**. Tristitia and Via, 2+1 one; 2+1 one; 2+1 one; 1+1 two: **Cauda Draconis**. Puer and Tristitia, 1+2 one; 1+2 one; 2+2 two; 1+1 two: **Fortuna Minor**. Tristitia and Caput Draconis, 2+2 two; 2+1 one; 2+1 one; 1+1 two: **Conjunctio**. Right Witness, Tristitia and Cauda Draconis, 2+1 one; 2+1 one; 2+1 one; 1+2 one: **Via**, four points. Left Witness, Fortuna Minor and Conjunctio, 1+2 one; 1+1 two; 2+1 one; 2+2 two: **Amissio**, six points. Judge, Via and Amissio: 1+1 two; 1+2 one; 1+1 two; 1+2 one: **Acquisitio**, six points. Parity: Witnesses four and six, both even, one parity; Judge even. True.

**Step 0.** Laetitia rises, so the chart proceeds.

**Step 1.** Recovery or death of the querent's own disease: the sixth. The querent is **Laetitia (1st)**. The quesited is **Tristitia (6th)**. The death-house, the triangle's third corner, holds **Caput Draconis (8th)**.

**Step 2.** The triad is Via, Amissio, Judge Acquisitio: the matter begins in change, continues through Loss, and ends in Gain. The table's row for this question's matter is *sickenes: health*. Hold it in its role.

**Step 3.** Repetitions: **Tristitia in 3, 6, 7 and 9**, the disease's figure four times around the wheel. Nothing else doubles.

**Step 4, the scan for this class.** Occupation: no. Conjunction: no. Company: the 5·6 pair joins nothing. Aspect: no regard joins the first to the sixth or eighth in any possible chart. Since no mode can join the first to the sixth or eighth, the scan gives no yes-or-no result for recovery. Read sickness through the triangle, as Cattan read the Novelaire case: the first, sixth, and eighth houses, the triad in its role, and the end-houses, with every finding read as finding about *recovery or death*.

**Step 5, the triangle.** The patient first, by Gerard's synthesis: no Saturn, Mars, or Tail in the first, but Jupiter's Laetitia, matching the recovery part: "if good Planets shall be in the first, sixth and eighth... the sick person shall live." Now the disease's own house, the quesited's whole column:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-06/tristitia" />

The part for this register: "the sicke person shall die **so that the viij. doe consent**." A death finding, *conditional on the eighth*: the entry's own cross-reference, doing exactly what the earlier rule promised. So consult the eighth:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-08/caput-draconis" />

"The person sicke shall be long sicke, **but he shall have no other disease**... for all the demaundes which may be propounded in this house this figure is good." The eighth does not consent. The sixth's death-part fails by its own terms, because Cattan's entry tells the reader to consult the eighth. The rest of the chart also supports recovery: the row reads *health*, the Judge is Acquisitio, and the patient's seat is good. The rest of the review: the disease is stable and quadrupled, so long though not fatal; the column is unfavorable about physic; no way of the point; the Part of Fortune falls in the twelfth on Conjunctio, mixed and tied to confinement. Timing: long.

**Step 6, the written answer.**

**Answer:** he will recover; the disease is not unto death. The sixth's death-part fails because the eighth does not consent, the death-house is favorable, and the table row reads *health*.
**Manner:** long and heavy, a Saturn sickness, with recovery by endurance rather than crisis.
**End:** gain. The Judge is Acquisitio, and the fourth house, the end of the matter, holds Via, so the disease passes.
**Timing:** slow, with changes before the stable end arrives; no date.
**Advice:** nurse him and spare him. The sixth-house entry is unfavorable about physic and physician for this disease, so the regimen is rest and patience. Do not let him despair at the length, because length is the manner of the cure, not the approach of death.
**Limits:** the reading answers this disease only; relapse, new infirmity, and later illness are other questions.

**Third-party case: the sick father.** Suppose the same querent had asked instead: *"Will my father recover from his disease?"* Lesson 5 promised this case to this lesson.

The father's own house does the main work. The question belongs in the **fourth**, which holds **Via**, and Cattan's fourth-house column answers directly: "the father is of long life" (Book II, ch. 4). For the disease's particulars, the course permits one counted house, marked as course generalization: the father's sixth is the **ninth** (Tristitia, the same long Saturn sickness this chart shows everywhere), and his death-house is the **eleventh** (Fortuna Minor, agreeing with "long life"). The scan still runs from the first to the **fourth**, because the querent asks about the father. The counted houses enter at Step 5, each with its method note.

### The theft sequence: the goods, the thief, the recovery, the servant test

The question, in the compound form real querents use: **"The querent's goods are stolen out of his house: who did the robbery, and will he get his own goods back?"** Two registers in one demand, the multi-house case Lesson 5 promised, read by the theft sequence: the second for the goods, the seventh for the thief, the tenth for the recovery, the sixth for the servant test. The primary house for the recovery question (*will he get them back*) is the second, the goods being the matter. The four Mothers: **Acquisitio, Carcer, Populus, Conjunctio.**

**Step 0.** Acquisitio rises, proceeds. **Step 1.** The recovery question is the second house; querent **Acquisitio (1st)**, quesited **Carcer (2nd)**. The triad's summary concerns the matter's course and end. **Step 3, the springings, which in this class are the investigation.** Carcer in 2 and **6**; Conjunctio in 4 and 10; Fortuna Minor in 8 and 9; Puer in 11 and 12. The main springing: **the second's figure is found in the sixth**, the goods' figure standing in the servants' house, joined by a related trine. The servant test is positive under Cattan's theft rule. **Step 4, the scan.** Occupation: no. Conjunction: no. Company: the 1·2 pair shares nothing. Aspect: adjacent houses hold no regard. **Perfection is denied by every mode: the goods are not restored.** **Step 5, the theft houses.** The goods' column first, Cattan on Carcer in the second:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-02/carcer" />

"Gaine by things hidden in the earth... things blacke" describes the goods as kept and concealed. The thief's house next, the seventh, **Tristitia**:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-07/tristitia" />

"**The theefe is in the house, and the thing is therein hidden**," and, for recovery, "the theefe or fugitive will not be found." The seventh supports the sixth's servant finding. The twelfth holds Puer, doubled with the eleventh: *young, near, and trusted* is as far as the finding goes, and naming a person is no part of the reading. The recovery station, the tenth, holds **Conjunctio** sprung from the **fourth**, the house of hidden treasure: recovery, if any, comes by searching within. The **way of the point** and the **Part of Fortune** both end in **house 2, on Carcer**: everything concentrates on the goods, kept.

**Step 6, the written answer.** **Denied: the goods are not restored to him.** The manner: no robbery from outside; the finding points to someone in the household, and the thing itself is still hidden within the house. The end: loss, with Judge Amissio and the table row *not found*; but not ruin, because the householder's own seat has Acquisitio, angular and stable. The timing: nothing important moves; waiting does not turn this denial into recovery. The advice: stop searching abroad, search the house quietly, and do not name or accuse without proof. Lock what remains. The limits: this reading answers whether the goods are recovered, and answers no. It does not name the thief, decide whether to accuse, or decide whether to dismiss anyone.
