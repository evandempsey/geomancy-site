This lesson applies the method to the main kinds of questions the old books treat: money, marriage, sickness, suits, journeys, offices, lost things, absent people, death, prison, and hidden enemies. Each class has its own houses and common mistakes. It also handles the cases Lesson 5 set aside: multi-house questions, another person's sickness, theft, and the *turning* of the chart, by which a third party's matter is found from that third party's house.

The method does not change: Step 0 through Step 6, the nine rules, and the six parts. What changes is the kind of question. The new discipline is reading Cattan's Book II as finding inside the method, not as a list of ready-made answers.

Many real questions are indirect: a sick father, a friend's debt, a brother's money, a thief who may be a servant. Turning the chart handles these cases. The sources support some of it plainly, some of it by practice, and some later manuals extend it beyond the Renaissance source. This lesson separates those layers so a reading can say what support each turn has.

## Placement entries as findings

The lesson's new discipline is placement lookup. Cattan's Book II gives each house a list of questions, then sixteen figure-in-house entries. The mistake is to look up the quesited figure, copy the matching sentence, and call it the answer. That is not a reading. The rule is: **the placement entry is finding, not answer; it enters at Step 5, speaks for the quesited house's own department, and is weighed by the nine rules like everything else.** Whether the thing reaches the asker still belongs to the perfection scan (Rule 1). The end still belongs to the triad (Rule 2). The Book II entry tells how the matter stands in its own house.

Use three corollaries. First, **the entry is a column, not a sentence**: a full entry answers many demands, and the question selects the relevant part. Second, **the cross-references are method**: Cattan often makes one house depend on another (Tristitia in the sixth kills the patient only "so that the viij. doe consent"), which shows the entries are finding to be resolved by the chart. Third, **know the source lines**: Cattan is the primary source, fuller and more conditional; the pseudo-Agrippa gives shorter entries; Heydon usually reprints the pseudo-Agrippa, so he is not a third independent source.

## The classes, one by one

The map below gives each class its primary house, secondary houses, usual geometry, and parallel sources, with placement-lookup errands at the end of each section. One structural point first: by the wheel's fixed geometry, every class has a default relation between the first house and the house of the matter. The second makes no aspect to the first; the fifth and ninth behold it by trine, the tenth by square, the eleventh by sextile; the sixth, eighth, and twelfth have no regard to it at all. This is the course's synthesis from Lesson 8's geometry, and it is used in practice.

### Substance and things lost: the second house

The second is "properly the house of gaine and profite": gain to come and how it comes ("with the travaile and sweate of his bodie, or by succession, or by guift"), movable goods, charges and expenses, the promise of a lord, the messenger's news, and, for the theft chain, "the place where the thing lost was stolen" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book II, ch. 2, quoted in full in Lesson 5).

The geometry is the adjacent pair: no aspect to the first is possible, but the 1·2 company pair is the wheel's own bond between a person and their money, and an empty pair is itself a finding. The secondary houses are the **tenth** ("any thing lost or stolen"), the **fourth** (hidden treasure), and the **eighth** (other people's goods). In every money question also check the **Part of Fortune**: "Consider also always in money questions where the Part of Fortune falls" (Crowley, ch. VII). Errands: **Fortuna Major in the second**, **Amissio in the second**, then **Carcer in the second**, held for the theft chain; Exercise 5 shows the two sources reading the last in opposite directions.

### Children and pleasures: the fifth house

The fifth, "the good fortune," owns the child (birth, legitimacy, nature), the woman with child and the child's sex, promises, messengers, friends, the earth's fruits, and "all pleasures, as to kisse, coll, sing, daunce, banquet" (Book II, ch. 5). Its native channel is the trine. Note one chapter-head entry for the turning section below: §12 asks "If the sonne be sicke, dead, or taken prisoner": three departments of matter absorbed into the son's *own* house. The triad's tables carry this class's row ("woman with child," with answers as particular as *a sonne*). Errands: **Puella in the fifth** and **Rubeus in the fifth**.

### Sickness: the sixth house

This class teaches the method most, because the houses give the least direct help. Here is the house's own scope:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-06/significations" />

Sickness and servants share a house; subjection is the common thread. The sickness parts are the most procedurally demanding in Book II: the disease's length, its humour, the patient's danger, the physician's skill, and whether to take physic.

The geometry comes first: the first and sixth hold no regard, and neither do the first and eighth. The sick man cannot behold his disease or his death, so this class cannot be read by the ordinary joining of significators. Read sickness as a state of affairs, as Cattan read the Novelaire case: the figures in the triangle of the first, sixth, and eighth, their conditions resolved against each other, the triad's row, and the end-houses.

The triangle has independent source support. Cattan's entries build it from inside, with the cross-references quoted above; Gerard states it from outside: "see if *Saturn,* or *Mars,* or the *Dragons Tail* be in the first... but if good Planets shall be in the first, sixth and eighth, and likewise in the Angles... then the sick person shall live and recover his health" (Gerard, *Of Astronomical Geomancy* (trans. Turner, 1655), the sixth house). Gerard's tools are planets and lords, which belong to Lesson 12; here the course takes only the triangle. The secondary houses are the **tenth** for the physician, the **fourth** and the triad for the end, and the **twelfth** for the incurable kind. Errands: **Tristitia in the sixth**, **Conjunctio in the sixth**, **Caput Draconis in the eighth**, **Puer in the eighth**; read each whole, and mark every conditional part.

### Love, marriage, suits and theft: the seventh house

The seventh owns the contrary party: the enemy, lawsuit, thief, fugitive, marriage partner, lover, war, peace, agreement, and whether the agreement endures (Book II, ch. 7, quoted in Lesson 5). Its native aspect is opposition, because the asker and the other party face one another by construction. Ask whether that opposition has kinship: Chart C's bare opposition was hostile; the worked example below has the same figure on both sides, which is what separates a lawsuit from a marriage. Secondary houses: the **eighth** as the seventh's companion, the **fifth** for the pleasures of love, the **tenth** for judges in suits by Lesson 10's Clermont rule, and, in theft, the chain of second, seventh, tenth, and sixth. For marriage, the precedents are Chart A and Chart B; for suits, Chart C. Errands: read **Puella in the seventh** and **Tristitia in the seventh** whole, and list which parts answer which demands in the chapter-head.

### Death and inheritance: the eighth house

"The house of death" owns the death of any person, its when and manner, the inheritance, and a mixed set the older texts file here: the absent person, the other party's substance, fear, the thing given in trust, the usurer's gain (Book II, ch. 8). No regard joins the first to the eighth, so death is read, like sickness, as a state of affairs, and most death questions arrive as sickness questions. What belongs especially to the eighth is manner and aftermath: a good death or ill, the estate, the absent person's condition. This is the first serious class; Exercise 7 is its quiz and Exercise 2 delivers its answer in full. Errands: **Caput Draconis in the eighth** against **Puer in the eighth**, then the same two placements in the pseudo-Agrippa, which give the lesson's clearest source disagreement.

### Journeys: the ninth house

The ninth owns the long voyage ("long or shorte, good and profitable, and whether it be as good by Sea as by land"), ships, ports and shipwreck, priests, scholars, dreams, and "if the common brute be true or not" (Book II, ch. 9). The native channel is the trine, so the class's yes answers often arrive by the aspect line, the weakest join, which suits a question that asks not for a meeting but a going. Secondary houses: the **seventh** for the route, the **fourth** for the ship's safe arrival, the **second** and **eighth** for profit at home and abroad. Errands: **Fortuna Minor in the ninth** in both Cattan and the pseudo-Agrippa; the two entries pull against each other, and Exercise 3's chart hangs on reconciling them.

### Office and honour: the tenth house

The tenth owns honour, praise, kings, princes, magistrates, decrees, office, the king's justice, and royal journeys. It also owns three matters a modern reader may not expect: the **mother** ("Upon the mother, grandmother, wife or Lemman"), the **physician** "and his ordinance," and "any thing lost or stolen" (Book II, ch. 10, quoted in Lesson 5). Its native aspect to the first is a square, so office questions usually show effort or resistance. Chart D is the worked precedent: occupation by a good figure, the triad's *worship: good*, and the fortune-point qualifying the revenues. Exercise 4 is its opposite case. In suits, the tenth is the judges' house; in sickness, the physician's house; in theft, the recovery house. The question decides which office the tenth is serving. Errand: read **Fortuna Major in the tenth** in both Cattan and Agrippa, and notice what that good placement can and cannot promise.

### Friends and hopes: the eleventh house

"The good Angle": the friend and the hope ("the thing ye love, and whereof ye hope to have consolation, ayde and profite"), the faithful or traitorous friend, the year's plenty and dearth, and a part to hold for the turning section: "over the mother and her treasure, and of all the treasures, rents and revenewes of the king" (Book II, ch. 11). The native sextile sets the class's expectations: gentle yes answers, rarely catastrophic denials. The real trap is the division between the eleventh's friend (a support) and the seventh's (a counterpart in a two-party bond), which Exercise 6 drills. Errand: **Fortuna Major in the eleventh** and **Tristitia in the eleventh**.

### Prison and hidden enemies: the twelfth house

The last class, and the second serious one. The twelfth owns the prison and the prisoner ("whether the prisoner shall parte out of prison, and when"), desolation, the incurable sickness, "a privie ennemy rather then upon an open," the traitor and "théefe of an house," banishment, debts, and the great beasts, "oxen, cammels, dromodaries... horses, mules, asses" (Book II, ch. 12). The twelfth has no regard to the first, so a privy-enemy question is read almost wholly by department. Prison questions show why register changes the reading: Amissio in the first "signifieth deliverance and escape out of prison"; losing the bond means release. Errands: **Carcer in the twelfth** and **Puer in the twelfth**.

## The third parties, and the turning of the chart

The central rule: modern geomancy manuals usually teach a complete method for third-party questions. Every house may be treated as a new first house and the wheel counted around from it, so that the father's money is the second-from-the-fourth (the fifth), the partner's illness the sixth-from-the-seventh (the twelfth), the father's father the fourth-from-the-fourth (the seventh), and so on through chains of any depth. This is called *turning the chart*. It is useful, but **it is not attested as a complete system in this course's Renaissance sources.** It is horary astrology's derived-house method, adapted to geomancy by modern books. Do not call it "the old method." The sources support narrower practices: direct third-party houses, single turns in specific cases, and chained house-reading in theft. Only the full systematic method is a modern import. Here are the four layers, with the evidence for each; Exercise 6's question map and the final practice assume you can reproduce them.

**Layer 1, the attested core: the third party has a house, and the question belongs there.** This is the sources' own practice. Cattan's chapter-heads assign every person of the querent's world a seat, and write the third party's questions directly into that house's list. The fourth's scope gives the standard case, and also contains the next layer:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-04/significations" />

"Whether the Father be dead or shall die quickly." The father's death (an eighth-house matter, were it the querent's) is asked and answered in the fourth, the father's own seat; the fifth likewise absorbs the son's sickness, death and prison, and the tenth the mother's secrets. This is Layer 1, and it is not turning: the person's own house answers questions about that person. When the worked sickness example reads the sick father, this layer does the work.

**Layer 2, the single turn: a move Cattan performs twice and never names.** The fourth's list, part 6: "the questions which may be made touching the silver of the brother or sister of the querant." The brother belongs to the third house. His silver is asked *in the fourth*: the second house counted from the third, the brother's own house of substance. Cattan gives no explanation and names no rule. He simply places the brother's money in the brother's second house. Cattan's own worked case shows the same move. You met this chapter in Lesson 8 for its aspect rule and in Lesson 10 for the Trivulsio case; the course quotes it here for one further part:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-15-of-the-tryne-aspect-chapt" />

"Because the second is gone into the eight, **which is the house of the substance of the Emperour**, I saide that the king should loose much of his substance." The Emperor is the seventh, so the eighth is the second-from-the-seventh, the Emperor's house of substance, used without being named as a rule. The same pattern appears in the eighth chapter-head ("the goods and substance of a friend") and in the eleventh's, where "the treasures, rents and revenewes of the king" sit one house after the king's tenth:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-11/significations" />

Each treasure sits one house on from its owner's seat: the brother's silver in the fourth, the Emperor's and the friend's substance in the eighth, the mother's and the king's in the eleventh, every one a **second-from**. On this evidence the course states its rule, flagged: *the single turn (the nth house from a party's house as that party's nth matter) is a generalization the course makes from a practice Cattan uses repeatedly and never names.* Used at one remove, for one step, it stands on the sources' practice; the worked sickness example uses it exactly so.

**Layer 3, the chains: theft, and the springing identification.** For one class the sources read a set of houses in sequence, as a connected investigation. Theft is the main case: the goods and the place of the theft in the second, the thief and his description in the seventh, the recovery in the tenth, the household suspects in the sixth, the "théefe of an house" in the twelfth. Cattan's robbery case assembles those houses in order, judging "that the servant was a theefe" from Cauda Draconis in the sixth and confirming from the seventh and the second (Book III, ch. 20). The chain uses the springing of figures as **identification**: where the goods' figure reappears, the chart identifies the relevant department. The pseudo-Agrippa states the rule in one worked sentence, and Heydon carries it forward:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/index-multiplication" />

"If a question shall be propounded of the second house concerning a Thief, and the figure of the second house shall be found in the sixth, it declareth the Thief to be some of ones own houshold or servants." Crowley compresses the same rule, the second-house figure in the sixth showing "the thief to be a servant in the house" (ch. VII): three centuries, one test, and the third worked example runs it on a live chart. The chains are source-direct for theft; what the course adds, flagged, is only the orderly procedure for walking them.

**Layer 4, the full systematic turning: the modern import, flagged.** Last, the full system: every house treated as a possible first house, derived houses counted from any seat, as deeply as the question requires. It comes from horary astrology, and modern manuals imported it because it answers real questions, but this course's Renaissance sources do not attest it as a complete system: where full turning would send the son's prison to the fourth-from-the-fifth, Cattan's fifth house itself asks "if the sonne be... taken prisoner." The full system is usable as a labeled hybrid, reserved for questions Layers 1 through 3 cannot reach, and always flagged. Gerard is different: his sixth-house chapter explicitly reassigns houses ("give the first house of the Physician, the tenth to the sick, the seventh to his diseases, and the fourth to the medicines"), so full reassignment is part of Gerard's system, not of Cattan's.

One paragraph of procedure before the worked examples, to fix the layers in practice. **Third-party questions run Lesson 10's method with exactly two amendments.** First, Step 1 assigns the question to the third party's house, and the party's figure becomes the quesited; or, where the question concerns a matter *of* the third party (his money, his disease), Step 1 names both the party's house and the turned house, with the layer written down: *father 4th (Layer 1); his disease 9th = 6th-from-4th (Layer 2, course generalization)*. Second, Step 4's scan runs from the first house to the primary quesited as always, because the querent is still the asker and the third party is still the person asked about. The turned houses speak at Step 5 with the rest of the departments. Everything else stands unchanged: the triad keeps the end, the nine rules keep their stations, the six parts close the reading.

### The marriage asked again: Puella at both ends

The question, framed and checked: **"Will the querent obtain in marriage the woman he seeks?"** The accord of marriage, "whether it shall take effect or not": the seventh house, one sentence, done. The four Mothers, cast for this question: **Puella, Acquisitio, Fortuna Major, Conjunctio.**

**The derivation, verified.** Mothers: Puella [1,2,1,1], Acquisitio [2,1,2,1], Fortuna Major [2,2,1,1], Conjunctio [2,1,1,2]. Nieces: Puella and Acquisitio, 1+2 odd, one; 2+1 one; 1+2 one; 1+1 even, two: **Cauda Draconis**. Fortuna Major and Conjunctio, 2+2 two; 2+1 one; 1+1 two; 1+2 one: **Acquisitio**. The pseudo-Agrippa gives the same positive marriage reading ("a wife fair, beautiful and pleasant, leading a peaceable conversation with her husband") and adds the same caution: "notwithstanding one that shall burn much with lust." The timing: sure and not swift, because the chart is stable; no date. The advice: marry, and settle the matter of her goods *before* the wedding, openly, for the chart's one unfavorable address is the eighth, her substance, where its fortune sits on the figure of loss. The limits: this reading answers the marriage and prices its purse; it does not answer the keeping; and the fidelity caution should be asked as its own question, on its own day, with its own cast.

### The sick man: the triangle with no aspect in it

The question: **"Shall the sick man recover of this disease, or die thereof?"** The querent is the sick man himself; the sickness is the sixth's matter; the triangle is the class's: first, sixth, eighth, with no regard joining any of them. The four Mothers: **Laetitia, Carcer, Tristitia, Via.**

**The derivation, verified.** Mothers: Laetitia [1,2,2,2], Carcer [1,2,2,1], Tristitia [2,2,2,1], Via [1,1,1,1]. Daughters: first lines 1,1,2,1 gives **Puer**; second lines 2,2,2,1 gives **Tristitia**; third lines 2,2,2,1 gives **Tristitia**; fourth lines 2,1,1,1 gives **Caput Draconis**. Nieces: Laetitia and Carcer, 1+1 two; 2+2 two; 2+2 two; 2+1 one: **Tristitia**. Tristitia and Via, 2+1 one; 2+1 one; 2+1 one; 1+1 two: **Cauda Draconis**. Puer and Tristitia, 1+2 one; 1+2 one; 2+2 two; 1+1 two: **Fortuna Minor**. Tristitia and Caput Draconis, 2+2 two; 2+1 one; 2+1 one; 1+1 two: **Conjunctio**. Right Witness, Tristitia and Cauda Draconis, 2+1 one; 2+1 one; 2+1 one; 1+2 one: **Via**, four points. Left Witness, Fortuna Minor and Conjunctio, 1+2 one; 1+1 two; 2+1 one; 2+2 two: **Amissio**, six points. Judge, Via and Amissio: 1+1 two; 1+2 one; 1+1 two; 1+2 one: **Acquisitio**, six points. Parity: Witnesses four and six, both even, one parity; Judge even. True.

**Step 0.** Laetitia rises, so the chart proceeds.

**Step 1.** Recovery or death of the querent's own disease: the sixth. The querent is **Laetitia (1st)**. The quesited is **Tristitia (6th)**. The death-house, the triangle's third corner, holds **Caput Draconis (8th)**.

**Step 2.** The triad is Via, Amissio, Judge Acquisitio: the matter begins in change, continues through Loss, and ends in Gain. The table's row for this question's matter is *sickenes: health*. Hold it in its role.

**Step 3.** Repetitions: **Tristitia in 3, 6, 7 and 9**, the disease's figure four times around the wheel. Nothing else doubles.

**Step 4, the scan translated for this class.** Occupation: no. Conjunction: no. Company: the 5·6 pair joins nothing. Aspect: no regard joins the first to the sixth or eighth in any possible chart. The scan therefore cannot speak its usual sentence; read the class as a state of affairs, as Cattan read the Novelaire case: the triangle's three houses, the triad in its role, and the end-houses, with every finding read as finding about *recovery or death*.

**Step 5, the triangle.** The patient first, by Gerard's synthesis: no Saturn, Mars, or Tail in the first, but Jupiter's Laetitia, matching the recovery part: "if good Planets shall be in the first, sixth and eighth... the sick person shall live." The part for this register: "the sicke person shall die **so that the viij. doe consent**." A death sentence, *conditional on the eighth*: the entry's own cross-reference, doing exactly what the earlier rule promised. So consult the eighth:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-08/caput-draconis" />

"The person sicke shall be long sicke, **but he shall have no other disease**... for all the demaundes which may be propounded in this house this figure is good." The eighth does not consent.
