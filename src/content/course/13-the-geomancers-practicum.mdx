This is the capstone the last three lessons promised: three complete written judgments against staged master solutions, the synthesis quiz, and the thirty-day journal that turns a course you took into a practice you keep. **It teaches no new doctrine, by design and on principle.** There is no figure you have not met, no rule you have not cited, no instrument you have not run. What there is, for the first time, is a course gone silent, watching you work, and what it witnesses is the only thing the art has ever accepted as proof: one judge, one question, and the points falling where they fall.

This has an examination's shape, not a lesson's. Three judgments, sat like sittings, each on a chart you have never seen: the first on a question-class no worked example ever modeled, to prove the method travels; the second on a divided chart that will try to talk you out of your own scan, to prove the hierarchy holds under weight; the third on one question asked in both of the art's tongues, to prove the bilingual discipline Lesson 12 left running in your journal. Each judgment stands against a **staged master solution**: three sealed stages, opened in order, so that you can verify your cast without glimpsing the verdict and take a hint without taking the answer. And every judgment is delivered in the form Lesson 10 made law and the journal has spoken ever since (the six clauses, **answer, manner, end, timing, counsel, reservation**) because the capstone requires the litany whole, and a clause skipped under examination pressure is the precise failure the examination exists to catch. After the judgments, the quiz; after the quiz, the thirty days; after the thirty days, nothing, which is the point. The last thing this course does is stop being necessary.

## The check-in

Lesson 12's Exercise 8 told you to build the one-page Gerard reference your practice will use and bring it, with the journal, to Lesson 13. This is the asking. Lay on the desk, before anything is cast: the **Gerard card**, complete to Exercise 8's specification, with the sixteen-figure ascendant table, the projection order, the remainder rule written out in words, the rulership table with its one asterisk, the years as printed, the fortunes and infortunes with the luminaries left blank, the tongue-hygiene line at the foot. The **taxonomy card** from Lesson 11 beside it. And the **journal**, current to this morning: every verdict final and six-claused, the seven doubled days of Lesson 12's protocol recorded with their concord notes, nothing provisional anywhere. If any of that is missing, stop here and finish it: the practicum assumes the whole kit because the practice it examines does.

Lesson 10's Exercise 4 ("The hierarchy, defended") tested the method you now practice. Re-take it now, cold, before the first sitting, and read your answers the way you read every witness: noting what they state, what they assume, and what they would die defending. You are about to test that hierarchy three times. Judgment II in particular is built to attack exactly the ground it holds; and when you reach the synthesis quiz at the end of this lesson, the doctrines you retest tonight are the ones it gathers. Note in the journal anything you no longer believe; the synthesis quiz will ask why.

The retake should notice three things, in rising order of value. First, the gaps a season of practice exposes: you first answered as a judge of four charts, and you have since judged dozens; somewhere the old answer was more confident than you now are, and that question deserves its journal note. Second, the borders the defence never imagined: it argued a hierarchy for one system, and you now practice two. Does the argument *transfer*, and if Gerard's configurations need no Rule 8 because they are never ranked, what exactly was being defended: a rule, or the deeper principle that every voice needs a jurisdiction? That question is the synthesis quiz's heart, and it is better to have met it days early. Third (and this is the reading the course most hopes you make) the answers that held. Some doctrine in that defence has been true in every judgment since, and finding it is not vanity; it is the discovery of what, in your method, is actually load-bearing. The practicum will lean on exactly those beams.

## How to sit the practicum

Three sittings, one judgment each, with at least a day between them; the spacing is not ceremony, and a judge who runs three unfamiliar charts in one evening is practicing fatigue, not method. Each sitting takes the shape your practice already has: three to four unhurried hours, the worksheet run whole, everything in writing. The rest of this lesson's hours belong to the quiz and the month.

**What is open.** Everything a working geomancer would honestly have: this site's corpus and library, [the placement lookup](/tools/placement-lookup/), the technique pages, your two cards and your journal. The art was never a memory contest; Cattan wrote with his own book open. The examination is not whether you can recall the eleventh house's commission; it is whether you can *weigh* what you look up.

**On judging supplied questions.** The practicum's charts come with their questions attached, already cast, and a fair objection has been waiting since Lesson 4: the casting doctrine demands an earnest asker, and these askers are the course's inventions. The answer is the one the tradition itself lived by, because supplied charts are how the art was always examined: Cattan's own case-chapters hand the reader charts already cast and ask him to follow the judgment, so the student of 1591 sat exactly this practicum. The discipline transfers, it does not lapse: take each question as a brief, the way an advocate takes a client, for the hope, the prisoner, the marriage are real enough somewhere, and the judge's seriousness is owed to the *form* of the asking, not to an introduction. Practically: read the question's framing slowly, decide who the querent is and what hangs on the answer for them, write that understanding into the journal entry's first line, and judge for that person. A judgment delivered to nobody drifts into cleverness; the six clauses (the counsel above all) only mean anything addressed to a face, even an imagined one.

**What is closed.** The master solutions, until their stages fall due; and the discipline of the stages is the practicum's one piece of machinery, so fix it now. Each judgment carries three sealed stages. **Stage 1, the cast, verified**: only the chart's structural facts, nothing judged. Open it after your derivation is written and parity-checked (or, for the Gerard chart, after your erection's divisions are written out), and before you begin to judge, because a judgment built on a miscast chart examines nothing, and the course would rather you verify than gamble three hours. **Stage 2, the instruments to weigh**: the testimonies that matter, the rules that will earn their keep, the columns and clauses worth pulling, all of them hints, with no verdict in them. Open it only if you are genuinely stuck, and if you open it, record that you did; the rubric will ask. **Stage 3, the master judgment**: the full written sentence, six clauses, every weight cited. Open it only when your own judgment is finished, signed and dated in the journal, and then read it as you have learned to read a parallel source: not as the answer, but as a second judge's opinion against which yours is graded. Where you and the master solution differ, the question is never "who is right" first; it is *whose weighing cited its warrant*, and sometimes, as the rubric says, a defended divergence outranks an undefended agreement.

Be clear about what the master solutions are, because the phrase has been a promise for three lessons and its meaning is a doctrine in miniature. They are not answer keys, and the difference is not rhetorical. An answer key implies that the judgment of a chart is a computation with one output, and everything this course taught from Lesson 10 onward says otherwise: the *scan* is a computation (perfection is present or it is not, and on that question the master solution is simply correct and you are simply checked) but the *weighing* is a judgment, which is why the art needed judges and not clerks. The master solutions are the examiner's own sittings, written under the same rules you sit under: every weight cited, every rule named, every flag flown. They are the standard you are graded against precisely because they show their work, and the rubric's first law (a defended divergence outranks an undefended agreement) is what keeps the grading honest in both directions. Copying a master solution's verdict into your journal teaches the same amount as copying a friend's: nothing, minus the self-respect. Arguing with one, in writing, citation against citation, is the most instructive hour this course can give you.

**What each sitting must produce.** The deliverable is the same for all three, and it is the journal entry your practice has built since Lesson 9: the question and its class, with the one-sentence defense of the house; the cast in full (sixteen rows and the derived shield, parity-verified, or the erection with every division written out); the pre-judgment dossier, all its lines, in the Lesson 9 form; the weighing, with each rule named at the moment it does work; the six clauses, complete; and the grading note after Stage 3, whether agreed, or diverged and defended, with the rubric's five criteria each given their word. Date it and sign it. The signature is not ceremony either: it is the practice this course has been building all along, a judge who puts a name to a sentence and lets the outcome find it.

## Judgment I: the thing hoped for

*A querent comes with a hope he will not name, a thing longed for, within honest reach but not within his grasp, resting partly on the goodwill of others. He asks:* **"Will the querent get what they hope for?"**

The first examination is the method on fresh ground. Assign the question its house and defend the assignment in one sentence: your taxonomy card knows this class, and so does Lesson 11's map, but no worked example in this course has ever judged it whole, which is precisely why it is here. A class you have only read about is the honest test of whether you learned a method or memorized a casebook: the casebook student knows what marriage charts and office charts look like because the course showed him; the method student can walk into a house he has never judged, read its commission, find its native geometry, and weigh what he finds there by rules that never change. Then the whole method, Step 0 through the six clauses: derivation parity-verified in writing, the bench and its row, significators and every springing, the perfection scan with its direction read, the audit, the instruments, the weighing, the sentence. Quote the quesited figure's Book II column for this house, and at least one parallel source against it; and if the parallels divide, adjudicate them on the record, by the protocol you own. One craft note before you begin, offered because it is protocol and not hint: in an unfamiliar class, read the house's own chapter-head *before* looking at the chart, because the commission tells you what the house can be asked, and the question's register selects within it; a judge who looks at the figures first starts weighing before he knows what the scales are for, which is the beginner's error this course spent Part 3 burning out of you.

## Judgment II: the prisoner

*A man lies in prison. One who loves him asks for him:* **"Will the prisoner be released?"**

The second examination is the one this lesson was built around, and the course owes you fair warning of an unfair chart: everything in it will tempt you toward one verdict, and the method (if you hold it) delivers the other. This is the dire register Lesson 11's Exercise 7 tested, in the class whose geometry the course called the doctrine itself: the twelfth holds no regard to the first; the prison stands where the asker has no line of sight. Run the method *exactly*, in order, writing each step before the next, because the chart's whole strategy is to get you weighing before you have scanned. Lesson 10's hierarchy is the instrument on trial. This is what it is for.

What is being examined is not your stomach for dark figures; Lesson 11's exercises already proved that. What this chart examines is the *architecture*: whether the jurisdictions you defended in writing hold when every instinct and half the testimony pull against them. The flat-balance reader (the rival your Lesson 10 quiz weighed and refused) will count this chart's darks, find them many, and deny. The bench-supremacist will read the Judge's name and deny. The fortune-cookie reader will find the quesited's column and deny. Three wrong methods, one wrong verdict, reached comfortably and in agreement; and one right method, reached only by a judge who scans before weighing, ranks before counting, and lets each voice speak exactly in its own jurisdiction and not one syllable outside it. Deliver the verdict in the full pastoral form: this is also the practicum's examination of the dire-answer protocol, and the counsel clause will have to work harder here than anywhere in the course.

## Judgment III: one question, two tongues

*A marriage is intended, and the families are asking. The question both of the art's traditions know:* **"Will the intended marriage go ahead?"**

The last examination is Lesson 12's discipline under seal: "it reserves the right to ask for either tongue," and the practicum exercises the right by asking for *both*. The question is the course's oldest: Chart A, the first chart you ever judged, asked it; you now ask it again with the whole art in your hands. At one sitting, two casts, two independent acts: first the shield, with sixteen rows of points, the full derivation, the question held in mind once; then, separately and completely, Gerard's erection on the same question, with its own four lines for the Ascendant, its own nine projections, no figure carried over, nothing shared but the asking. Judge the shield chart whole, in the six clauses. Judge the celestial chart whole, in Gerard's clauses, with the six-clause form lent and flagged as Lesson 12 taught. Then write the concord note, one line, the doubled journal's own form: *agree*, *disagree*, or *speak past each other*, and in what. Tongue hygiene is graded mercilessly here: not one sign, lord, rule or term may cross the border in either direction.

The order of the two judgments matters, and the protocol fixes it: shield first, sealed before the second cast is read; write the shield judgment to its last clause and date it before you so much as erect the celestial figure. The reason is the reason for everything in this lesson: the concord note is only worth keeping if the two judgments were genuinely independent, and a judge who knows the first verdict while weighing the second will harmonize without meaning to; the same quiet hand that makes witnesses agree in court testimony will make tongues agree in a journal, and the discipline against it is mechanical, not moral: finish one before beginning the other. And know what this sitting examines that the first two could not: not whether you can run each system (Lesson 12 settled that) but whether you can keep two complete arts in one head without letting either borrow the other's authority. The bilingual judge's characteristic failure is not error but *leakage*: a shield judgment that quietly leans on the celestial chart's bright lords, a Gerard judgment that ranks its configurations because ranking is what the judge's other tongue does. The concord note is where the discipline pays: two verdicts, each standing entirely on its own warrant, compared only after both are sealed.

## The thirty-day journal

<Exercise n={2} title="The thirty days" kind="practice">
  The last exercise of the course is the first month of the practice, and its protocol is already running: Lesson 12's Exercise 8 said of its doubled week, "The practicum's thirty-day journal **continues exactly this discipline**," and so it does, changed in nothing but length. For thirty days from the third sitting: every real question you cast, cast in both systems, with the shield first, in your established discipline, then Gerard's erection on the same question, each judged whole in its own tongue, every shield verdict in the six clauses, and the one-line concord note after: *agree*, *disagree*, or *speak past each other*, and in what. Cast only real questions, earnestly asked; a day without one is a review day, and review days are working days: reread an open judgment, check an outcome, walk one technique page. Where only one system is consulted (and Lesson 12's when-to-reach-for-which counsel governs the choosing) the concord note records *one tongue only, and why*. Four weekly reviews: each seventh day, read the week's judgments cold and grade them by this lesson's rubric, as if another judge had written them. Record outcomes throughout: every verdict whose matter resolves in the world gets its outcome written against it, in the outcome's own words, kind or not. And on the thirtieth day, read the whole book, end to end, and write one page: what the month's concord notes say about the two arts, what the outcomes say about the judge, and what the next month's practice should do differently. That page is the course's true final examination, and no one grades it but you, which is what *practice* means.

  The entry-form, fixed here so the month's pages can be compared: the date and the question, in its final wording; the class and house, one line; the shield cast and its dossier, the verdict in the six clauses, signed; the erection and its placements, the verdict in Gerard's clauses, signed; the concord note; and a blank line left for the outcome, which is the entry's most important line and the only one the day cannot fill. Leave the blank visibly; an empty outcome line at the foot of every judgment is the journal's standing reminder that the verdicts are claims about the world, and the world gets the last word.

  <ModelAnswer label="What a sound month looks like">
    No model journal exists, and the variance is the information; but a sound month has a recognizable shape. Volume: somewhere between eight and fifteen real questions; fewer means the questions were not earnest, more means they were not questions. Concord notes: mostly *speak past each other* (the systems' instruments overlap so little that full agreement or full collision is the rare case) with a handful of clean agreements treasured and any clean disagreement handled as Lesson 12 taught: the question called unripe, patience counseled, both verdicts recorded without a forced winner. Outcomes: several written in, including at least one that shamed its judgment; a month of unbroken vindication means the questions were too easy or the outcomes read too kindly, and the rubric's honesty criterion applies to outcome-reading too. The weekly reviews show their teeth marks: at least one verdict regraded, at least one clause found missing and said so. And the thirtieth-day page does the one thing no single judgment can: it reads the *judge*, naming a habit (a clause chronically thin, a register chronically dodged, a tongue favored beyond its warrant) and sets the next month against it. A journal like that is not course-work anymore. It is the practice, kept.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

## Grading yourself

The rubric, and why there is one. Self-assessment is the practice's weakest joint (every solitary discipline's is) and the failure runs in both directions: the anxious student grades savagely and learns nothing but discouragement; the comfortable one grades kindly and learns nothing at all. The remedy is the one this whole course has used for every other judgment: criteria stated in advance, applied in writing, with warrants. You do not ask "was that good?" (a question with no instruments) but five questions that have answers, and you write the answers down where the weekly reviews can audit them. Grade each sitting on these, in the journal, beside the work:

| Criterion | Passes when | Fails when |
|---|---|---|
| The cast | Your derivation or erection agrees with Stage 1 exactly, verified in writing before judging | Any figure, sign or placement differs: a miscast chart fails the cast, not the judge; recast and resit |
| The placements | Every weight assigned cites its testimony: a column, a row, an instrument, a clause | Any sentence of the judgment that cannot answer "says who?" |
| The rules | Each rule that did work is named where it worked, and no rule is made to do another's work | Weighing by instinct with citations decorating it afterward |
| The clauses | All six present, each in its own jurisdiction, the dark ones said plainly | A clause missing, two clauses fused, or counsel doing the answer's job of softening |
| The tongues | In Judgment III and ever after: each system judged wholly in its own terms, the lent form flagged | One sign, lord or rule found across the border |

Three further laws of the grading. First, **a defended divergence outranks an undefended agreement**: where your verdict differs from the master judgment but every weight in yours cites its warrant and the hierarchy is kept, record the divergence, argue it in one paragraph, and pass yourself; the master solutions are a second judge's opinion, and judges may differ at the weighing while agreeing at the scan; but a divergence *at the scan* (perfection found where there is none, or missed where it stands) is an error of fact, not of judgment, and fails. Second, **Stage 2 openings are recorded, not punished**: consulting the hints once, noted in the journal, costs nothing but the noting; consulting them as a substitute for being stuck costs the sitting. Third, **one resit per judgment**, after a day, on the same chart, with the journal recording both attempts; the practice you are entering allows revision and forbids forgetting.

## You have finished this course when…

- The three judgments are sat, graded by the rubric, and standing in the journal: the cast exact in all three, every divergence from the master solutions either conceded or defended in writing.
- Judgment II's verdict was *delivered*, or your denial is the best-argued failure in your journal and you have resat the chart and watched the hierarchy hold the second time.
- Judgment III's two judgments share a question and not one term, and its concord note is written in the journal's own form.
- The synthesis quiz is taken cold, checked against its key, and every miss conceded or argued in the journal, dated.
- The thirty days are complete: the casts doubled, the concord notes kept, the outcomes recorded against their verdicts kind and unkind, the four weekly reviews showing their teeth marks, and the thirtieth-day page written.
- The Gerard card and the taxonomy card live where the work happens, and the journal has not needed this course to tell it what to do for a month.

## Where the practice goes

There is no next lesson, and the absence is the design. What there is, is the art; and this site was built as the working library of exactly the practice you now keep. The [library](/library/) holds the sources whole, and you have earned a different way of reading them: read Cattan's Book III now not as assigned chapters but as a colleague's case-notes, for the Clermont lawsuit, the Trivulsio peace, the robbery and the Lord of Tays's horse are the only complete worked judgments the English tradition left us, and a practitioner rereads them the way a lawyer rereads the great opinions, for the moves and not the holdings; read the eight chapters of Gerard this course never assigned (his second, third, fourth, fifth, eighth, ninth, tenth and eleventh houses) with his whole method in your hands; read the pseudo-Agrippa and Heydon knowing precisely whose voice doubles whose. The [technique pages](/techniques/) are the compressed shelf, built to be consulted mid-judgment, which is how you will use them now. The [figures](/figures/) and [houses](/houses/) hold every placement-column the corpus owns, and the witness–judge tables (all hundred and twenty-eight surviving rows) repay a slow study no lesson had room to assign: read one headline figure's table end to end and watch how the 1591 compilers thought about compounding. The [course charts](/course/charts/) keep all the standing questions (A through D, E through G, and now J through M) and one of the better exercises of your second year will be to recast their questions in your own hand and watch different charts answer the same askings. And the journal is the only teacher left, which was always the tradition's own school: the Renaissance geomancers learned from masters when they could and from their own recorded judgments always.

One thing remains, and the course has saved it deliberately for last, because it is the tradition's own last word about itself. Cattan, in the middle of Book III, pausing between techniques, wrote this about a use of the art he was about to teach:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-19-to-knowe-what-an-other-thinketh" />

"Not to the intent that any man give any credit or trust to it more then to the rest of the Arte, which was for none other purpose invented (as I have many times protested and said) but for the pastime and pleasure of the wittie heads and inventive." That sentence is **the reservation clause, applied by the tradition to itself.** The man who judged for queens and cardinals, who wrote three hundred pages of method with his book open and his cases named, places on the record (protesting that he has said it many times) that the art does not demand belief, that its purpose includes delight, and that an honest judge says so. Whether Cattan's protest was conviction or a churchman-shaped prudence, the course leaves with the historians, in its own last act of pedigree-keeping. What it takes, and hands to you, is the posture: hold the art exactly as seriously as you have learned to hold it (the full method, the honest flags, the outcomes recorded against the verdicts) and not one degree more solemnly than the tradition held it. Honesty's clause, all the way down.

The first lesson of this course began with a picture: the heavens acting on the earth and on everything made of earth, and a person, moved by a serious question, pricking a row of points that are not an accident: "the lowest visible link of a chain that runs up through the soul, through the order of the planets and signs, to the source of order itself. To read the points is to read the chain." Thirteen lessons ago that was a claim to be understood, "not yet about believing it, and not yet about casting." Now it is simply a description of what you do. You have read the chain in two languages. You have judged the hope, the prison and the marriage, and graded your own sentences. You keep a book in which the verdicts face their outcomes, which is more honesty than most arts ever ask of their practitioners. The course is finished; it stopped teaching some pages ago, witnessed what it needed to witness, and signs the journal's first month gladly. The rest is yours: one judge, one question, and the points falling where they fall.
