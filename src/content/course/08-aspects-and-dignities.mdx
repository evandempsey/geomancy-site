Lesson 7's perfection scan answers whether the significators connect. It leaves two things unmeasured: the quality of the findings around the parties, and the strength of each figure's placement. Two charts may both perfect with very different support, and one denial may be much stronger than another. Quality and strength are this lesson's subject.

The sources use two tools. **Aspects** tell you which houses behold which: which figures regard your significators, from what angle, and with what agreement. **Dignities** tell you how strongly each figure stands. Together they turn placements and the yes-or-no scan into a fuller reading.

They also teach a distinction that is easy to miss: **strength is not goodness.** A figure can be ill and strongly placed, or good and weakly placed. By the end you will review all three course charts on both axes; one comes out worse than before, and Chart C remains unresolved until Lesson 10.

## Before you begin

This lesson uses Lesson 3's natures (every aspect is judged by elemental and planetary kinship), Lesson 5's angular, succedent, and cadent places, and the reading procedure of Lessons 6 and 7. It also quotes Cattan using his own attributions, so keep Lesson 3's rule in mind: one system in your working chart, the wider source set in your notes. Everything here qualifies the perfection answer; nothing replaces the scan.

Cattan's Book III chapters 12-17 give the aspects, with two of his own cases, and chapters 22-23 give the triplicities and exaltations. The modern comparison is Crowley's chapter VI: a mechanical dignity table and a short aspect rule. ## The regards in general

Cattan opens the aspect rule with a four-sentence chapter. Read all four parts:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-12-of-the-aspectes-in-generall" />

Five aspects: conjunction, sextile, trine, quadrate (the square), opposition. The names and distances come from astrology, where planets behold one another at a sixth, a quarter, a third, and half of the circle; the sextile and trine are friendly, the square and opposition harder. Geomancy applies the same distances to the twelve houses: a figure beholds the figures standing at those distances from it. Exercise 5 asks what the transposition preserves and what it gives up.

Cattan's last part gives the rule: figures that behold by aspect, "if they be good they be the better, if they be ill they be the worse... according to the houses where they be, and according to the figures good or bad." The aspect is a relation. The finding is decided by **the figure found in the beholding house**: its goodness or illness, its element, its planet, and the house it occupies. A trine is friendly in form, but if Tristitia stands there, the finding is still sorrowful. In geomancy, sextile and trine are not automatically good news, and square and opposition are not automatically bad news. The figure at the other end decides what the aspect carries.

One housekeeping note before the distances. Cattan's first aspect, the conjunction, is not new to you. His chapter on it points straight back at material you already own:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-13-of-the-aspect-of-conjunction" />

The conjunction "of the figure" is the material of Lesson 7's modes of perfection: one figure doubling into a neighbouring house and making company. This chapter does not change that rule. It adds one note: certain figures contain a conjunction of planets within themselves. Via, the Moon's figure, is read by Cattan as Sun and Moon conjoined, hot against cold, ill by inner contrariety "except the question be made for the way, for then it is good." Chart A uses that exception later. The new material in this lesson is the four aspects at a distance.

## Counting the regards

The four distant aspects are defined by distance around the wheel, and Cattan states each distance in the inclusive counting of his century: both endpoints counted, so that neighbours two doors apart are "from three to three." Learn his phrasing, because you will meet it in every Renaissance text, and learn the translation, because your reviews will be done in plain steps:

- **Sextile.** Cattan: the first beholds the third, "and so consequently from three houses to three," and also the first beholds the eleventh. In plain steps: **two houses away, on either side**.
- **Square** (quadrate): "from foure fygures unto foure figures": the first to the fourth, or the first to the tenth. **Three houses away, on either side.**
- **Trine.** "fygures from 5. to 5. doe beholde themselves": the first, the fifth and the ninth. **Four houses away, on either side.**
- **Opposition.** "from seven to seven": the first beholds the seventh. **Six houses away**, and six is half of twelve, so there is only one opposition, directly across the wheel, with no two sides.

Each of the first three aspects therefore looks both ways, and the two directions have names inherited from astrology. Counting *backward*, against the order of the houses, from the first house to the eleventh, the tenth, the ninth, is the **dexter** aspect; counting *forward* with the order, from the first to the third, the fourth, the fifth, is the **sinister**. The terms are simply Latin for right and left; a sinister trine is not an ominous thing. Cattan describes both directions every time, "the first beholdeth the third... and also, when the first beholdes... the 11," without naming them; the names, and one sharpening, come from the modern comparison:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/aspects" />

There is the whole counting rule in two lines, plus a rule the Renaissance text does not state: the dexter aspect is the *stronger* of the two. This course adopts the dexter-stronger rule as a working refinement, flagged as modern; nothing in Cattan contradicts it. The same passage also gives the modern summary of figure-friendships (elements and planets friendly or hostile), a quick reckoner for when the strict agreements (same figure, same element, same planet) run out.

The skill is mechanical. Take any house; the houses it beholds are found by stepping ±2, ±3, ±4 and +6 around the wheel, wrapping past twelve back to one. For the first house:

| Aspect | Dexter (backward) | Sinister (forward) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sextile (±2) | 11 | 3 |
| Square (±3) | 10 | 4 |
| Trine (±4) | 9 | 5 |
| Opposition (+6) | 7 | none |

Seven houses behold the first; four (the second, sixth, eighth and twelfth) do not behold it at all. Run the same arithmetic from the seventh and you get its mirror, which means **the first and seventh houses always behold one another, in opposition**. Every two-party question carries one built-in opposition, and its quality is judged like every other aspect: by the agreement of the figures at its two ends.

The wrap-around takes practice: from the ninth, the trine dexter lands on the fifth and the trine sinister on the *first* (9 + 4 = 13, wrapping to 1); from the eleventh, the sextile sinister is the first house and the opposition the fifth. Exercise 1 drills it.

## The Sextile

Here is the sextile chapter: definition, rule, and Cattan's example of a question about buying a horse.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-14-of-the-sextile-aspect" />

Take the rule first. The sextile "signifies means and unperfit amitie" (a mild, imperfect friendship, the weakest of the channels) and the reading rule is the general rule in miniature: a good figure in the sextile place, agreeing with the first "and be of the same Element and of one planet, or that they be both alike," promises "good fortune and good issue"; an ill figure there, of contrary planet and element, and "you shall judge ill of the question made, and of enmitie." The aspect carries the relation; the figures decide whether it is friendly.

The case shows the rule in use. Cattan reads by his own attributions, not this course's defaults. The querent's Puella (water, in his system) is beheld by sextile from Fortuna Minor in the third (fire, contrary in planet and element): advice from someone close, but not fully helpful. The dexter sextile from Puer in the eleventh agrees poorly by element, but Venus and Mars are friendly in his system, so a friend is still involved. The finding is mixed because the agreements are mixed, and Cattan then goes on to the angles, Witnesses, Judge, Part of Fortune, and way of the point. One aspect is one finding, never the whole reading.

## The Trine

The trine chapter gives a question of war and peace between Francis I and Emperor Charles V, cast when Cardinal Trivulsio came as legate to broker terms:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-15-of-the-tryne-aspect-chapt" />

The rule: the trine (first, fifth and ninth, "from 5. to 5.") "is naturally good and perfect amitie... by reason of the house, place and fygure." But the case shows that the figure still decides the finding. The first house is beheld by trine from the fifth, and the fifth holds **Tristitia**, Saturn's figure of sorrow. Cattan writes: "because the first is beheld with an ill Aspect from the fift which is a fygure of ♄ retrograde, called *Tristitia*, it signifyed that there should bee no peace made at this time." The fifth beholds the first by trine, yet Cattan calls it an "ill Aspect" because the figure in the fifth is ill. **The aspect is judged by the figure found in it.**

The rest of the reading follows the same rule: the ninth, house of churchmen, holds a good figure, so the Cardinal works honestly for peace; the seventh holds an ill figure, so the Emperor refuses it; the Witnesses and Judge incline the same way. The answer is no peace, and Cattan reports the outcome: the Emperor seized Provence as far as Aix, held it briefly, and lost all he had taken.

## The square and the opposition

The two hard channels. First the square:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-16-of-the-quadrats-aspect" />

"From foure to foure" means three plain steps, the first house to the fourth or the tenth. Cattan does not treat the square as simply evil; he judges it by agreement: a good figure of the same quality and planet is a good sign, a good figure of different element and planet makes the aspect "meane," and planetary friendship can make the regard good. His worked case, the Count of Touerra's question about when the Emperor would leave Nice, turns the rule to timing: stable figures in the first, the square, the Witnesses and the Judge, so Cattan judges delay, and the Emperor waits for Andrea Doria and provisions.

Then the opposition, with the chapter's summary rule:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-17-of-the-aspect-of-opposition" />

The opposition "is an ill Aspect as well for the distance of the place as for the contrarietie," and it is the one aspect with no second side. But it is not simply ill: if the figures at the two ends are akin, "both of one Element, qualitie, nature and Planet," the opposition has "no contrarietie... but contrariwise singuler allyance and friendshippe." A good figure facing an evil one is "great discord, contrarietie, and enmitie." Cattan's case, whether the wounded Count of Novellara was dead, turns on this: first and seventh of one element, water with water, so the man lived; fourth and tenth contrary, so he would die of the wound. The chapter closes with the rule for all regards: "when yee fynde a good fygure in any of these Aspectes, you shall judge well and goodnesse: and if yee fynde an ill Planet there yee shall judge ill."

The four chapters yield one working rule. For any house you care about, usually the first and the house of the quesited, find the seven houses that behold it. For each beholding house, ask three questions. What figure stands there? What agreement does it have with the figure beheld: same figure, same element, same planet, or only planetary friendship? What aspect carries the relation: trine, sextile, square, or opposition, dexter or sinister? A good figure with strong agreement gives supportive finding. An ill figure with no agreement gives hostile finding. You will run this review on the significators of all three standing charts before the lesson ends.

## The triplicities of figures

Now the second tool: dignity, strength by place. The aspects ask who beholds a figure; the dignities ask how firmly the figure itself is planted. The sources' first answer is the triplicity, and Cattan's chapter is the source:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-22-of-the-triplicitie-of-figures-and" />

The main point: figures of one element form a *triplicity*, with planetary governors by day and night on the astrological model. "Above all thinges you must have a regarde unto the triplicitie," Cattan says: if most of the chart belongs to one element, judge the chart by that element's nature, and if it is split, judge by the stronger part. A figure is stronger when the chart around it shares its element.

Here the sources split. Cattan's air triplicity is Acquisitio, Laetitia, Puer, and Conjunctio; his fire is Rubeus, Amissio, Cauda Draconis, and Fortuna Minor; his water is Via, Populus, Albus, and Puella; his earth is Fortuna Major, Tristitia, Caput Draconis, and Carcer. Lesson 3 fixed this course's default from Agrippa. Several figures move between systems: Acquisitio is water in the default but air for Cattan; Puella is air in the default but water for Cattan; Albus, Fortuna Minor, and Fortuna Major also move. Keep the course policy: **one system in the hands** and **the whole source set in the head.** This lesson's reviews use the course default. Cattan's cases use Cattan's system. Do not mix them in one chart.

## Exaltation, joy and fall

Cattan's next chapter pushes the astrological transposition one step further. In astrology, the planets are not equally strong everywhere: each has signs of exaltation, joy, fall, and detriment. Cattan maps the rule onto the figures in a table:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-23-the-exaltation-joy-and-fall-of" />

Each row is a planet. Around it stand three signs: the sign of the planet's exaltation, joy, and fall. Beneath each sign are the geomantic figures that sign owns in Cattan's attributions. The working sense is this: **a figure is strong where its planet is dignified and weak where its planet falls.** Cattan gives the table with little prose. His opening sentence says that "a figure can not well be judged" without knowing "the exaltation and the fall of the fygures," and he leaves the mapping from signs to houses to the practitioner's astrological knowledge.

The modern comparison makes that last step explicit:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/dignities" />

Crowley's rule treats the twelve houses as the twelve signs, Ascendant as Aries through to the twelfth as Pisces, which turns the apparatus into a lookup: a figure is strongest in its planet's own sign, very strong in exaltation, very weak in fall, weakest in detriment. Keep the systems labelled: the houses-as-signs equation is a modern development of a rule Cattan states only as a planetary table, and the two do not always agree. Every exaltation call must name its system. For daily practice, Cattan gives a simpler tool that needs no zodiac.

## Every figure's good and ill houses

Book III, chapter 2 is the main practical source for geomantic dignity: a figure-by-figure list of where each stands well and where it stands ill. It opens with a general grading of the houses:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/intro" />

Good houses are the first, fifth, tenth, and eleventh; mean houses are the second, third, fourth, and ninth; evil houses are the sixth, seventh, eighth, and twelfth. This is not the same as Lesson 5's angular, succedent, and cadent strength. The seventh and fourth are angles, but Cattan classes them as evil and mean. The eleventh is succedent, but he classes it as good. Strength by angle measures how firmly a figure is placed. The good-and-ill grading measures whether the house suits the figure and matter. Keep them as separate columns in your review.

Then the figure-by-figure entries, each a sentence or two of concentrated experience. Here are the ones the course charts will need, beginning with the figure that owns Chart A's question:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/via" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/cauda-draconis" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/amissio" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/conjunctio" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/puella" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/carcer" />

Via "breaketh and spoileth al the goodnes of the others" and yet is "good in the third, fift and seventh houses, because she signifyeth that letters shall come which shall bring good newes," with the standing exception for journeys renewed. Cauda Draconis is "found good in the fourth, sixt, ninth and twelfth houses": the Tail can work well in houses of endings, sickness, and confinement, where passing out of a condition may be wanted. Its opening part, "very good with the ill, and very ill with the good," is the strength-is-not-goodness rule in short form. Amissio is good in the eighth and "very ill in the second," where loss stands on the querent's own purse. Carcer is "ill in all the houses, and especially in the sixt, eight, seventh and twelfth"; mark the seventh, because Chart B will need it. Puella is "very good in all things that ye may demaunde."

Count the sources, as in Lesson 6. Heydon prints the same series in *Theomagia*:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/good-ill-houses" />

Heydon reprints Cattan nearly verbatim seventy years later, so cite the series as Cattan's rule repeated by Heydon. The Golden Dawn later gives a more mechanical version.

The entries do not always align with the triplicity and exaltation apparatus, because the series follows meaning while the apparatus follows planetary dignity: Amissio is good in the eighth because loss belongs there, not because Venus is dignified there. For this course, the good-and-ill-houses series is the primary dignity tool, clear, citable, and independent of attribution disputes; triplicities, exaltations, and angular strength refine it.

## Strength is not goodness

Every figure in a chart carries two independent measures. Its **nature** tells you what it signifies; its **condition** tells you how strongly it can act. Keep them separate:

- **Good and strong:** a fortunate figure, angular, dignified, and well beheld.
- **Good and weak:** a fortunate figure cadent, badly housed for its kind, or unsupported by aspect.
- **Ill and weak:** an unfortunate figure, badly placed and unsupported.
- **Ill and strong:** an unfortunate figure angular, suited to the house, or strongly supported by aspect. A well-dignified ill figure does not become kind; it becomes effective.

The hard cases are the last two. Students fear the ill figure and trust the good one without asking how strongly either is placed. The question is never only "which figure is better?" but "better where, for what, and in what strength?" Exercise 6 tests the hard case, dignified ill against debilitated good: the dignified ill figure is usually worse for the querent's hopes and always more reliable, because strength is the likelihood that what the figure signifies will be carried out.

## The course charts checked

Now the three standing charts. Chart B's complete review is the worked example below. Here are Charts A and C: one where the aspects qualify a perfected chart, and one where they make an existing conflict stronger.

### Chart A: the marriage, perfected and qualified

"Will the marriage go ahead?" Lesson 7's scan found the chart perfects by conjunction, with the quesited's own Via standing in the second house beside the querent. The provisional answer remains *yes, by conjunction*. Now ask this lesson's questions: how well supported is the meeting, and where is the strength?

The querent first: Conjunctio in the first. The tables below are the review performed:

| Aspect | Side | House | Figure | Agreement |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| sextile | dexter | 11 | Puella | same element (air) |
| sextile | sinister | 3 | Amissio | none |
| square | dexter | 10 | Puer | none |
| square | sinister | 4 | Caput Draconis | none |
| trine | dexter | 9 | Carcer | none |
| trine | sinister | 5 | Conjunctio | same planet (Mercury), same element (air) |
| opposition | none | 7 | Via | none |

Two aspects support the querent. The trine sinister finds the querent's own figure, Conjunctio, repeated in the fifth, with same planet and same element. The sextile dexter finds Puella in the eleventh, agreeing by element in the house of friends and hopes. The querent is therefore supported by the fifth and eleventh. The harsher figures that behold him, Puer by square and Carcer by trine dexter, have no agreement with him.

Now the marriage's own figure: Via in the seventh. Its agreement column is empty from top to bottom: sextiles from Conjunctio and Carcer, squares from Caput Draconis and Puer, trines from Amissio and Puella, opposition from Conjunctio, and not one agreement of figure, element, or planet among the seven. Puella beholds the seventh by trine and still has no agreement with Via. The quesited is regarded from every side but supported by none. Its dignity line is mixed: Via's entry includes the seventh among her good houses because she signifies letters and good news, but the intro classes the seventh among evil houses, and Via remains the figure that "breaketh and spoileth al the goodnes of the others." For marriage, this is not an easy placement: mobile, angular, and unsupported by aspect.

The review line: *Perfected by conjunction; supported unevenly.* The yes stands, because aspects do not overturn perfection, but the support is on the querent's side, not on the marriage's own figure. Set that beside the table row "a wife: ill" under a gainful Judge: the accord takes effect, but the chart gives little support to the bond itself. Lesson 10 gives the finished reading.

### Chart C: the conflict strengthened

"Will the querent win the lawsuit?" Lesson 6 left the chart divided: a favorable summary against difficult placements. Lesson 7 denied perfection. This lesson measures the condition of the difficult finding, and the measurement makes the conflict stronger.

Read the seventh's Cauda Draconis by both tools. Dignity first: angular, and doubled in the eleventh, so strong by place; but the seventh is an evil house and is not among the Tail's good seats (the fourth, sixth, ninth, and twelfth, where endings may be wanted). An ill figure, ill-suited to the question, placed strongly. Now the aspects:

| Aspect | Side | House | Figure | Agreement |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| sextile | dexter | 5 | Via | same element (water) |
| sextile | sinister | 9 | Populus | same element (water) |
| square | dexter | 4 | Fortuna Minor | none |
| square | sinister | 10 | Caput Draconis | none |
| trine | dexter | 3 | Puella | none |
| trine | sinister | 11 | Cauda Draconis | same planet (South Node), same element (water) |
| opposition | none | 1 | Amissio | none |

Three agreements appear, the most any significator has in the course charts. Both sextiles agree by element, water with water, from the fifth and ninth. The trine sinister finds Cauda Draconis repeated in the eleventh, same planet and same element. The seventh's figure is therefore strongly supported. The querent's Amissio in the first is angular too, but its map has only one agreement: the sextile sinister from Puella in the third, Venus with Venus. The opposition between Amissio and Cauda Draconis has no agreement at all.

The Witness–Judge summary is unchanged: the matter still ends in gain, and an aspect map cannot amend the Judge. What has changed is the strength of the difficult finding: by angularity, doubling, and aspect support, Cauda Draconis in the house of the suit is strong. Strength is not goodness; the seventh's figure is ill, ill-suited, and strongly placed, and what that strength means for the querent requires the finished reading of Lesson 10. Update the provisional denial: *denied; the denial rests on a strong, difficult seventh-house finding*.

## Worked example

Two examples follow: first the lesson's central distinction on one figure in two places, then the complete aspect-and-dignity review of Chart B, the chart Lesson 7 denied.

### One figure, two places

Take Amissio in two houses from one sentence of the good-and-ill-houses series: "*Amissio* is good for losse of substance, and therefore is good in the eight house, and very ill in the second" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 2).

Set the two placements side by side. In the **second house**, Amissio stands on the querent's own substance: the figure of loss in the house of keeping. The series calls this "very ill." In the **eighth house**, the same figure stands among death, debts, dowries, and other people's goods. There the series calls her good, because loss may be proper to that house. The figure has not changed. The department has changed, and the same finding that harms the second house can work properly in the eighth.

This is not hypothetical: **Chart C's second house holds Amissio**, the placement the series grades "very ill," and it is a double of the first-house Amissio. In a lawsuit, the querent and his money both carry loss; add that dignity line beside the seventh-house review. The method is the same for every figure: grade a placement by the fit between what the figure does and what the house wants, not by the figure's nature alone.

**The dignity lines**, figure by figure, for everything the reading leans on (all series entries: Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 2):

- **Puella, 1st:** angular, good house, "very good in all things that ye may demaunde"; firmly placed but almost unsupported by aspect.
- **Carcer, 7th:** angular in an evil house, and the entry is severe on exactly this placement: "ill in all the houses, and especially in the sixt, eight, *seventh* and twelfth." Stable, doubled, supported by dexter sextile from its own double: ill and strong by every check.
- **Carcer, 5th:** succedent; its role in this question is to support the seventh.
- **Rubeus, 2nd:** succedent; the second is among Rubeus's ill houses: disorder on the querent's substance.
- **Amissio, 4th:** angular, mean house; loss in the house of the matter's end, and the querent's one planetary agreement: mixed.
- **Tristitia, 12th:** cadent; good in the twelfth by the series, but far from the question.
- **Cauda Draconis, 10th:** angular in a good house, but the entry's good seats do not include the tenth; reason from the general parts and say so.

**The strength order**, by condition (place, house-class, series grading, attendance), with nature kept in a separate column:

1. **Carcer in the 7th:** angular, doubled, twin-sextiled dexter with double agreement, exalted by the modern table: the strongest figure in the chart, and an ill one, sitting on the question.
2. **Puella in the 1st:** angular, good house, excellent nature, one agreement by square: a good figure at middling force.
3. **Cauda Draconis in the 10th:** angular and ill-suited: difficult finding in a strong place.
4. **Amissio in the 4th:** angular in a mean house: end-of-matter finding, moderately strong and mixed.
5. **Tristitia in the 12th:** dignified by the series but cadent and far from the question.
6. **Rubeus in the 2nd and 3rd:** ill by place in the second, succedent and cadent: real but secondary finding about substance and kin.

**Do the aspects soften the denial?** A softened denial would show agreement between the significators or strong support for the querent. Chart B shows neither: the only aspect between the parties is an opposition without agreement (Cattan's "great discord, contrarietie, and enmitie" configuration), and the strongest agreement in either map serves Carcer, the figure in the house of the broken partnership. The aspects of Chart B do not soften the denial; they strengthen it. The review line: *Denied, and the review makes the denial stronger: the quesited's Carcer is the strongest figure in the chart; the significators share no agreement.* The final wording belongs to Lesson 10.

    1. It stands *between the significators themselves*; it is *continuous*, planets applying, perfecting and separating, so the angle carries tense and narrative; and its quality is *doubly determined*, by the geometry and by the natures of the two planets in it.

<Exercise n={6} title="Strong ill and weak good" kind="quiz">
  This quiz separates two ideas: whether a finding is good or bad for the querent, and whether that finding is strong enough to operate.

  1. Define the two cases precisely.
  2. Which of the two is the more *reliable* finding, and why?
  3. What standing demonstration does Chart B give of a strong ill figure?
  4. Tristitia strongly placed in a twelfth-house question of imprisonment is:

     - b) ill-natured but well-tasked: sorrow doing exactly the work the matter needs

  5. Why is the debilitated good figure treacherous rather than merely weak?
  6. State the maxim this lesson built toward, about nature and condition.

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. A well-dignified ill figure is an unfortunate nature in a strong condition: angular, suited to its house or exalted, attended by agreeing aspects ("a figure can not well be judged" without "the exaltation and the fall of the fygures," Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 23). A debilitated good figure is a fortunate nature in a weak condition: cadent, ill-housed for its kind, or unsupported by aspect.
    2. The dignified ill figure, whatever it portends. Strength is the probability of performance, so the strong figure's finding is the one more likely to take effect.
    3.     6. Nature tells you what the finding means. Condition tells you how strongly that finding can act.

## You are ready to continue when…

- You can build the aspect map of any house in under a minute, wraps correct, and name the four houses that behold nothing.
- You can state and apply the rule from chapter 12: the figure found in the beholding house decides the finding.
- You can judge an aspect's quality by agreement (same figure, element, planet, then the friendships) in the course-default system.
- You can call any figure strong or weak by place three ways: angular class, exaltation with its system named, and the good-and-ill-houses series with the entry cited.
- You can say, with cases, why strength is not goodness.
- You can summarize the reviews of all three course charts: Chart A's aspect support belongs mainly to the querent, Chart B's denial becomes stronger, and Chart C's difficult seventh-house finding becomes stronger but is not resolved yet.
