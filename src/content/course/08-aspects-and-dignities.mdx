Lesson 7's perfection scan answers whether the querent's figure and the quesited's connect, and therefore whether the thing asked for reaches the asker. It leaves two things unmeasured: the quality of the findings around the parties, and the strength of each figure's placement. Two charts may both perfect, but with very different support. Two charts may both deny, but one denial may be much stronger than the other. Those questions, quality and strength, are this lesson's subject.

The sources use two tools for this weighing. **Aspects** tell you which houses behold which: which figures regard your significators, from what angle, and with what agreement or hostility. **Dignities** tell you how strongly each figure stands: well placed, badly placed, exalted, fallen, or supported by a house that suits it. Together they turn the placements of Lesson 5 and the yes-or-no scan of Lesson 7 into a weighed reading. Each finding has both content and force.

They also teach a distinction that is easy to miss: **strength is not goodness.** A figure can be ill and strongly placed, or good and weakly placed. A chart is not fortunate merely because it contains many good figures; those figures may be weak, while one ill figure stands strongly in the decisive house. By the end you will review all three course charts on both axes. One chart comes out worse than before, and Chart C remains unresolved until Lesson 10.

## The regards in general

Cattan opens the aspect rule with a four-sentence chapter, and every part matters:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-12-of-the-aspectes-in-generall" />

Five aspects: conjunction, sextile, trine, quadrate (the square), opposition. The names and the basic idea come from astrology. In astrology, planets behold one another at certain angular distances around the zodiac: a sixth of the circle, a quarter, a third, and half. The sextile and trine are friendly aspects; the square and opposition are harder aspects. Geomancy applies the same distances to the twelve houses. A figure beholds the figures standing at those distances from it. Exercise 5 will ask what the transposition preserves and what it gives up.

Cattan's last part gives the rule: figures that behold by aspect, "if they be good they be the better, if they be ill they be the worse... according to the houses where they be, and according to the figures good or bad." The aspect is a relation. The finding is decided by **the figure found in the beholding house**: its goodness or illness, its element, its planet, and the house it occupies. A trine is friendly in form, but if Tristitia stands there, the finding is still sorrowful. In geomancy, sextile and trine are not automatically good news, and square and opposition are not automatically bad news. The figure at the other end decides what the aspect carries.

One housekeeping note before the distances. Cattan's first aspect, the conjunction, is not new to you. His chapter on it points straight back at material you already own:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-13-of-the-aspect-of-conjunction" />

The conjunction "of the figure" is the material of Lesson 7's modes of perfection: one figure doubling into a neighbouring house and making company. This chapter does not change that rule. It adds one note: certain figures contain a conjunction of planets within themselves. Via, the Moon's figure, is read by Cattan as Sun and Moon conjoined, hot against cold, ill by inner contrariety "except the question be made for the way, for then it is good." That exception matters for Chart A later. The new material in this lesson is the four aspects at a distance.

## Counting the regards

The four distant aspects are defined by distance around the wheel, and Cattan states each distance in the inclusive counting of his century: both endpoints counted, so that neighbours two doors apart are "from three to three." Learn his phrasing, because you will meet it in every Renaissance text, and learn the translation, because your reviews will be done in plain steps:

- **Sextile.** Cattan: the first beholds the third, "and so consequently from three houses to three," and also the first beholds the eleventh. In plain steps: **two houses away, on either side**.
- **Square** (quadrate): "from foure fygures unto foure figures": the first to the fourth, or the first to the tenth. **Three houses away, on either side.**
- **Trine.** "fygures from 5. to 5. doe beholde themselves": the first, the fifth and the ninth. **Four houses away, on either side.**
- **Opposition.** "from seven to seven": the first beholds the seventh. **Six houses away**, and six is half of twelve, so there is only one opposition, directly across the wheel, with no two sides.

Each of the first three aspects therefore looks both ways, and the two directions have names inherited from astrology. Counting *backward*, against the order of the houses, from the first house to the eleventh, the tenth, the ninth, is the **dexter** aspect; counting *forward* with the order, from the first to the third, the fourth, the fifth, is the **sinister**. The terms are simply Latin right and left, with none of the modern stain on "sinister"; a sinister trine is not a sinister thing. Cattan describes both directions every time, "the first beholdeth the third... and also, when the first beholdes... the 11," without naming them; the names, and one sharpening, come from the modern comparison:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/aspects" />

There is the whole counting rule in two lines (the Ascendant beheld by 11, 10, 9 dexter, by 3, 4, 5 sinister, with 7 in opposition) plus a rule the Renaissance text does not state: the dexter aspect, thrown against the grain of the houses, is the *stronger* of the two. This course adopts the dexter-stronger rule as a working refinement, flagged as the modern formulation it is; nothing in Cattan contradicts it, and the astrological sources behind both authors support it. The same Crowley passage also hands you the modern summary of figure-friendships (elements sympathetic to themselves, friendly or hostile to others, and the planets' friends and enemies listed) which you will use as a quick reckoner when the strict agreements (same figure, same element, same planet) run out.

The skill is mechanical. Take any house; the houses it beholds are found by stepping ±2, ±3, ±4 and +6 around the wheel, wrapping past twelve back to one. For the first house:

| Aspect | Dexter (backward) | Sinister (forward) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sextile (±2) | 11 | 3 |
| Square (±3) | 10 | 4 |
| Trine (±4) | 9 | 5 |
| Opposition (+6) | 7 | none |

Seven houses behold the first; four houses (the second, the sixth, the eighth and the twelfth) do not behold it at all, and figures standing there regard the querent with no aspect whatsoever. Run the same arithmetic from the seventh and you get its mirror: sextiles to 5 and 9, squares to 4 and 10, trines to 3 and 11, opposition to 1. The mirror means **the first and seventh houses always behold one another, in opposition**. The querent and the other party stand eye to eye across the wheel in every chart ever cast, by the geometry of the houses themselves. Every two-party question therefore carries one built-in opposition, and its quality is judged like every other aspect: by the agreement of the figures at its two ends. Cattan's opposition chapter, below, is largely an essay on exactly this.

The wrap-around takes practice: from the ninth, the trine dexter lands on the fifth and the trine sinister on the *first* (9 + 4 = 13, wrapping to 1); from the eleventh, the sextile sinister is the first house and the opposition the fifth. Exercise 1 drills it.

## The Sextile

Here is the sextile chapter: definition, rule, and Cattan's example of a question about buying a horse.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-14-of-the-sextile-aspect" />

Take the rule first. The sextile "signifies means and unperfit amitie" (a mild, imperfect friendship, the weakest of the channels) and the reading rule is the general rule in miniature: a good figure in the sextile place, agreeing with the first "and be of the same Element and of one planet, or that they be both alike," promises "good fortune and good issue"; an ill figure there, of contrary planet and element, and "you shall judge ill of the question made, and of enmitie." The aspect is the introduction; the figures decide whether the introduction is to a friend.

The case shows the rule in use. Cattan reads by his own attributions, not this course's defaults: in his triplicities Puella is water, Fortuna Minor fire, and Puer air. The shield as printed runs right to left, so count the houses accordingly. The querent's first house holds Puella, Venus's figure, water in Cattan's system. Her sinister sextile, the third house, holds Fortuna Minor, the Sun's figure, fire: planet and element both contrary to the querent's. Cattan reads this as advice from someone close, because the third house is kin and neighbours, but not fully helpful advice. The dexter sextile, the eleventh house of friends, holds Puer, Mars, air. Air agrees poorly with water, but Venus and Mars are friendly in his system, so a friend is still involved. The finding is mixed because the agreements are mixed. Cattan then says it is not enough to judge by the sextile alone, and he goes on to the angles, Witnesses, Judge, Part of Fortune, and way of the point. One aspect is one finding, never the whole reading.

## The Trine

The trine chapter gives a question of war and peace between Francis I and Emperor Charles V, cast when Cardinal Trivulsio came as legate to broker terms:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-15-of-the-tryne-aspect-chapt" />

The rule: the trine (first, fifth and ninth, "from 5. to 5.") "is naturally good and perfect amitie... by reason of the house, place and fygure." But the case shows that the figure still decides the finding. The first house is beheld by trine from the fifth, and the fifth holds **Tristitia**, Saturn's figure of sorrow. Cattan writes: "because the first is beheld with an ill Aspect from the fift which is a fygure of ♄ retrograde, called *Tristitia*, it signifyed that there should bee no peace made at this time." The fifth beholds the first by trine, yet Cattan calls it an "ill Aspect" because the figure in the fifth is ill. **The aspect is judged by the figure found in it.**

The rest of the reading follows the same rule. The ninth, house of churchmen, holds a good figure, so the Cardinal would work honestly for peace. The seventh, the other party, holds an ill figure, so the Emperor would refuse it. The sextile from the eleventh is ill too. The Witnesses and Judge also incline to Saturn with the fifth. The answer is no peace. Cattan then reports the outcome: the Emperor seized Provence as far as Aix, held it briefly, and lost all he had taken. The transcription also records a flaw in the print: the narrative names the first figure Conjunctio while the cramped rightmost compartment of the woodcut shows Via.

## The square and the opposition

The two hard channels. First the square:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-16-of-the-quadrats-aspect" />

"From foure to foure" means three plain steps, the first house to the fourth or the tenth. Cattan does not treat the square as simply evil. He judges it by agreement: a good figure in the square place, of the same quality and planet, is a good sign; a good figure of different element and planet makes the aspect only "meane"; and a figure whose planet is friendly to the significator can make the regard good. Acquisitio in the first and Puella in the tenth, Jupiter and Venus, is his example. His worked example, the Count of Touerra's question about when the Emperor would leave Nice for Provence, turns the rule to timing. Acquisitio, a stable figure, stands in the first; Puella stands at the square in the tenth; the Witnesses and Judge are also stable. Cattan judges delay, and the Emperor waits for Andrea Doria and provisions for his soldiers. Strength of place and fixity of figure are already part of the case.

Then the opposition, with the chapter's summary rule:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-17-of-the-aspect-of-opposition" />

The opposition "is an ill Aspect as well for the distance of the place as for the contrarietie," and it is the one aspect with no second side. But it is not simply ill. If the figures at the two ends are both good and akin, "both of one Element, qualitie, nature and Planet," then the opposition has "no contrarietie... but contrariwise singuler allyance and friendshippe," and the issue is good. A good figure facing an evil one is "great discord, contrarietie, and enmitie," and ill for the querent. The opposition is the fixed confrontation between the two parties of a question, and the figures decide whether that confrontation is friendly or hostile. Cattan's case, whether the Count of Novellara was dead after being wounded in camp, turns on this: first and seventh of one element, water with water, so the man lived when the news was brought; fourth and tenth contrary, so he would die of the wound. The chapter closes with the rule for all regards: "when yee fynde a good fygure in any of these Aspectes, you shall judge well and goodnesse: and if yee fynde an ill Planet there yee shall judge ill."

The four chapters yield one working rule. For any house you care about, usually the first and the house of the quesited, find the seven houses that behold it. For each beholding house, ask three questions. What figure stands there? What agreement does it have with the figure beheld: same figure, same element, same planet, or only planetary friendship? What aspect carries the relation: trine, sextile, square, or opposition, dexter or sinister? A good figure with strong agreement gives supportive finding. An ill figure with no agreement gives hostile finding. You will run this review on the significators of all three standing charts before the lesson ends.

## The triplicities of figures

Now the second tool: dignity, strength by place. The aspects ask who beholds a figure; the dignities ask how firmly the figure itself is planted. The sources' first answer is the triplicity, and Cattan's chapter is the source:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-22-of-the-triplicitie-of-figures-and" />

The main point: figures of one element form a *triplicity*, a group of shared quality, with planetary governors by day and by night on the astrological model. The triplicities are a way to weigh whole charts. "Above all thinges you must have a regarde unto the triplicitie," Cattan says. If most of the chart belongs to one element, judge the chart according to that element's nature. If the chart is split, judge by the stronger part and by the governors. A chart dominated by fire behaves differently from a chart dominated by earth, whatever the individual placements. This is dignity at a broad scale: a figure is stronger when the chart around it shares its element.

Here the sources split. Cattan's air triplicity is Acquisitio, Laetitia, Puer, and Conjunctio; his fire is Rubeus, Amissio, Cauda Draconis, and Fortuna Minor; his water is Via, Populus, Albus, and Puella; his earth is Fortuna Major, Tristitia, Caput Draconis, and Carcer. Lesson 3 fixed this course's default from Agrippa. Several figures move between systems: Acquisitio is water in the default but air for Cattan; Puella is air in the default but water for Cattan; Albus, Fortuna Minor, and Fortuna Major also move. Keep the course policy: **one system in the hands** and **the whole source set in the head.** This lesson's reviews use the course default. Cattan's cases use Cattan's system. Do not mix them in one chart.

## Exaltation, joy and fall

Cattan's next chapter pushes the astrological transposition one layer further. In astrology, the planets are not equally strong everywhere: each has signs of exaltation, joy, fall, and detriment. Cattan maps the rule onto the figures in a table:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-23-the-exaltation-joy-and-fall-of" />

Each row is a planet. Around it stand three signs: the sign of the planet's exaltation, joy, and fall. Beneath each sign are the geomantic figures that sign owns in Cattan's attributions. The working sense is this: **a figure is strong where its planet is dignified and weak where its planet falls.** Cattan gives the table with little prose. His opening sentence says that "a figure can not well be judged" without knowing "the exaltation and the fall of the fygures," and he leaves the mapping from signs to houses to the practitioner's astrological knowledge.

The modern comparison makes that last step explicit:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/dignities" />

Crowley's rule treats the twelve houses as the twelve signs: Ascendant as Aries, second house as Taurus, and so on to the twelfth as Pisces. That turns the apparatus into a lookup. A figure is strongest in the house answering to its planet's own sign, very strong in exaltation, strong in triplicity, very weak in fall, and weakest in detriment. His chapter VI prints the full table. Keep the layers labelled: the houses-as-signs equation is a modern development of a Renaissance rule that Cattan states only as a planetary table. The two do not always agree, because Crowley's attributions differ from Cattan's. Use the modern table as a reckoner; cite Cattan for the Renaissance rule.

Retain three points. Dignity by place is a real rule in both source lines. The rule depends on sign attributions, so every exaltation call must name its system. For daily practice, Cattan gives a simpler tool that needs no zodiac.

## Every figure's good and ill houses

Book III, chapter 2 is the main practical source for geomantic dignity: a figure-by-figure list of where each stands well and where it stands ill. It opens with a general grading of the houses:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/intro" />

Good houses are the first, fifth, tenth, and eleventh; mean houses are the second, third, fourth, and ninth; evil houses are the sixth, seventh, eighth, and twelfth. This is not the same as Lesson 5's angular, succedent, and cadent strength. The seventh and fourth are angles, but Cattan classes them as evil and mean. The eleventh is succedent, but he classes it as good. Strength by angle measures how firmly a figure is placed. The good-and-ill grading measures whether the house suits the figure and matter. Keep them as separate columns in your review.

Then the figure-by-figure entries, each a sentence or two of concentrated experience. Here are the ones the course charts will need, beginning with the figure that owns Chart A's question:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/via" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/cauda-draconis" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/amissio" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/conjunctio" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/puella" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/carcer" />

Via "breaketh and spoileth al the goodnes of the others" and yet is "good in the third, fift and seventh houses, because she signifyeth that letters shall come which shall bring good newes," with the standing exception for journeys renewed. Cauda Draconis is "found good in the fourth, sixt, ninth and twelfth houses": the Tail can work well in houses of endings, sickness, and confinement, where passing out of a condition may be wanted. Its opening part, "very good with the ill, and very ill with the good," is the strength-is-not-goodness rule in short form. Amissio is good in the eighth and "very ill in the second," where loss stands on the querent's own purse. Carcer is "ill in all the houses, and especially in the sixt, eight, seventh and twelfth"; mark the seventh, because Chart B will need it. Puella is "very good in all things that ye may demaunde."

Count the sources, as in Lesson 6. Heydon prints the same series in *Theomagia*:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/good-ill-houses" />

Set it against Cattan part by part and you will find what you found with the Witnesses in Lesson 6: Heydon reprints Cattan nearly verbatim seventy years later. The series therefore has one printed source transmitted twice, not two independent sources. Heydon's closing material about telesmes and astromancy is his own addition and not part of the technique. The tally is: Cattan's rule, transmitted by Heydon, and mechanised differently by the Golden Dawn.

The entries do not always align with the triplicity and exaltation apparatus. Sometimes they agree: Saturn's relation to the twelfth and Tristitia's goodness in the eighth and twelfth fit well together. Sometimes they differ because the series follows meaning while the astrological apparatus follows planetary dignity. Amissio is good in the eighth because loss belongs there, not because Venus is dignified there. Via is good in the third because the third is the house of letters and Via signifies things arriving. For this course, the good-and-ill-houses series is the primary dignity tool: clear, citable, and independent of attribution disputes. Triplicities and exaltations refine it; angular strength supports it; together they form the dignity review.

## Strength is not goodness

The lesson's two halves form one skill. Every figure in a chart carries two independent measures. Its **nature** tells you what it signifies. Its **condition** tells you how strongly it can act: angular or cadent, in a good or bad house for its kind, well or badly beheld, supported by its element or isolated. Keep the two measures separate:

- **Good and strong:** a fortunate figure, angular, dignified, and well beheld.
- **Good and weak:** a fortunate figure cadent, badly housed for its kind, or unsupported by aspect.
- **Ill and weak:** an unfortunate figure, badly placed and unsupported.
- **Ill and strong:** an unfortunate figure angular, suited to the house, or strongly supported by aspect. A well-dignified ill figure does not become kind; it becomes effective.

The hard cases are the last two. Students often fear the ill figure and trust the good one without asking how strongly either is placed. Cauda Draconis in the twelfth is an ill figure that can be good there, because the house of confinement may want release or ending. Laetitia is a fortunate figure, but the series calls it ill in the sixth, eighth, and twelfth. The question is never only "which figure is better?" It is "better where, for what, and in what strength?" Exercise 6 tests the hard case: dignified ill against debilitated good. The short answer is that the dignified ill figure is usually worse for the querent's hopes and always more reliable, because strength makes any figure's finding likelier to operate. Strength is not goodness. Strength is the likelihood that what the figure signifies will be carried out.

## Update the journal

The procedure now adds its fifth tool. Keep everything already recorded: the cast and verification from Lesson 4, Step 0 and placements from Lesson 5, the triad summary from Lesson 6, and the perfection scan and provisional answer from Lesson 7. Add the **aspect-and-dignity review** for the three course charts and every new cast:

1. **The aspect maps, built.** For the first house and the quesited's house: the seven beholding houses by the ±2/±3/±4/+6 arithmetic, dexter and sinister labelled, the figure found in each, and its agreement with the significator (same figure, same element, same planet, in the course-default system) with disagreements left visibly blank. A map with no agreements is a finding, not a failure.
2. **The dignity lines, written.** For each significator, and for any figure the reading leans on: angular, succedent or cadent; the house's class from the intro (good, mean, evil); the figure's own entry from the good-and-ill-houses series, quoted or cited; and, where you use them, triplicity and exaltation calls with their system named.
3. **The provisional answer, weighted.** Lesson 7 let you write a provisional answer; this lesson lets you weight it. Under each answer add one line: *strengthened*, *unchanged*, or *qualified*, with the review fact that does the weighting. The answer itself still comes from perfection. An aspect review never turns denial into perfection, or perfection into denial. It tells you how strongly the answer is supported.
4. **Still short of a finished reading.** The full weighing of all findings belongs to Lesson 10. Until then the review is evidence recorded, not a final answer. Chart C below shows why the distinction matters.

## The course charts checked

Now the three standing charts. Chart B's complete review is the worked example below. Here are Charts A and C: one where the aspects qualify a perfected chart, and one where they make an existing conflict stronger.

### Chart A: the marriage, perfected and qualified

"Will the marriage go ahead?" Lesson 7's scan found the chart perfects by conjunction, with the quesited's own Via standing in the second house beside the querent. The provisional answer remains *yes, by conjunction*. Now ask this lesson's questions: how well supported is the meeting, and where is the strength?

The querent first: Conjunctio in the first. The tables below are the review performed, as your journal should perform it:

| Aspect | Side | House | Figure | Agreement |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| sextile | dexter | 11 | Puella | same element (air) |
| sextile | sinister | 3 | Amissio | none |
| square | dexter | 10 | Puer | none |
| square | sinister | 4 | Caput Draconis | none |
| trine | dexter | 9 | Carcer | none |
| trine | sinister | 5 | Conjunctio | same planet (Mercury), same element (air) |
| opposition | none | 7 | Via | none |

Two aspects support the querent. The trine sinister finds the querent's own figure, Conjunctio, repeated in the fifth, with same planet and same element. The sextile dexter finds Puella in the eleventh, agreeing by element in the house of friends and hopes. The querent is therefore supported by the fifth and eleventh. The harsher figures that behold him, Puer by square and Carcer by trine dexter, have no agreement with him.

Now the marriage's own figure: Via in the seventh. Its agreement column is empty from top to bottom: sextiles from Conjunctio and Carcer, squares from Caput Draconis and Puer, trines from Amissio and Puella, opposition from Conjunctio, and not one agreement of figure, element, or planet among the seven. Puella beholds the seventh by trine and still has no agreement with Via. The quesited is regarded from every side but supported by none. Its dignity line is mixed: Via's entry includes the seventh among her good houses because she signifies letters and good news, but the intro classes the seventh among evil houses, and Via remains the figure that "breaketh and spoileth al the goodnes of the others." For marriage, this is not an easy placement: mobile, angular, and unsupported by aspect.

The journal's weighting line should read: *Perfected by conjunction; supported unevenly.* The yes stands because aspects do not overturn perfection. But the support is on the querent's side, not on the marriage's own figure. The asker is well supported; the match itself is changeable and unsupported by aspect. Set that beside Lesson 6's table row for this triad ("a wife: ill" under a gainful Judge): the matter ends in gain, the accord takes effect, but the chart gives little support to the bond itself. Lesson 10 will weigh the whole.

### Chart C: the conflict strengthened

"Will the querent win the lawsuit?" Lesson 6 left the chart divided: a favorable Witness–Judge summary, Caput Draconis, Albus, Acquisitio, against difficult placements in the houses. The querent has Amissio doubled into his money's house, while Cauda Draconis stands in the seventh house of the adversary and repeats in the eleventh. Lesson 7 denied perfection: no occupation, no conjunction joins the first house to the seventh. This lesson does not resolve the conflict. It measures the condition of the difficult finding, and that measurement makes the conflict stronger.

Read the seventh's Cauda Draconis by both tools. Dignity first. By the angular skeleton, it is strong because it stands in the seventh, and it is doubled by repetition in the eleventh. By the series, the seventh is an evil house and is not among Cauda Draconis's good houses. Her entry names the fourth, sixth, ninth, and twelfth, houses where endings and release may be wanted. A suit the querent hopes to win is not such a matter. The entry's first part also applies: "very good with the ill, and very ill with the good." This is an ill figure, ill-suited to the question, placed strongly. Now the aspects:

| Aspect | Side | House | Figure | Agreement |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| sextile | dexter | 5 | Via | same element (water) |
| sextile | sinister | 9 | Populus | same element (water) |
| square | dexter | 4 | Fortuna Minor | none |
| square | sinister | 10 | Caput Draconis | none |
| trine | dexter | 3 | Puella | none |
| trine | sinister | 11 | Cauda Draconis | same planet (South Node), same element (water) |
| opposition | none | 1 | Amissio | none |

Three agreements appear, the most any significator has in the course charts. Both sextiles agree by element, water with water, from the fifth and ninth. The trine sinister finds Cauda Draconis repeated in the eleventh, same planet and same element. The seventh's figure is therefore strongly supported. The querent's Amissio in the first is angular too, but its map has only one agreement: the sextile sinister from Puella in the third, Venus with Venus. The opposition between Amissio and Cauda Draconis has no agreement at all.

The Witness–Judge summary is unchanged: the matter still ends in gain, and an aspect map cannot amend the Judge. What has changed is the strength of the difficult house finding. By angularity, doubling, and aspect support, Cauda Draconis in the house of the suit is strong. The modern table also supports that reading, if cited only as a modern comparison: by Crowley's houses-as-signs reckoner, the seventh is a place of the Tail's triplicity-strength. Strength is not goodness. The seventh's figure is ill, ill-suited, and strongly placed. Whether that strength harms the querent, harms the adversary, or means something subtler requires the finished reading of Lesson 10. Weight the provisional denial accordingly: *denied; the denial's key finding is now measured and found strong*. Leave the answer column empty.

## Worked example

Two examples follow: first the lesson's central distinction on one figure in two places, then the complete aspect-and-dignity review of Chart B, the chart Lesson 7 denied.

### One figure, two places

Take Amissio and weigh it twice from one sentence of the good-and-ill-houses series: "*Amissio* is good for losse of substance, and therefore is good in the eight house, and very ill in the second" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 2).

Set the two placements side by side. In the **second house**, Amissio stands on the querent's own substance: the figure of loss in the house of keeping. The series calls this "very ill." In the **eighth house**, the same figure stands among death, debts, dowries, and other people's goods. There the series calls her good, because loss may be proper to that house. The figure has not changed. The department has changed, and the same finding that harms the second house can work properly in the eighth.

This is not hypothetical: **Chart C's second house holds Amissio**, the placement the series grades "very ill," and it is a double of the first-house Amissio. In a lawsuit, the querent and his money both carry loss. Add that dignity line to Chart C's journal entry beside the seventh-house review. The method is the same for every figure: grade a placement by the fit between what the figure does and what the house wants, not by the figure's nature alone.

### Chart B, checked entire

"Will the querent recover the partnership?" The chart, dealt into its houses:

By the modern reckoner, Venus and Saturn are enemies and air and earth are hostile; the softer checks agree with the blank strict agreements. The two significators share nothing.

**The dignity lines**, figure by figure, for everything the reading leans on (all series entries: Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 2):

- **Puella, 1st:** angular; the first is a good house by the intro; the figure is "very good in all things that ye may demaunde." Good and firmly placed, but almost unsupported by aspect.
- **Carcer, 7th:** angular; the seventh is an evil house; and the entry is severe on exactly this placement: "ill in all the houses, and especially in the sixt, eight, *seventh* and twelfth." The seventh is named among the worst seats of a figure ill everywhere, a figure that "signifyeth alwayes to be stayed." Carcer is stable, doubled in the chart, and supported by dexter sextile from its own double. The modern table also sets Saturn's figures exalted in the seventh, but cite that only as the modern comparison. Every check returns the same measurement: ill and strong.
- **Carcer, 5th:** succedent, in a good house, lending its strength to its twin by the strongest agreement in the chart. The fifth's own matters take Carcer badly, but this figure's role in this question is to support the seventh.
- **Rubeus, 2nd:** succedent; the entry names the second among Rubeus's ill houses: disorder on the querent's substance, moderately seated.
- **Amissio, 4th:** angular, mean house; the figure of loss in the house of the matter's end, and the querent's one planetary agreement. This is mixed finding.
- **Tristitia, 12th:** cadent; Tristitia is "a very ill figure in all the houses," but is good in the eighth and twelfth. It is a dignified ill figure, though far from the question.
- **Cauda Draconis, 10th:** angular in a good house, but the entry's good seats (4, 6, 9, 12) do not include the tenth, and the figure is "very ill with the good." The series is silent on the tenth by name; reason from the parts, and say so when you cite.

**The strength-ranking**, by condition (place, house-class, series grading, attendance), with nature kept in a separate column:

1. **Carcer in the 7th:** angular, doubled, twin-sextiled dexter with double agreement, exalted by the modern table: the strongest figure in the chart, and an ill one, sitting on the question.
2. **Puella in the 1st:** angular, good house, excellent nature, one agreement by square: a good figure at middling force.
3. **Cauda Draconis in the 10th:** angular and ill-suited: difficult finding in a strong place.
4. **Amissio in the 4th:** angular in a mean house: end-of-matter finding, moderately strong and mixed.
5. **Tristitia in the 12th:** dignified by the series but cadent and far from the question.
6. **Rubeus in the 2nd and 3rd:** ill by place in the second, succedent and cadent: real but secondary finding about substance and kin.

**And the question: do the aspects soften the denial?** The answer must come from the maps. A softened denial would show good agreement between the significators, or strong support for the querent's side. Chart B shows neither. The only aspect between the significators is opposition, and it has no agreement. That is Cattan's "great discord, contrarietie, and enmitie" configuration, not his "singuler allyance" one. The querent's only agreement is a square from Amissio in the fourth. The strongest agreement in either map serves Carcer, the figure in the house of the broken partnership. The aspects of Chart B do not soften the denial; they strengthen it. Write the weighting line under Lesson 7's answer: *Denied; and the denial weighted heavier by review: the quesited's Carcer is the strongest figure in the chart, twin-supported by sextile, especially ill in the house it holds; the significators share no agreement; the querent's one friendship arrives by square, from Amissio, in the house of ends.* The final wording of the reading belongs to Lesson 10.

## You are ready to continue when…

- You can build the aspect map of any house in under a minute (±2, ±3, ±4, +6, dexter and sinister labelled, wraps correct) and name the four houses that behold nothing.
- You can state the crucial rule from chapter 12 and apply it: the aspect is a channel, the figure found decides the finding, and you can cite Cattan calling a trine "an ill Aspect" because Tristitia stood in it.
- You can weigh an aspect's quality by agreement (same figure, element, planet, then the friendships) in the course-default system, while reading Cattan's own cases correctly in his.
- You can call any figure strong or weak by place three ways (the angular skeleton, the exaltation rule with its system named, and the good-and-ill-houses series with the entry cited) and you know why this course uses the good-and-ill-houses series as its main tool.
- You can say, with cases, why strength is not goodness, and grade a placement on both axes without letting either contaminate the other.
- You can recite the reviews of all three course charts: why Chart A's support belongs mostly to the querent, why Chart B's denial now weighs heavier, how Chart C's conflict was strengthened, and which lesson holds its resolution.
- Your journal carries the full procedure through the weighting lines, your two new casts are checked end to end, and the dignity entries come to you without the book.
