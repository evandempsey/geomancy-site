Lesson 7's perfection scan answers *whether* the querent's figure and the quesited's connect, and whether the matter is granted or denied. It leaves two things unmeasured: how warmly the chart regards the parties, and how strongly each one stands. Two charts may both perfect, one carrying the matter home, the other scraping it over the line; two may both deny, one a locked gate, the other merely left unlatched. Those two questions, quality and intensity, are this lesson's business.

A court does not weigh testimonies by counting them; it asks of each witness whether the testimony is friendly or hostile, and from where it is given. The tradition built two instruments for that weighing. The **aspects** tell you which houses behold which: which figures look at your significators, from what angle, with what friendship or enmity. The **dignities** tell you how strongly each figure stands: at home or in exile, exalted or fallen, in a house that favours it or starves it. Together they turn the flat placements of Lesson 5 and the bare yes-or-no of Lesson 7 into a weighed reading, where every testimony carries not only content but *force*.

They also deliver a distinction that is easy to miss: **strength is not goodness.** A figure can be ill and strongly placed (an enemy with the high ground) or good and weakly placed, a friend too far off to help. A student who has not separated the two axes will call a chart fortunate because its good figures are many, never noticing that they whisper from cadent corners while one ill figure, dignified and well-attended, holds the only house that matters. By the end you will audit all three course charts on both axes; one comes out looking worse than it went in, and the conflict this course has tended since Lesson 4 is sharpened again and handed, still unresolved, to Lesson 10.

## Before you begin

This lesson leans on Lesson 3's natures (planet and element for all sixteen figures, cold, since every aspect is judged by elemental and planetary kinship; and it quotes Cattan in his *own* differing attributions, so Lesson 3's policy of one system in the hands gets its hardest workout here); on Lesson 5's houses, especially the angular, succedent and cadent skeleton, which now becomes the first column of every dignity audit; and on the reading protocol of Lessons 6 and 7. Everything here *qualifies* the perfection verdict; nothing replaces the scan.

Cattan's Book III chapters 12–17 (the aspects, with two of his own consulting-room cases) and chapters 22–23 (the triplicities and the exaltations) are quoted and walked through below. The modern comparison is Crowley's chapter VI, the densest in his handbook: a fully mechanised dignity table and a one-paragraph aspect doctrine, where the Golden Dawn tightened screws the older art left deliberately loose. The [Essential Dignities](/techniques/essential-dignities/) and [Aspects and Company](/techniques/aspects-and-company/) technique pages are the reference summaries.

## The regards in general

Cattan opens the aspect doctrine with a chapter four sentences long, and every clause of it is load-bearing:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-12-of-the-aspectes-in-generall" />

Five aspects: conjunction, sextile, trine, quadrate (the square), opposition. The names are astrology's, and so is the idea beneath them. In the mother art, two planets behold one another when they stand at certain privileged angular distances around the zodiac (a sixth of the circle, a quarter, a third, half) and the geometry carries moral weight: the sextile and trine are aspects of friendship, the square and opposition aspects of strife. Geomancy, having no sky, did what it always does: it transposed the doctrine onto the instrument it has. The twelve houses stand in a circle; distances around that circle become the aspects; and a figure "beholds" the figures standing at the privileged distances from it. The transposition is audacious (Exercise 5 will quiz you on exactly how much survives it) but it is not arbitrary, and the working rule it yields is one of the most used in the Renaissance art.

Cattan's last clause is the chapter's real cargo: the figures which behold by these aspects, "if they be good they be the better, if they be ill they be the worse... according to the houses where they be, and according to the figures good or bad." The aspect itself is only a *channel*. What flows down the channel is decided by **the figure found in the beholding house** (its goodness or illness, its element, its planet) and by the house it stands in. A trine, the aspect of perfect amity, delivers warm testimony only if a warm figure stands at the trine; let Tristitia stand there and the channel of friendship carries you sorrow with excellent efficiency. Cattan applies exactly this in his casework below, a "good" aspect turning ill in his hands because of the figure found in it. In geomancy the sextile and trine are not good news, and the square and opposition are not bad news; they are *louder and quieter, friendlier and harder channels*, and the news is whatever figure is standing at the other end.

One housekeeping note before the distances. Cattan's first aspect, the conjunction, is not new to you. His chapter on it points straight back at doctrine you already own:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-13-of-the-aspect-of-conjunction" />

The conjunction "of the figure" (one figure doubling into the next house, making company) is the material of Lesson 7's modes of perfection, treated, as Cattan says, in his chapters of conjunctions and companies; nothing here amends it. What the little chapter adds is a curiosity worth filing: certain figures *contain* a conjunction of planets within themselves. Via, the Moon's figure, he reads as Sun and Moon conjoined, hot against cold, ill by inner contrariety "except the question be made for the way, for then it is good." It is one more instance of a pattern: every doctrine that touches Via ends with the same exception, and the exceptions will matter when Chart A comes back to the bar. The four aspects that are genuinely new business are the ones at a distance, and to those we now turn.

## Counting the regards

The four distant aspects are defined by distance around the wheel, and Cattan states each distance in the inclusive counting of his century: both endpoints counted, so that neighbours two doors apart are "from three to three." Learn his phrasing, because you will meet it in every Renaissance text, and learn the translation, because your audits will be done in plain steps:

- **Sextile.** Cattan: the first beholds the third, "and so consequently from three houses to three," and also the first beholds the eleventh. In plain steps: **two houses away, on either side**.
- **Square** (quadrate): "from foure fygures unto foure figures": the first to the fourth, or the first to the tenth. **Three houses away, on either side.**
- **Trine.** "fygures from 5. to 5. doe beholde themselves": the first, the fifth and the ninth. **Four houses away, on either side.**
- **Opposition.** "from seven to seven": the first beholds the seventh. **Six houses away**, and six is half of twelve, so there is only one opposition, directly across the wheel, with no two sides.

Each of the first three aspects therefore looks both ways, and the two directions have names inherited from astrology. Counting *backward*, against the order of the houses, from the first house to the eleventh, the tenth, the ninth, is the **dexter** aspect; counting *forward* with the order, from the first to the third, the fourth, the fifth, is the **sinister**. The terms are simply Latin right and left, with none of the modern stain on "sinister"; a sinister trine is not a sinister thing. Cattan describes both directions every time, "the first beholdeth the third... and also, when the first beholdes... the 11," without naming them; the names, and one sharpening, come from the modern comparison:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/aspects" />

There is the whole counting doctrine in two lines (the Ascendant beheld by 11, 10, 9 dexter, by 3, 4, 5 sinister, with 7 in opposition) plus a rule the Renaissance text does not state: the dexter aspect, thrown against the grain of the houses, is the *stronger* of the two. This course adopts the dexter-stronger rule as a working refinement, flagged as the modern formulation it is; nothing in Cattan contradicts it, and the astrological tradition behind both authors supports it. The same Crowley passage also hands you the modern summary of figure-friendships (elements sympathetic to themselves, friendly or hostile to others, and the planets' friends and enemies listed) which you will use as a quick reckoner when the strict agreements (same figure, same element, same planet) run out.

The skill is mechanical. Take any house; the houses it beholds are found by stepping ±2, ±3, ±4 and +6 around the wheel, wrapping past twelve back to one. For the first house:

| Aspect | Dexter (backward) | Sinister (forward) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sextile (±2) | 11 | 3 |
| Square (±3) | 10 | 4 |
| Trine (±4) | 9 | 5 |
| Opposition (+6) | 7 | none |

Seven houses behold the first; four houses (the second, the sixth, the eighth and the twelfth) do not behold it at all, and figures standing there regard the querent with no aspect whatsoever. Run the same arithmetic from the seventh and you get its mirror: sextiles to 5 and 9, squares to 4 and 10, trines to 3 and 11, opposition to 1. The mirror means **the first and seventh houses always behold one another, in opposition**. The querent and the other party stand eye to eye across the wheel in every chart ever cast, by the geometry of the houses themselves. Every two-party question therefore carries one built-in opposition, and its quality is judged like every other aspect: by the agreement of the figures at its two ends. Cattan's opposition chapter, below, is largely an essay on exactly this.

The wrap-around takes practice: from the ninth, the trine dexter lands on the fifth and the trine sinister on the *first* (9 + 4 = 13, wrapping to 1); from the eleventh, the sextile sinister is the first house and the opposition the fifth. Exercise 1 drills it.

## The sextile: a horse bought by friends

Here is the sextile chapter entire: definition, doctrine, and one of the most charming worked examples in the whole 1591 text, a gentleman's question about buying a horse:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-14-of-the-sextile-aspect" />

Take the doctrine first. The sextile "signifies means and unperfit amitie" (a mild, imperfect friendship, the weakest of the channels) and the judgment rule is the general doctrine in miniature: a good figure in the sextile place, agreeing with the first "and be of the same Element and of one planet, or that they be both alike," promises "good fortune and good issue"; an ill figure there, of contrary planet and element, and "you shall judge ill of the question made, and of enmitie." The aspect is the introduction; the figures decide whether the introduction is to a friend.

The case shows a working geomancer doing what the doctrine says. Cattan reads his own attributions, not this course's defaults: in his triplicities Puella is water, Fortuna Minor fire, Puer air. (The shield as printed runs right to left, in the manner of shields; count the houses accordingly.) The querent's first house holds Puella, Venus's figure, water in Cattan's system. Her sinister sextile, the third house, holds Fortuna Minor, the Sun's figure, fire: planet and element both contrary to the querent's, "the which two Planets & Elements be contraries, and love not together." From a hostile figure in the house of kin and neighbours, beheld by the aspect of imperfect amity, Cattan reads: someone close will *advise* the purchase (the sextile carries the approach) but the friendship in it is flawed. Then the dexter side: the eleventh house, the house of friends, holds Puer, Mars, and the element of the air, which agrees poorly with water; "I judged that the horse should be scarsely bought." But Venus and Mars "bee Planets which doe love well together," so the talk of buying would come, and a friend would be the cause. Element against element, planet against planet, *each kinship weighed separately*, the verdict mixed because the testimony is mixed. And he refuses to stop there: "it is not enough to have thus judged by the Sextill Regard which hath no respect unto the other rules," and he proceeds to the angles, the witnesses, the Judge, the Part of Fortune and the way of point before concluding that the horse will be bought. One aspect is one testimony, "a great part of the judgement" and never the whole of it. The man who wrote the aspect chapters tells you, inside the aspect chapters, that aspects do not judge alone.

## The trine: no peace for the Emperor

The trine chapter raises the stakes: the worked example is a question of war and peace between Francis I and the Emperor Charles V, cast when Cardinal Trivulsio came as legate to broker terms:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-15-of-the-tryne-aspect-chapt" />

The doctrine: the trine (first, fifth and ninth, "from 5. to 5.") "is naturally good and perfect amitie... by reason of the house, place and fygure." Naturally good: the warmest channel on the wheel. And then the case demonstrates, beautifully and grimly, what "naturally" is worth when the figure found is wrong. The question's first house is beheld by trine from the fifth, and the fifth holds **Tristitia**, Saturn's figure of sorrow. Hear Cattan's own clause: "because the first is beheld with an ill Aspect from the fift which is a fygure of ♄ retrograde, called *Tristitia*, it signifyed that there should bee no peace made at this time." The fifth house beholds the first by *trine*, the aspect of perfect amity, and Cattan calls it, without hesitation, "an ill Aspect," because the figure standing in it is ill. The channel's natural warmth did not survive one cold figure: **the aspect is judged by the figure found in it.**

The rest of the reading is a master's tour you can now mostly follow: the ninth, house of churchmen, holds a good figure, so the Cardinal would labour honestly for the peace; the seventh, the other party, holds an ill one, so the Emperor would have none; the sextile from the eleventh is ill too; the witnesses and Judge lean to Saturn with the fifth; no peace. And the chart's afterlife, which Cattan reports with undisguised satisfaction (the Emperor seized Provence as far as Aix, held it briefly, and lost all he had taken) gives you the Renaissance genre at full strength: doctrine, case, verification. (The transcription records, with this site's usual candour, a flaw in the print itself: the narrative names the first figure Conjunctio while the cramped rightmost compartment of the woodcut shows Via. You convicted a compositor yourself in Lesson 6; here is another suspect, noted and left on the record.)

## The square and the opposition

The two hard channels. First the square:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-16-of-the-quadrats-aspect" />

"From foure to foure," three plain steps, the first house to the fourth or the tenth, and Cattan does not call the square evil; his treatment is all about *agreement*: a good figure in the square place, of the same quality and planet, "is a good signe that the querant shall have a good issue"; a good figure of a different element and planet makes the aspect merely "meane"; and a figure whose planet is *friend* to the significator's redeems the regard entirely. Acquisitio in the first and Puella in the tenth, Jupiter and Venus, he counts as good. The square is a hard channel, not a poisoned one; sufficient friendship between the figures carries good testimony even across it. His worked example, the Count of Touerra's question of when the Emperor would leave Nice for Provence, turns the doctrine to timing: Acquisitio, a stable figure, in the first, with Puella at the square in the tenth (the very Jupiter-and-Venus friendship his doctrine paragraph names) and the witnesses and Judge stable too, so the Emperor sits still for days, waiting (it transpired) for Andrea Doria and the biscuit for his soldiers. Strength of place and fixity of figure, read off the square: the dignity doctrine of this lesson's second half is already operating in the case.

Then the opposition, with its chapter-ending summary of the whole system:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-17-of-the-aspect-of-opposition" />

The opposition "is an ill Aspect as well for the distance of the place as for the contrarietie" (the first house is the Orient and the seventh the Occident, sunrise staring at sunset) and it is the one aspect with no second side. But it is subtler than "ill." If the figures at the two ends are both good *and akin*, "both of one Element, qualitie, nature and Planet," then the opposition holds "no contrarietie... but contrariwise singuler allyance and friendshippe," and the issue is good. The same figure at both ends (Fortuna Major in the first and again in the seventh) is his example of the alliance at its strongest. A good figure facing an evil one (Fortuna Major against Cauda Draconis) is "great discord, contrarietie, and enmitie," and ill for the querent. The opposition, in other words, is the wheel's built-in confrontation between the two parties of every question, and the figures decide whether the parties meet as allies or as enemies. His case (whether the Count of Novellara, wounded in camp, were dead) turns exactly on this: first and seventh of one element, water with water, so the man lived when the news was brought; the fourth and tenth "contraries and of great enmitie," so he would die of the hurt; and so it fell out. And the chapter closes with the general rule restated for all five regards, in case six chapters had left any doubt: "when yee fynde a good fygure in any of these Aspectes, you shall judge well and goodnesse: and if yee fynde an ill Planet there yee shall judge ill."

The four chapters yield one working doctrine, the one your audits execute. For any house you care about, usually the first and the house of the quesited, find the seven houses that behold it. For each beholding house, ask three questions in order. *What figure stands there?* Its nature, good or ill, from Lesson 3. *What agreement does it have with the figure beheld?* Same figure best of all, then same element, then same planet; failing those, the planets' friendship or enmity as the softer reckoner. *Through what channel does it speak?* The trine warmest, then the sextile, the square hard, the opposition confrontational, dexter stronger than sinister. A good figure, well-agreed, in a warm channel, is the front-row neighbour speaking for you; an ill figure, contrary in element and planet, in a hard channel, is hostile testimony given loudly; everything between is between. You will run this audit on the significators of all three standing charts before the lesson ends.

## The triplicities of figures

Now the second instrument: dignity, strength by place. The aspects ask who beholds a figure; the dignities ask how firmly the figure itself is planted. The tradition's first answer is the triplicity, and Cattan's chapter is the source:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-22-of-the-triplicitie-of-figures-and" />

The kernel: figures of one element form a *triplicity*, a family of shared quality, with planetary governors by day and by night, on the astrological model, and the triplicities are a weighing instrument for whole charts. "Above all thinges you must have a regarde unto the triplicitie," Cattan says: if three parts of the chart's figures belong to one element, judge the chart according to that element's nature; if the chart is split, judge by the stronger part and by the governors. A chart whose figures run heavily to fire behaves like fire (hasty, consuming, quick to flare and quick to pass) whatever its individual placements; a chart heavy with earth grinds slow and holds. This is dignity at the coarsest grain: a figure standing among many of its own element stands *in family*, supported, at home in the chart's prevailing weather; a lone watery figure in a chart of fire is a stranger at the feast.

Here the sources split. Cattan's own tables: his air triplicity is Acquisitio, Laetitia, Puer and Conjunctio; his fire is Rubeus, Amissio, Cauda Draconis and Fortuna Minor; his water is Via, Populus, Albus and Puella; his earth is Fortuna Major, Tristitia, Caput Draconis and Carcer. Set that against the course default fixed in Lesson 3 (Agrippa's triplicities "according to the natures of the planets, and figures themselves") and several figures jump families: the default makes Acquisitio water and Cattan makes it air; the default's Puella is air and Cattan's is water; Albus earth against Cattan's water; Fortuna Minor air against Cattan's fire; Fortuna Major fire against Cattan's earth. You met this quarrel at full length in Lesson 3 and adopted the course policy then: **one system in the hands** (the default, which this site's figure pages print and every later technique assumes) and **the whole tradition in the head.** This lesson's audits, and the agreement tables on the charts page, are computed in the course default, and your own must be too; but when you read Cattan's cases above, his judgments of element agreement run in *his* system, and they are coherent in it. A geomancer who mixes the two mid-chart is not weighing testimony; he is contradicting himself. The deeper lesson of the chapter survives every variant untouched: figures have elemental families, family is strength, and the chart's prevailing element colours every judgment made in it.

## Exaltation, joy and fall

Cattan's next chapter pushes the astrological transposition one layer further. The planets, in the mother art, are not equally strong everywhere: each has signs in which it is exalted, a sign in which it rejoices, signs of its fall and detriment. Cattan maps the doctrine onto the figures in a table:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-23-the-exaltation-joy-and-fall-of" />

The table is all grammar. Each row is a planet. Around it stand three signs (the sign of the planet's *exaltation*, of its *joy*, and of its *fall*) and beneath each sign, the geomantic figures that sign owns in Cattan's attributions. So the first row says: Saturn is exalted in Libra (where stand Libra's figures, Puella and Amissio), rejoices in Aquarius (Fortuna Major's sign in his system), and falls in Aries (Puer and Acquisitio). The working sense, transposed to the chart: **a figure is strong where its planet is dignified and weak where its planet falls.** Saturn's figures (Carcer, Tristitia) stand exalted in the places of Libra and fall in the places of Aries; the Moon's figures are at their best in the places of Taurus, where the Moon is exalted, and at their worst in Scorpio's. Cattan gives the table almost without prose (his opening sentence says simply that "a figure can not well be judged" without knowing "the exaltation and the fall of the fygures") and leaves the mapping from signs to houses to the practitioner's astrological literacy.

The modern comparison mechanises exactly that last step, and this is the one place in the lesson where the Golden Dawn's love of machinery genuinely helps a student see the structure:

<Quote id="crowley/techniques/dignities" />

Crowley's rule (treat the twelve houses as the twelve signs, the Ascendant as Aries, the second house as Taurus, and so around to the twelfth as Pisces) turns the whole apparatus into a lookup: a figure is *strongest* in the house answering to its planet's own sign, *very strong* in its exaltation, *strong* in its triplicity, *very weak* in its fall, *weakest of all* in its detriment, the fall opposite the exaltation and the detriment opposite the own house. His chapter VI prints the full table; one of its rows will matter to a course chart shortly. But keep the layers labelled: the houses-as-signs equation is the modern hardening of a Renaissance doctrine that Cattan states only as a planetary table, and the two do not everywhere agree, because Crowley's figure-to-planet-to-sign chains run through the Golden Dawn's attributions, not Cattan's. Use the modern table as a reckoner; cite the Renaissance table as the doctrine; never present the one as the other.

Three things to retain. That dignity by place is real doctrine in both lineages: a figure's testimony gains or loses force by the house it stands in, through its planet's affinities. That the apparatus runs through the sign-attributions and therefore inherits all of Lesson 3's quarrels, so every exaltation-call you make should carry its system on its sleeve. And that for daily practice the tradition supplied a blunter, sturdier instrument that needs no zodiac at all.

## Every figure's good and ill houses

Book III, chapter 2 (placed by Cattan before the aspects, used by every practitioner after them) is the workhorse of geomantic dignity: a plain list, figure by figure, of where each stands well and where it stands ill. It opens with a general grading of the houses themselves:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/intro" />

Good houses the first, fifth, tenth and eleventh; mean the second, third, fourth and ninth; evil the sixth, seventh, eighth and twelfth. This is *not* Lesson 5's skeleton of strength: the angles are the strong houses, but not all of them are good. The seventh and the fourth, two angles, land in the evil and mean classes respectively, while the eleventh, a mere succedent, sits among the good. Strength by angle measures how *firmly* a figure is planted; the good-and-ill grading measures how *kindly* the ground treats it. The two will be separate columns in your audit, and a figure can score high on one and low on the other: angular in an evil house, like every seventh-house significator you will ever read.

Then the figure-by-figure entries, each a sentence or two of concentrated experience. Here are the ones the course charts will need, beginning with the figure that owns Chart A's question:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/cauda-draconis" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/amissio" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/conjunctio" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/puella" />

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/good-ill-houses/carcer" />

Via "breaketh and spoileth al the goodnes of the others" (the strongest single sentence against any figure in the series) *and yet* is "good in the third, fift and seventh houses, because she signifyeth that letters shall come which shall bring good newes," with the standing exception for journeys renewed once more. Cauda Draconis, a figure dreadful enough to trip Step 0, is "found good in the fourth, sixt, ninth and twelfth houses": the Tail does sound work in the houses of endings, sickness and confinement, where passing-out-of-an-affair is exactly what is wanted, and the entry's opening clause, "very good with the ill, and very ill with the good," is the whole strength-is-not-goodness doctrine in nine words. Amissio is *good in the eighth* (loss standing in the house of death and other men's goods is loss well spent) and "very ill in the second," where loss stands on the querent's own purse. Carcer is "ill in all the houses, and especially in the sixt, eight, seventh and twelfth": mark the seventh on that list; Chart B will. And Puella is "very good in all things that ye may demaunde": some witnesses are simply kind.

Count the sources, as in Lesson 6. Heydon prints the same series in *Theomagia*:

<Quote id="heydon/techniques/good-ill-houses" />

Set it against Cattan clause by clause and you will find what you found with the Witnesses in Lesson 6: this is Cattan reprinted nearly verbatim seventy years on (the editorial note says so, and the verification is yours to repeat) so the series' printed attestation is one witness in two coats, not two witnesses. (Heydon's chapter adds a closing flourish about telesmes and astromancy that is pure Heydon, and not technique.) The honest tally for the good-and-ill-houses series: Cattan's doctrine, transmitted by Heydon, mechanised differently by the Golden Dawn. One lineage, well documented, which is exactly the kind of fact a working geomancer should know about his own instruments.

How do the entries sit with the dignity apparatus of the previous section? Imperfectly, and usefully. Sometimes they agree: Saturn rejoicing in the twelfth's gloom and Tristitia "good in the eight and twelfth houses" tell one story. Sometimes they cut across each other, and the series follows *meaning* where the apparatus follows *astronomy*: Amissio is good in the eighth not because Venus is dignified there but because loss belongs there; Via is good in the third because the third is the house of letters and Via is the figure of things arriving. The series, in other words, is the placement-logic of Lesson 5 (figure refracted through department) hardened into verdicts by somebody's long practice. For this course it is the primary dignity instrument: blunt, citable, and independent of every attribution quarrel. The triplicities and exaltations refine it; the angular skeleton underlies it; the three together are the dignity audit.

## Strength is not goodness

The lesson's two halves fuse into one skill. Every figure in a chart carries two independent measures. Its **nature** (good or ill, Puella or Rubeus) tells you *what it says*: the content of the testimony, fixed in Lesson 3 and never altered by anything in this lesson. Its **condition** (angular or cadent, in a good house or an evil one for its kind, well or ill beheld, in family or alone) tells you *how forcefully it says it*: the volume and credibility of the witness. The axes are perpendicular, and all four quadrants occur:

- **Good and strong:** a fortunate figure, angular, dignified, warmly beheld: the best testimony a chart can give, a friend in possession of the field.
- **Good and weak:** a fortunate figure cadent, in an ill house for its kind, unbefriended by aspect: a friend, but far off and faint; promise without the power to perform. The debilitated good figure flatters the careless reader and delivers little.
- **Ill and weak:** an unfortunate figure, badly placed and unattended: a real shadow, but a passing one; malice without means.
- **Ill and strong:** an unfortunate figure angular, in a house that suits its ill work, fortified by its own kind in aspect: the dangerous quadrant. The harm it signifies is *resourced*. A well-dignified ill figure does not become kind by being dignified; it becomes effective.

The last two are where untrained intuition fails. Asked to choose, students reliably fear the ill figure and trust the good one, ignoring condition entirely; and the tradition's own entries rebuke them. Cauda Draconis in the twelfth is an ill figure *good there*: the Tail in the house of prisons passes the prisoner out of his affair, and its testimony, strongly given, is testimony of release. Laetitia, among the kindest figures in the alphabet, the series calls ill in the sixth, eighth and twelfth: joy debilitated in the houses of sickness and death is not comfort but mockery, a bright face at the wrong bedside. The question "which figure is better?" is malformed until it is asked *of a place*: better where, for what, and in what strength? Exercise 6 quizzes you on the hard case (dignified ill against debilitated good); the short answer you will earn there is that the dignified ill figure is usually the worse testimony for the querent's hopes and always the more *reliable* one, because strength makes any figure's sentence likelier to be performed. Strength is not goodness. Strength is the probability that what the figure says, happens.

## The journal, upgraded

The protocol takes its fifth instrument. Everything stands (the cast and verification of Lesson 4, Step 0 and the placements of Lesson 5, the triad and sentence of Lesson 6, the perfection scan and provisional verdict of Lesson 7) and to it you now add, for the three course charts and every cast, current and future, the **aspect-and-dignity audit**:

1. **The aspect maps, built.** For the first house and the quesited's house: the seven beholding houses by the ±2/±3/±4/+6 arithmetic, dexter and sinister labelled, the figure found in each, and its agreement with the significator (same figure, same element, same planet, in the course-default system) with disagreements left visibly blank. A map with no agreements is a finding, not a failure; record it as flatly as a warm one.
2. **The dignity lines, written.** For each significator, and for any figure the reading leans on: angular, succedent or cadent; the house's class from the intro (good, mean, evil); the figure's own entry from the good-and-ill-houses series, quoted or cited; and, where you use them, triplicity and exaltation calls with their system named.
3. **The provisional verdict, weighted.** Lesson 7 let you write a provisional verdict; this lesson lets you *weight* it, and that is the entire upgrade. Under each verdict add one line of the form: *strengthened*, *unchanged*, or *qualified*, with the audit fact that does the weighting. "Denied; and the denial weighted heavier, the 7th's figure is the strongest in the chart." "Granted; granted thinly, the quesited is unbefriended by any aspect." The verdict's *substance* still comes from perfection and nowhere else: an aspect audit never turns a denial into a grant, nor a grant into a denial. It tells you how much force the answer arrives with.
4. **Still short of judgment.** The full weighing of all testimonies (sentence against perfection against aspect against dignity, the trained act this course has been building toward) is Lesson 10's. Until then the audit is evidence filed, not a verdict reached, and Chart C below will show you exactly why the filing must stay open.

## Worked example

Two performances: first the lesson's central distinction on a single figure in two places, then the complete aspect-and-dignity audit of Chart B (the chart Lesson 7 denied), ending at the question the audit exists to answer: do the aspects soften the denial?

### One figure, two places

Take Amissio (loss itself, the emptied purses, a figure no list calls good) and weigh it twice from one sentence of the good-and-ill-houses series: "*Amissio* is good for losse of substance, and therefore is good in the eight house, and very ill in the second" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 2).

Set the two placements side by side, and reason as the series reasons. In the **second house**, Amissio stands on the querent's own purse: the house asks "what of my substance?" and the figure answers *it leaves you*. Loss, in the place of keeping, performing exactly its nature against exactly what the querent wants held: "very ill," the series says, and the strongest negative grading the entry gives. In the **eighth house**, the same figure with the same nature stands among death, debts, dowries and other men's goods (the house of substance that is *not* the querent's, of what passes through other hands and out of the world) and there the series calls her *good*. Why? Not because loss has grown kind. Because the eighth is where loss *belongs*: what stands to be lost there is the other party's wealth, the debt that burdens, the matter that ought to pass away, and "good for losse of substance" means precisely that she performs her losing where losing serves. The figure never changed. The department changed, and the same testimony that ruins the second house does honest work in the eighth.

The dark half is not hypothetical: **Chart C's second house holds Amissio** (the very placement the series grades "very ill") and holds it as a *double*, the first house's Amissio repeated onto the querent's money in a question of law, where money is how suits are lost. That dignity line now goes into Chart C's journal entry beside the seventh's audit above, and it weights the same side of the scale. And the contrast teaches the method you will use on every figure from now on: a placement is never graded by the figure's nature alone, nor by the house's class alone, but by the *fit* between what the figure does and what the house wants, which is what the good-and-ill-houses series is: two centuries of fit, written down.

- **Puella, 1st:** angular; the first is a good house by the intro; the figure "very good in all things that ye may demaunde." Good and firmly planted, and, per her map, almost unattended. A kind witness with no supporters.
- **Carcer, 7th:** angular; the seventh an evil house; and the entry is merciless on exactly this placement: "ill in all the houses, and especially in the sixt, eight, *seventh* and twelfth," the seventh named among the worst seats of a figure ill everywhere, a figure that "signifyeth alwayes to be stayed." - **Carcer, 5th:** succedent, in a good house, lending its strength to its twin by the warmest agreement in the chart. The fifth's own matters (pleasures, children, good news) take the prison badly, but this figure's role in *this* question is the lending.
- **Rubeus, 2nd:** succedent; the entry names the second among Rubeus's ill houses: disorder on the querent's substance, moderately seated.
- **Amissio, 4th:** angular, mean house; the figure of loss in the house of the matter's end, and the querent's one planetary friend. Loss, at the root and end of things, kindly disposed: the chart's most double-edged testimony.
- **Tristitia, 12th:** cadent; and here the series surprises the careless: Tristitia, "a very ill figure in all the houses," is *good* in the eighth and twelfth: sorrow at home in the house of sorrow, the dignified ill figure of this lesson's doctrine, working its small dark work competently in a corner of the chart far from the question.
- **Cauda Draconis, 10th:** angular in a good house, but the entry's good seats (4, 6, 9, 12) do not include the tenth, and the figure is "very ill with the good": an ill figure strongly planted in a house whose matters (honour, authority, the standing of the querent's affairs) it can only injure. The series is silent on the tenth by name; reason from the clauses, and say so when you cite.

**The strength-ranking**, by condition (place, house-class, series grading, attendance), with nature kept in a separate column:

1. **Carcer in the 7th:** angular, doubled, twin-sextiled dexter with double agreement, exalted by the modern table: the strongest figure in the chart, and an ill one, sitting on the question.
2. **Puella in the 1st:** angular, good house, excellent nature, one square-borne friendship: a good figure at middling force.
3. **Cauda Draconis in the 10th:** angular and ill-suited: dark testimony at strength, over the chart's high places.
4. **Amissio in the 4th:** angular in a mean house: the end-of-matter testimony, moderately strong, ambiguously friendly.
5. **Tristitia in the 12th:** dignified by the series but cadent and far from the question: a strong *kind* of testimony at low volume.
6. **Rubeus in the 2nd and 3rd:** ill by place in the second, succedent and cadent: real but secondary shadows on substance and kin.

**And the question: do the aspects soften the denial?** The answer must come from the maps, not from hope. What would softening even look like? Warm, well-agreed regards flowing *between* the significators' houses, or friendly figures fortifying the querent's side: circumstances under which a denial arrives gently, leaving room for the matter to mend outside the chart's grant. Look at what the maps actually hold. Between the significators stands exactly one aspect, the opposition they cannot avoid, and it is bare of every agreement: the parties confront each other as pure strangers, Cattan's "great discord, contrarietie, and enmitie" configuration, not his "singuler allyance" one. The querent's only warm regard is a square from Loss. And the one genuinely warm, well-agreed aspect in either map (the dexter sextile, double agreement, strongest side) does not serve the querent at all: it serves *Carcer*, fortifying the figure of the stayed and the shut in the house of the broken partnership. The aspects of Chart B do not soften the denial; they *resource* it. The holding figure is the attended figure; the kind figure is the lonely one. Write the weighting line under Lesson 7's verdict exactly as the protocol prescribes: *Denied; and the denial weighted heavier by audit: the quesited's Carcer is the strongest figure in the chart, twin-fortified by sextile, especially ill in the very house it holds; the significators share no agreement; the querent's one friendship arrives by square, from Amissio, in the house of ends.* The sentence said change; perfection said no; the audit says the no is well-garrisoned. What a trained judge finally *says* to this querent, and how the one mercy in the file, gentle Puella firmly seated, tempers the saying of it, is Lesson 10's craft, and the file is now as ready for it as this lesson can make it.

  2. Give the four geomantic distances in Cattan's inclusive counting.

<Exercise n={6} title="The strong enemy and the faint friend" kind="quiz">
  Dignified ill against debilitated good. The questions keep the two halves apart: worse for the querent is not the same as less reliable.

  6. State the maxim this lesson built toward, about nature and condition.

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. A well-dignified ill figure is an unfortunate nature in a strong condition: angular, suited to its house or exalted, attended by agreeing aspects ("a figure can not well be judged" without "the exaltation and the fall of the fygures," Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 23). A debilitated good figure is a fortunate nature in a weak condition: cadent, ill-housed for its kind, unbefriended.
    2. The dignified ill figure, whatever it portends. Strength is the probability of performance, so the strong figure's sentence is the one likelier to be executed.
    3. Carcer in the seventh: an ill figure "ill in all the houses, and especially in the sixt, eight, seventh and twelfth" (Book III, ch. 2), yet angular, doubled, sextiled by its own twin, and exalted by the modern houses-as-signs reckoner (Crowley, ch.     4. b). Tristitia is "a very ill figure in all the houses, but in the eight and twelfth houses, where she is good" (Book III, ch. 2): the series makes the figure's contribution depend on what the question wants, and strength amplifies the contribution either way.

Because it tempts the reader to lean on support that cannot bear weight: Laetitia, ill in the sixth, eighth and twelfth (Book III, ch. 2), is joy promising what the house of sickness cannot deliver, and a judge who counts it as comfort misjudges the chart worse than any honest enemy could make him.
    6. Nature tells you what the witness says; condition tells you whether the world will back the witness up; and the judge's craft is never to confuse the volume of a voice with the quality of its news.

## You are ready to continue when…

- You can build the aspect map of any house in under a minute (±2, ±3, ±4, +6, dexter and sinister labelled, wraps correct) and name the four houses that behold nothing.
- You can state the crucial doctrine from chapter 12 and apply it: the aspect is a channel, the figure found decides the testimony, and you can cite Cattan calling a trine "an ill Aspect" because Tristitia stood in it.

## Where you stand

You now read with weights and measures: a chart yields its sentence, its grant or denial, and the *condition* of every witness behind both, who beholds whom and with what kinship, who stands established and who faint, which testimonies arrive resourced and which merely sincere.

What remains before that reckoning is the art's fine grain. The tradition watched figures *spring* from house to house, cast a Part of Fortune into the wheel and followed the way of the point, and asked what the querent secretly intended. These subtle doctrines (smaller instruments, easily abused, indispensable in trained hands) are the business of Lesson 9, at [the course index](/course/). Two you have already brushed against: the repetitions you have catalogued since Lesson 4 are springings, and Cattan's horse-buyer was judged partly by the way of point.
