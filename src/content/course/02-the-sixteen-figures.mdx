Geomancy uses sixteen figures. You must know them from memory before you can cast or read a chart. This lesson teaches their structure, which is what a geomantic figure is apart from any meaning. The meanings begin in Lesson 3.

A figure has four stacked lines. Each line has either one point or two. There is no third state and no fifth line. Four lines with two possible states each yield exactly sixteen figures.

A figure's structure shapes its meaning. When Cattan says that a six-pointed figure in the first house can signify six hours, six days, six months, or six years, he is using the point total. When the sources say that the Judge of a chart must be even or the chart is false, they are using parity.

Most of Cattan's chapter concerns meanings you are not ready to use yet. ## The anatomy of a figure

A geomantic figure is four lines, stacked vertically and counted from the top. Each line contains either a single point or a pair of points. Draw the pair spread apart so the two states cannot be confused. So a figure is a column of four lines, each line one of two states.

Why one point or two, and never three or none? This comes from how the lines are made. To generate one line, the geomancer makes a row of points rapidly and without counting, holding the question in mind. The number of points is deliberately not controlled. The points are then counted off in pairs. If one point remains unpaired, the line is *odd* and is recorded as a single point. If the points pair off exactly, the line is *even* and is recorded as two. A row is either odd or even, so a line is single or double, and nothing else can appear. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa describing this:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-mothers" />

Agrippa's phrase is "according to the even or uneven marking every several line." He means that each row is reduced to odd or even. Count the row in pairs. If one point is left over, write one point. If none is left over, write two. Four reduced rows make one figure.

The two states of a line have traditional and modern names. The old texts say a line is *uneven* (one point) or *even* (two points); modern writers say *active* and *passive*. This course uses the modern terms, with the caution that they are glosses, not source vocabulary.

The four positions also carry associations. The historical texts speak spatially: Cattan divides figures by "more pointes on high" or "more pointes below." The Golden Dawn later assigned each line an element, fire, air, water, earth from top to bottom; that system is not in Cattan or Agrippa, so this course flags it as modern, but it is now standard and this site's glyph data follows it.

Now a note on drawing. The figures are drawn from the top line downward. By old custom the points are set down from right to left, the Arabic habit Agrippa preserves ("from the right hand towards the left"). Practice drawing a few figures now, large and deliberate. Center a single point in the line, and spread a double point across it. Sloppy figures cause real misreadings, because a cramped double taken for a single changes one figure into another.

Throughout this course, a figure's pattern will sometimes be written in a compact notation: the four lines from top to bottom, single as 1 and double as 2. Acquisitio is 2·1·2·1; Via is 1·1·1·1. The notation is the site's, not source vocabulary, but it is handy for checking work.

## Sixteen and only sixteen

How many figures can the rule produce? Each line is one of two states, and there are four lines. The first line can be single or double, so two possibilities. Each further line doubles the count: four, then eight, then sixteen. So there are exactly sixteen figures, and every combination is realized and named. No combination of four lines is left without a name. Whatever the casting produces, the result is always one of the sixteen. Because the set is closed, the sources can build tables of Witnesses, Judges, and figure-against-figure results that cover every case, as Cattan's and Heydon's tables do.

The order of a figure-list is a convention, and the sources do not agree on one. Crowley's chapter runs through the zodiac, because the Golden Dawn organized everything zodiacally. Cattan groups the figures by planet. The order above is this site's own, chosen so that structurally related figures sit side by side. Via stands next to Populus, each Fortune beside the other, Acquisitio beside Amissio, and so on. Whatever order you meet them in, the names are stable across the source set. They are Latin, with an English gloss alongside. Caput Draconis and Cauda Draconis, the Dragon's Head and Tail, are the old names for the lunar nodes, the crossing-points of the Moon's path with the Sun's.

Many of the names come already paired (Gain and Loss, Joy and Sorrow, Boy and Girl), and the pairs in the words correspond to pairs in the dots, as the mirrors section shows.

## Counting points

Add up the points in a figure (one for each single line, two for each double) and you get its point total. The smallest total is four: every line single, which is Via. The largest is eight: every line double, which is Populus. Every other figure falls between, and the total is fixed by how many lines are double: no doubles gives four points, one double gives five, two doubles six, three doubles seven, four doubles eight.

The classes run one, four, six, four, one. Via stands alone at four points. There are four five-point figures (Caput Draconis, Puella, Puer, Cauda Draconis), six six-point figures (the two Fortunes, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer), and four seven-point figures (Laetitia, Rubeus, Albus, Tristitia). Populus stands alone at eight.

The sources use these totals. At the close of his chapter of qualities, Cattan reads a span of life from a point-count. A figure of six points in the first house "signifyeth that a man shall live sixe houres, sixe dayes, sixe moneths, sixe yeares, or 72" (Cattan, *The Geomancie*, Book III, ch. 27). Timing techniques use these numbers, so learn to read the point total as readily as the figure's name.

The most important use of the totals is parity. Eight of the sixteen figures have an even total: Via with four; Fortuna Major, Fortuna Minor, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, and Carcer with six; Populus with eight. The other eight are odd, namely the four five-point figures and the four seven-point figures. This split decides whether a chart is valid, through a rule you will meet in full in Lesson 4. At the foot of a geomantic chart sit two figures called the Witnesses. From them you derive a final figure called the Judge, which gives the answer to the whole question. Of the Judge, Cattan says this:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-04-of-the-judge-chap" />

"The which judge ought alwayes of necessitie to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false." Only the eight even figures can ever stand as Judge. If an odd figure appears in that place, the chart has been miscast and must be done again, so the rule works as a built-in check on the casting. Lesson 4 shows why the arithmetic of the chart makes an odd Judge impossible. For now, learn the eight even figures as a set: Via, Populus, the two Fortunes, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer.

## Entering, exiting, and stability

Now sort the figures by where their points sit, that is, whether more points stand in the upper half or the lower half. Cattan gives this rule at the opening of the chapter of qualities you read for this lesson:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

Compare the figure's upper half (the first and second lines) with its lower half (the third and fourth). Figures with more points above than below are "entring in," and Cattan calls them good. Figures with more points below than above are "going out," and evil. Figures with equal points above and below are "meanes," middling. Each class has one exception, which Cattan names. Tristitia is top-heavy but not good, Laetitia is bottom-heavy but not evil, and Carcer is balanced but not middling. Those exceptions concern goodness, not the count. The sorting itself is just arithmetic.

Five figures are top-heavy and entering: Fortuna Major (four points above, two below), Tristitia (four above, three below), Albus (four above, three below), Puella (three above, two below), and Caput Draconis (three above, two below). Five are bottom-heavy and exiting: Fortuna Minor, Laetitia, Rubeus, Puer, and Cauda Draconis. Each of these is the top-to-bottom reflection of one of the first five. Six are balanced: Via, Populus, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer. Note that these balanced six are six of the eight even figures. Only an even figure can have halves that balance.

Crowley's table keeps the same idea in the names of the two nodes. Caput Draconis is "the upper threshold," and Cauda Draconis is "the threshold lower, or going out." Caput (2·1·1·1) has its double at the top, and Cauda (1·1·1·2) has its double at the bottom.

This old rule lines up with the last column of the master table, which this site's figure pages call *mobility*. Eight figures are stable: Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Tristitia, Puella, Albus, Carcer, Caput Draconis. Eight are mobile: Via, Fortuna Minor, Amissio, Laetitia, Puer, Rubeus, Conjunctio, Cauda Draconis. The five entering figures are stable and the five exiting figures are mobile. The six balanced figures split between the two classes. Populus, Acquisitio, and Carcer are stable, while Via, Amissio, and Conjunctio are mobile.

What does the classification do in practice? In a reading, a stable figure signifies a matter that endures and resists change. It is slow to arrive, slow to leave, and fixed in its place. A mobile figure signifies a matter in motion, one that is quick, passing, and apt to change or escape. The same quality is good or bad depending on the question. If you ask whether a sickness will pass, you want mobility. If you ask whether a marriage will hold, you want stability. Cattan's blunt sorting of entering figures as "good" and exiting as "evill" reads the same way, since for most questions a matter arriving is better news than a matter departing. Lesson 3 refines this figure by figure. For now, learn the two eights as firmly as you learned the even and the odd. Note that the two divisions cut across each other. Even Amissio is mobile while even Acquisitio is stable, and odd Laetitia is mobile while odd Tristitia is stable. Parity and mobility are independent properties, and a figure has both.

## Mirrors and counterparts

The sixteen figures pair off in regular ways. Two operations generate the pairings, and both are simple enough to do in your head.

The first is *inversion*. Turn the figure upside down, so the first line becomes the fourth and the second becomes the third. In the notation, reverse the string. Invert Fortuna Major, 2·2·1·1, and you get 1·1·2·2, Fortuna Minor. Invert Laetitia and you get Tristitia. In the same way, Puer gives Puella, Albus gives Rubeus, Caput Draconis gives Cauda Draconis, and Acquisitio gives Amissio. So six pairs swap under inversion. The remaining four figures read the same upside down, so inversion leaves them unchanged: Via, Populus, Conjunctio, and Carcer.

Inversion flips the names, too. Greater Fortune flips into Lesser Fortune, Joy into Sorrow, Boy into Girl, White into Red, the Dragon's Head into the Dragon's Tail, and Gain into Loss. The paired names you noticed earlier are exactly the inversion pairs in the dots. The figures were named structurally, and the names were fixed before the Latin sources began. The site's figure data records this pairing under the name *counterpart*. On this site the counterpart of every figure is its inversion. Each of the six mirror-pairs is a counterpart-pair, and each of the four palindromes is its own counterpart. When a figure page says the counterpart of Albus is Rubeus, you can check it by turning Albus upside down.

The second operation is *complementation*. Leave the figure right side up, but exchange the state of every line, single for double and double for single. The complement of Via is Populus. The complement of Puer (1·1·2·1) is Albus, not Puella. The complement of Laetitia is Caput Draconis, not Tristitia. The complement of Conjunctio is Carcer. Complementation can never leave a figure unchanged, so it sorts the sixteen into eight pairs: Via–Populus, the two Fortunes, Acquisitio–Amissio, Laetitia–Caput Draconis, Tristitia–Cauda Draconis, Puer–Albus, Puella–Rubeus, Conjunctio–Carcer.

For most figures the two operations disagree. Inversion turns Puer into Puella, while complementation turns it into Albus. For two pairs the operations agree: the two Fortunes, and Acquisitio with Amissio. Invert Acquisitio or complement it, and either way you get Amissio. So gain and loss are opposites by both operations. Here is Crowley's summary of each, from the modern end of the source set:

Acquisitio is good for gain. Amissio is bad for gain and good only for losing, with the exception "sometimes for love," which Lesson 3 covers. The older sources draw the same opposition through planetary dignity rather than through dots. The pseudo-Agrippa makes Amissio a weakened Venus:

Puella is fortunate, and Amissio is "as it were retrograde, or combust." This is the same planet, once strong and once ruined. The other extreme complement-pair is Via and Populus:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/via-populus" />

Via and Populus are both figures of the Moon. Via is the mobile, all-single figure, and Populus is the stable, all-double figure. The sources read them as two phases of one planet.

The old sources also pair the figures a third way, by planet, as the Agrippa quotes above show. Planetary pairing sometimes tracks the structural pairings and sometimes cuts across them. Puella and Amissio share Venus, but structurally Puella's mirror is Puer and Amissio's is Acquisitio. Acquisitio shares Jupiter with Laetitia, not with Amissio:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/acquisitio-laetitia" />

So a pairing in the texts is one of three kinds: inversion, complement, or shared planet. These are three different relations, and a figure has all three at once. The exercises will make you compute them until the distinction is automatic.

## The elemental reading of the lines

So far the four lines have been positions: first, second, third, fourth. The Golden Dawn school gave them elements: fire, air, water, earth from top to bottom. The caution from earlier still holds. Cattan and the pseudo-Agrippa do not assign fire to the top line of a figure. The line-elements are a nineteenth-century system. They are useful and now standard in modern practice, so this site adopts them as its glyph convention. When a figure's dots are given as 2·1·2·1, the four numbers are its fire, air, water, and earth lines, in that order.

The fire line is will and drive. The air line is thought, speech, and communication. The water line is emotion and appetite. The earth line is matter, money, and physical result. A single point means the element is *active* in the figure. A double point means it is *latent* or contained. Again, "active" and "latent" are modern interpretive vocabulary, not source diction. Use them as a mnemonic.

The system organizes the sixteen figures well. The four seven-point figures each have one active element: Laetitia fire, Rubeus air, Albus water, Tristitia earth. The four five-point figures each have one latent element: Caput Draconis fire, Puella air, Puer water, Cauda Draconis earth. The six six-point figures balance two active lines against two latent.

The line-elements are distinct from a second elemental assignment, one that *is* in the old sources. This second one assigns a whole figure to an element. Cattan does this throughout. His chapter of qualities says that for the fruitfulness of the year "the figures entring into the elements of water and earth be good, and those which be of fire and ayre be ill," and his per-figure chapters say things like "of the element of the fire" of Amissio. The pseudo-Agrippa arranges the sixteen into four elemental triplicities:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/triplicities" />

Crowley's table has an element column of its own, derived from each figure's zodiacal sign. It disagrees with Agrippa and Cattan about half the time. It assigns Acquisitio to fire by way of Sagittarius, where the older scheme gives it to water by way of Pisces. The disagreements are real, and the site's figure pages record every variant. Keep the two systems apart. The *figure-element* is a system claim on which the sources differ, so label the system you are using when it matters. The *line-elements* are a fixed modern convention, the same in every book that uses them, because they follow the geometry. So when this course says "Amissio's fire line is single," that is a statement about dots, checkable by counting. When it says "Amissio is a figure of fire," that is Cattan's claim, and another source may say otherwise.

## Worked example

Now apply every tool from this lesson to one pair, Acquisitio and Amissio.

<FigureGlyph dots={[2,1,2,1]} name="Acquisitio" size={4} /> <FigureGlyph dots={[1,2,1,2]} name="Amissio" size={4} />

In notation, Acquisitio is 2·1·2·1: double fire, single air, double water, single earth. Amissio is 1·2·1·2: single fire, double air, single water, double earth. The two strings disagree in every line, so they are complements. Reversed, Acquisitio's string 2·1·2·1 becomes 1·2·1·2, Amissio again, so they are also inversions of each other. With the Fortunes, they are one of only two pairs for which the two operations agree.

Count the points. Acquisitio is two, one, two, one, giving six. Amissio is one, two, one, two, giving six. Both are even, so both belong to the eight figures that can stand as Judge. These are the only two figures that alternate single and double down all four lines, and an alternating figure must have two of each.

Compare the halves. Acquisitio's upper half holds three points (two and one), and its lower half holds three as well (two and one). So Acquisitio is balanced, one of Cattan's "meanes," and the same is true of Amissio. Cattan's threshold rule cannot separate this pair, because both are balanced. The sources assign their mobility by nature instead. Acquisitio is stable, since what is gained is kept. Amissio is mobile, since loss is a thing failing to stay. The two have the same points, parity, and balance, but different mobility.

Read the lines elementally, single as active and double as latent. Acquisitio is active in air and earth, which gives planning and material result. Amissio is active in fire and water, which gives will and desire without calculation or possession. These are mnemonics, not source rules, but the sources support them. Crowley makes Acquisitio good for profit and Amissio bad for gain. Agrippa gives Acquisitio to Jupiter and Amissio to Venus retrograde or combust. Cattan lists Acquisitio among figures of gain and Amissio among figures of loss, war, and false love.

Now the extreme pair, more briefly.

<FigureGlyph dots={[1,1,1,1]} name="Via" size={4} /> <FigureGlyph dots={[2,2,2,2]} name="Populus" size={4} />

Via, 1·1·1·1, is the minimum. It has four points, every element active, and is mobile, the figure of the road and of change. Populus, 2·2·2·2, is the maximum. It has eight points, every element latent, and is stable, the figure of the crowd. Each is its own inversion and counterpart, and by complementation each becomes the other. Both are even and can stand as Judge, and Agrippa assigns both to the Moon.

That is a complete structural analysis, and you can do it on any figure in about a minute: notation, point total, parity, balance of halves, mobility, inversion, complement, counterpart, and active and latent lines.   </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={4} title="Line ladder in practice" kind="quiz">
  A few words each. Use the line ladder as a reading aid.

  1. In the elemental reading of the lines, which elements belong to the four lines, top to bottom?
  2. What does a single point in a line mark, and what does a double point mark?
  3. Laetitia, 1·2·2·2, has its only single line in fire. What does that structure make the figure, in one phrase?
  4. How should the line ladder be used in a reading?
     - a) as a practical aid for describing the figure's shape
     - b) as a rule that overrides the figure's whole meaning
     - c) as a reason to ignore the question being asked
  5. Tristitia, 2·2·2·1, has its only single line in earth. In a reading, does that suggest easy movement or fixity?
  6. A student says, "Because the fire line is single, Laetitia is always good in every question." What is wrong with that shortcut?

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. Fire, air, water, earth.
    2. A single point marks the element active (present, expressed, discharging outward); a double point marks it latent (settled, contained, held in reserve).
    3. A single act of will above three settled elements. The figure leads with its fire, which fits Joy.
    4. a). It helps describe the figure's shape. It does not override the figure's whole meaning or the question being asked.
    5. Fixity. Its active line is the lowest line, so the figure suggests weight and staying power rather than easy release.
    6. The line structure helps describe the figure; it does not replace the question. A figure's practical value still depends on what is being asked and where the figure stands.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={5} title="Stable and mobile from memory" kind="drill">
  From memory, sort the sixteen figures into stable and mobile. Then write two examples: one question in which a mobile figure would be welcome, and one question in which a stable figure would be welcome.
  <ModelAnswer>
    Stable: Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Tristitia, Puella, Albus, Carcer, Caput Draconis. Mobile: Via, Fortuna Minor, Amissio, Laetitia, Puer, Rubeus, Conjunctio, Cauda Draconis.

    In a reading, a stable figure signifies a matter that endures and resists change, slow to arrive and slow to leave. A mobile figure signifies a matter in motion, quick and passing and apt to change or escape. Mobility is welcome in "will this illness pass quickly?" Mobility is unwelcome in "will this marriage endure?" or "will I keep this position?" Stability is welcome in "will this agreement hold?" or "will I keep the land?"
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>
