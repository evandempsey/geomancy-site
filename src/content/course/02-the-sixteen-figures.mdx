Geomancy uses sixteen figures. You must know them from memory before you can cast or read a chart. This lesson teaches their structure: what a geomantic figure is, before anyone has said what it means. The meanings begin in Lesson 3.

A figure has four stacked lines. Each line has either one point or two. There is no third state and no fifth line. Four lines with two possible states each yield exactly sixteen figures.

A figure's structure matters to its meaning. When Cattan says that a six-pointed figure in the first house can signify six hours, six days, six months, or six years, he is using the point total. When the sources say that the Judge of a chart must be even or the chart is false, they are using parity.

## Before you begin

This lesson assumes Lesson 1. If any of it has gone soft, return there first.

The assigned reading comes first. Crowley's chapter of attributions is a modern comparison. Its tables descend from the Golden Dawn's late nineteenth-century system, and this lesson identifies any use it makes of them. Cattan's chapter of qualities (Book III, chapter 27) is the older text for this lesson. Most of the chapter concerns meanings you are not ready to use yet. Read especially the opening paragraph, where Cattan divides the figures by the disposition of their points, and the closing paragraph, where he counts points to measure a life. The site's [table of the sixteen figures](/figures/) sets out every column this lesson teaches except the meanings.

## The anatomy of a figure

A geomantic figure is four lines (in the older English texts, four "ranks" or rows of points), stacked vertically. The lines are counted from the top: first, second, third, fourth. Each line contains either a single point or a pair of points, and the pair is drawn spread apart so that the eye can never confuse the two states. That is the entire anatomy. A figure is a column of four binary choices.

Why one point or two, and never three or none? This comes from how the lines are made. The full casting procedure belongs to Lesson 4. To generate one line of a figure, the geomancer makes a row of points rapidly and without counting, while holding the question in mind. This may be done in sand, on paper, with a wand, or with a pen. The row might contain nine points, fourteen points, or twenty-three points; the number is deliberately not controlled.

Then the points are counted off in pairs. If a point remains unpaired at the end, the line is *odd*, and is recorded as a single point. If the points pair off exactly, the line is *even*, and is recorded as two points. The mark you write down is the row's parity: odd or even. A row is either odd or even, so a line is single or double, and nothing else can appear. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa describing exactly this, in the passage on projecting the figures:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-mothers" />

The phrase that matters is "according to the even or uneven marking every several line." Agrippa means that each row is reduced to odd or even. Count the row in pairs. If one point is left over, write one point. If none is left over, write two. Four reduced rows make one figure.

The two states of a line have traditional and modern names. The old texts say what Agrippa says: a line is *uneven* (one point) or *even* (two points). Modern writers say *active* for the single point and *passive* for the double. This course uses those terms, with the caution that they are interpretive glosses, not source vocabulary. A single point is read as expressed; a pair as contained or settled. Lesson 3 shows where the older meanings support that reading and where they do not.

The four positions also carry associations. The historical texts speak spatially: Cattan divides figures by whether they have "more pointes on high" or "more pointes below," and Crowley's table calls Caput Draconis "the upper threshold" and Cauda Draconis "the threshold lower." The Golden Dawn school later assigned each line an element: fire, air, water, earth from top to bottom. That line-element system is not stated in Cattan or Agrippa, so this course flags it as modern, but it is now standard and this site's glyph data follows it. A later section returns to it.

A word on drawing. The figures are drawn from the top line downward, and by old custom the points within each row are set down from right to left, a habit inherited from the Arabic practice in sand, which Agrippa preserves in the passage above ("from the right hand towards the left"). For a finished figure the direction no longer shows: what survives on the page is one dot centered in the line, or two dots spread apart. Practice drawing a few figures now, large and deliberate. A single point should sit in the middle of the line; a double should straddle it symmetrically. Sloppy figures cause real misreadings: a cramped double taken for a single changes one figure into another, and in a chart that error propagates.

Throughout this course, a figure's pattern will sometimes be written in a compact notation: the four lines from top to bottom, single as 1 and double as 2. Acquisitio is 2·1·2·1; Puella is 1·2·1·1; Via is 1·1·1·1. This notation is the site's, not source vocabulary (the old books drew dots and nothing but dots), but it is handy for checking your work, and it matches the data tables that drive this site's figure pages.

## Sixteen and only sixteen

How many figures can the rule produce? Each line is one of two states, and there are four lines, so the count is two multiplied by itself four times: sixteen. The first line can be single or double: two possibilities. For each of those, the second line can be single or double, giving four. The third doubles the count again, to eight, and the fourth doubles it once more: sixteen, exactly, with every combination realized and named. There is no seventeenth figure, and there is no combination of four binary lines that lacks a name. The system is *complete* in the strict sense: whatever parities the casting produces, the result is one of the sixteen, every time.

The sixteen figures come from the rule itself. The medieval texts list the complete set because four binary lines have only sixteen possible forms. Because the set is closed, the sources can build tables of Witnesses, Judges, and figure-against-figure results that cover every possible case, as Cattan's and Heydon's tables do.

The order of a figure-list is a convention, and the sources do not agree on one. Crowley's chapter runs through the zodiac, because the Golden Dawn organized everything zodiacally. Cattan groups the figures by planet. The order above is this site's own, chosen so that structural relatives sit side by side: Via next to Populus, each Fortune beside the other, Acquisitio beside Amissio, and so on down the column. Whatever order you meet them in, the names are stable across the source set, and they are Latin, with an English gloss alongside. Caput Draconis and Cauda Draconis, the Dragon's Head and Tail, are the old names for the lunar nodes, the crossing-points of the Moon's path with the Sun's.

Many of the names arrive already paired: Gain and Loss; Joy and Sorrow; Boy and Girl; White and Red; Greater and Lesser Fortune; Head and Tail. The pairing is no accident: the pairs in the words correspond to pairs in the dots.

## Counting points

Add up the points in a figure (one for each single line, two for each double) and you get its point total. The smallest possible total is four: every line single, which is Via, 1·1·1·1, four points in a vertical stroke down the page. The largest is eight: every line double, which is Populus, 2·2·2·2, a full double column. Every other figure falls between. A figure's total is fixed by how many of its four lines are double: no doubles gives four points, one double gives five, two doubles six, three doubles seven, four doubles eight. Count the ways of choosing which lines are double and you have counted the figures in each class. There is one figure with no double lines (Via) and one with four (Populus). There are four figures with exactly one double line (Caput Draconis, Puella, Puer, and Cauda Draconis, the five-point figures) because the lone double can sit in any of four positions. Symmetrically, there are four figures with exactly one *single* line: Laetitia, Rubeus, Albus, and Tristitia, the seven-point figures. And there are six figures with two doubles and two singles, the six-point figures: Fortuna Major, Fortuna Minor, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer, because two positions can be chosen out of four in six ways. One, four, six, four, one: sixteen.

The totals matter to the sources. You have already met one use in the assigned reading: at the close of his chapter of qualities, Cattan measures out a life from a point-count, telling you that a figure of six points found in the first house "signifyeth that a man shall live sixe houres, sixe dayes, sixe moneths, sixe yeares, or 72," and that a five-point figure gives five of each, "and likewise of other figures according to the number of the points which they have" (Cattan, *The Geomancie*, Book III, ch. 27). Whatever you make of that as biography, it presupposes that the point total is a property a geomancer reads as fluently as the figure's name. Timing techniques throughout the sources lean on these numbers.

But the deepest use of the totals is coarser than the number itself: it is the number's parity. Eight of the sixteen figures have an even total: Via with four; Fortuna Major, Fortuna Minor, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, and Carcer with six; Populus with eight. The other eight are odd: the four five-point figures and the four seven-point figures. This split into even and odd halves governs the validity of every chart, through a rule you will meet in full in Lesson 4. At the summit of a geomantic chart stand two figures called the Witnesses, and from them is derived a final figure called the Judge, which gives the answer to the whole question. And of the Judge, Cattan says this:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-04-of-the-judge-chap" />

"The which judge ought alwayes of necessitie to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false." Only the eight even figures can ever stand as Judge; if an odd figure appears in that place, the chart has been miscast and must be done again. The rule is the geomancer's checksum, an error-detecting code built into the method four hundred years before anyone coined the term. Why the Judge *must* come out even (why the arithmetic of the chart makes an odd Judge impossible rather than merely forbidden) is a short proof, deferred to Lesson 4, where you will have the chart-construction in front of you. For now, learn the eight even figures as a set: Via, Populus, the two Fortunes, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer.

## Entering, exiting, and stability

What matters now is not how many points a figure has but where they sit: whether its weight hangs in the head or the feet. Cattan considered exactly this. At the opening of the chapter of qualities you read for this lesson, he lays down a rule for sorting the whole alphabet by the disposition of its points:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

The figure's upper half (the first and second lines) weighs against its lower half (the third and fourth). Figures with more points above than below are "entring in," and Cattan calls them good; figures with more points below than above are "going out," and evil; figures equally weighted are "meanes," middling. Each class carries one exception, which Cattan names: Tristitia is top-heavy but not good, Laetitia is bottom-heavy but not evil, and Carcer is balanced but not middling. The exceptions are about goodness, not geometry; the sorting itself is pure arithmetic. Five figures are top-heavy, entering: Fortuna Major (four points above, two below), Tristitia (four above, three below), Albus (four above, three below), Puella (three above, two below), and Caput Draconis (three above, two below). Five are bottom-heavy, exiting: Fortuna Minor, Laetitia, Rubeus, Puer, and Cauda Draconis, each the top-to-bottom reflection of one of the first five. Six are balanced: Via, Populus, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer, which are, you will notice, six of the eight even figures, and the only figures whose halves *can* balance.

The language is spatial. A figure with more points above is treated as entering; a figure with more points below as going out. Crowley's table keeps the same idea in the names of the two nodes: Caput Draconis is "the upper threshold," and Cauda Draconis is "the threshold lower, or going out." The dots show the distinction: Caput (2·1·1·1) has its double at the top, while Cauda (1·1·1·2) has it at the bottom.

This old rule lines up with the last column of the master table, which this site's figure pages call *mobility*. Eight figures are stable: Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Tristitia, Puella, Albus, Carcer, Caput Draconis. Eight are mobile: Via, Fortuna Minor, Amissio, Laetitia, Puer, Rubeus, Conjunctio, Cauda Draconis. The five top-heavy entering figures are stable, and the five bottom-heavy exiting figures are mobile. The six balanced figures are decided by nature rather than geometry: Populus, Acquisitio and Carcer are stable; Via, Amissio and Conjunctio are mobile.

What does the classification do in practice? In a reading (this is anticipating Lesson 3 and beyond, but the structural point belongs here) a stable figure signifies a matter that endures, settles, resists change: slow to arrive, slow to leave, fixed in its place. A mobile figure signifies a matter in motion: quick, transient, apt to change or escape. The same quality is good or bad depending entirely on the question. Ask whether a sickness will pass, and you want mobility; ask whether a marriage will hold, and you want stability; ask after a prisoner, and Carcer's immovable balance is the worst possible news. Even Cattan's blunt sorting of entering figures as "good" and exiting as "evill" is best read this way: a matter arriving is, for most questions, better news than a matter departing. You will learn in Lesson 3 to refine that bluntness figure by figure; for now, learn the two eights as firmly as you learned the even and the odd. The two divisions cut across each other: even Amissio is mobile while even Acquisitio is stable; odd Laetitia is mobile while odd Tristitia is stable. Parity and mobility are independent coordinates, and a figure is located by both.

## Mirrors and counterparts

The sixteen figures form a structure riddled with symmetry. Two operations generate the symmetries that matter, and both are simple enough to do in your head.

The first is *inversion*: turn the figure upside down, so that the first line becomes the fourth and the second becomes the third. In the compact notation, reverse the string. Invert Fortuna Major, 2·2·1·1, and you get 1·1·2·2, which is Fortuna Minor. Invert Laetitia, 1·2·2·2, and you get 2·2·2·1, which is Tristitia. Invert Puer, 1·1·2·1, and you get 1·2·1·1, which is Puella. Invert Albus and you get Rubeus; invert Caput Draconis and you get Cauda Draconis; invert Acquisitio, 2·1·2·1, and you get 1·2·1·2, which is Amissio. Six pairs of figures exchange identities under inversion. The remaining four figures are palindromes (they read the same downward as upward) and so inversion leaves them exactly where they stood: Via (1·1·1·1), Populus (2·2·2·2), Conjunctio (2·1·1·2), and Carcer (1·2·2·1). The four self-inverse figures are all even, and not by accident: a palindrome's halves weigh the same, so its total is twice its upper half.

Inversion flips the names, too. Greater Fortune flips into Lesser Fortune. Joy flips into Sorrow. Boy into Girl, White into Red, the Dragon's Head into the Dragon's Tail, Gain into Loss. The rhyming pairs you noticed in the names are precisely the inversion pairs in the dots. The semantic opposites in the names are the geometry's mirror images. Whoever first named these figures (and the names were fixed before the Latin sources began) named them structurally. The site's figure data records this pairing under the name *counterpart*, and on this site the counterpart of every figure is exactly its inversion: each of the six mirror-pairs is a counterpart-pair, and each of the four palindromes is its own counterpart. When a figure page tells you that the counterpart of Albus is Rubeus, it is telling you something you can verify by turning Albus upside down.

The second operation is *complementation*: leave the figure right side up, but exchange the state of every line, so every single becomes a double and every double becomes a single. In notation, swap every 1 for 2 and every 2 for 1. The complement of Via is Populus. The complement of Puer (1·1·2·1) is Albus, not Puella. The complement of Laetitia is Caput Draconis, not Tristitia. The complement of Conjunctio is Carcer. Complementation cannot leave a figure unchanged, because no line can equal its own opposite. It sorts the sixteen figures into eight pairs: Via with Populus, Fortuna Major with Fortuna Minor, Acquisitio with Amissio, Laetitia with Caput Draconis, Tristitia with Cauda Draconis, Puer with Albus, Puella with Rubeus, Conjunctio with Carcer. A complement-pair's totals always sum to twelve.

For most figures the two operations disagree: inversion sends Puer to Puella, while complementation sends him to Albus. But for two pairs the operations coincide: the two Fortunes, and Acquisitio with Amissio. Invert Acquisitio or complement it, and either way you get Amissio. A pairing in the texts is one of three kinds: a mirror in the dots, a negative in the dots, or a kinship in the heavens. They are three different relations, and a figure stands in all three at once. The exercises will make you compute them until the distinction is reflex.

## The elemental reading of the lines

So far the four lines have been positions: first, second, third, fourth. The Golden Dawn school gave them elements: fire, air, water, earth from top to bottom. The caution from earlier holds: Cattan and the pseudo-Agrippa do not assign fire to the top line of a figure. The line-elements are a nineteenth-century systematization. They are useful and now standard in modern practice, so this site adopts them as its glyph convention: when a figure's dots are given as 2·1·2·1, the four numbers are its fire, air, water and earth lines, in that order.

The fire line is will and drive. The air line is thought, speech, and communication. The water line is emotion and appetite. The earth line is matter, money, and physical result. A single point means the element is *active* in the figure. A double point means it is *latent* or contained. Again, "active" and "latent" are modern interpretive vocabulary, not source diction; use them as a mnemonic.

The system organizes the alphabet well. The four seven-point figures each have one single line, so one active element. Laetitia is active in fire; Rubeus in air; Albus in water; Tristitia in earth. The four five-point figures each have one double line, so one latent element among three active: Caput Draconis in fire, Puella in air, Puer in water, Cauda Draconis in earth. The six six-point figures balance two active lines against two latent lines: Fortuna Major active in water and earth, Fortuna Minor in fire and air, Acquisitio in air and earth, Amissio in fire and water, Conjunctio in air and water, and Carcer in fire and earth.

The line-elements are distinct from a second elemental assignment, one that *is* in the old sources: the assignment of a whole figure to an element. Cattan does this throughout (you saw him close his note on figure-qualities with the remark that for the fruitfulness of the year "the figures entring into the elements of water and earth be good, and those which be of fire and ayre be ill") and his per-figure chapters say things like "of the element of the fire" of Amissio. The pseudo-Agrippa arranges the sixteen into four elemental triplicities:

Crowley's table carries an element column of its own, derived from each figure's zodiacal sign; and it disagrees with Agrippa and Cattan as often as not, assigning Acquisitio to fire by way of Sagittarius where the older scheme gives it to water by way of Pisces. The disagreements are real and the site's figure pages record every variant; do not paper over them. For this course: the *figure-element* is an attribution, a source claim on which the sources differ and must be cited; the *line-elements* are a fixed modern convention, the same in every book that uses them at all, because they follow the geometry. When this course says "Amissio's fire line is single," that is a statement about dots, checkable by counting. When it says "Amissio is a figure of fire," that is Cattan's claim, and another source may say otherwise.

## Worked example

Now every tool from this lesson, on the type-case pair: Acquisitio and Amissio.

<FigureGlyph dots={[2,1,2,1]} name="Acquisitio" size={4} /> <FigureGlyph dots={[1,2,1,2]} name="Amissio" size={4} />

In notation, Acquisitio is 2·1·2·1: double fire, single air, double water, single earth. Amissio is 1·2·1·2: single fire, double air, single water, double earth. Stacked one above the other, the two strings disagree in every line. Where Acquisitio is double, Amissio is single; where Acquisitio is single, Amissio is double. They are complements. Reversed, Acquisitio's string 2·1·2·1 becomes 1·2·1·2, Amissio again. They are also inversions of each other, one of only two pairs in the alphabet (with the Fortunes) for which the two mirror-operations agree. Gain and loss are each other's reflection in both mirrors: perfect opposites.

Count the points. Acquisitio: two, one, two, one, giving six. Amissio: one, two, one, two, giving six. Both even, both therefore members of the eight-figure family from which alone a Judge can come; when either stands at the foot of a chart as Judge, the chart is at least validly cast. Their evenness has the same source: each figure alternates single and double down its four lines, the only two figures in the alphabet that do, and an alternating figure must have two of each.

Weigh the halves. Acquisitio's upper half holds three points (two and one), and so does its lower (two and one): balanced, one of Cattan's "meanes," and the same is true of Amissio. Cattan's threshold rule cannot separate this pair, because both are balanced. The sources assign their mobility by nature instead. Acquisitio is stable: what is gained is held, banked, kept. Amissio is mobile: loss is the failure of a thing to stay. Same points, same parity, same balance; different mobility.

Read the lines elementally, single as active and double as latent. Acquisitio is active in air and earth: planning and material result. Amissio is active in fire and water: will and desire, but not calculation or possession. These are mnemonics, not source rules, but the sources support them: Crowley makes Acquisitio good for profit and Amissio bad for gain; Agrippa gives Acquisitio to Jupiter and Amissio to Venus retrograde or combust; Cattan lists Acquisitio among figures of gain and Amissio among figures of loss, war, and false love.

Now the extreme pair, more briefly.

<FigureGlyph dots={[1,1,1,1]} name="Via" size={4} /> <FigureGlyph dots={[2,2,2,2]} name="Populus" size={4} />

Via, 1·1·1·1, is the minimum: four points, every line single, every element active. It means the road, journey, and change, and it is mobile. Populus, 2·2·2·2, is the maximum: eight points, every line double, every element latent. It means the people or crowd, and takes much of its character from company. It is stable. Each figure is its own inversion and counterpart; by complementation, each becomes the other. Both are even and can stand as Judge. Agrippa assigns both to the Moon, as the passage shows.

That is a complete structural dissection, doable on any figure in about a minute: notation, point total, parity, balance of halves, mobility, inversion, complement, counterpart, active and latent lines. Before the exercises, drill the flashcards below (glyph to name) until you have made two consecutive passes through all sixteen without a single error or hesitation. Not one pass: two, clean, in a row. Recognition must run ahead of reflection from now on, because every chart you ever read will begin with fifteen acts of recognition performed before the thinking starts.

<Exercise n={5} title="The standing and the walking" kind="drill">
  From memory, sort the sixteen figures into stable and mobile. Then answer in a few sentences each: first, what Cattan's rule of entering and exiting figures (Book III, ch. 27) has to do with the division, including the three exceptions he names; second, what stability and mobility imply for the speed and permanence of a matter in a reading, with one example of a question in which a mobile figure would be welcome and one in which it would not.
  <ModelAnswer>
    Stable: Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Tristitia, Puella, Albus, Carcer, Caput Draconis. Mobile: Via, Fortuna Minor, Amissio, Laetitia, Puer, Rubeus, Conjunctio, Cauda Draconis.

    Cattan's rule sorts figures by weighing the upper two lines against the lower two: "all the fygures which have more pointes on high then belowe, be entring in and good, saving Tristitia. And those which have more pointes below then on hie, be going out and evill, saving Lætitia. And those which have as many above as beneath be meanes, saving Carcer" (Cattan, *The Geomancie*, Book III, ch. 27, p. 215). The five top-heavy, entering figures (Fortuna Major, Tristitia, Albus, Puella, Caput Draconis) are exactly the stable figures that are not balanced; the five bottom-heavy, exiting figures (Fortuna Minor, Laetitia, Rubeus, Puer, Cauda Draconis) are exactly the unbalanced mobile ones. The six balanced "means" (Via, Populus, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer) the sources divide by nature: the crowd, the kept gain and the prison stand; the road, the departing loss and the dissolving meeting move. Cattan's three exceptions concern goodness, not geometry: Tristitia enters but is not good, Laetitia exits but is not evil, Carcer is balanced but not middling.

    In a reading, a stable figure signifies a matter that endures and resists change: slow to arrive, slow to leave, fixed; a mobile figure signifies a matter in motion: quick, transient, apt to change or escape. Welcome mobility: ask "will this illness pass quickly?" and a mobile figure promises movement and release (compare Cattan's listing of Via, Cauda Draconis and Amissio for amendment of sickness, Book III, ch. 27). Unwelcome mobility: ask "will this marriage endure?" or "will I keep this position?" and there one wants the matter to stand, and a mobile figure warns that it will not stay as it is.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>
