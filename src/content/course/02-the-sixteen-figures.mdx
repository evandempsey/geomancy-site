Geomancy uses sixteen figures. You must know them from memory before you can cast or read a chart. This lesson teaches their structure: what a geomantic figure is, before anyone has said what it means. The meanings begin in Lesson 3.

A figure has four stacked lines. Each line has either one point or two. There is no third state and no fifth line. Four lines with two possible states each yield exactly sixteen figures.

A figure's structure shapes its meaning. When Cattan says that a six-pointed figure in the first house can signify six hours, six days, six months, or six years, he is using the point total. When the sources say that the Judge of a chart must be even or the chart is false, they are using parity.

## The anatomy of a figure

A geomantic figure is four lines (in the older English texts, four "ranks" or rows of points), stacked vertically. The lines are counted from the top: first, second, third, fourth. Each line contains either a single point or a pair of points, and the pair is drawn spread apart so that the eye can never confuse the two states. That is the entire anatomy. A figure is a column of four binary choices.

Why one point or two, and never three or none? This comes from how the lines are made. To generate one line, the geomancer makes a row of points rapidly and without counting, holding the question in mind; the number of points is deliberately not controlled. The points are then counted off in pairs. If one point remains unpaired, the line is *odd* and is recorded as a single point; if the points pair off exactly, the line is *even* and is recorded as two. A row is either odd or even, so a line is single or double, and nothing else can appear. Here is the pseudo-Agrippa describing exactly this:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/casting-mothers" />

Agrippa's phrase is "according to the even or uneven marking every several line." He means that each row is reduced to odd or even. Count the row in pairs. If one point is left over, write one point. If none is left over, write two. Four reduced rows make one figure.

The two states of a line have traditional and modern names. The old texts say what Agrippa says: a line is *uneven* (one point) or *even* (two points). Modern writers say *active* for the single point and *passive* for the double. This course uses those terms, with the caution that they are interpretive glosses, not source vocabulary. A single point is read as expressed; a pair as contained or settled. Lesson 3 shows where the older meanings support that reading and where they do not.

The four positions also carry associations. The historical texts speak spatially: Cattan divides figures by whether they have "more pointes on high" or "more pointes below," and Crowley's table calls Caput Draconis "the upper threshold" and Cauda Draconis "the threshold lower." The Golden Dawn school later assigned each line an element: fire, air, water, earth from top to bottom. That line-element system is not stated in Cattan or Agrippa, so this course flags it as modern, but it is now standard and this site's glyph data follows it. A later section returns to it.

A word on drawing. The figures are drawn from the top line downward, and by old custom the points are set down from right to left, the Arabic habit Agrippa preserves ("from the right hand towards the left"). Practice drawing a few figures now, large and deliberate: a single point centered in the line, a double straddling it. Sloppy figures cause real misreadings, because a cramped double taken for a single changes one figure into another.

Throughout this course, a figure's pattern will sometimes be written in a compact notation: the four lines from top to bottom, single as 1 and double as 2. Acquisitio is 2·1·2·1; Via is 1·1·1·1. The notation is the site's, not source vocabulary, but it is handy for checking work.

## Sixteen and only sixteen

How many figures can the rule produce? Each line is one of two states, and there are four lines. The first line can be single or double: two possibilities. Each further line doubles the count: four, eight, sixteen. So there are exactly sixteen figures, with every combination realized and named. There is no seventeenth figure, and no combination of four binary lines lacks a name. Whatever parities the casting produces, the result is one of the sixteen, every time. Because the set is closed, the sources can build tables of Witnesses, Judges, and figure-against-figure results that cover every possible case, as Cattan's and Heydon's tables do.

The order of a figure-list is a convention, and the sources do not agree on one. Crowley's chapter runs through the zodiac, because the Golden Dawn organized everything zodiacally. Cattan groups the figures by planet. The order above is this site's own, chosen so that structural relatives sit side by side: Via next to Populus, each Fortune beside the other, Acquisitio beside Amissio, and so on. Whatever order you meet them in, the names are stable across the source set, and they are Latin, with an English gloss alongside. Caput Draconis and Cauda Draconis, the Dragon's Head and Tail, are the old names for the lunar nodes, the crossing-points of the Moon's path with the Sun's.

Many of the names arrive already paired: Gain and Loss; Joy and Sorrow; Boy and Girl; White and Red; Greater and Lesser Fortune; Head and Tail. The pairs in the words correspond to pairs in the dots, as the section on mirrors below shows.

## Counting points

Add up the points in a figure (one for each single line, two for each double) and you get its point total. The smallest total is four: every line single, which is Via. The largest is eight: every line double, which is Populus. Every other figure falls between, and the total is fixed by how many lines are double: no doubles gives four points, one double gives five, two doubles six, three doubles seven, four doubles eight.

The classes run one, four, six, four, one: Via alone at four points; the four five-point figures (Caput Draconis, Puella, Puer, Cauda Draconis); the six six-point figures (the two Fortunes, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer); the four seven-point figures (Laetitia, Rubeus, Albus, Tristitia); Populus alone at eight.

The totals matter to the sources. At the close of his chapter of qualities, Cattan measures out a life from a point-count: a figure of six points in the first house "signifyeth that a man shall live sixe houres, sixe dayes, sixe moneths, sixe yeares, or 72" (Cattan, *The Geomancie*, Book III, ch. 27). Timing techniques lean on these numbers, so read the point total as fluently as the figure's name.

The most important use of the totals is the number's parity. Eight of the sixteen figures have an even total: Via with four; Fortuna Major, Fortuna Minor, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, and Carcer with six; Populus with eight. The other eight are odd: the four five-point figures and the four seven-point figures. This split governs the validity of every chart, through a rule you will meet in full in Lesson 4. At the foot of a geomantic chart stand two figures called the Witnesses, and from them is derived a final figure called the Judge, which gives the answer to the whole question. Of the Judge, Cattan says this:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-04-of-the-judge-chap" />

"The which judge ought alwayes of necessitie to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false." Only the eight even figures can ever stand as Judge. If an odd figure appears in that place, the chart has been miscast and must be done again, so the rule works as a built-in check on the casting. Lesson 4 shows why the arithmetic of the chart makes an odd Judge impossible. For now, learn the eight even figures as a set: Via, Populus, the two Fortunes, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer.

## Entering, exiting, and stability

Now sort figures by where their points sit: whether more points stand in the upper half or the lower half. Cattan gives this rule at the opening of the chapter of qualities you read for this lesson:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-27-entering-exiting" />

The figure's upper half (the first and second lines) weighs against its lower half (the third and fourth). Figures with more points above than below are "entring in," and Cattan calls them good. Figures with more points below than above are "going out," and evil. Figures equally weighted are "meanes," middling. Each class carries one exception, which Cattan names: Tristitia is top-heavy but not good, Laetitia is bottom-heavy but not evil, and Carcer is balanced but not middling. The exceptions are about goodness, not geometry; the sorting itself is pure arithmetic.

Five figures are top-heavy, entering: Fortuna Major (four points above, two below), Tristitia (four above, three below), Albus (four above, three below), Puella (three above, two below), and Caput Draconis (three above, two below). Five are bottom-heavy, exiting: Fortuna Minor, Laetitia, Rubeus, Puer, and Cauda Draconis, each the top-to-bottom reflection of one of the first five. Six are balanced: Via, Populus, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer. Note that the balanced six are six of the eight even figures, the only figures whose halves *can* balance.

Crowley's table keeps the same idea in the names of the two nodes: Caput Draconis is "the upper threshold," Cauda Draconis "the threshold lower, or going out"; Caput (2·1·1·1) has its double at the top, Cauda (1·1·1·2) at the bottom.

This old rule lines up with the last column of the master table, which this site's figure pages call *mobility*. Eight figures are stable: Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Tristitia, Puella, Albus, Carcer, Caput Draconis. Eight are mobile: Via, Fortuna Minor, Amissio, Laetitia, Puer, Rubeus, Conjunctio, Cauda Draconis. The five entering figures are stable, the five exiting figures mobile, and the six balanced figures are divided by nature: Populus, Acquisitio and Carcer stand, Via, Amissio and Conjunctio move.

What does the classification do in practice? In a reading, a stable figure signifies a matter that endures, settles, and resists change: slow to arrive, slow to leave, fixed in its place. A mobile figure signifies a matter in motion: quick, transient, apt to change or escape. The same quality is good or bad depending on the question. Ask whether a sickness will pass, and you want mobility. Ask whether a marriage will hold, and you want stability. Even Cattan's blunt sorting of entering figures as "good" and exiting as "evill" is best read this way: a matter arriving is, for most questions, better news than a matter departing. Lesson 3 refines this figure by figure; for now, learn the two eights as firmly as you learned the even and the odd. Note that the two divisions cut across each other: even Amissio is mobile while even Acquisitio is stable; odd Laetitia is mobile while odd Tristitia is stable. Parity and mobility are independent properties, and a figure has both.

## Mirrors and counterparts

The sixteen figures pair off in regular ways. Two operations generate the pairings, and both are simple enough to do in your head.

The first is *inversion*: turn the figure upside down, so the first line becomes the fourth and the second the third. In the notation, reverse the string. Invert Fortuna Major, 2·2·1·1, and you get 1·1·2·2, Fortuna Minor. Invert Laetitia and you get Tristitia; Puer gives Puella; Albus gives Rubeus; Caput Draconis gives Cauda Draconis; Acquisitio gives Amissio. Six pairs exchange identities under inversion. The remaining four figures read the same upside down, so inversion leaves them where they stood: Via, Populus, Conjunctio, and Carcer.

Inversion flips the names, too. Greater Fortune flips into Lesser Fortune, Joy into Sorrow, Boy into Girl, White into Red, the Dragon's Head into the Dragon's Tail, Gain into Loss. The paired names you noticed earlier are exactly the inversion pairs in the dots. Whoever first named these figures (and the names were fixed before the Latin sources began) named them structurally. The site's figure data records this pairing under the name *counterpart*, and on this site the counterpart of every figure is exactly its inversion: each of the six mirror-pairs is a counterpart-pair, and each of the four palindromes is its own counterpart. When a figure page says the counterpart of Albus is Rubeus, you can verify it by turning Albus upside down.

The second operation is *complementation*: leave the figure right side up, but exchange the state of every line, single for double and double for single. The complement of Via is Populus; of Puer (1·1·2·1), Albus, not Puella; of Laetitia, Caput Draconis, not Tristitia; of Conjunctio, Carcer. Complementation cannot leave a figure unchanged, so it sorts the sixteen into eight pairs: Via–Populus, the two Fortunes, Acquisitio–Amissio, Laetitia–Caput Draconis, Tristitia–Cauda Draconis, Puer–Albus, Puella–Rubeus, Conjunctio–Carcer.

For most figures the two operations disagree: inversion sends Puer to Puella, while complementation sends him to Albus. But for two pairs the operations coincide: the two Fortunes, and Acquisitio with Amissio. Invert Acquisitio or complement it, and either way you get Amissio. Acquisitio is good for gain; Amissio is bad for gain and good only for losing, with the exception "sometimes for love," which waits for Lesson 3. The older sources draw the same opposition through planetary dignity rather than through dots. The pseudo-Agrippa makes Amissio a Venus gone wrong:

Fortunate Puella and Amissio "as it were retrograde, or combust": the same planet strong and ruined. The other extreme complement-pair is Via and Populus:

Via and Populus are both figures of the Moon. Via is the mobile, all-single figure; Populus is the stable, all-double figure. The sources read them as two phases of one planet.

The old sources also pair the figures a third way, by planet, as the Agrippa quotes above show. Planetary pairing sometimes tracks the structural pairings and sometimes cuts across them. Puella and Amissio share Venus, but structurally Puella's mirror is Puer and Amissio's is Acquisitio. Acquisitio shares Jupiter not with Amissio but with Laetitia:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/acquisitio-laetitia" />

So a pairing in the texts is one of three kinds: inversion, complement, or shared planet. They are three different relations, and a figure stands in all three at once. The exercises will make you compute them until the distinction is automatic.

## The elemental reading of the lines

So far the four lines have been positions: first, second, third, fourth. The Golden Dawn school gave them elements: fire, air, water, earth from top to bottom. The caution from earlier holds: Cattan and the pseudo-Agrippa do not assign fire to the top line of a figure. The line-elements are a nineteenth-century systematization. They are useful and now standard in modern practice, so this site adopts them as its glyph convention: when a figure's dots are given as 2·1·2·1, the four numbers are its fire, air, water and earth lines, in that order.

The fire line is will and drive. The air line is thought, speech, and communication. The water line is emotion and appetite. The earth line is matter, money, and physical result. A single point means the element is *active* in the figure. A double point means it is *latent* or contained. Again, "active" and "latent" are modern interpretive vocabulary, not source diction; use them as a mnemonic.

The system organizes the sixteen figures well. The four seven-point figures each have one active element: Laetitia fire, Rubeus air, Albus water, Tristitia earth. The four five-point figures each have one latent element: Caput Draconis fire, Puella air, Puer water, Cauda Draconis earth. The six six-point figures balance two active lines against two latent.

The line-elements are distinct from a second elemental assignment, one that *is* in the old sources: the assignment of a whole figure to an element. Cattan does this throughout. His chapter of qualities says that for the fruitfulness of the year "the figures entring into the elements of water and earth be good, and those which be of fire and ayre be ill," and his per-figure chapters say things like "of the element of the fire" of Amissio. The pseudo-Agrippa arranges the sixteen into four elemental triplicities:

<Quote id="agrippa/general/triplicities" />

Crowley's table carries an element column of its own, derived from each figure's zodiacal sign, and it disagrees with Agrippa and Cattan as often as not, assigning Acquisitio to fire by way of Sagittarius where the older scheme gives it to water by way of Pisces. The disagreements are real, and the site's figure pages record every variant. Keep the two systems apart. The *figure-element* is a source claim on which the sources differ, and it must be cited. The *line-elements* are a fixed modern convention, the same in every book that uses them, because they follow the geometry. When this course says "Amissio's fire line is single," that is a statement about dots, checkable by counting. When it says "Amissio is a figure of fire," that is Cattan's claim, and another source may say otherwise.

## Worked example

Now every tool from this lesson, on the type-case pair: Acquisitio and Amissio.

<FigureGlyph dots={[2,1,2,1]} name="Acquisitio" size={4} /> <FigureGlyph dots={[1,2,1,2]} name="Amissio" size={4} />

In notation, Acquisitio is 2·1·2·1: double fire, single air, double water, single earth. Amissio is 1·2·1·2: single fire, double air, single water, double earth. The two strings disagree in every line, so they are complements. Reversed, Acquisitio's string 2·1·2·1 becomes 1·2·1·2, Amissio again, so they are also inversions of each other. With the Fortunes, they are one of only two pairs for which the two operations agree.

Count the points. Acquisitio: two, one, two, one, giving six. Amissio: one, two, one, two, giving six. Both are even, so both belong to the eight figures from which alone a Judge can come. These are the only two figures that alternate single and double down all four lines, and an alternating figure must have two of each.

Weigh the halves. Acquisitio's upper half holds three points (two and one), and so does its lower (two and one): balanced, one of Cattan's "meanes," and the same is true of Amissio. Cattan's threshold rule cannot separate this pair, because both are balanced. The sources assign their mobility by nature instead. Acquisitio is stable: what is gained is held, banked, kept. Amissio is mobile: loss is the failure of a thing to stay. Same points, same parity, same balance; different mobility.

Read the lines elementally, single as active and double as latent. Acquisitio is active in air and earth: planning and material result. Amissio is active in fire and water: will and desire, but not calculation or possession. These are mnemonics, not source rules, but the sources support them: Crowley makes Acquisitio good for profit and Amissio bad for gain; Agrippa gives Acquisitio to Jupiter and Amissio to Venus retrograde or combust; Cattan lists Acquisitio among figures of gain and Amissio among figures of loss, war, and false love.

Now the extreme pair, more briefly.

<FigureGlyph dots={[1,1,1,1]} name="Via" size={4} /> <FigureGlyph dots={[2,2,2,2]} name="Populus" size={4} />

Via, 1·1·1·1, is the minimum: four points, every element active, mobile, the figure of the road and change. Populus, 2·2·2·2, is the maximum: eight points, every element latent, stable, the crowd that takes its character from company. Each is its own inversion and counterpart; by complementation, each becomes the other. Both are even and can stand as Judge, and Agrippa assigns both to the Moon.

That is a complete structural analysis, doable on any figure in about a minute: notation, point total, parity, balance of halves, mobility, inversion, complement, counterpart, active and latent lines. <Exercise n={5} title="The standing and the walking" kind="drill">
  From memory, sort the sixteen figures into stable and mobile. Then answer in a few sentences each: first, what Cattan's rule of entering and exiting figures (Book III, ch. 27) has to do with the division, including the three exceptions he names; second, what stability and mobility imply for the speed and permanence of a matter in a reading, with one example of a question in which a mobile figure would be welcome and one in which it would not.
  <ModelAnswer>
    Stable: Populus, Fortuna Major, Acquisitio, Tristitia, Puella, Albus, Carcer, Caput Draconis. Mobile: Via, Fortuna Minor, Amissio, Laetitia, Puer, Rubeus, Conjunctio, Cauda Draconis.

    Cattan's rule sorts figures by weighing the upper two lines against the lower two: "all the fygures which have more pointes on high then belowe, be entring in and good, saving Tristitia. And those which have more pointes below then on hie, be going out and evill, saving Lætitia. And those which have as many above as beneath be meanes, saving Carcer" (Cattan, *The Geomancie*, Book III, ch. 27, p. 215). The five top-heavy, entering figures (Fortuna Major, Tristitia, Albus, Puella, Caput Draconis) are exactly the stable figures that are not balanced; the five bottom-heavy, exiting figures (Fortuna Minor, Laetitia, Rubeus, Puer, Cauda Draconis) are exactly the unbalanced mobile ones. The six balanced "means" (Via, Populus, Acquisitio, Amissio, Conjunctio, Carcer) the sources divide by nature: the crowd, the kept gain and the prison stand; the road, the departing loss and the dissolving meeting move. Cattan's three exceptions concern goodness, not geometry: Tristitia enters but is not good, Laetitia exits but is not evil, Carcer is balanced but not middling.

    In a reading, a stable figure signifies a matter that endures and resists change: slow to arrive, slow to leave, fixed; a mobile figure signifies a matter in motion: quick, transient, apt to change or escape. Welcome mobility: ask "will this illness pass quickly?" and a mobile figure promises movement and release (compare Cattan's listing of Via, Cauda Draconis and Amissio for amendment of sickness, Book III, ch. 27). Unwelcome mobility: ask "will this marriage endure?" or "will I keep this position?" and there one wants the matter to stand, and a mobile figure warns that it will not stay as it is.
  </ModelAnswer>
</Exercise>

<Exercise n={6} title="Why sixteen?" kind="quiz">
  The structural argument first, then what follows from it.

  1. Give the structural argument for sixteen figures in one sentence.
  2. What does the closure of the set of figures guarantee about any casting, however performed?
  3. What did the set's small, fixed size let the sources build that an open-ended symbol set never could?
  4. How many figures carry exactly six points?
     - a) four, one for each element
     - b) six, the ways of choosing two double lines from four
     - c) eight, half of the figures
  5. Which figures can stand as Judge, and under what rule? Cite it.
  6. Which phrase of the pseudo-Agrippa's definition names the method's whole generative principle?
  7. Name an independently evolved system that exhibits the same closure and mirror-structure.

  <ModelAnswer>
    1. A figure is four lines, each odd or even, so the figures number two times two times two times two: sixteen, by enumeration, not by custom or taste.
    2. No casting can produce a figure outside the sixteen; every possible outcome of the procedure has a name and a meaning waiting for it.
    3. Complete tables: every pair of Witnesses with its Judge, every figure in every house, as Cattan's and Heydon's books in fact print.
    4. b). The totals run four to eight in classes of one, four, six, four and one, counting the ways of placing the double lines.
    5. Only the eight even figures: "the which judge ought alwayes of necessitie to be even: for if it be not, the figure should be false" (Cattan, *The Geomancie*, Book III, ch. 4).
    6. "According to the reason or rule of equallity or inequallity" (Agrippa attrib., *Of Geomancy*, trans. Turner, 1655): even or odd is the generative principle entire.
    7. The I Ching's sixty-four hexagrams, six binary lines instead of four, with the same closure and the same mirror-structure.
