Everything before this lesson was preparation. This lesson teaches the finished reading method, resolves Chart C, and judges a fourth chart, Chart D, from a standing start. It answers the questions Lesson 9 deferred: what outranks what, how much evidence is enough, and how to handle findings that seem to disagree.

Almost nothing here is new material. You already know Step 0, the triad, the perfection scan, the review, the refinements, and the chart record. What is new is the final weighing: the rule-governed act that turns the chart record into a finished reading. The method is a short list of rules about rank, role, and conflict. We will state those rules, source them, and then use them on Chart B, Chart A, Chart C, and the new Chart D.

Every answer line in your journal now reads *provisional, pending Lesson 10's method*. By the end of this lesson, those entries become finished six-part readings.

## The method, end to end

Here is the full method in order. It follows Cattan's own sequence: "after that we have judged by the houses, figures, angles, companies, aspectes, the way of point, and by all other sortes and manners before saide, nowe resteth it to judge by the parte of fortune" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 21).

**Step 0, inspect the first house** (Lesson 5). Before anything else: Rubeus or Cauda Draconis rising, and the chart is recorded, marked unjudged, and the question rests. Anything else, and you proceed.

**Step 1, the question and its house** (Lesson 5). The question exactly as asked, the house it lives in, the one-sentence defence of the assignment. Everything downstream keys to this step, because the question always has the last word: the same chart answers a marriage question and a money question differently, and a misplaced question misplaces the whole reading after it.

**Step 2, the triad** (Lesson 6). The Witnesses and the Judge, with points and parities verified; the summary in one line (beginning, course, end); the table's row for the question's matter, in the column's own words; and the standing doctrines: when Populus is Judge, the summary is withheld and the reading passes to the angles; and the triad concerns the *matter*, not yet whether the thing asked for reaches the asker.

**Step 3, the significators and their springings** (Lessons 5 and 7). Querent's figure and house, quesited's figure and house, every repetition of both located, and the chart's other repetitions noted: the chart record of doubled figures that the scan is about to consume.

**Step 4, the scan** (Lesson 7). The six-step perfection scan, in order, every mode tested and every result recorded: occupation, conjunction, company, the aspect line, the conclusion. This is the hinge: do the significators meet, or do they remain apart? Perfection found, and the thing asked for reaches the asker in the mode's manner and the figure's quality; perfection denied, and it does not. Denial is an answer, as complete as yes.

**Step 5, the review and the later techniques** (Lessons 8 and 9). The weights, in Cattan's order of march: strength of place and dignity for the significators and the findings that touch them; the aspects they behold and suffer, read by the figures found in the beholding houses; then the later techniques: the way of the point traced or honestly absent, the Part of Fortune computed and weighed figure-with-house, the timing estimate with its uncertainty written into the timing line. Refinements, not foundations, every one, entered as weights and details, never as answers.

**Step 6, the weighing and the answer** (this lesson). Rank the findings by the nine rules below, decide what each finding is allowed to decide, and write the finished reading in six parts: answer, manner, end, timing, advice, and limits.

The worksheet maps to these steps: question, triad, significators, scan, strength and quality, answer. Step 0 stands before the worksheet.

Much of Crowley's order is familiar: demand and house, Witnesses and Judge, the figure in the required house and its repetitions, aspects, the fourth house for the end, and the Part of Fortune last for money questions. The difference is the final step. Crowley says to judge "according to the balance of Good and Evil": put each finding into a good or evil column and decide by the heavier side. This course does not use that flat balance. Cattan's cases rank findings by what they are entitled to decide: perfection decides whether the thing reaches the asker, the triad describes the course and end, and the rest qualifies. The nine rules make that ranking explicit.

## The nine rules of weighing

These nine rules are the lesson's doctrine. Where a rule is source-direct, the source is named. Where a rule is the course's synthesis of source practice, it is flagged as synthesis. The order matters.

**Rule 1. Perfection alone decides whether the thing reaches the asker.** The yes half is Cattan direct: the modes of perfection are his chapters 6 through 9, and his practice performs them: the Lord of Clermont's suit won "especially, for that the occupation was good," the Lord of Ferte's marriage takes effect from the springings of the first and seventh (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, chs. 6 and 9). The denial half you have held since Lesson 7: no perfection, no meeting of significators; denial is an answer. And the rule's sharp edge, *alone*, has a negative corollary this course states outright: **the triad cannot make the significators meet, and cannot undo a meeting already shown by perfection.** That corollary is the course's synthesis, flagged as such: Cattan never writes the sentence, but his chapter of the Judge confines the office to "the ende of all the signification of the demaund" (Book III, ch. 4), limiting language, an end-role and not the role of joining the parties. Lesson 6 drew exactly this boundary when it taught you that the triad concerns the matter's issue while the houses hold the parties and their relation. Chart C, below, is this rule's example.

**Rule 2. The Judge rules the end and manner of the whole matter; the Witnesses the beginning and the course.** Source-direct, both halves: the Judge "is procreated of the two witnesses to judge the ende of all the signification of the demaund… if the Judge be good, the signification of the demaund shall come to a good ende, and if he be ill, it shall come to an ill ende" (Book III, ch. 4); the Right Witness signifies the querent's side and "the thing lately passed," the Left Witness "the thing to come, and put in question" (Book III, ch. 3). The triad's role is *trajectory* (how the business begins, how it is carried, how it concludes) and within that role it is supreme: no house can amend a Judge on the question of how the matter ends, any more than a Judge can amend the houses on whether the asker gets the thing asked.

**Rule 3. The quality of the perfecting figures describes how a yes happens.** Source-direct, from the occupation chapter: a good figure occupying "is the best signe that may be. But if the figure be ill, it is an ill signe." In Cattan's example, two figures of Fire show that an accord will be made, but "not… without fraude or deceipt" (Book III, ch. 9). The mode answers *whether*; the figure answers *in what manner*.

**Rule 4. Angles outweigh succedents, succedents cadents; and strength is not goodness.** The first half is source-direct: the declaration of places gives the angles strongest, the succedents mean, the cadents "weak and feeble" (Book III, ch. 10), and Lesson 5 has had you classifying by it since the houses were new. The second half, that strength and goodness are independent axes, so that a finding must be graded on both before it is weighed at all, is the course's articulation, flagged: it is Lesson 8's doctrine, distilled from Cattan's practice rather than his statement, and Chart C's seventh house is its standing proof: ill, ill-suited, and excellently resourced.

**Rule 5. Aspects and dignities add weight to findings; they never reverse the scan's substance.** Course synthesis, flagged (this is Lesson 8's central discipline restated as a rule of rank) and defended from the sources' conduct: in every one of Cattan's worked cases, the aspects arrive after the houses and qualify what the houses have established. In the Trivulsio peace case below, an ill trine *confirms and colors* a refusal the first and seventh houses have already shaped; in the Novelaire case, the good trines and sextiles soften the timing of a death the angles have already decreed; nowhere, in any case this course has found, does a regard overturn a perfection or un-deny a denial. An aspect is a channel and a weight. It tunes the answer; it does not own one.

**Rule 6. The later techniques detail and break ties; they never replace foundations.** The order is source-direct: the way of the point and Part of Fortune come late in Cattan's sequence (Book III, ch. 21). The force of the rule is course synthesis from the cases. In the Lord of Tays' horse-buying reading, mixed foundations are tipped by later techniques; in the robbery case, the houses identify the servant and the way of the point adds a detail. Refinements are not foundations.

**Rule 7. Every house speaks for its own department: the fourth gives the end of the matter; in lawsuits, the ninth and tenth show counsellors and judges.** The fourth-house part is the modern comparison's most useful contribution, stated plainly in the method you just read, "add the meaning of the figure in the 4th, to signify the end of the matter" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. VII), and it is fully consonant with Cattan, whose fourth-house chapter judges inheritances, grounds and conclusions by exactly that house's figure (Book II, ch. 4). The lawsuit part is Cattan direct, from the Clermont reading: "Because therefore that the ninth is a good figure and well accompanied with an other that is good, which is the tenth: this signifyeth that the Counsellours and Judges shall give sentence" (Book III, ch. 9). The rule generalizes what Lesson 5 always taught: the wheel is a map of departments, and a reading that needs to know who profits, where the cost falls, or by whose hand the end comes, asks the house whose department it is. The fourth's "end of the matter" and the Judge's "end" are different findings in different registers: the Judge rules how the whole business concludes; the fourth house describes the state of things at its conclusion. They can agree, qualify one another, or price one another, and Chart D shows the pricing at full scale.

**Rule 8. Rank first, count second.** Crowley's balance counts good and evil findings. Cattan ranks them. The course's rule is synthesis: **clear perfection with a concurring triad outweighs any number of subtle findings; counting decides only among findings of the same rank.** If two regards of the same rank pull opposite ways, count them and consider kinship. Do not let a low-rank tally appeal over a higher-rank finding.

**Rule 9. The question always has the last word.** Every finding is finding about the question asked. Carcer means one thing in a journey question and another in a question about imprisonment. A fortune-point in the eighth house means one thing for money and another for marriage. The rules above tell you how findings rank; this rule tells you what they are ranked for. A well-weighed reading that answers the wrong question still fails.

Together, the rules answer Lesson 9's open questions. Perfection decides whether the thing reaches the asker. The triad describes the course and end. Places give strength and timing. Houses speak for their departments. A divided chart is usually not a contradiction; its findings answer different parts of the question. The Judge does not decide whether the significators meet, and the houses do not rewrite the Judge's end.

## The method in use

First the rules at work in the hands that taught them. These chapters, met in Lesson 8 for their aspect doctrine, each carry a complete worked reading end to end (the course quotes two entire, the third by its result), a model of the prose a finished reading lives in.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-15-of-the-tryne-aspect-chapt" />

The Trivulsio case asks whether peace will be made. The first figure springs to the third, so there will be negotiation. An ill aspect from the fifth says there will be no peace at this time. The ninth, house of churchmen, is good, so the Cardinal will do what he can. The seventh is ill, so the Emperor will have no peace. The Witnesses and Judge concur in the unfavorable course. The second going into the eighth shows the King's loss of substance; the trine from the eighth to the twelfth says the Emperor will not keep what he wins. The structure is clear: answer, manner, end, timing, and cost, each from the finding entitled to give it.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-17-of-the-aspect-of-opposition" />

The Novelaire case asks whether the Count is dead. Cattan starts from the first and seventh: they agree in element, so "the saide Countie was not dead." Then he turns to the end of the matter. The fourth and tenth "be contraries and of great enmitie," so "he should die of this disease." The good trines and sextiles do not reverse that end; they say he will live for a time after the wound. Other figures agree with the tenth, Carcer, which Cattan says always signifies death in this place; Acquisitio in the eighth and Conjunctio as Judge support the same result. The answer is that he is not dead yet, but he will die from the hurt. The reading gives answer, manner, end, timing, and verification, with each part sourced.

The third model, the Emperor's departure from Nice (Book III, ch. 16), you may reread in your own copy (Lesson 8 printed it entire) and it earns its place in this trio for a different virtue: it is the tradition's best surviving timing reading. Asked which day the Emperor would depart, Cattan reads fixity against motion exactly as Lesson 9's procedure does: Acquisitio in the first, "a fygure fixed," of one planet with the fixed tenth: "I judged that the Emperour shall not part thence in sixe dayes"; the witnesses and Judge fixed figures: "he should stay there fiue or sixe daies"; and around the timing core, the departments fill in the circumstances: the seventh and eighth retrograde and ill ("his long stay… should be much to his hinderance"), the fifth for the country he would go to, the ninth and eleventh ill for borrowed money, the first springing into the house of sadness for the end of the voyage: "a short returne with dishonour, losse, and discomfyture of his men." The timing part is delivered with a number and a hedge ("fiue or sixe daies") and verified at eight; the tradition's own best practice states pace with uncertainty inside the timing line, exactly as your procedure requires.

Three cases, one method. In none of them does Cattan count good against evil across the whole chart; in all of them he assigns: this finding answers whether the thing happens, that finding describes the end, this one shows the cost, that one shows the time. The answer assembles the assignments. That is the prose your journal is about to learn to write.

## The four charts, read

Now fill the answer column. The chart records are complete and the rules are stated. Each reading below ends in the six-part form, which Exercise 7 has you copy into the journal over the struck-through word *provisional*.

### Chart B: the partnership, denied and done

The easiest final answer this course will ever ask of you: nothing in Chart B's chart record needs to be re-decided, because every instrument, at every level, has said the same thing; what Lesson 9 left provisional is promoted on the chart record's own unanimity.

Rule 1: the scan finds no occupation, no conjunction, no joining company, and no aspect between the significators. Perfection is denied, so the partnership is not restored. Rule 2 agrees: Tristitia, Cauda Draconis, Via gives a sorrowful beginning, departing course, and end in change over a question of restoration. The table gives substance very ill and the thing lost not found. Rules 4 and 5 show the stalemate is strong and present. Rule 6 agrees: the way of the point ends on Carcer in the fifth, and the Part of Fortune is mean on Cauda Draconis in the tenth. Nothing material dissents.

The finished reading: **Denied: the partnership is not recovered.** The manner: the matter remains fixed, with no party moving toward meeting. The end: change, not restoration. The timing: slow, with a later alteration; no date. The advice: release the question and build around the change. The limits: this judges only recovery of the partnership, not the partner, money owed, or any other question.

Strike through *provisional* in Chart B's chart record and write the six parts beneath it. That is the first promotion, and the form of all the rest.

### Chart A: the marriage, yes and qualified

Chart A says the marriage takes effect, but not easily.

Rule 1: the chart perfects by conjunction, because the quesited's Via stands in the second house beside the querent. The marriage takes effect. Rule 3 qualifies the meeting: it happens by approach, through Via, a mobile figure. Rule 2 gives the trajectory: Rubeus, Tristitia, Acquisitio: ill begun, hard carried, ending in gain. The table row for the bond reads *a wife: ill*, so the yes is not easy. Rules 4 and 5: both significators are angular and mobile, so the meeting is strong and in motion. Rule 6: the way of the point is absent, and the Part of Fortune is good in the fourth on Caput Draconis. Rule 7 reads the fourth as the end of the matter, favorable here. Rule 9 keeps the answer on the question asked: *shall it take effect*, and it shall.

The finished reading: **Yes: the marriage takes effect.** The manner: ill begun and hard carried, with friction around the match. The end: gain; the Judge and fourth house agree that the conclusion is favorable. The timing: swift rather than slow, and lasting once concluded. The advice: proceed, but handle the early difficulty and the money clearly. The limits: the table's *a wife: ill* remains part of the reading; the marriage takes effect, but it will require work to keep.

### Chart C: the divided case

Six lessons brought Chart C here: the conflict seen, located, sharpened and reserved for this lesson by name, the failed perfection added, the denial's key finding measured and found strong, the chart record completed. Nothing remains to gather. Weigh.

<HouseChart mothers={['amissio', 'amissio', 'puella', 'fortuna-minor']} caption="Chart C in the twelve houses, the divided chart: Amissio doubled in 1 and 2, Cauda Draconis in 7 and 11, Caput Draconis in the 10th, and over it all the Judge Acquisitio." />

**First, the answer from perfection: Rule 1.** The scan denies victory. There is no occupation and no conjunction. Company appears only as Amissio with Amissio in the 1·2 pair, joining the querent to his own Loss. The only regard between the significators is an unkinned opposition. **The querent does not prevail.** No favorable triad finding can amend that, because the Judge never joins significators.

**Second, the force of the denial: Rule 4.** Lesson 8's review now matters. The Tail in the seventh is angular, doubled into the eleventh, and strongly attended by aspect. The adversary holds strong ground. Strength is not goodness, but strength serving a denial makes the denial forceful. On the querent's side the same review reads the cost: Amissio is doubled into the second, so his person and purse both carry Loss. The suit drains his substance whether or not it reaches a decision.

**Third, the triad: Rule 2, in its own role.** Caput Draconis, Albus, Acquisitio: well begun, quietly carried, ends in gain; the table's column fair in nearly every row. For six lessons this has looked like a contradiction. Apply the rule and it is not one. The triad describes the end and manner of the *matter* (the suit as a business in the world). It does not decide whether the querent wins. The suit concludes; it concludes well; it concludes in gain. The scan has already shown that the gain is not the querent's victory. Both are true.

**Fourth: whose gain? Rule 7: follow the fortune to its department.** The Part of Fortune sits on Caput Draconis in the tenth: good figure, good house. In a lawsuit, the tenth is the house of magistrates and judges. Cattan reads the good ninth and tenth in the Clermont case as a sign that the counsellors and judges will give sentence (Book III, ch. 9). Here the authorities are disposed to act. The matter ends by decision or settlement from above. The gain promised by the Judge falls to the tenth-house side, not to the querent.

**Fifth, the manner of it: Rules 2 and 5 on the adversary's side.** Lesson 8 left a question standing: does the Tail's strength deny the querent, help the adversary, or show the suit ending badly for both? The weighing gives a mixed answer. Cauda Draconis is strong in the suit's own house, so the suit itself carries the figure of departure. The matter goes out swiftly, because both significators are mobile, and finally, because the Judge is stable. The adversary prevails only in the narrow sense that the querent does not. The suit ends, and the parties bear its cost.

**Sixth, the answer.** Write it in the journal in full:

**Denied: the querent does not prevail.** The matter still ends, by decision or settlement from above, swiftly and finally. It costs him in substance, because his figure doubles Loss into the house of goods. The Judge's gain falls to the tenth-house authorities, not to him. Advice: do not pursue a decision; settle while the tenth is favorable and disposed to end the matter fairly. Limits: this answers whether he prevails, not the merits of the case.

The point of Chart C is simple: a divided chart is not necessarily a contradiction. Perfection denies the querent's victory; the triad promises a good end; the Part of Fortune shows where that good falls; and the review explains the cost and timing. No finding cancels another. Each stays in its role.

## Worked example

Chart D is the first chart you meet with the complete method already in hand. It is read from a standing start, the way later practice charts will be.

The question, framed and defended: **"Will the querent get the office they seek?"** A man seeks an appointment: an office, a dignity, a place under authority. The house: the tenth, honours, offices, magistracy, "the office or dignitie which he shall have" in Cattan's own tenth-house words; one sentence, defensible, done. (The novelty, by Rule 9: the first three charts all asked seventh-house questions, and Lesson 7 warned against letting that accident harden into a rule. Here the quesited is the *tenth*, and every test below runs first-to-tenth.)

The four Mothers, cast for this question: **Acquisitio, Fortuna Major, Via, Amissio.** The shield:

Verify before you touch it. Witnesses Fortuna Major and Populus, six points and eight: same parity, as must be. Judge Fortuna Major, six points: even, as must be. The chart is true. Deal it into the houses:

**Step 0.** The first house holds Acquisitio: neither Rubeus nor Cauda Draconis. Proceeds.

**Step 1.** The question and its house, as framed above: the office, the tenth. Querent's figure: **Acquisitio (1st)**. Quesited: the tenth holds Acquisitio. That is Step 4's business; the method's discipline is not to leap there yet.

**Step 2, the triad.** Right Witness Fortuna Major; Left Witness Populus; Judge Fortuna Major. Populus is a Witness here, not the Judge, so the recusal doctrine does not apply. The summary begins in Fortuna Major, is carried by Populus, and ends in Fortuna Major. For an office question, use the table's *worship* row:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/fortuna-major--populus" />

Worship: good. The triad could hardly speak fairer. The summary rules the end; perfection decides whether the office reaches the querent. Scan.

**Step 3, the significators and the springings.** Querent Acquisitio in the first: angular, stable, the figure of gain in the asker's own seat. Quesited Acquisitio in the tenth: angular, stable, the same figure. The full chart record of repetitions: **Acquisitio in 1 and 10; Fortuna Major in 2 and 5; Amissio in 4 and 6; Carcer in 11 and 12.** Four doubled figures, eight houses spoken for: a chart almost as densely rhymed as Chart A, and every rhyme about to matter.

**Step 4, the scan.** Occupation: the tenth holds Acquisitio, and the first also holds Acquisitio. **Yes.** The querent's figure stands in the house of the office. Occupation is the strongest mode, and the occupying figure is good. Cattan says that if the first figure is good and passes into the house of the thing demanded, it is "the best signe that may be" (Book III, ch. 9). For completeness: there is no conjunction; the 1·2 pair has only light kinship; the 11·12 pair is Carcer with Carcer, noted for Step 5. **The chart perfects by occupation of a good figure: the querent obtains the office.**

**Step 5, the review and the instruments.** Both significators are angular, so the perfection is strong. The first and tenth behold one another by square, but Acquisitio stands at both ends of the aspect. The office is won by effort, and the effort belongs to the querent. The main qualification is Amissio in the fourth, opposing the tenth: the end of the matter carries loss or cost. Amissio also doubles into the sixth. The way of the point completes in houses 1 and 2, so the hidden root is the querent himself and his substance; the office grows from his own merit and means. The Part of Fortune totals 68, remainder **8**, the eighth house on **Cauda Draconis**: an ill figure in an ill house. In an office question, this points to disappointment around fees, dues, or other people's money. Carcer doubled in the eleventh and twelfth shows slow friends and hidden hindrances. Timing: the querent, quesited, and Judge are stable, so the matter is sure but not swift.

**Step 6, the weighing and the answer.** Rule 1 shows the office is obtained by occupation, the strongest mode. Rule 3 strengthens the answer because the occupying figure is good. Rule 2 concurs: the triad begins and ends in Fortuna Major, and the table row gives *worship: good*. Rule 7 keeps the qualification in its proper place. The Judge gives a favorable end to the whole matter, but the fourth house, the state of things at the conclusion, holds Amissio. The result is not "mixed" in the flat sense. He gets the office and keeps the standing, but the keeping costs him. Cattan's fourth-house entry for Amissio is severe, "This figure is ill for all the demaunds which may be made in this house" (Book II, ch. 4), while the tenth-house entry for Acquisitio is directly favorable:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-10/acquisitio" />

"The man shall have the honor he pretendeth… it is good for the office or dignitie which he shall have." The placement, scan, and triad agree. Rule 6 keeps the instruments in place: the ill Part of Fortune and the Carcer pair describe the cost of that yes; they do not reverse it. The completed way adds that the office rests on the querent and his means. Rule 8 needs no counting here. Rule 9 keeps the answer on the question asked: whether he obtains the office.

The finished reading: **Yes: the querent obtains the office he seeks.** The manner: by his own merit and means, with effort, slow help from friends, and some hidden hindrance. The end: favorable; the standing holds once gained. The timing: sure but not swift; no date. The advice: take the office, but enter it provisioned and do not rely on quick revenue from it. The limits: Amissio in the fourth and the ill Part of Fortune show cost around the conclusion and the office's money. The yes is clear, and so is its cost.

Chart C and Chart D show the method in opposite cases. Chart C is a denial with a favorable triad. Chart D is a yes with real limits. The same rules handle both.

## The finished reading: the six parts

The answers above all use the same shape: the **final-answer procedure**. Every finished reading from now on has six parts, in order. A part may be short, but it may not be absent:

1. **The answer.** Yes or no, in the question's own words, from the scan, by Rule 1; first, always, because the querent asked a question and the first words of a reading answer it. No throat-clearing, no "the chart suggests."
2. **The manner.** How the matter begins and is carried, and how the yes or no takes shape: the Witnesses' indications, the quality of the perfecting figures (Rule 3), the springings read by their houses, the review's texture. The answer's adverbs live here.
3. **The end.** The Judge's summary of the whole matter's issue, and the fourth house's end-of-the-matter where the question wants it (Rules 2 and 7); held distinct when they differ, and the difference stated, as Chart D's answer stated it.
4. **The timing.** Pace and direction from mobility, entrance and place, by Lesson 9's procedure, with the uncertainty written into the timing line. Never a date.
5. **The advice.** What the querent should *do*. The chart usually supplies this through the strongest open path or the clearest warning.
6. **The limits.** The qualification the chart itself demands: the table-row that stays ill inside a yes, the favorable finding that remains inside a denial, or the question the reading deliberately does not answer. This part marks the reading's limits so the querent cannot mistake them.

Answer, manner, end, timing, advice, limits. Lesson 11 uses this form, the capstone requires it, and your journal uses it from now on. Part one comes from Rule 1, part two from Rules 3 through 5, part three from Rules 2 and 7, part four from the timing procedure, and parts five and six turn the answer into usable advice with clear limits.

## The journal, promoted

The last procedure upgrade is simple.

1. **Every provisional answer is promoted.** For each of the four course charts and every cast in the journal: re-run Step 6 against the standing chart record (the chart record is complete; this is weighing, not gathering) and write the finished reading in the six-part form directly beneath the provisional line. Strike through *provisional, pending Lesson 10's method*; leave it legible. The strike-through is the record of a discipline kept.
2. **Every new cast gets a finished reading.** From this lesson forward the procedure runs: cast, verify, Step 0, chart record, weighing, finished reading, all six parts, the same day. The era of the deferred answer is over; what remains permanently is its ethic: no part the chart record cannot defend.
3. **The worksheet runs whole.** Steps 1 through 6, every chart you take to a finished answer, the assembled paragraph copied into the journal, no longer marked *dry run*.
4. **The answers are dated and final, and finality has a discipline of its own.** A finished reading is not revised when the outcome arrives; it is *checked*. Where the matter resolves in the world, record the outcome beside the reading, as Cattan recorded Lyon and Provence and the wound in the belly: hits and misses alike, in the same ink. The misses are part of the training.

## Delivering the ill answer

Chart B and Chart C now have final denials. A denial must be said plainly, but it should not be said bare. The querent needs the answer, the manner, the end, the timing, the advice, and the limits. A softened denial misleads; a bare denial is less useful than the chart allows. The advice part helps the querent act: settle while the tenth is favorable; build where the change leads; stop searching and secure the household. The limits part marks what was not judged. Say the negative answer clearly, give the advice the chart supports, and leave the limits visible.

## Where you stand

You can now take a chart from sixteen rows of points to a finished, defended, six-part reading. Every part has an address: a rule, a house, an instrument, or a source. The answer column is now full: Chart B denied; Chart A yes and qualified; Chart C denied but resolved by role; Chart D yes with limits.
