Everything before this lesson was preparation. This lesson teaches the finished reading method, resolves Chart C, and judges a fourth chart, Chart D, from a standing start. It answers the questions Lesson 9 deferred: what outranks what, how much evidence is enough, and how to handle findings that seem to disagree.

Almost nothing here is new material. You already know Step 0, the triad, the perfection scan, the review, the refinements, and the chart record. What is new is the final weighing: the rule-governed act that turns the chart record into a finished reading. The method is a short list of rules about rank, role, and conflict. We will state those rules, source them, and then use them on Chart B, Chart A, Chart C, and the new Chart D.

## The method, end to end

Here is the full method in order. It follows Cattan's own sequence: "after that we have judged by the houses, figures, angles, companies, aspectes, the way of point, and by all other sortes and manners before saide, nowe resteth it to judge by the parte of fortune" (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, ch. 21).

**Step 0, inspect the first house** (Lesson 5). Rubeus or Cauda Draconis rising, and the chart is set aside unjudged; anything else, and you proceed.

**Step 1, the question and its house** (Lesson 5). The question exactly as asked, the house assigned, the one-sentence source note. Everything downstream keys to this step, because the question always has the last word.

**Step 2, the triad** (Lesson 6). The Witnesses and Judge, points and parities verified; the one-line summary; the table's row for the question's matter; and the standing rules: Populus as Judge withholds the summary, and the triad concerns the *matter*, not yet whether the thing reaches the asker.

**Step 3, the significators and their springings** (Lessons 5 and 7). Querent's figure and house, quesited's figure and house, every repetition located.

**Step 4, the scan** (Lesson 7). The six-step perfection scan, every mode tested and every result recorded. This is the hinge: perfection found, and the thing reaches the asker in the mode's manner and the figure's quality; denied, and it does not. Denial is an answer, as complete as yes.

**Step 5, the review and the later techniques** (Lessons 8 and 9). Strength of place and dignity; the aspects, read by the figures found in the beholding houses; then the way of the point, the Part of Fortune, and the timing estimate. Refinements, not foundations, entered as weights and details, never as answers.

**Step 6, the weighing and the answer** (this lesson). Rank the findings by the nine rules below, decide what each finding is allowed to decide, and write the finished reading in six parts.

The worksheet maps to these steps: question, triad, significators, scan, strength and quality, answer. Step 0 stands before the worksheet.

Much of Crowley's order is familiar. The difference is the final step: Crowley judges "according to the balance of Good and Evil," each finding in a good or evil column, decided by the heavier side. This course does not use that flat balance. Cattan's cases rank findings by what they are entitled to decide: perfection decides whether the thing reaches the asker, the triad describes the course and end, and the rest qualifies. The nine rules make that ranking explicit.

## The nine rules of weighing

These nine rules are the lesson's method. Where a rule is source-direct, the source is named. Where a rule is the course's synthesis of source practice, it is flagged as synthesis. Use the rules in this order.

**Rule 1. Perfection alone decides whether the thing reaches the asker.** The yes half is Cattan direct. The modes of perfection are his chapters 6 through 9, and his practice performs them: the Lord of Clermont's suit won "especially, for that the occupation was good," and the Lord of Ferte's marriage takes effect from the springings of the first and seventh (Cattan, *The Geomancie* (London, 1591), Book III, chs. 6 and 9).

The denial half is the rule you have held since Lesson 7: no perfection, no meeting of significators; denial is an answer. This course states the limit plainly, as synthesis: **the triad cannot make the significators meet, and cannot undo a meeting already shown by perfection.** Cattan never writes that sentence, but his chapter of the Judge confines the office to "the ende of all the signification of the demaund" (Book III, ch. 4), an end-role, not the role of joining the parties. Lesson 6 drew the same boundary: the triad concerns the matter's issue, while the houses hold the parties and their relation. Chart C, below, is this rule's example.

**Rule 2. The Judge rules the end and manner of the whole matter; the Witnesses the beginning and the course.** Source-direct, both halves: the Judge "is procreated of the two witnesses to judge the ende of all the signification of the demaund… if the Judge be good, the signification of the demaund shall come to a good ende, and if he be ill, it shall come to an ill ende" (Book III, ch. 4); the Right Witness signifies the querent's side and "the thing lately passed," the Left Witness "the thing to come, and put in question" (Book III, ch. 3). The triad's role is *trajectory* (how the business begins, how it is carried, how it concludes) and within that role it is supreme: no house can amend a Judge on the question of how the matter ends, any more than a Judge can amend the houses on whether the asker gets the thing asked.

**Rule 3. The quality of the perfecting figures describes how a yes happens.** Source-direct, from the occupation chapter: a good figure occupying "is the best signe that may be. But if the figure be ill, it is an ill signe." In Cattan's example, two figures of Fire show that an accord will be made, but "not… without fraude or deceipt" (Book III, ch. 9). The mode answers *whether*; the figure answers *in what manner*.

**Rule 4. Angles outweigh succedents, succedents cadents; and strength is not goodness.** The first half is source-direct: the declaration of places gives the angles strongest, the succedents mean, the cadents "weak and feeble" (Book III, ch. 10). The second half, that strength and goodness are independent axes, so that a finding must be graded on both before it is weighed at all, is the course's articulation, flagged: it is Lesson 8's rule, distilled from Cattan's practice rather than his statement, and Chart C's seventh house is its proof: ill, ill-suited, and excellently resourced.

**Rule 5. Aspects and dignities add weight to findings; they never reverse the scan's substance.** Course synthesis, flagged (this is Lesson 8's central discipline restated as a rule of rank) and supported by the sources' conduct: in every one of Cattan's worked cases, the aspects arrive after the houses and qualify what the houses have established. In the Trivulsio peace case below, an ill trine *confirms and colors* a refusal the first and seventh houses have already shaped; in the Novelaire case, the good trines and sextiles soften the timing of a death the angles have already decreed; nowhere, in any case this course has found, does a regard overturn a perfection or un-deny a denial. An aspect adds weight and color; it does not decide the answer.

**Rule 6. The later techniques detail and break ties; they never replace foundations.** The order is source-direct: the way of the point and Part of Fortune come late in Cattan's sequence (Book III, ch. 21). The force of the rule is course synthesis from the cases. In the Lord of Tays' horse-buying reading, mixed foundations are tipped by later techniques; in the robbery case, the houses identify the servant and the way of the point adds a detail. Refinements are not foundations.

**Rule 7. Every house speaks for its own department: the fourth gives the end of the matter; in lawsuits, the ninth and tenth show counsellors and judges.** The fourth-house part comes from the modern comparison: "add the meaning of the figure in the 4th, to signify the end of the matter" (Crowley, *A Handbook of Geomancy*, The Equinox I(2) (London, 1909), ch. VII). It agrees with Cattan's fourth-house practice, where inheritances, grounds, and conclusions are judged by that house's figure (Book II, ch. 4).

The lawsuit part is Cattan direct, from the Clermont reading: "Because therefore that the ninth is a good figure and well accompanied with an other that is good, which is the tenth: this signifyeth that the Counsellours and Judges shall give sentence" (Book III, ch. 9).

The rule generalizes what Lesson 5 taught: the wheel is a map of departments. If a reading needs to know who profits, where the cost falls, or by whose hand the end comes, ask the house whose department it is. The fourth's "end of the matter" and the Judge's "end" are different findings: the Judge rules how the whole business concludes; the fourth house describes the state of things at its conclusion. Chart D shows the difference at full scale.

**Rule 8. Rank first, count second.** Crowley's balance counts good and evil findings. Cattan ranks them. The course's rule is synthesis: **clear perfection with a concurring triad outweighs any number of secondary findings; counting decides only among findings of the same rank.** If two regards of the same rank pull opposite ways, count them and consider kinship. Do not let a low-rank tally appeal over a higher-rank finding.

**Rule 9. The question always has the last word.** Every finding is finding about the question asked. Carcer means one thing in a journey question and another in a question about imprisonment. A fortune-point in the eighth house means one thing for money and another for marriage. The rules above tell you how findings rank; this rule tells you what they are ranked for. A well-weighed reading that answers the wrong question still fails.

Together, the rules answer Lesson 9's open questions. A divided chart is usually not a contradiction; its findings answer different parts of the question. The Judge does not decide whether the significators meet, and the houses do not rewrite the Judge's end.

## The method in use

First the rules at work in the source cases. These chapters, met in Lesson 8 for their aspect rules, each carry a complete worked reading end to end. The course quotes two in full and the third by its result. They show how a finished reading assigns each finding to its proper role.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-15-of-the-tryne-aspect-chapt" />

The Trivulsio case asks whether peace will be made. The first figure springs to the third, so there will be negotiation; an ill aspect from the fifth says no peace at this time; the ninth is good, so the Cardinal will do what he can; the seventh is ill, so the Emperor refuses; the second going into the eighth shows the King's loss of substance. Answer, manner, end, timing, and cost, each from the finding entitled to give it.

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/ch-17-of-the-aspect-of-opposition" />

The Novelaire case asks whether the Count is dead. The first and seventh agree in element, so "the saide Countie was not dead"; the fourth and tenth "be contraries and of great enmitie," so "he should die of this disease"; the good trines and sextiles do not reverse that end, they stretch its timing. The answer: not dead yet, but he will die of the hurt.

The third model, the Emperor's departure from Nice (Book III, ch. 16, printed in Lesson 8), is the sources' best surviving timing reading. Asked which day the Emperor would depart, Cattan reads fixity against motion: Acquisitio in the first, "a fygure fixed," with fixed Witnesses and Judge, so "he should stay there fiue or sixe daies," and around the timing core the departments fill in the circumstances, down to "a short returne with dishonour, losse, and discomfyture of his men." The timing is delivered with a number and a hedge, and verified at eight days.

Three cases, one method. In none of them does Cattan count good against evil across the whole chart; in all of them he assigns: this finding answers whether the thing happens, that finding describes the end, this one shows the cost, that one shows the time. The answer assembles the assignments. That is the form a finished reading takes.

## The four charts, read

The chart records are complete and the rules are stated. Each reading below ends in the six-part form.

### Chart B: the partnership, denied and done

The easiest final answer in the course, because every tool at every level has said the same thing. Rule 1: no occupation, no conjunction, no joining company, no aspect between the significators; perfection is denied, so the partnership is not restored. Rule 2 agrees: a sorrowful beginning, departing course, and end in change. Rules 4 and 5 show the stalemate is strong and present, and Rule 6 agrees: the way ends on Carcer in the fifth, and the Part of Fortune is mean on the Tail in the tenth. Nothing material dissents.

The finished reading: **Denied: the partnership is not recovered.** The manner: the matter remains fixed, with no party moving toward meeting. The end: change, not restoration. The timing: slow, with a later alteration; no date. The advice: release the question and build around the change. The limits: this judges only recovery of the partnership, not the partner, money owed, or any other question.

That is the first finished answer, and the form of all the rest.

### Chart A: the marriage, yes and qualified

Chart A says the marriage takes effect, but not easily.

Rule 1: the chart perfects by conjunction, so the marriage takes effect. Rule 3 qualifies the meeting: by approach, through Via, a mobile figure. Rule 2 gives the trajectory: ill begun, hard carried, ending in gain, with the table row *a wife: ill*. Rules 4 and 5: both significators angular and mobile, so the meeting is strong and in motion. Rule 6: no way of the point, and a good Part of Fortune in the fourth. Rule 7 reads the fourth as the end of the matter, favorable here. Rule 9 keeps the answer on the question asked: *will it take effect*, and it does.

The finished reading: **Yes: the marriage takes effect.** The manner: ill begun and hard carried, with friction around the match. The end: gain; the Judge and fourth house agree that the conclusion is favorable. The timing: swift rather than slow, and lasting once concluded. The advice: proceed, but handle the early difficulty and the money clearly. The limits: the table's *a wife: ill* remains part of the reading; the marriage takes effect, but it will require work to keep.

### Chart C: the divided case

Six lessons brought Chart C here: the conflict located, the perfection denied, the denial's key finding measured and found strong, the chart record completed. Nothing remains to gather, so weigh.

<HouseChart mothers={['amissio', 'amissio', 'puella', 'fortuna-minor']} caption="Chart C in the twelve houses, the divided chart: Amissio doubled in 1 and 2, Cauda Draconis in 7 and 11, Caput Draconis in the 10th, and over it all the Judge Acquisitio." />

**First, the answer from perfection: Rule 1.** The scan denies victory: no occupation, no conjunction, company only as Amissio with Amissio joining the querent to his own Loss, and an unkinned opposition between the parties. **The querent does not prevail.** No favorable triad finding can amend that, because the Judge never joins significators.

**Second, the force of the denial: Rule 4.** Use Lesson 8's review here. The Tail in the seventh is angular, doubled into the eleventh, and strongly attended by aspect. The adversary holds strong ground. Strength is not goodness, but strength serving a denial makes the denial forceful. On the querent's side the same review reads the cost: Amissio is doubled into the second, so his person and purse both carry Loss. The suit drains his substance whether or not it reaches a decision.

**Third, the triad: Rule 2, in its own role.** Caput Draconis, Albus, Acquisitio: well begun, quietly carried, ends in gain. For six lessons this has looked like a contradiction; apply the rule and it is not one. The triad describes the end and manner of the *matter*; it does not decide whether the querent wins. The suit concludes well and in gain, and the scan has already shown that the gain is not the querent's victory. Both are true.

**Fourth: whose gain? Rule 7: follow the fortune to its department.** The Part of Fortune sits on Caput Draconis in the tenth: good figure, good house. In a lawsuit, the tenth is the house of magistrates and judges. Cattan reads the good ninth and tenth in the Clermont case as a sign that the counsellors and judges will give sentence (Book III, ch. 9). Here the officials are disposed to act. The matter ends by decision or settlement from above. The gain promised by the Judge falls to the tenth-house side, not to the querent.

**Fifth, the manner: Rules 2 and 5 on the adversary's side.** Cauda Draconis is strong in the suit's own house, so the suit itself carries the figure of departure: the matter goes out swiftly (both significators mobile) and finally (the Judge stable). The adversary prevails only in the narrow sense that the querent does not. The suit ends, and the parties bear its cost.

**Sixth, the answer.** In full:

**Denied: the querent does not prevail.** The matter still ends, by decision or settlement from above, swiftly and finally. It costs him in substance, because his figure doubles Loss into the house of goods. The Judge's gain falls to the tenth-house officials, not to him. Advice: do not pursue a decision; settle while the tenth is favorable and disposed to end the matter fairly. Limits: this answers whether he prevails, not the merits of the case.

The point of Chart C is simple: a divided chart is not necessarily a contradiction. Perfection denies the querent's victory; the triad promises a good end; the Part of Fortune shows where that good falls; and the review explains the cost and timing. No finding cancels another. Each stays in its role.

## Worked example

Chart D is the first chart you meet with the complete method already in hand. It is read from a standing start, the way later practice charts will be.

The question, framed and checked: **"Will the querent get the office they seek?"** The house: the tenth, "the office or dignitie which he shall have" in Cattan's tenth-house words. The first three charts all asked seventh-house questions; here the quesited is the *tenth*, and every test runs first-to-tenth.

The four Mothers, cast for this question: **Acquisitio, Fortuna Major, Via, Amissio.** The shield:

Verify before you touch it. Witnesses Fortuna Major and Populus, six points and eight: same parity, as must be. Judge Fortuna Major, six points: even, as must be. The chart is true. Deal it into the houses:

**Step 0.** The first house holds Acquisitio: neither Rubeus nor Cauda Draconis. Proceeds.

**Step 1.** The office, the tenth. Querent's figure: **Acquisitio (1st)**. Quesited: the tenth holds Acquisitio.

**Step 2, the triad.** Right Witness Fortuna Major; Left Witness Populus; Judge Fortuna Major. Populus is a Witness here, not the Judge, so the Populus rule does not apply. The summary begins in Fortuna Major, is carried by Populus, and ends in Fortuna Major. For an office question, use the table's *worship* row:

<Quote id="cattan/book-3/witness-judge/fortuna-major--populus" />

Worship: good. The triad could hardly speak fairer. The summary rules the end; perfection decides whether the office reaches the querent. Scan.

**Step 3, the significators and the springings.** Querent Acquisitio in the first, angular and stable; quesited Acquisitio in the tenth, the same figure. The repetitions: **Acquisitio in 1 and 10; Fortuna Major in 2 and 5; Amissio in 4 and 6; Carcer in 11 and 12.**

**Step 4, the scan.** Occupation: the tenth holds Acquisitio, and so does the first. **Yes.** Occupation is the strongest mode, and the occupying figure is good: "the best signe that may be" (Book III, ch. 9). For completeness: no conjunction; the 11·12 pair is Carcer with Carcer, noted for Step 5. **The chart perfects by occupation of a good figure: the querent obtains the office.**

**Step 5, the review and the later checks.** Both significators are angular, so the perfection is strong. The first and tenth behold one another by square, but Acquisitio stands at both ends of the aspect. The office is won by effort, and the effort belongs to the querent. The main qualification is Amissio in the fourth, opposing the tenth: the end of the matter carries loss or cost. Amissio also doubles into the sixth. The way of the point completes in houses 1 and 2, so the hidden root is the querent himself and his substance; the office grows from his own merit and means. The Part of Fortune totals 68, remainder **8**, the eighth house on **Cauda Draconis**: an ill figure in an ill house. In an office question, this points to disappointment around fees, dues, or other people's money. Carcer doubled in the eleventh and twelfth shows slow friends and hidden hindrances. Timing: the querent, quesited, and Judge are stable, so the matter is sure but not swift.

**Step 6, the weighing and the answer.** Rule 1: the office is obtained by occupation. Rule 3 strengthens the yes, since the occupying figure is good. Rule 2 concurs: the triad begins and ends in Fortuna Major, with *worship: good*. Rule 7 keeps the qualification in its place: the Judge gives a favorable end, but the fourth house, the state of things at the conclusion, holds Amissio. He gets the office and keeps the standing, but the keeping costs him. Cattan's fourth-house entry for Amissio is severe ("This figure is ill for all the demaunds which may be made in this house," Book II, ch. 4), while the tenth-house entry for Acquisitio is directly favorable:

<Quote id="cattan/book-2/house-10/acquisitio" />

"The man shall have the honor he pretendeth… it is good for the office or dignitie which he shall have." Rule 6 keeps the later checks in place: the ill Part of Fortune and the Carcer pair describe the cost of the yes; they do not reverse it. Rule 9 keeps the answer on the question asked: whether he obtains the office.

The finished reading: **Yes: the querent obtains the office he seeks.** The manner: by his own merit and means, with effort, slow help from friends, and some hidden hindrance. The end: favorable; the standing holds once gained. The timing: sure but not swift; no date. The advice: take the office, but enter it provisioned and do not rely on quick revenue from it. The limits: Amissio in the fourth and the ill Part of Fortune show cost around the conclusion and the office's money. The yes is clear, and so is its cost.

Chart C and Chart D show the method in opposite cases. Chart C is a denial with a favorable triad. Chart D is a yes with real limits. The same rules handle both.

## The finished reading: the six parts

The answers above all use the same shape: the **final-answer procedure**. Every finished reading from now on has six parts, in order. A part may be short, but it may not be absent:

1. **The answer.** Yes or no, in the question's own words, from the scan, by Rule 1; first, always. No throat-clearing, no "the chart suggests."
2. **The manner.** How the matter begins and is carried, and how the yes or no takes shape: the Witnesses, the quality of the perfecting figures (Rule 3), the springings. How, not whether.
3. **The end.** The Judge's summary, and the fourth house's end-of-the-matter where the question wants it (Rules 2 and 7); held distinct when they differ.
4. **The timing.** Pace and direction by Lesson 9's procedure, uncertainty stated. Never a date.
5. **The advice.** What the querent should *do*, from the strongest open path or the clearest warning.
6. **The limits.** The qualification the chart itself demands: the table-row that stays ill inside a yes, the favorable finding inside a denial, or the question the reading does not answer.

Answer, manner, end, timing, advice, limits. Lesson 11 uses this form and the final practice requires it.

## Delivering the ill answer

Chart B and Chart C receive final denials. A denial must be said plainly, but it should not be said bare. The querent needs the answer, the manner, the end, the timing, the advice, and the limits. A softened denial misleads; a bare denial is less useful than the chart allows. The advice part helps the querent act: settle while the tenth is favorable; build where the change leads; stop searching and secure the household. The limits part marks what was not judged. Say the negative answer clearly, give the advice the chart supports, and leave the limits visible.
