### To know for what intent a figure found was made, which is called the point of instruction. Chap. 18.



<div class="source-locator source-locator--block">p. 190</div>

This is a thing of no small difficultie and consequence howe to tell for what purpose a Fygure hath beene made, for it may happen sometimes that a person may finde a fygure, and knoweth not for what intent and ende it hath beene made, neyther findeth any inscription in the same, whereby he may know the cause. But this rule that I will hereafter shewe you shall serve to that purpose, if that ye doe well understand and practise the same. I will not here recite the opinion of all the Doctours in this Arte, for that woulde be an infinite worke, but I will shewe it unto you as briefly as I can. The first rule howe to know the intent, is, to see when the first house goeth into any of the other, and to judge according unto the signification of the house where she goeth. An other rule is to take all the uneven points of all the twelve figures, and give one to the first, one to the second, one to the third, and so consequently unto all the others, untill that all the points be bestowed, and then if the last point remaine on the first house, it signifyeth thereby that the person hath desired to have that figure to be made upon some of the demaundes which be of the first house: if it rest upon the second, it signifyeth that the question or demaunde is of moveable goodes, or other things contained in the second house: and so shal you Judge of the other houses where the point doth stay. And if it doe happen that the point of the intent doe stay in the house of the thing demaunded, or in the fift, yee must judge according to the signification that the Judge doth shew unto you: and when ye will judge by the same Judge, you must also take the uneven points of the witnesses and the judge, and bestowe them amongest them: but that rule which is onely by the 12. houses, is the better, more sure and certaine, as ye shall see by the example following.

<div class="source-locator source-locator--block">p. 191</div>

[chart: a complete geomantic scheme pricked in star-points within a ruled frame — eight figures in the first row, four in the second, two witnesses in the third, and the Judge alone beneath]

Upon a day a Gentleman desired me to give judgement on a fygure which he had found, and because that neither he nor I knew the cause why it was made, I tooke all the uneven points of the twelve houses, and gave them (as before I have saide) to eache fygure one, unto the twelfth: and the uneven points, being in number foure and twentie, and thereby having twice run over all the fygures, I found the last point to fall upon the 12. fygure, so that I judged that the fygure had beene made uppon the imprisonment of some person. But if the point of the intent fall upon the Judge, you shall judge the fygure as the Judge is, be it good or badde. Yet there is another rule how to judge the point of the intent which I will nowe tell you: take the first and seaventh fygures, and out of them take an other, and judge them according as they be, from whence the fygure was taken, as by example: if the fyrst be Populus, and the seventh be Aquisitio, and of these two commeth an other Aquisitio, you shall judge according to the signifycation of that Aquisitio: and so shall you doe of the other Fygures which come of the fyrst and seaventh.
