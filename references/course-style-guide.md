# Course Plain Style Guide

This guide governs prose in the course lessons, course support pages, course components, and course-facing script comments. Its purpose is plain informative style: clear, source-aware, and direct, without legal, arch, archaic, elevated, or portentous phrasing.

The course can use historical language when it quotes historical sources. The course's own prose should not imitate that language.

## Core Standard

Write the sentence a careful teacher would say to a serious student.

- Prefer common words over formal ones.
- Prefer concrete roles over grand labels.
- Prefer short claims with source support over ceremonial framing.
- Keep the argument; remove the posture.
- Do not make the prose sound older because the sources are old.

The course is allowed to be exact, severe, and technical. It should not sound legal, priestly, academic for its own sake, or artificially antique.

## Plain Instructional Prose

Plain instructional prose always has priority. Explain the rule, give the source or example, and state how to use it.

Avoid literary emphasis, metaphors, and dramatic contrast in the course's own prose. Do not make a rule sound larger than it is by calling it the engine, root, key, signal, edge, chain, machinery, or hidden structure of the lesson unless that word is literally needed.

Bad:

> This is the engine of the whole method: parity, the irreducible residue of a random act.

Good:

> The rule reduces each row to odd or even. Count the row in pairs. If one point is left over, write one point. If none is left over, write two.

Bad:

> The value of the course lives in the gap between the attempt and the answer.

Good:

> Do the exercise before reading the answer. You learn by comparing your written attempt with the model answer.

Avoid:

- writerly abstractions such as `lowest visible link`, `irreducible residue`, `signal hygiene`, `the sharp edge`, `the pull`, and `the machinery`
- dramatic phrases such as `at a stroke`, `in one breath`, and `not X, not Y, but Z`
- metacommentary such as `what matters is`, `what matters now`, `this matters`, and `the point is`
- skeptical distancing such as `you do not need to believe this`, `suspend disbelief`, and `your own affair`
- personifying charts, houses, figures, sources, or lessons unless the phrase is a necessary technical shorthand
- redundant pacing language such as `no dates and no cohort; speed is irrelevant`

If something matters, state the rule directly. If it does not matter, cut the sentence.

Bad:

> What matters is not the medium but the conditions.

Good:

> Use any medium, but keep the casting conditions.

Bad:

> You do not need to believe this. What you make of it afterwards is your own affair.

Good:

> Study the course inside the worldview of the sources. Do not translate the rules into psychology or coincidence while learning them.

Use technical terms when they belong to the method. `Traditional` is allowed when it is accurate. Do not replace it with a weaker term.

## Preserve Technical Terms

Do not simplify terms that are part of the method.

Keep:

- `querent`
- `quesited`
- `significator`
- `Witness`, `Judge`, `Right Witness`, `Left Witness`
- `perfection`
- `occupation`, `conjunction`, `company`, `aspect`
- `dignity`, `angle`, `succedent`, `cadent`
- `Part of Fortune`
- `way of the point`
- figure names, house names, source titles, quote IDs, chart labels, and chart data

When a technical term first appears in a lesson, explain it plainly. After that, use it without apology.

## Do Not Modernize Quotes

Leave quoted source text alone, including `shall`, old spelling, and old syntax. The point is to preserve the source.

Modernize the course's own framing around the quote.

Good:

> Cattan says the agreement "shall continue long." The course reads that as a lasting agreement.

Bad:

> Cattan's doctrine provides authoritative confirmation of the agreement's continuance.

## Preferred Replacements

Use these replacements unless the old word is inside a direct quote, source title, site name, or other proper name.

| Avoid | Prefer |
|---|---|
| the art | geomancy, the method, the system |
| tradition | sources, older texts, source line, source set |
| doctrine | rule, method, idea, material |
| instrument | tool, check, technique |
| authority | source, source weight, official |
| authorities | sources, officials |
| adjudicate | decide, weigh, choose |
| canon | rule, source set, standard list |
| canonical | standard |
| hierarchy | reading order, ranking |
| subtle | later, secondary, finer |
| practicum | final practice |
| taxonomy | classification, map, list |
| delineation | entry |
| defend, defence | support, source note, explain |
| defensible | supportable |
| evidence | source support, support, finding |
| lawful | valid, allowed |
| licence | permission, source support |
| on the record | recorded, in the journal, noted |
| promote, promotion | finish, update |
| testify, testifies, testified | show, indicate, support, appear |
| testimony | source support, support |
| term of art | technical term |
| verdict | answer |
| witness | source, example, support |
| shall | will, should, must, does |

Use `shall` only inside direct quotation or when naming a quoted historical question exactly.

Use lower-case `witness` only for ordinary English inside a quotation. Keep the technical chart terms `Witness`, `Witnesses`, `Right Witness`, and `Left Witness`.

## Titles And Slugs

Lesson titles should name what the lesson does.

Changes already made:

- `The Subtle Doctrines` became `Later Techniques`.
- `The Questions of Life` became `Question Types`.
- `The Geomancer's Practicum` became `Final Practice`.

Use direct lesson titles in this style. Avoid titles that sound ceremonial, antique, or grander than the work.

Do not add redirect pages for old lesson links unless the project decision changes. The current rule is: old course links do not need to be maintained.

## Headings

Headings should be short and procedural when the section teaches an action.

Changes already made:

- `The journal, upgraded` became `Update the journal`.
- `Where you stand` became `Next`.
- `The instrument: placement lookup` became `Use placement lookup`.
- `The hierarchy, defended` became `The reading order, supported`.

Prefer verbs when the section asks the student to do something.

## Source Support

The course should keep its source discipline, but phrase it plainly.

Use:

- `support the assignment from the sources`
- `source note`
- `source-direct`
- `course synthesis`
- `flagged as the course's own`
- `supported from Cattan's practice`

Avoid:

- `defend the placement`
- `carry authority`
- `canonical doctrine`
- `licence`
- `pedigree`
- `testify`
- `on the record`
- `adjudicate`

Keep the source standard. State it without legal ceremony.

## Reading Order

Use `reading order` or `ranking`, not `hierarchy`.

The concept remains the same:

- foundations decide;
- refinements refine;
- findings answer only the question they are entitled to answer;
- counting never outranks a higher-order finding.

Good:

> The reading order keeps each finding in its role.

Bad:

> The hierarchy enforces the superior authority of the primary doctrine.

## Course Voice

Prefer direct instruction.

Good:

> Write the source note beside the house assignment.

Bad:

> The student shall furnish a defensible account of the house's authority.

Good:

> If the chart gives no perfection, the answer is no.

Bad:

> In the absence of perfection, the doctrine forbids an affirmative judgment.

## Compress Lesson Setup

Do not make a lesson transition narrate the course's memory before stating the point. Say what the lesson teaches or what the student should do.

Good:

> Lesson 6 teaches how to read the two Witnesses and the Judge at the foot of the shield.

Bad:

> The next step is the triad at the foot of the shield. The two Witnesses and the Judge have stood there since Lesson 4, derived, named, and unread. Lesson 6 reads them.

Use this pattern across the course:

- Replace `The next step is...` with `Lesson N teaches...`.
- Replace `What remains is...` with a direct next task.
- Avoid suspense words such as `awaits`, `unread`, `has stood`, and `has been waiting`.
- Avoid lesson archaeology unless it prevents confusion. A prerequisite may be named once, but the sentence should not rehearse the whole course path.
- Keep useful worked-example recaps, but shorten any recap that delays the action.

## Serious Subjects

Keep difficult material plain. Do not soften the answer by becoming vague, and do not heighten it by becoming portentous.

Use:

- `serious class`
- `he will not recover`
- `this disease is unto death` when the phrase is part of the course's established reading form
- `say the answer plainly`

Avoid:

- `dire class`
- `mortal doctrine`
- `fateful judgment`
- `the authorities of death`

## Source Comparisons

When sources disagree, say so directly.

Good:

> The sources divide. Cattan supports the yes; the pseudo-Agrippa gives the contrary finding. Record the minority finding and explain why it does not decide the answer.

Bad:

> The tradition preserves a divergence of authority whose doctrinal weight must be adjudicated.

## Frontmatter

Frontmatter should follow the same style as the lesson body.

- Titles should be short and literal.
- Descriptions should state the lesson's work.
- Objectives should begin with active verbs.
- Quote objective strings that contain a colon, so YAML does not parse them as objects.

Good:

```yaml
title: Question Types
description: The main question classes, their houses, and the four labeled ways to turn a chart.
objectives:
  - "Complete three readings: a fresh class, a divided chart, and one question answered in both systems"
```
