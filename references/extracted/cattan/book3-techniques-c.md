# Book III, Chap. 18 — To know for what intent a figure found was made, which is called the point of instruction. Chap. 18.
- leaves: 224, 225, 226, 227 (begins at head of leaf 224; closing paragraph at head of leaf 227, where Chap. 19 follows)
- pages: 190, 191, 192, 193 (running heads: "The third Booke" / "of Geomancie.")

[p. 190]

This is a thing of no small difficultie and consequence howe to tell for what purpose a Fygure hath beene made, for it may happen sometimes that a person may finde a fygure, and knoweth not for what intent and ende it hath beene made, neyther findeth any inscription in the same, whereby he may know the cause. But this rule that I will hereafter shewe you shall serve to that purpose, if that ye doe well understand and practise the same. I will not here recite the opinion of all the Doctours in this Arte, for that woulde be an infinite worke, but I will shewe it unto you as briefly as I can. The first rule howe to know the intent, is, to see when the first house goeth into any of the other, and to judge according unto the signification of the house where she goeth. An other rule is to take all the uneven points of all the twelve figures, and give one to the first, one to the second, one to the third, and so consequently unto all the others, untill that all the points be bestowed, and then if the last point remaine on the first house, it signifyeth thereby that the person hath desired to have that figure to be made upon some of the demaundes which be of the first house: if it rest upon the second, it signifyeth that the question or demaunde is of moveable goodes, or other things contained in the second house: and so shal you Judge of the other houses where the point doth stay. And if it doe happen that the point of the intent doe stay in the house of the thing demaunded, or in the fift, yee must judge according to the signification that the Judge doth shew unto you: and when ye will judge by the same Judge, you must also take the uneven points of the witnesses and the judge, and bestowe them amongest them: but that rule which is onely by the 12. houses, is the better, more sure and certaine, as ye shall see by the example following.

[p. 191]

[chart: a complete geomantic scheme pricked in star-points within a ruled frame — eight figures in the first row, four in the second, two witnesses in the third, and the Judge alone beneath]

Upon a day a Gentleman desired me to give judgement on a fygure which he had found, and because that neither he nor I knew the cause why it was made, I tooke all the uneven points of the twelve houses, and gave them (as before I have saide) to eache fygure one, unto the twelfth: and the uneven points, being in number foure and twentie, and thereby having twice run over all the fygures, I found the last point to fall upon the 12. fygure, so that I judged that the fygure had beene made uppon the imprisonment of some person. But if the point of the intent fall upon the Judge, you shall judge the fygure as the Judge is, be it good or badde. Yet there is another rule how to judge the point of the intent which I will nowe tell you: take the first and seaventh fygures, and out of them take an other, and judge them according as they be, from whence the fygure was taken, as by example: if the fyrst be Populus, and the seventh be Aquisitio, and of these two commeth an other Aquisitio, you shall judge according to the signifycation of that Aquisitio: and so shall you doe of the other Fygures which come of the fyrst and seaventh.

# Book III, Chap. 20 — Of the way of point. Chap. 20.
- leaves: 228, 229, 230, 231 (begins mid leaf 228; concludes at head of leaf 231)
- pages: 194, 195, 196, 197

[p. 194]

The Geomanciens have many Rules to judge their figures aright, and amongest others, that which they doe call the way of point, as a thing much necessarie and profitable in this Arte. After therefore that the figure is made with the witnesses and the Judge, you shall take the first point of that Judge, be it even or odde: and afterwardes the points of the right witnes, if it be like unto the said Judge, if not, then take the points of the left Judge, if it be like unto the Judge. But if the two witnesses have not points above like to the said Judge, then is there no way of point in y[e] figure: but if it happen that one of the witnesses be like to the Judge, and that in the upper parte the points be even or uneven, as the said judge is, yee shal take the 9. or 10. fygure, whether of those two be like to the Judge. And

[p. 195]

if it be the 9. you shall also have regard to the first & second: if the point on high be like unto that of the Judge, witnesses and ninth, you shall determine the question for the which the figure hath beene made. By the first house, if the way of point goe into the second, ye shall judge according to the second, & then if it be good ye shal judge good, and if it be ill you shal judge ill. But if the way of point goe into the tenth, you must have respect unto the third and fourth, to whether of them that is like in the points on high of the Judge and witnesses. And so shall you judge all the figure, according unto that which is like unto the Judge. And if the left witnes be like unto the Judge, you shall take the eleventh or twelfth, whether of them is like unto the Judge: and then if it be the eleventh, ye shall have respect unto the fift and sixt, and according unto that of the two shall be like on high in points unto the judge, witnesses and eleventh house you shall judge all the question propounded. And if it be good, ye shall judge it to good, and if it be evill, ye shall judge it to evill. If the twelfth have like points on high unto the witnesses and the judges, yee shall have respect unto the seaventh and eight, and according to that which of them twaine shall be like, you shall judge (as before is saide) that is, to good if it be good, and unto ill if it be ill. For ye must understand that the way of point intendeth properly unto one point, for when they be two it is not so perfite as when they be found in one: and behold this example.

[p. 196]

A figure and an example of the way of point.

[chart: a complete scheme pricked in star-points within a ruled frame — eight figures in the first row, four in the second, the two witnesses in the third, and the Judge beneath]

A Friend of mine desired me to make a figure for to knowe, whether that a servant of his had robbed him or not: and by chance I made him this figure which ye here see. And bicause that therein the first house is not very good, and because that in the house wherein the demaunds of servants be made, which is the sixt, I found an ill fygure called Cauda draconis, the which doth signifie robbery, I judged that the servant was a theefe. And bicause the seventh which is an ill figure signifies the same: and because the second which signifieth the substance of the master doth goe into that which containeth the demaundes which be upon substance: I judged that it was a signe that the Maister had beene robbed by the servant before his saide Maister did knowe thereof, and had stolen much more then was missed. And because that the regard Trine and Sextile be figures of ☿ and ☉, I say that the servant should be taken in doing the facte, which thing the fourth doth also signifie the same. And because

[p. 197]

the way of point goeth into one of the figures of ☉, by that it is manifest that the saide servant shall be discovered by means of the goodes which he hath given unto his kinsfolkes. This is the way to judge a figure by the way of point.

# Book III, Chap. 21 — Howe to knowe the parte of fortune.
- leaves: 231, 232, 233 (begins mid leaf 231; concludes at head of leaf 233) — [no chapter number is printed with this heading]
- pages: 197, 198, 199

[p. 197]

The question being made, after that we have judged by the houses, figures, angles, companies, aspectes, the way of point, and by all other sortes and manners before saide, nowe resteth it to judge by the parte of fortune, the which fashion of judgement is very necessarie and profitable. The part of fortune is taken on this wise, by accompting the points of the 12. figures, which afterwards ye must divide into twelve partes, and that which remaineth give unto the figures. As if there rest two ye must give it unto the second figure, if there do remaine foure to the fourth figure, if it be sixe to the sixt figure, if it bee eight to the eight, if it be ten to the tenth fygur, if it be twelve to the twelfth fygure. As by example, if the fygure be of 72. points, or 84. or 96. or of 108. points, then the parte of fortune shall goe into the twelfth. But if the saide points of fygure made, being divided by twelve, there doe remaine but two, as if there remaine seaventie and foure where there remaineth but two, then (as before we have saide) ye must give that unto the second house, and there shall be the parte of fortune. The which if the fygure and house be good (for both the one and the other must be looked uppon) you shall judge good, and if it bee evill ye shall also judge evill: and so likewise shall ye doe of all the other fygures. But if the fygure be good, and the house ill, or contrary, the house good and the fygure ill, you shall judge the saide parte of fortune to be meane. And, to the end ye may the more easier knowe the place where the fygure falleth, which is called the parte of fortune, ye shall marke it with this marke ☼, and thereafter ye shall judge all the question for the which the saide Figure was made, as ye shall see by the example that followeth.

[p. 198]

An example and figure made in the favour of my Lorde of Tays to knowe the parte of fortune.

[chart: a complete scheme pricked in star-points — eight figures in the first row, four in the second; against the second row in the left margin stands the label "the part of For-tune" with the mark ☼ set beside the twelfth-house figure (the leftmost of that row); in the third row the two witnesses, the left headed "second" with the numbers 73, 16, 88 set beside its lower rows [figure: 2,1,1,2 — Conjunctio], the right headed "first" with 24, 24, 24 beside its lower rows [figure: 1,1,1,1 — Via]; beneath, the Judge [figure: 1,2,2,1 — Carcer]]

Upon a day my Lorde of Tays minding to buy a horse, commaunded me to make a fygure, to knowe if that the horse that he should buy were good or bad: and I minding to pleasure him, as unto my lorde and maister, made for him this present figure, and because that therein the first figure and his companion be good figures, and likewise the fourth and seaventh, and the Angles for the better parte were good, I judged indifferent well, but bicause the aspects were ill, and the opposition meane, and that the way of point went into an ill fygure: and also because the parte of fortune happened on an ill fygure and house, I judged it to be ill. And because the twelfth house is the house wherein be contained the questions and demaundes which may be put touching horses and other great beasts: and as touching the members of the body, those which may be put concerning the feete and legges, and forbecause that in that place I founde

[p. 199]

an ill fygure called Amissio, I judged that the horse had an evill foote, and was worth nothing: and so it was founde true in the Towne of Lyon. Many doe use an other manner to finde part of fortune, in taking all the points as well of the twelve houses as of the two witnesses, and the Judge, which they do part by twelve (as is aforesaide) but because I have founde no trueth therein I will speake no more thereof.
